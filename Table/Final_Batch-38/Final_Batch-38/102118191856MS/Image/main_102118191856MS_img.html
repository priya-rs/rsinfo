According to the prospective resources assessment performed by the CNH and based on previous Pemex analysis for unconventional plays in shales (PEMEX Exploración y Producción, 2014), there is five prospective provinces for oil-gas shale in Mexico (Figure 1)

In those provinces two potential horizons has been identified; Upper Jurassic, represented by the formations La Casita (northern portion) and Pimienta (southern portion), and Mid Cretaceous, known as Eagle Ford (in the north), Agua Nueva (in the center) and Maltrata (in the south) (Figure 2) (CNH, 2017).

 

The vertical monitor well was H-105 (an old exploratory well that produced from below conventional reservoirs), positioned approximately 90m from the horizontal section. Due to wellbore restrictions, VSI tool with 11 geophones were positioned between the depths of 2236m and 2536m, establishing distances between 270m and 600m (measured from the middle point of the seismic tool to the middle points of every stage) (Figure 4).

The hydraulic fractures are dominated by tensile failure mechanisms, which generally are slow and a-seismic processes (generally low amplitude, low frequency) [Taleghani et.al. 2011] and therefore, do not generate important acoustic energy. Often, these tensile failures are not detected during microseismic monitoring. Microseismic events of high amplitude are generally related to rapid events produced by induced shear failure mechanisms (Figure 5).

Pre-evaluation filtering using event QC parameters was conducted (orthogonality > 0.85 and biggest uncertainty in location < 100ft, Figure 7a). Large moment magnitude values (−2.5 to −0.5) and superior signal-to-noise ration (SNR) range (5 to 30) were observed; meanwhile, the uncertainties ranges (distance and depth) showed reasonable values. Figure 7b shows examples of magnitude, SNR and uncertainties histograms for the first five stages.

In addition to the above conclusions, Figure 9 shows the microseismic height of each stage and the M-1 computed logs; most of the event growth in a high stress zone above Pimienta Fm, behavior that also could be considered unexpected. Horizontal stress barriers are present below and above the target zone.

Following other case studies [Cipolla et.al. 2011], evaluation of the impact of SNR on microseismic event patterns and magnitude normalization (magnitude cutoff based on the lowest magnitude that can be detected farthest from the sensor array) were assessed. Microseismic events were filtered using different SNR and magnitude normalization cutoff values (−1.9); the main idea was to select high-SNR events and compare the differences between interpretations using all events or only filtered events. Figure 10 and Figure 11 show the comparison between (a) all events versus (b) filtered event (magnitude>-1.9 and SNR>30) for map view and south view, respectively. In Figure 11b, notice that filtered events are mainly located at Taraises Fm, far away from the horizontal section; and once again, notice that microseismic activity diminished or suddenly stopped at a certain depth (approximately 2470mTVD), despite VSI tool location.

Distance measured from the midpoint of the VSI tool to the event location allows us to define the distance–magnitude relationship, which indicates the detection limit for the events according to their magnitude. Figure 12a shows the magnitude-distance plot for all stages. The normalized line shows a magnitude value of −1.9 (minimum magnitude that is most likely detected at all distance).

Figure 13 shows magnitude vs. distance plots for the Taraises and Pimienta formations. A huge difference between the numbers of events detected within both formations can be observed. In addition, Taraises Fm exhibits events with higher magnitudes and greater distances than Pimienta Fm.

Distance measured from the middle of the perforation (stage) to the event location illustrates the evolution of microseismic event detection during pumping time. Figure 14 shows an example of normal behavior generally observed in this type of plot; two different wells (conventional and slickwater jobs) show how the microseismic events follow the fracture growth (length and height). It is also possible to observe fracture behaviors such as near wellbore screen-out (corroborated with treatment pressure trend).

For A-1 stages (Figure 15 shows examples), the distance vs. time plots showed microseismic event patterns with relatively constant distances during the entire pumping time. In some cases, the first events appeared far away from the perforations (200–300m). These behaviors (relatively constant distances during the entire pumping time), were observed at all the stages and could be interpreted as planes of weakness activated due to fracturing fluid leakoff or fluid compression (stress disturbance and shear failure). These pre-existing planes of weakness are located at specific distances from the perforations (stages) and will appear at relatively constant distances in a distance vs. time plot.

In the A-1 case, post-fracture events (after pumping stopped) were recorded over a considerable period of time (30–60min, Figure 16), and represent 26% of the total events recorded, which is a considerable quantity compared with what is usually observed.

Comparing the behaviors of the totality of the microseismic events versus post-fracture events (Figure 17a&b), similarities were found between features observed in both cases. This observation could means that these clouds are related to the activation of pre-existing planes of weakness within Taraises Fm, during pumping and after pumping stopped, rather than to a hydraulic fracture response.

The SH/P plot is used to identify fault–plane orientation and slip directions, assuming pure shear failure, and could help define the predominant type of failure mechanism (shear, tension, or a combination of both) and their alignment/orientation in what might be a plane of weakness or a hydraulic fracture. In the case of A-1, a 3D view of this amplitude ratio property (Figure 18) did not show evidence of nodal planes (blind spots in the microseismic event patterns).

Considering the first six stages (Figure 19a), clouds of events can be observed that seem to be aligned in what would be the same planes (faults), orientated with an approximate azimuth of N24E. The SH/P graph shows a predominance of shear failure mechanism and corroborates an alignment and preferential orientation.

The event patterns of last stages (Figure 20a) do not show a clear alignment; the general azimuth was N30W, but the clouds were more dispersed than in the first stages (some degree of complexity). In stage 10 (Figure 20b), the principal cloud presents a lack of clear alignment, and the SH/P plot shows a preferential azimuth (green line, N30W) and a secondary azimuth (blue line, N21E) with almost 60-degree difference between them (possible conjugate fractures). This behavior could be related to the activation of a natural fracture network. The following section shows that seismic ant-tracking corroborates this preliminary conclusion.

There are two types of diagnostic plots based on this methodology (Figure 21a&b); one is an histogram of the magnitude-frequency relationship, where the slope determines the "b" value (Figure 21a), and the other one is the graph of the evolution of the "b" value during pumping (Figure 21b), which can be used, for example, to distinguish at what time during pumping a fault was activated.

Figure 22a shows "b" value histograms for stages 3, 5, 9, and 11; for all these stages, the slopes have "b" values near to 1; therefore most of the events seem to be related to planes of weakness activation.

Logs from the M-1 well (old vertical well taken as pilot) showed a clear change at 2474mMD (Figure 23); at this depth, sonic logs, porosity, and density show clear deflections (Figure 23a), and static FMI shows an abrupt lithofacie change (Figure 23b), from a high resistivity formation (i.e., clean calcium carbonate composition) to an intercalation of conductive layers with possible clay presence.

Natural fracture distribution can be controlled by local structure (e.g., natural fracture character with respect to folding, or notable increase in natural fracture density along major structural features) [Miller et.al. 2011, Ramsay 1967]. In the A-1 case, the structural model (surfaces interpreted with well topes and seismic) corroborated the presence of an anticline (Figure 24a) or fold structure, which divided the horizontal well into the following three structural sections:

The final conclusion is that a complete structural feature was illuminated with the microseismic monitoring, showing the faulting associated with a fold structure. Figure 25a shows that: on the concave zone of the fold (heel of the horizontal section), compression dominates, leading to bedding-plane thrusts; on the limb zone (middle of the horizontal section), relaxation shows low-density faulting; and finally, on the convex side of the fold (toe of the horizontal section), tension produces normal extensional faults. Figure 25b only considers the filtered events by magnitude and SNR, making the different zones associated with the fold structure clearer. A change of azimuth observed along the lateral was not related to the hydraulic fracture, but to the local fold structure and its pre-existing plane of weakness (faults and natural fractures).

Figure 26 shows the surface seismic attribute "3D curvature", this attribute highlight areas where major deformations exist, it has been found to be useful in delineating complex faults or fractures related to fold structures. Positive curvature (Figure 26a red seismic line) represent an anticline; negative curvature (Figure 26a blue seismic line) represent a syncline, between them is the limb zone. Figure 26b shows the 3D curvature contours and the microseismic events, the dash line emphasizes the limits of the structure observed in the seismic attribute interpretation (Figure 26a).

Microseismic events directly related to hydraulic fractures (2% remaining events, Figure 27) were used to calibrate the unconventional fracture model (UFM) [Cipolla et.al. 2010], following the final hydraulic fractures characterization. UFM can simulate the interaction of hydraulic fractures with pre-existing natural fractures when they intersect (complex fracture network), also considering the interaction among adjacent hydraulic fracture branches by computing the stress shadow effect [Cipolla et.al. 2010].

For microseismic jobs, the estimated stimulated volume (ESV) or stimulated reservoir volume (SRV) parameter can be calculated using different algorithms based on distribution of events, event density, and other microseismic properties [Maxwell et.al. 2011]. Figure 28a and b show the acoustic volumes for the different stages; due to low event density, some stages have small acoustic volumes.

Finally, Figure 29 shows a summary table with all stage volumes (acoustic and fracture), and the average geometries for the hydraulic fracture network. Total volume values show that the fracture SRV (~20,600,000m3) is 50% less than the acoustic SRV (~43,100,000m3), detail that highlights the importance of performing hydraulic fractures simulations and do not base conclusion only on acoustic volumes. In addition, if all microseismic events are considered (directly and indirectly related to the hydraulic fractures), the total acoustic SRV will be 360,000,000m3 (almost 20 times the fracture SRV). Total fluid pumped was 15,000m3 for the 11 stages.
