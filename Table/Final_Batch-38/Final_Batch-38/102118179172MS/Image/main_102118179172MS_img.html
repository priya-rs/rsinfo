The Wattenberg Field is a basin-center petroleum accumulation located northeast of Denver, CO (Figure 1) (Nelson et al. 2011) from which hydrocarbons have been extracted over the last 50 years. Target horizons include the Dakota-Lakota, J sandstone, D sandstone, Greenhorn, Codell, Niobrara (A, B, C, and Fort Hays), Shannon, Sussex, and Parkman. Development in Wattenberg field began in 1970 from vertical J sandstone wells with production of the Niobrara following in 1976.

The upper Cretaceous sequence from Codell to Niobrara is the current focus of horizontal development in Wattenberg field. The formations were deposited in the Cretaceous interior seaway (Longman et al. 1998) and vary in lithology from silty sand (Codell) to limestone (Ft Hays) to interbedded chalks and marls (Niobrara). The stratigraphic column from Sonnenberg (2013), Figure 2, shows the depositional sequence and approximate thickness of each formation. Published estimates of resource in place of the Niobrara formation are between 2 and 4 billion barrels equivalent of oil (Sonnenberg, 2013).

Using a horizontal exposure of the Niobrara formation, a 2D map of the natural fracture system was generated (Figure 3). This image was digitized and compared to natural fracture interpretations from 11 vertical and 5 horizontal formation microimager logs to confirm that the general orientation and spacing/density of natural fractures observed at the surface were representative of the reservoir at depth and did not contain a significant exhumation overprint.

To construct a proper reservoir simulation grid that honors the resolution of the complex hydraulic fractures and the 3D mechanical earth model (MEM), the following gridding parameters were tuned: number of reservoir layers, fracture cell width, vertical layering factor (refinement of the 3D MEM based on hydraulic fracture results contrast) and zone height. The final grid used during this project included hydraulic fracture results from eight wells, had 14 layers, and consisted of approximately two and a half million cells (Figure 4)

As described above, in the process, the software combines production attributes with the mechanical earth model and the complex fracture planes to create a non-uniform, locally refined, cellular description of the stimulated reservoir (Figures 4 and 5). Cells are divided into regions defined by proppant type, i.e. matrix, unpropped and propped regions. In addition to the properties carried from the mechanical earth model, each of these regions can be defined by unique set of relative permeability and pressure-dependent permeability curves, as well as fluid/pressure initializations.

Different reservoir fluids with different petrophysical properties were included in the simulation. Multiple vertical wells were drilled and produced prior to the completion of the eight wells referred above; the effect of depleted areas was incorporated into the reservoir simulation model. Finally, compaction tables for each proppant region within the production grid were used to account for the loss of transmissibility associated to the decreasing pore pressure experienced during production. See Figure 6 for the initial grid states of both reservoirs.

Our study area is an eight-well pad in Wattenberg field targeting the Niobrara, Codell, and Ft Hays formations (Figure 2). The wells are drilled horizontally with ~7500-ft lateral lengths and ~330-ft interwell spacing with staggered targeting (making interwell spacing between like formations ~660 ft)(Fig. 7). Existing vertical wells typically target the J sandstone, Niobrara, and Codell formations and are relatively densely distributed throughout the pad.

Local refinements were made to the regional mechanical earth model over this location. Using the geosteering solution in conjunction with 3D seismic and offset vertical wells, an enhanced structural model was produced. Three DFITs from horizontal wells were collected within one mile of the study pad and analyzed for pore pressure and minimum horizontal stress magnitude. A 3D seismic volume was inverted for Poisson’s ratio, Young’s modulus, and porosity and sampled to the mechanical earth model (Figure 8). The regional DFN was supplemented with additional observations of fracture orientation inferred from microseismic focal mechanisms inverted from surface recording (Figure 9). Specifically, the primary fracture set was rotated 60° (Figure 3).

The wells were stimulated using a high-yield friction-reducing fluid with a viscosity similar to slick water and 40/70 mesh sand for proppant. Job size varied across the pad where Codell and Ft Hays wells were treated with 600 pounds of proppant per foot of lateral length (lb/ft) and Niobrara wells were treated with either 800 lbs/ft (small job) or 1600 lbs/ft (large job) as denoted in Fig. 10. Fluid job sizes on Codell/Ft Hays, small Niobrara, and large Niobrara treatments were 2300, 2700, and 4500 barrels/stage respectively.

The actual rate and proppant concentration for each of the 388 stages on the 8 wells was used as the input for the hydraulic fracture simulator. The simulation was run in a multi-well mode that accounted for stress shadowing between stages and between wells as a function of time and distance between proximal stages. The quality of treating pressure and ISIP matches varied from stage to stage, with a typical example shown in Fig. 11.

Fig. 12 shows the aggregated microseismic data in the XZ plane for large Niobrara completions, small Niobrara completions, Codell, and Ft Hays completions in the form of a heat map of microseismic frequency. Figure 10 shows several wells history matched to microseismic data. The model encompasses multiple targets and job sizes within a single mechanical earth model description and, without well-to-well modification, produces relatively close matches to the observed geometry.

The magnitude of pore pressure drawdown was assumed to be related to the cumulative production of the well and was disproportionately attributed to the Codell to match observations of lower initial pressure and higher gas-oil-ratio in horizontal Codell wells.
