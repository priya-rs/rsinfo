Figures 2 (a) and (b) show the typical workflow of IAM optimization. The optimization process is called “black-box” optimization. The non-linear solver is calling the integrated model (the black-box) multiple times. After each execution of the integrated model, the objective function and the constraints are evaluated and fed back to the solver. At each solver iteration, partial derivatives in respect of each variables may be required and estimated (when not analytically available) using finite differences, see Fig. 2 (c).

The integrated model is used to forecast optimal downhole (by well) and topside diluent requirements over time and foresee eventual bottlenecks in the surface infrastructure design. Results are shown in Fig. 5. The solution enables the additional recovery of 3 million std m3 (+ 6 % compared to the base case) while minimizing the need of downhole diluent injection. Topside diluent injection optimization contributes to lower OPEX by minimizing the quantity of diluent required topside.

The studied asset is located in South America and consists of seven fields including gas condensate, near-critical oil and volatile oil reservoirs. Each reservoir fluid is characterized with its own EOS model. A total of 85 production wells are connected to 5 processing facilities, see Fig. 7.

The molar rates coming from different wells are then aggregated together and fed to the process simulation model. Each processing facility is modelled within Pipe-It using a multi-stage separation model and simplified model for units involving energy balance (e.g. distillation tower), see Fig. 8. Process (p, T) conditions are given as user input for each separation vessel.

The well molar rates are then processed individually using the pseudo K-values as a proxy model for the process and volumetric contributions of individual wells can be calculated. The reason for using the pseudo K-values instead a full EOS process simulation when processing individual well lies in the influence of the feed stream composition on the process efficiency. The pseudo K-values capture that effect with a satisfying degree of accuracy. Figure 9 summarizes the proposed workflow.

The difference between simulated and fiscal rates reaches 20 % when using traditional back-allocation methods. When using a full compositional approach, the discrepancy drops to 2 % for oil and 3-4 % for gas, see Fig. 10. This significant improvement lies in the very detailed fluid description and stream management. Molecules are tracked from the well to the sales point and back-allocation is performed on a compositional basis rather than a volumetric basis.

The South Natuna Sea Block B (SNSBB) PSC, shown in Fig. 11, contains numerous oil and gas fields from various sub-basins in greater West Natuna. In the Western area of SNSBB, also referred to as the “dry gas” area, several fields are already on stream and other fields are in various stages of development.
