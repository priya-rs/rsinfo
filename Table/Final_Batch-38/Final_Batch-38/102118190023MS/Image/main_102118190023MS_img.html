As described, both dynamic fluid flow and pseudo-static particle deposition are involved in the fluid diversion process. The characterizations of the applied particle diverters, the geometry of the target openings and flow conditions can fundamentally dictate the diversion efficiency and hence should be included in the engineering design of the entire diversion treatments. Considering the complexities of the underlying mechanisms and availabilities of the modelling tools, an engineering design workflow and integrated analysis of particulate diverter applications is proposed, which is comprised of three major steps shown in Fig. 1.

As mentioned previously, an advanced simulator (CFD-DEM) was used in this work to model the dynamic plugging caused by particle accumulation, as shown in Fig. 2(a). With the embedded premium features of the simulator, the numerical model can accurately capture the phenomena with the opening (such as a single slot opening) and particles specifically tested in the lab.

Using the learnings from advanced modelling (CFD-DEM) and extensive field and experimental data, we can predict the critical concentration of particulate diverters demanded for an effective fluid diversion when subjected to a specific wellbore condition. An analytical model was proposed by Shahri et al. (2017b) to estimate the minimum required particle concentration. This model had been verified against CFD-DEM models and experiments from our analyses and other literature work (Lafond, 2013; Mondal, 2013; Gomma et al., 2016), as demonstrated in Fig. 3.

As suggested from both simulation and experimental results, two complementary mechanical processes, jamming and sealing, are identified when applying solid particles in fluid diversion (Gomaa et al., 2016; Shahri et al., 2016). By fully understanding these two dominant mechanisms, two sized particles (7 and 100 mesh respectively) are designed and used in this next generation particulate diverter system (Fragachán et al., 2016a and b), as shown in Fig. 4.

From our experiments, the diverter pills comprised of the mixture of two appropriately sized particles can effectively bridge off the openings with different geometries shown in Fig. 5(a). As observed from Fig. 5(b), the relatively large particles (7 mesh) can jam the opening as also suggested from our numerical simulations; meanwhile, the small particles (100 mesh) can occupy and fill the void space between the large particles to mitigate fluid passage, as illustrated in Fig. 5(c).

Fig.7 also shows that, if the small particle ratio is low (<55%), less pressure will be required to flow fluid through diverter pack due to its relative high porosity and permeability. However, if choosing higher value for small particle ratio (> 80%), the impermeable diverter pack cannot be created, as there are no sufficient large particles to form a stable jammed structure, which works as the skeleton to support the formation of this impermeable cake (as discussed in the previous sections and shown in Fig. 2 and 3). These findings agree well with the available field data, as presented in Fig.8.

After treatment, these next generation particulate diverters can self-degrade from a solid polymer state to a clear, non-damaging, liquid monomer solution, which eliminates the need for mechanical removal after intervention and hence greatly enhances operational efficiency. All the solids, including both large and small particles, can completely degrade into liquid within a controlled amount of time, as demonstrated in Fig.9.

The particle dissolution rate varies significantly with reservoir fluid type under the same temperature, as shown in Fig.10. Even the proper particle design can be obtained from numerical modeling and analysis, the particle degradation rate could significantly alter the performance of particulate diverter and the following wellbore clean-up. If the diverter is not compatible with the fracturing or reservoir fluid, it might dissolve very rapidly (probably less than an hour); thus the required differential pressure for fluid diversion cannot be maintained or built-up and failure of the re-fracturing operations could be faced.

Our ultimate goal is to design the applied diverter pill to enhance the probability of jamming and moreover withstand sufficient pressure to effectively re-direct the fluid, as illustrated in Fig. 11 without having to add excessive amount of diverter to achieve fluid diversion. Based on the presented modeling results, field experience and real-time monitoring of pressure response and microseismicity could help to optimize the field executions by adjusting the particle designs.

The perforation pressure drop would vary significantly with the diversion designs as shown in Fig.12. For the non-engineered design or complete failure of fluid diversion (Case-1), its Cd will be relatively high and close to the default value and no additional pressure drop can be obtained. Case-2 represents an improved design scenario, in which an obvious pressure drop can be created. If engineered properly (Case-1), Cd will be reduced to an extremely low value to further constrain the fluid passage through plugged perforations. Following, these three typical scenarios will be further tested through fracturing simulations to quantify the influence of diverter design on re-fracturing efficiency.
