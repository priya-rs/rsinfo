The produced oil is exported via pipeline to an oil terminal, and exported via shuttle tanker to the market. The produced gas is re-injected into the reservoir for pressure support and gas storage. The field achieved first oil production in December 2013, and had produced 43 MMSTB of crude oil as at December 2016 (13% current oil recovery factor). Figure 2 is a North-South schematic section of the Cretaceous and Tertiary reservoirs in the region between Cote d’Ivoire and Nigeria. Field E’s hydrocarbon fluids are located in the low permeability, unconsolidated Turonian sandstone reservoir formation, and the fluids are most likely sourced from Cenomanian and Albian black shales. The Turonian formation is of Upper Cretaceous age, underlies the prolific Tertiary formation seen in Nigeria’s Niger Delta oil fields, and was deposited in a turbidite environment.

The nine development wells were fitted with 13Cr 5.5 inch tubings and permanent downhole gauges. The five production wells were also fitted with gas lift valves for artificial lift and chemical injection valves for scale and hydrate management. Sand screens were installed in all of the nine wells for sand control, and typical lower completion lengths range from 2000 to 3000 feet measured depth. Figure 3 shows some examples of sand control solutions used in the petroleum industry.

The development wells were drilled from an offshore platform which can process 120 000 stock tank barrels per day (stb/d) of liquids, 100 million standard cubic feet per day (MMSCF/D) of gas, and 200 000 stb/d of water injection. Figure 4 shows typical offshore platforms used to produce oil and gas in the petroleum industry. The produced water is treated on Field E’s production platform and disposed overboard. Field E’s produced water rate is expected to rise over time because water injection is now the dominant reservoir drive mechanism in the field.

The A1 oil production well achieved first oil production in December 2013, and produced an initial oil rate of 9 300 stock tank barrels per day (stb/d). A1 produced dry oil (zero water cut) for three years and died in December 2016. Figure 5 is the production history plot created for the A1 well. Erratic oil production rates were observed during the life of the well, and most of these production upsets occurred during its late life between January 2016 and December 2016. These upsets could have been been due to sanding events, scale formation build up, or other reasons.

Figure 6 is the well diagnostic plot created for the A1 well for the period after January 2016. Stable bottom-hole pressures (BHP) and unstable tubing head pressures (THP) were observed after the well died in December 2016, and these suggested tubing restriction. The team validated the THP data by ensuring that the hydraulic master valve was open and the THP gauge was communicating with the well-bore.

Figure 7 is the sand count plot created for the A1 well. It shows that the A1 sand detector recorded over 20 sanding events during the well life, and these tallied with the unstable production incidents. Large volumes of sand and rocks were also recovered from the production separator during the life of the well.

The A2 oil production well achieved first oil production in December 2013, and produced an initial oil rate of 14 500 stock tank barrels per day (stb/d). A2 produced dry oil (zero water cut) for ten months and died in October 2014. Figure 8 is the production history plot created for the A2 well. The plot shows erratic oil production rates during the life of the well, and these upsets could have been been due to sanding events, scale formation build up, or other reasons.

Figure 9 is the well diagnostic plot created for the A2 well. The BHP gauge failed in April 2014, and this could have been caused by sand and rocks breaking the gauge. Stable BHP and unstable THP were observed after the well died in October 2014, and these suggested tubing restriction.

Figure 10 is the sand count plot created for the A2 well. It shows that the A2 sand detector recorded over 10 sanding events during the well life, and these tallied with the unstable production incidents. Further review of Figure 9 indicated that the frequency of the sanding events increased after the well choke opening was increased from 52% to 67% in February 2014. The choke was increased again to 91% in early October 2014, and the well died within a month. Large volumes of sand and rocks were also recovered from the production separator during the life of the well.

The A3 oil production well achieved first oil production in July 2014, and produced an initial oil rate of 7 900 stock tank barrels per day (stb/d). A3 produced dry oil (zero water cut) for four months and died in November 2014. Figure 11 is the production history plot created for the A3 well. Erratic oil production rates were observed during the life of the well, and these upsets could have been been due to sanding events, scale formation build up, or other reasons.

Figure 12 is the well diagnostic plot created for the A3 well. Unlike A1 and A2, stable BHP and THP pressures were observed in the A3 well after it died in November 2014. These suggested that the restriction in the well was upstream of the BHP gauge and could be in the lower completion area, at or near the sand screens.

Figure 13 is the sand count plot created for the A3 well. The A3 sand detector recorded over five sanding events during the well life, and indicated that A3 died after its biggest sanding event in November 2014. This sanding event was also the largest sand detection spike recorded in Field E to date.

The A4 oil production well achieved first oil production in December 2013, and produced an initial oil rate of 8 300 stock tank barrels per day (stb/d). Water breakthrough occurred in September 2014, and the A4 well died in March 2015 (15 months production, 20% water cut). Figure 14 is the production history plot created for the A4 well. Erratic oil production rates were observed during the life of the well, and these upsets could have been been due to sanding events, scale formation build up, or other reasons.

Figure 15 is the well diagnostic plot created for the A4 well. Stable BHP and unstable THP were observed after the well died in March 2015, and these suggested tubing restriction.

Figure 16 is the sand count plot created for the A4 well. The A4 sand detector recorded minimal sanding events during the well life, which suggested that the tubing restriction may not be a sand bridge in the tubing. The well also produced 20% water cut for at least six months, and its water chemistry data suggested scale formation build up caused by the mixing of formation water from the reservoir and injected sea water from the W2 water injection well.

The A2 well was re-drilled as the A6 production well and completed with frac-pack sand control solution in December 2016. A big sanding event (Figure 18) and drilling mud were seen in the existing A5 production well during the A6 drilling operation in December 2016. The A5 well was quickly shut in after the incident, and restarted after A6 came online. The A6 frac-pack completion was also partially broken due to the A5 incident, and sanding events have been observed in the A6 well since first oil production in January 2017 (see Figures 19 and 20).

The A3 well was re-drilled as the A7 production well and completed with frac-pack sand control solution in April 2017. All of the nearby wells were shut in during the drilling of the A7 reservoir section after lessons were learned from the A6 – A5 drilling mud incident. No sanding events were detected by the A7 well sand detector between April 2017 and August 2017 because the well was slowly ramped up with care (see Figures 21 and 22). Minimal sanding events were recorded in August 2017 and September 2017, and the A7 well choke opening is currently being managed to preserve oil production.
