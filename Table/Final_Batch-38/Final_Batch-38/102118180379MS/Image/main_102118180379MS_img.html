Central to the Williston Basin, the formation is Late Devonian to Early Missis-sippian age and made up of five oil-bearing zones. The geologically unique top two oily layers, the Middle Bakken and the first bench of the Three Forks (commonly referred to as Three Forks 1), account for most of the Williston basin’s production. The Three Forks Formation is overlain by the Bakken throughout the central portion of the Williston Basin. Along the margins of the basin, where the Bakken is absent, the Three Forks is overlain by the Lodgepole Formation (Figure 1).

Along the margins of the basin, where the Bakken is absent, the Three Forks is overlain by the Lodgepole Formation (Figure 1). The Three Forks 1 has an average of 30.5 ft pay zone thickness, 65% oil saturation, and 6.9% porosity (Panjaitan et al, 2011). The wells referenced in this study are located in the Three Forks 1 layer, which is an interbedded dolomite and mudstone (Figure 2).

Study Wells 1 and 2 are both located in Dunn County (Hector area) where Study Wells 3 and 4 are located in Mountrail County (Myrmidon area).

Four wells in the study were completed in December 2014 – August 2015. Two of them (Study Wells 1 and 2) are located in Dunn County, ND, Bailey field (Hector Area); the other two (Study Wells 3 and 4) are located in Mountrail County, ND, Reunion Bay field (Myrmidon Area in Figure 5).

All four laterals were landed in the first bench of the Three Forks formation with a true vertical depth (TVD) of approximately 10,750 feet and measured depth (MD) of 21,000 feet, providing roughly 10,250 feet of horizontal lateral. Study Well 1 was drilled in south direction with Azimuth of 183°. Study Wells 2, 3, and 4 were drilled in the north direction with Azimuth of 0-5°. The size distribution and amount of components of the degradable composite fluid were optimized to create a low permeability plug with a minimum amount of material. Large particles are designed to pass through the perforations but be intercepted at the fracture entrance, while the smaller particles reduce the permeability of the formed plug (Figure 7). All the particles and fibers are made of degradable materials and require no additional treatment to be removed; thus, after the treatment, the solid materials degrade completely, opening the zone for production (Kraemer et al., 2014).

The degradation of the composite pill is triggered by the temperature, and it is important that this process takes place at the correct time and in a controlled way. Material needs to hold its properties during fracturing treatment and then dissolve once reservoir begins heating back up. The bottom-hole static temperature for study wells varied between 230 to 235°F. Figure 8 shows largest particle degradation at 230°F.

In new well completions, diversion is a fundamental concept to access additional reserves through increased wellbore coverage. Multiple methodologies had been used to achieve diversion in a wellbore through the years that may be sorted in two categories, based on their target: restrict fluid to flow in the axial direction of the wellbore, and restrict fluid to flow in the perpendicular direction of the wellbore (Figures 9a and 9b).

In this study, since the ISIPs were quickly collected and pumps were immediately brought back on-line at the same rate as before the temporary shutdown, it can be assumed that the net pressure from before to after the pill’s placement is maintained (keeping the fracture open), and it is therefore unchanged from Point 0 to Point 3 in Figure 17.

A comparison was then made between the pressure relief achieved between the 1st acid spear (following the ball seat) and that of the 2nd spear (following the pill’s placement), and the results from this comparison are illustrated below in Figure 18. Data for the pressure relief following the ball seat – the pressure reduction from the 1st acid spear – was collected only for Study Well 3, as is shown in Figure 18 below, but this data set was used as benchmark for comparison of the 2nd acid spear relief for both Study Well 3 and 4.

The results from this experiment indicated that very little pressure relief was achieved by the spear pumped following stage 1 (prior to the pill), but the acid spear following the pill generated a larger pressure reduction by, on average, a factor of 4.5 (with an average pressure reduction of 340 psi) than the former. These results confirmed that the pill was in fact plugging the fractures that were accepting fluid during Stage 1 of the sequenced fracturing treatment, due to the distinct difference in acid relief exhibiting the “newness” of the perfora-tions following the pill’s placement.
