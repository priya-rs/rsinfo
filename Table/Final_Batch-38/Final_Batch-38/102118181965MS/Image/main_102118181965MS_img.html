The main site of the above mentioned field (hereinafter referred to as NGKM-1) is a massive carbonate deposit (Figure 4) lithologically sealed and represented by a wide range of carbonate rocks, non-uniform as to lithology and reservoir porosity and permeability.

Additional specific features are availability of two types of fluid contacts (GOC, WOC), hydrogen sulphide content in the product, as well as abundance of faults throughout the occurrence area (Figure 5) and heavy compartmentalization of the reservoir. The latter fact played an important role in determination of the process solution for the field drilling using horizontal (subhorizontal) wells to ensure full involvement of all veins (Figure 6).

Survey carried out in one of the wells, using an imported hardware system (FSI Schlumberger), enabled confirmation of the informative value of domestic analogues regarding high-quality determination of inflow intervals in the current geotechnical conditions (Figure 22).

The undeniable advantage of an imported hardware system is availability of a flow meter distributed across the cross-section (Figure 23), which enables determination of the fluid flow rate in various parts of the borehole and assessment of stratification of such fluid in composition by using additional optical sensors. This enables more detailed identification of fluid ingress intervals, as well as quantitative assessment of inflow in some interval.

This method in combination with highly precise temperature logging enables detailing inflow intervals with the best vertical resolution (to dozens of centimeters) and determination of the inflow nature. This method is based on noise sensing in an operating well, and the frequency-amplitude characteristic of noise could indicate the environment of fluid filtration. Applying various mathematical filters to the noise spectrum in the well, the flow intervals can be directly divided by the reservoir matrix (0.3-1 kHz) and by fractures (15-30 kHz) (Figures 24-25).
