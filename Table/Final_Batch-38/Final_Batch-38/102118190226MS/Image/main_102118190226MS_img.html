The Wattenberg Field is located in the northeast of Denver Colorado, which is presented by the red polygon in Figure 1. It has multiple pay zones, as listed by the stratigraphic column on the right. Starting from 1970, the completion in the Cretaceous J Sandsone made Wattenberg Field discovered. Since early 1980s, Niobrara B Chalk, Niobrara C Chalk, and the underlying Codell Sandstone have been the three targeting zones. Because of low permeability and porosity, they were regarded as unconventional, which requires horizontal drilling with multistage fracture stimulation (Sonnenberg 2015).

The study area is a one-square mile section. The seismic map shows that there are numerous faults in this area, as shown at bottom right corner in Figure 3. There are eleven horizontal wells in this study area. These wells were named based on the completion order and the placement of their trajectories. The wells that have an “N” in their names indicate they were located in Niobrara formation. There are seven Niobrara wells: six were drilled in Niobrara C Chalk while Well 11N was in Niobrara B Chalk.

During history match, bottom hole pressure was used as the primary constraint. Figure 4 shows Well 6N’s oil production rate. The green solid curve represents simulated oil production; blue dots were the previously imported actual oil production dataset when the model was created. After the blue dots were overlapped with the green curve, the model ran for another one and half years to predict future production. More real production data were displayed by the gray dots, which matches the green simulation curve. Similar with Well 6N, the simulated oil rate of the other ten wells was also well matched with the real production. This verifies the simulation model.

Rate transient analyses (RTA) were conducted for all eleven wells, based on both actual production data and simulation results. When Well 6N was taken for example, the relationship between rate-normalized pressure (the ratio of pressure change to formation rate) and production time period was plotted on a log-log diagnostic plot (the left plot in Figure 5).

Three scenarios of injection components were tested: 1. the combination of N2, CO2 and CH4;2 the hydrocarbon mixture which includes alkanes from methane (C1)to decane (C10); 3. the mix of methane (CH4), ethane (C2H6), propane (C3H8) and butane (C4H10). Well 5C’s oil rate in the three scenarios is displayed in Figure 6. The injected components that include C1, C2, C3 and C4 generate the highest oil production rate. Thus, the third scenario of hydrocarbon mixture (C1 – C4) was selected in the following simulation tests.

Two types of gas huff-n-puff cycles were investigated, as displayed in Figure 7. In Scenario 1,each cycle has a total period of one year: gas is injected into Well 6N for three months, then Well 6N is shut in and soaking for three months. Finally, it is put back into production for six months. In Scenario 2, each huff-n-puff cycle spans two years. A period of six months, six months and twelve months is used for injection, soak and production, respectively. The blue curve in Figure 7 represents Scenario 2, which results in higher oil production. The reason is that longer injection time makes reservoir pressure to increase more and adds more energy into the reservoir, which pushes more oil to be produced.

Figure 9 compares the oil rate of Well 5C in the above two scenarios, which were about whether 6N was re-openned for production or not. For the two scenarios, each injection cycle covers two years. There were three cycles in each scenario. The gray curve represents the scenario when 6N is injected for six months, soaked for six months, and produced for twelve months; the blue curve displays the scenario that 6N is injected for six months and shut in for the following eighteen months. It is obvious that the blue curve shows higher production than the gray curve, which demonstates the previous hypothesis that there is no need to re-open the injector Well 6N.

Three different injection rates were tested: 0.5 MMscf/day, 1 MMscf/day, and 1.5 MMscf/day. The oil rate of Well 5C in the three cases was represented in Figure 10. When injection rate is 1 MMscf/day(orange curve) and 1.5 MMscf/day (blue dots), 5C’s oil production is the same, which is much higher than the case when injection rate is 0.5 MMscf/day (gray curve). Therefore, 1 MMscf/day was selected as the injection rate.

Figure 11 shows that Scenario 1 (blue curve), which has the lowest amount of ethane, has the least incremental oil production. However, for Scenario 2 and 3, though the injected gas contains different ethane composition, 50% and 70%, respectively, the resulted oil production rate curves overlap. Considering ethane can be sold, it is better to inject less ethane and thus Scenario 2 was selected. Therefore, the mole composition of 20%methane, 50% ethane, 20% propane and 10% butane was selected for gas injection.

To investigate the effect of gas diffusion on oil recovery,a diffusion coefficient of 10−4 cm2/s was used initially, as shown in Figure 12. The gray dash curve indicates that when the diffusion coefficient is 10−4 cm2/s, the incremental oil production was not significant. However, when gas diffusion coefficient is greater than 10−4 cm2/s, we see significant improvement in oil recovery.

Many diffusion coefficient values in the range of 10−5 to 10−3 cm2/s were used to test their influence on incremental oil recovery, as displayed in Figure 13. When diffusion coefficient is 10−5 cm2/s (pink dash curve), the oil recovery curve overlaps with the case when no diffusion is considered (blue solid curve), and the oil recovery factor is 4.22%. When diffusion coefficient is 10−4 cm2/s (gray dash curve), though the oil production for each well does not improve a lot, as discussed in Figure 12, the accumulation shows that the oil recovery factor is 4.41%, which is about 4.5% higher than the case without diffusion.

In the previous scenarios, each injection-shut in cycle covers two years. Here, the cycle was shortened to one year and four cycles were included, as shown by the green curve in Figure 14. The higher oil production rate indicates that the one year injection-shut in cycle is a better injection procedure we should follow.
