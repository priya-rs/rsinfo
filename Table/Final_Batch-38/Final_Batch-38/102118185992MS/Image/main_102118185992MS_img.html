Ordos basin is located in middle China[1] (Figure 1) and well-known for its richness in low-permeability oil and gas resources[2, 3]. Unlike booming shale tapping and researches in the north America[4-6], tight low-permeability sandstones, in the last decades, were exploited at a large scale and a high pace, accounting for a high percentage of oil and gas production for China. Due to their inherently tight texture and extensively applied stimulations, low-perm sandstone reservoirs are highly populated by all kinds of fractures (natural, injection induced and hydraulic fractures), which undoubtedly complicated the waterflooding scenarios. On the other hand, the narrow pore-throat and relatively high bound water saturation of low-perm reservoirs differentiates their displacement performance from that of well-known conventional ones[7-8].

Zeng Lian-bo, LI Xiang-yang. Fractures in sandstone reservoirs with ultra-low permeability: A case study of the upper Triassic Yanchang formation in the Ordos basin, China[J]. AAPG Bulletin, 2009, 93(4): 461– 477 .

Dynamic fracture cannot be directly monitored, but can be indirectly reflected by various types of data. The commonly used way is production or injection data, which can be obtained for each well. A symbolic feature of injection increasing of injector under constant or declining pressure is regarded as the indication of dynamic fracture propagation, and drastic watercut and fluid rate jump-up of corresponding producer is considered as the connecting of injector and producer by dynamic fractures (Figure 2a). The fluid rate of producers flooded by dynamic fractures is abnormally high compared with that of these producers flooded through matrix. At the same time, injection profile, well test of injector and tracer monitoring are also capable of detecting dynamic fractures (Figure 2).

In order to study the real mechanical properties under subsurface environment, they were measured under ambient pressure of 35 MPa and increasing pore pressure. It shows that, as the pore pressure increases, the compressive strength and Young’s modulus remarkably decrease (figure 3a, b), and the core samples were all finally shear-fractured (figure 3c). The gradual saturation of cores by water lowers their fracturing pressure. However, the fracturing pressure estimated in the chart above neglects the mechanical property change as injection goes. Therefore, the forming of dynamic fractures in intact rock is somehow possible, which is up to the pore pressure change by injection.

 

The waterflooding developments of many low-permeability reservoirs in China share a common history as illustrated by figure 5. After several years’ flooding, producers located in the direction of the in-situ maximum stress orientation of injectors are first flooded by dynamic fracture breakthrough; these flooded producers are then shut-down or inverted to be injectors to form a linear pattern; after another several years’ injection, producers in ther other directions are all gradually flooded. This could be well explained by the simulation results showed above. The predominance of dynamic fractures along the maximum stress orientation causes early waterbreakthrough in this direction, and their growths in other directions under high enough injection pressure results in the ultimate flooding of the rest producers[9].

The scenarios showed in figure 6 are all simulation results after the same injection duration. In case 1, no dynamic fracture near injector was simulated, showing a fan-shape sweep radiating from the injector and a gradually increasing watercut. This one was also designed as the base case for this comparative study. In case 2, the dynamic fracture grew a limited distance from the injector, and the sweep still showed a fairly even water front that was slightly faster in the direction of dynamic fracture. In case 3, a dynamic fracture with faster growth rate caused the sweep narrower than in case 2. In case 4, a dynamic fracture quickly connecting injector and producer was simulated, showing the poorest sweep and the most drastic watercut jump. Case 1 and 4 respectively represent the best and worst sweep scenario for low permeability sandstone reservoirs.
