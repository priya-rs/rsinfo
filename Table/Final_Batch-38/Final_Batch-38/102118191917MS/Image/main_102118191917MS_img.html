The Walloon Coal Measures (previously referred to as the Walloon Subgroup) are an unconventional CSG reservoir in the Jurassic sequence of the north-eastern Surat Basin, with production primarily from the coal prone Juandah and Taroom coal measures. Along with the underlying Bowen Basin that extends to the north, the Surat Basin is currently a key focus of appraisal and development for the CSG to LNG projects in Queensland. The high degree of variability in the deposition of the Walloon Coal Measures is illustrated in Figure 3 by the contrast in reservoir intersected by an offset well drilled only 50m away from the original. Example zones of coal, clastic, and shale facies have been highlighted for reference, and the percentage change in net coal per formation is summarised on the right. Note how the distribution and character of coal seams differs significantly within each formation between the two wells. Most important is how the three main coal packages observed in the Lower Juandah formation in Well 1 are completely absent from Well 2. They are instead replaced by thick sections of shale and clastic facies, resulting in 90% less net coal in this formation at this well location.

The quantity of reservoir sampling taken and the range of results seen from exploration wells across an example development area of the Walloon Coal Measures is summarised in Figure 4. This figure illustrates the large variability in static and dynamic properties between exploration wells. These four exploration wells are the only reservoir sampling for an area that would ultimately contain approximately 100 development wells.

The variability in permeability seen in these four wells is within the four log-cycle range of what has been observed locally and documented by other operators across the basin (Kabir et al. 2011, Mazumder et al. 2013). The variability over short lateral distances for reservoir properties such as permeability is clearly illustrated by the test results from closely spaced (500m) infill wells overlaid on the regional dataset. These wells are in a development area immediately adjacent to that discussed in Figure 4, with the results shown in Figure 5. Each unit on the vertical axis represents one log-cycle.

The regional gas content trend shown in Figure 6 also shows a high degree of variability. In this diagram, each unit on the horizontal axis represents 1 m3/ton. While there is a general trend of increasing gas content with depth, for a given depth there is large variability even within the same formation. Combined with changes in coal quality, the gas holding capacity associated with each of these samples will vary and lead to a wide range of gas saturations and production behaviour from individual coal seams.

To illustrate the challenge of upscaling with highly variable permeability, an example of upscaling five separate seams with a four log-cycle variation in permeability representative of the Walloon Coal Measures can be considered. This is represented in Figure 9 below.

For simplicity, all seams in this example are defined as the same thickness, as well as having equal gas content, desorption isotherms, and static conditions. Permeability was upscaled to match the permeability-thickness product for both cases. Figure 10 shows that although the resulting water production is broadly equivalent between both cases, the gas production behaviour differs significantly.

The number of reservoir units modelled is increased to 30 to construct an example more representative of reality. All other parameters were kept constant between the reservoir units. This scenario is equivalent to upscaling all reservoir units within a well to a single gas content and isotherm. To honour the hydrocarbon volumetrics for both cases, the initial gas content and reservoir pressure will be a net coal weighted average from the reservoir units in the fine scale system. The upscaled isotherm is then defined such that the initial gas saturation and isotherm curvature yield a good match of the gas production history. The match achieved for gas and water production is illustrated in Figure 11.

The resulting upscaled isotherm for this example is plotted in red in Figure 12. It also shows the mid-case isotherm for each of the formations in solid colours, as well as the high and low bounds defined by the +/-15% saturation variability as dotted lines.

The inherent reservoir heterogeneity coupled with the low sampling density both laterally (widely spaced exploration wells) and vertically (not all reservoir units within a well were tested) leads to a high degree of variability and uncertainty when populating such a model for the Walloon Coal Measures. This means it will not have the ability to accurately characterise the properties of individual reservoir units at specific well locations.
