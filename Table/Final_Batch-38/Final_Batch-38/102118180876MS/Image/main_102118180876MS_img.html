There are only four commercial dry steam geothermal fields at present. These are the Geysers in California; the Larderello in central Italy; the Matsukawa in Japan; and the Kawah Kamojang in western Java, Indonesia (Aapg, 2016). Vapor-dominated sites offer temperatures from 240 to 300 °C that produce superheated steam. Liquid-dominated reservoirs (LDRs) are found near young volcanoes and in rift zones and hot spots. Typical example of this is the Bouillante geothermal field, Guadeloupe. Fig 1 shows a schematic of a typical geothermal system.

This enhanced geothermal system (EGS) generates geothermal fluid without the need for natural hot fluid resources. This concept is illustrated in Fig. 2 and is similar to steam injection system used in the oil and gas sector where steam is injected into the reservoir via the injection well and the production well produces the hydrocarbon to surface. In the EGS system however water is injected via the injection well and steam and hot water is produced through the production well.

The reason for the existence of these thermal features is the westward subduction of the North Atlantic crustal plate beneath the Caribbean plate (Huttrer and LaFleur, 2015). There are nine English-speaking islands in the Eastern Caribbean region that have potential for the discovery of geothermal resources capable of economically feasible power generation (Figure 5). From north to south, they are: Saba, St. Eustatius, St. Kitts & Nevis, Montserrat, Dominica, St. Lucia, St. Vincent, and Grenada.

The reason for the active volcanism in that the Caribbean islands results from the subduction of the westward moving North American plate beneath the eastern margin of the Caribbean Plate (Figure 6). Accordingly, volcanic arcs typical of plate boundaries have formed over time and, in the Caribbean, each volcano or group of volcanoes forms the foundation of a discrete island. The active subduction zone formed from the oceanic lithosphere of the Atlantic Ocean subducting under the thicker Caribbean Plate give rise to these discrete islands that start as far North as Saba and stretch to Grenada in the South.
