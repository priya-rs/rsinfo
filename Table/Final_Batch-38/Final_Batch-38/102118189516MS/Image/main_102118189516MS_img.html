The near wellbore area is the most important part of the reservoir where the flow area is reduced and the flow streamlines are trying flow through perforation tunnels. The below fig. 3 shows that most noticeable damage affects the well in the first 30 cm or 12 inches of the formation. The problem is more critical when the zone of interest is considerably permeable because it would be easier to damage and can have a major impact on productivity. The pressure drop across an unfractured wellbore shows the importance of the near wellbore on inflow.

 

From previous experiences in the same layer, acid stimulation was not found effective as it should due to the very low (sometimes zero) injectivity in the wellbore due different possible reasons. The success rate was low and did not gain much improvement by designing different acid recipes. From here, the idea of bypassing the formation damage and drill laterals into the formation came up. Radial drilling technique is believed to bypass the damage nearby wellbore and reach extended virgin areas in the reservoir. fig. 5 shows the effect of radial drilling on IPR curve compared to matrix acidizing.

 

 

HCl pre-flush is an essential step to be considered in sandstone acidizing. It is designed to remove/dissolve any carbonate compounds, like calcium carbonate and magnesium carbonate, and iron compounds that may exists, typically in formations with a little over 1% carbonate content.
