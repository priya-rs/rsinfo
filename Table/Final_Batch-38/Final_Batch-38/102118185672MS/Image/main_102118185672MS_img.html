This paper discusses the integrated workflow implemented on a single horizontal well to evaluate multiple completion and stimulation designs to understand its impact on hydraulic fracture geometry and production performance. The standardized workflow shown in Fig.1 can be applied to any unconventional play and has been proven effective in determining the completion and stimulation design for maximized production. Economic analysis should be incorporated for the optimal completion design.

The integrated workflow was applied in the Avalon shale formation located southeast of Loving in Eddy County, New Mexico (Fig. 2). The Avalon shale is of Leonardian age and is part of the uppermost member of the Bone Spring formation in the Delaware basin within the greater Permian basin.

The Avalon shale is located in the Delaware basin, across southeast New Mexico and West Texas. The Delaware basin is part of the Permian basin, bounded by the Diablo platform in the west, Northwestern shelf and Capitan reef trend in the north, Central Basin platform in the east, and Southern shelf in the south (Fig. 3).

The Avalon shale is primarily a self-sourced rock consisting of siltstone, limestone, clay, chert, and organics. It is subdivided into three zones—upper, middle and lower shales — isolated by carbonate debris flows (Fig. 4). Each zone has variable rock properties inherited from variations in depositional environments across the Delaware basin. Siliciclastic mudstones in the upper and lower Avalon are considered to be of good reservoir quality and are highly productive, whereas the carbonate-rich middle Avalon is considered not productive because of the high percentage of carbonates, which is associated with bad reservoir quality.

The integrated workflow was applied to a single horizontal well on a two-well pad in Eddy County, New Mexico (Fig.5a). The horizontal well was landed in the Avalon shale and hydraulically stimulated with 11 stages along the lateral section with conventional plug-and-perforation technique. Microseismic data were acquired for target well A across all the stimulated intervals with geophones located in observation well B (Fig. 5b).

The UFM was used to model the actual hydraulic fracture geometry, which was validated by matching the microseismic footprint and actual treating pressure trends as well as instantaneous shut-in pressure (ISIP), refer to (SPE 179130) for calibration details. Fig. 6 shows a 2D map view of the hydraulic fracture extent for all 11 stages, matching the majority of the observed microseismic footprint. The hydraulic fracture geometry shows varying fracture lengths along the wellbore from ~860 to 1,500ft. Fig. 7 shows the hydraulic fracture height is mostly contained within the Avalon shale, varying from ~400 to 530ft, with an average height of ~460 ft for the entire lateral.

A 3D numerical reservoir simulator that uses an autogridding algorithm is applied to generate an unstructured grid for the simulated complex fracture network for production history match and forecast. History matching the well production after a period of stable production is necessary to understand the performance of the hydraulic fracturing treatment. After the complex hydraulic fractures are modeled and calibrated, a reservoir simulation unstructured grid that honors the hydraulic fracture geometry and proppant distribution was built. The grid consisted of 34 layers with ~820,000 cells; a finer cell size of 10' was assigned for the complex fracture and a larger cell size of 300' for the matrix (Fig. 8).

Gas production rate was used as the control variable and oil rate, water rate and bottomhole pressure (calculated from tubing pressure) were the matching parameters (Fig. 9).

Over the years, stimulation design in unconventional reservoirs has evolved from conservative to radical designs. Proppant job size and fluid volumes are among the top critical design parameters in hydraulic fracturing design. Proppant job size has a large impact on the hydraulic fracture geometry created. Fig. 10 shows the total surface area and propped surface area generated by the UFM for the proppant job size sensitivity. Increased surface area and propped surface area with increasing proppant job size is observed. This is driven primarily by both height containment and fracture length growth.

Finding a balance for the optimum cluster spacing is highly critical. In this category, to evaluate different cluster spacing configurations on the hydraulic fracture geometry, the proppant job size was kept the same. Fig.11 shows that as cluster spacing decreased, total surface area and propped surface area increased, likely due to increase in fracture complexity resulting in more stimulated area around the wellbore. The 30-ft scenario gave the largest surface area but also gave an overall smaller fracture length and shorter fracture height for each stage. The 30-ft scenario also had the highest number of stages.

Maximum proppant concentration is often another parameter in hydraulic fracturing design that can be optimized to increase fracture conductivity near wellbore. Fig. 12 shows minimal difference in the surface area that was created by varying the maximum proppant concentration, because the proppant job size and fluid volume were the same for all the scenarios. The average fracture conductivity is also displayed, which shows an increasing trend.

Fig. 13 shows the total and propped surface area for all the sensitivity scenarios. The proppant job size and cluster spacing scenarios have the most effect on total and propped surface area. Overall increasing proppant job size and tighter cluster spacing shows more surface and propped surface area.

Fig. 14 above shows production results in barrels of oil equivalent (BOE) for proppant job size sensitivity ranging from 370 lbm/lateral ft to 1000 lbm/lateral ft. The overall trend shows increasing production with proppant job size. The 750lbm/lateral ft shows less incremental production compared to the other cases because the added fracture geometry created was attributed to more height growth than fracture length extension, see also Fig. 15. The propped surface area in Fig. 10 also shows less incremental area created from the 500lbm/lateral ft compared to 750lbm/lateral ft case.

Fig. 16 shows the production prediction results for cluster spacing sensitivity ranging from 30 to 150ft. The overall trend shows significant production increase with smaller cluster spacing, keeping the proppant job size the same in all cases. Fig. 17 shows the pressure depletion map for the complex hydraulic fracture created in each scenario after 1 year of production. There is more stimulated area, hence depletion, as the cluster spacing is reduced, maximizing near-wellbore contact.
