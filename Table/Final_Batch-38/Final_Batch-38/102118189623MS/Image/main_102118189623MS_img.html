The bean-up guide provided was adhered to as much as operations allowed. During the flowback of the completion fluids, topside valves upstream of the test separator were continuously blocked by completion fluid and resolving this issue slowed the bean-up rate during the clean-up period. Also, the drawdowns experienced during the clean-up periods were not in line with expectations based on mobility data acquired while drilling the reservoir section of the well. Figure 3 shows data acquired during the A-1H clean-up period.

The decision was taken to proceed with the first main flow period. During the main flow period, the drawdowns were monitored throughout the bean-up to maximum well rates. Screen plugging was ruled out as reason for the excessive drawdowns observed (up to 50 bar drawdown) as there was no corresponding decrease in production rates at constant choke opening. It was suspected that there was a restriction in the well somewhere upstream of the PDHG. Figure 4 and Figure 5 show the well test results from the first main flow period.

The well shut-in event (due to the vent line leak) was included in the simulation as shown in Figure 21 by reducing choke opening to zero for 31.8 minutes followed by choke ramp-up to 20/64th inch. Figure 21 shows predicted completion fluid and oil flowrates at the rig during the 9-hour well clean-up agree with measurement data quite well; albeit calculated oil flowrate between 7.23 and 7.67 hours shows a response based on a choke opening increase to 26/64th inch, whereas measured oil rate shows a constant level without any obvious increase.

The simulation also revealed the reservoir inflow contributions from each reservoir unit with respect to choke schedule, as shown in Figure 22.

The LSOBM rig arrival time was predicted to be 3.51 hours, and entire unloading period was 6.7 hours, shown in Figure 23. Field records indicate it took 3.5 hours for the first traces of oil based mud to arrive at surface and a total of 6.5 hours to achieve the clean-up criteria of <1% BS&W. A total of 62 m3 of completion fluid was displaced from the well to slop tanks during the clean-up before any hydrocarbons were observed at surface which matches wells with the simulation prediction of 64 m3 (see Figure 23). The results also show it takes approximately 4.2 hours to unload NaBr.

Figure 24 illustrates the mass fraction profiles of NaBr (a) and LSOBM (b) at various time stamps to demonstrate the displacement of the different fluid phases as they move along the production tubing from the bottom of the well to the rig. The simulation captured the drilling mud arrival time at rig accurately and it verifies the BS&W measurement to be <1% which agrees with the tracer sampling results at the end of clean-up operation.

Calculated pressure and temperature at DHPT gauge depth (3,866 m MD) are plotted in Figure 25 to compare against measured data points. It shows simulation results capture the trends of downhole pressure and temperature changes. Discrepancy in pressure is observed from 0 to 0.8 hours and is explained due to the ‘stiff’ full liquid system at initial conditions. A better match was achieved once formation fluid begins flowing into wellbore, thus small simulation time steps are crucial to improve the model accuracy.

A sensitivity study was performed to acquire the representative choke diameter to better capture the pressure loss due to the flow restriction. As shown in Figure 26, calculated BHP matches the measured BHP quite well for most of the clean-up, except for the discrepancy observed between 4 to 5 hours. The noticeable increased downhole pressure measurements were due to plugging of the choke. During this period, calculated BHP is lower than measured data as the choke size in the model doesn’t reflect this plugging incident.

According to the field reports, during the subsequent main flow period, traces of LSOBM was still being flowed back despite the initial clean-up suggesting the well had cleaned up to BS&W <1%. This field observation was supported by simulation results as shown in Figure 27. The movements of both NaBr and LOSBM at various time stamps during the clean-up are presented through 3D animation in Figure 28. It depicts LSOBM arrival on the rig at 3.35 hour.

Figure 29 shows history matching results for BHP and liquid flowrates of A-2H. The flowrate calculation prior to the emergency shut-in matches with measured data reasonably well, except there is a gap between 3.2 and 4.25 hours where no flowrate records are available, thus no comparison was made. However, simulation results show the expected response with respect to the rig valve ramp up from 20/64th inch to 32/64th inch and subsequent choke-back to 24/64th inch during this period.

During the shut-in, calculated BHP agrees with gauge measurement in general, although discrepancies exist at the beginning of the shut-in; measured BHP shows a gradual increase before reaching equilibrium whereas simulation established the static BHP much faster. Again, this can be explained by the lack of a near wellbore model coupled to the simulator. Additionally, Figure 30 shows that most of the NaBr and LSOBM were cleaned up before the shut-in, which verifies the field records.
