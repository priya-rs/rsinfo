However, pressures relevant to the model that are measured and captured on a daily basis are those of the gas lift headers located at each wellpad; these average pressures were then pulled out of the field database. With these actual pressures as targets, the gas lift injection side of the model was run repeatedly with varying supply pressures until the offtake point pressures in the model (the locations of W, Z, V, and L pads) matched well to them. This supply pressure was then used in the combined model for a match run. This proved to be an effective solution, and the results are shown in Table B-1.

To ensure the first match was not simply an aberration, another day was chosen about a week later, when the compressors’ output pressure was ~50 psi lower, at 1,954 psi. This time the supply pressure in the gas injection side of the model was adjusted down to 1,870 psi, also approximately 50 psi less than in the first run, and the model matched oil and water production to within 4% and 2%, respectively, and gas production to within 1%. The mixture velocity in the 24” section of the EWE line was also again closely matched, to within 3% (see Table B-2).

With the gas lift supply pressure in the gas injection model reduced to 1,790 psi, the model was run (see Table B-3), matching oil and water rates to within 3% and 2%, respectively, and gas production to within <1%. Mixture velocity in the EWE 24-in LDF was not so well matched, but close scrutiny revealed this to be simply a data pull issue of the sonar meter values from that day. This excellent match proved the combined model was doing what the standalone model could not: matching production accurately despite fluctuating gas lift supply pressures.

It would nearly always converge on a solution where more oil and less water and gas were produced as compared to a base, or matched case (see Table B-4 for run results of subsequent weeks). Even with the occasional solution where less oil was produced, the program was still deemed to have performed well, for significantly less water and gas were produced in these runs - non-pay fluids which would have to be processed at GC2. As 80-90 gas lift flow rate changes were found during each run (one each for every online well), only the top few most beneficial changes were passed to operations each week.

The gas lift injection side of the model was set up and run to represent the system on an “average” day, using the mean offtake rate of 109 MM scf/d and a normal supply pressure of 1920 psi. Solved-for header pressures were in line with normal field gas lift header pressures. The production data showed that though this 109 MM scf/d was the mean, a relatively high offtake rate of 120 MM scf/d occurred frequently. The additional 11 MM scf/d was then added to each pad’s average offtake in succession so that the effects on gas lift header pressures could be studied (see Table B-6).
