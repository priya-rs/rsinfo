This re-development concept calls for the construction of four artificial islands (Figure 1), which will host the drilling of the majority of all new wells, as well as hosting the processing and treatment surface facilities for all the production and injection fluids. As part of the plans to increase and sustain production from the field, over 360 extended reach MRC wells are planned, consisting of both oil producers, water injectors and potentially Water-Alternating-Gas (WAG) injector wells.

The solution was, a non-damaging Non-Aqueous Fluid (NAF) RDF complemented with a filter-cake removal breaker system that is Barite-free and contains a CaCl2 or CaBr2 internal phase. The friction reduction (Figure 2) achieved from this RDF NAF compared to the RDF WBM is at least 100% and has been one of the key developments in being able to drill and complete longer laterals.

In line with the pilot well and early development plans for the laterals to be drilled with RDF WBM, the swellpackers that were initially used to isolate the compartments (Figure 3) of the 6 5/8” horizontal lower completion were designed to swell upon contact with produced oil after 2 days.

For this development to enable successful drilling and completion of these extended reach wells, drill string, running tools and lower completion connections are all high strength and high torque (Figure 4).

At this time three types of centralisers have been used on this extended reach development to deploy the 6 5/8” horizontal lower completions – Rigid, non-rotating centralisers & Fixed and bonded rotating centralisers (Figure 5).

The drillpipe swivel (Figure 6) was developed for another major ERD project and has been further enhanced with high strength connections and used on the pilot wells and through the current development. The drillpipe swivel is run in the unlocked mode and allows the drillpipe running string to be rotated above the liner running tool, such that the lower completion does not rotate.

If modelling shows that using the swivel in the running string is not sufficient and there is a high likelihood of having to lock the swivel, then the casing swivel is deployed to allow rotation of the running string and upper portion of the 6-5/8” horizontal lower completion. The casing swivel (Figure 7) was again initially developed for another major ERD project, but further optimised and qualified to a higher specification for this development, including using CRA metallurgy in the flow wetted components.

The five case histories that will be presented are on wells that have measure depths in excess of 27,700ft MD, reaching to almost 30,900ft MD and horizontal lateral lengths in excess of 14,320ft up to almost 18,850ft. These have been plotted against each other (Figure 8), to show how the wells differ from each other, to set a perspective of the different challenges that were faced on each well.

Simulations for deployment of the 6-5/8” horizontal lower completion (Figure 9) showed that without the drillpipe swivel, if friction factors exceeded 0.25FC then it was unlikely that the liner would be able to be deployed to bottom. With the ability to selectively rotate the drillpipe running string an additional 80,000lbs of hookload is gained at 0.2FC. Therefore, with rotation of the drillpipe running string, the 6-5/8” horizontal lower completion could be deployed to total depth with a 0.3-0.35FC.

The actual deployment of the 6-5/8” horizontal lower completion was excellent (Figure 10), taking five days and requiring no rotation of the drillpipe running string as the friction factors were approximately 0.2FC. This was attributed to several reasons, 1) Lubricious NAF mud system, 2) Good hole cleaning after reaching total depth and 3) low dogleg severity and tortuosity; averaging 1.28deg/100ft.

Extensive simulations were carried out to confirm that the liner could be deployed prior to committing to the sidetrack and once the well total depth had been reached with the actual wellpath data. The actual wellpath data showed significant micro-doglegs (Figure 11) at the sidetrack point which would likely cause problems during deployment of the 6-5/8” horizontal lower completion.

Deployment simulations for this well (Figure 12) show that at above a 0.3FC, the 6-5/8” horizontal lower completion cannot be deployed to total depth without rotation of the upper string. At a depth between 25 - 26,000ft MD is where the buckling occurs, if the friction factor exceeds 0.3FC. Using the swivel to enable rotation of the drillpipe running string adds approximately 70,000lbs of hookload and allows the 6-5/8” horizontal lower completion to be deployed at friction factors up to 0.35FC.

When deploying the 6-5/8” horizontal lower completion there were no issues until the liner reached the sidetrack point (Figure 13). It can be seen that the trip was tracking a 0.25FC until this point, but quickly started increasing towards 0.3FC and buckling. This was evaluated to be when the 1st of the 14 swellpackers were pushed past the openhole whipstock sidetrack, due to the high DLS at this point. At 25,567ft MD the hookload dropped to 100Klbs (Block weight of the DDM) and the drillpipe running string was rotated to try to alleviate the buckling.

This well was designed as a ‘Combination’ well (Figure 14), which means from a single well slot the reservoir targets of two wells were combined. This is a significant departure from the original field development plan as the lateral length vs. 9 5/8” casing length ratio switches significantly, with the lateral length almost doubling and thus well length increasing by 1/3rd.

This however, creates significant challenges for successful deployment of the 6-5/8” horizontal lower completion, but based on additional in depth modelling the multi-disciplinary team concluded that this liner could be deployed successfully. In the end it was decided that this would be the 1st deployment of the casing swivel as the simulations (Figure 15) showed that running a drillpipe swivel would not give sufficient safety margin, if the friction factors were higher than expected.

To rotate this length of drillpipe running string and top portion of the 6-5/8” horizontal lower completion, requires significant torque (Figure 16) to be overcome at surface. This justified the use of the high torque running tool which could see torque in excess of 30,000ft-lbs (depending on the friction coefficient) and also the liner itself. The high torque connections on the liner would allow rotation up to 0.35FC.

As mentioned previously, this was a combination well and due to the length of the lateral, 21 swellpackers were deployed on the 6-5/8” horizontal lower completion. This number of swellpackers did not affect the deployment (Figure 17), as it is clear to see that the friction coefficient had a maximum value of 0.2FC dropping to almost 0.1FC by the end of the deployment.

Like the previous combination well case history, this well was run with a casing swivel, to allow rotation of the upper portion of the 6-5/8” horizontal lower completion. This was used to generate significant increases in hookload (Figure 18) and increase the safety margins to allow the 6-5/8” horizontal lower completion to be deployed to total depth with higher friction coefficients.

It should be noted that on this well, the 6-5/8” horizontal lower completion, was fitted with the axially and rotationally locked centralisers, in case the liner had to be rotated. If the upper portion of the 6-5/8” horizontal lower completion had to be rotated, the required surface torque and expected torque through the liner running tools and upper portion of the liner (Figure 19) was within acceptable limits at a 0.3FC.

However as can be seen (Figure 20) the deployment of the 6-5/8” horizontal lower completion was very successful, not requiring rotation of the casing swivel as the liner was successfully deployed to total depth with a 0.15FC. It is considered that similar contributing factors accounted to the successful deployment of this 6-5/8” horizontal lower completion as the previous case histories presented, along with the first time use of this modified RDF NAF system in this record length well.

The same technologies as used on Well 3 were used on this 6-5/8” horizontal lower completion; such as casing swivel position over 5,000ft from the top of the 6-5/8” horizontal lower completion (Figure 21) and bonded centralisers above this point. The pore pressure and length of the lateral allowed the standard RDF NAF system to be used, and the same best practices such as low DLS (1.3deg/100ft), good hole cleaning, etc were incorporated into the drilling and prior steps, before running the 6-5/8” horizontal lower completion.

Like previous wells the preparatory work that had been done prior to deployment of the 6-5/8” horizontal lower completion, allowed the successful deployment without the need for rotation (Figure 22). In this case history deployment friction factors were 0.1 - 0.15FC and this is consistent with previous wells.

Prior to this well the benefits of the casing swivel had not actually been realised, as it had not been required to be rotated. Before execution it was decided to determine the hookload gains and required surface torque when rotating above the casing swivel when the 6 5/8” lower completion was at toal depth (Figure 23).
