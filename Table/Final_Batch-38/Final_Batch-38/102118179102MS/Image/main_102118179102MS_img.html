The well referred to in this paper, is on the Valhall field which is located in the southern Norwegian sector of the North Sea (see Fig. 1). It was completed in 1992 with a 5 in. liner and 5 ½ in. production tubing. Over the producing life of the well, a number of intervention operations (typically deployed on coiled tubing) were performed in the well, with heavy oil based mud circulated in the tubing and annulus most recently in 2008.

Fig. 2 shows the completion and wellbore schematic after these operations, with the junk catcher and plug represented as item 4C. In 2015, the well was re-entered to retrieve the plug, however it was not possible to go below 1980 meters and it was believed that this was due to settling of the heavy mud and barites. A review of the options to clean out the well and enable access to the top of the junk catcher was then undertaken.

Fig. 3 illustrates the various mechanical clean out functions. The transportation mechanism is based on the Archimedes screw principle, also referred to as an auger. A retaining valve installed in the bottom chamber ensures the debris remains in the chambers until the assembly is pulled out of the wellbore. A tractor provides weight on bit and rotational anchoring.

Suction methods, in which the electrical power drives a motor and pump, creating a suction effect to lift loose debris and wellbore fluid. The mixture passes through a filter mechanism, the debris is retained in collection chambers and the fluids circulated back into the wellbore. Fig. 4 shows an illustration of the wellbore cleanout system with suction.

A rig up height of 23 meters was expected, but the actual available rig up height was only 19 meters. This limited the 3.8 in. OD collection chambers to a maximum of five and total capacity of 30 liters. Fig. 6 is a schematic of the key components of the tool string with a 4.2 in. Rockbit BHA.

At this point it was felt that the suction tool technology would be more appropriate and the tool string was rigged up. Fig. 7 shows a schematic of the suction tool configuration. With the shorter length of the suction toolstring it was possible to increase the capacity of the collection chambers to 35 liters (nine 3.5 in. OD collection chambers). Previous runs with the debris collector showed that the pickup weights were increasing. It was deduced that this was possibly owing to a more fluidic type of debris in the hole and that it was possible that the toolstring could go through this debris and reach the top of the junk basket.

The graphs in Fig. 9 give a graphical comparison of wellbore cleanout performance between the slickline bailers and the powered cleanout system. The depth progress chart shows quite clearly that there was no further progress with the slickline bailers and, when correlated with the qualitative debris consistency chart, indicates that the debris recovered by the bailers was limited to loose material, which further confirms the lack of progress due to compaction. The mechanical powered wellbore cleanout system was, however, able to agitate and break up the compacted debris and make significant progress in the first two runs.
