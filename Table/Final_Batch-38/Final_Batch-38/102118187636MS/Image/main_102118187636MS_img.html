Permeability values of the Upper Sandstone falls below 10 mD, as shown in Figure 1. In some areas higher permeabilities can be observed, with values above 30 mD to the east of the reservoir.

Remaining oil saturation (ROS) used in the evaluation was exported from history matched simulation model. ROS at each reservoir subunit is averaged using weighted average method. Average ROS in the areas of good formation permeability ranges from 30% to 45% (Figure 2). However, areas of ROS higher than 45% can be observed at relatively low permeability areas (< 10 mD), using advanced drilling technologies might be the proper way to produce this high remaining oil. Additional maps of remaining hydrocarbon pore volume (HCPV) were generated to describe the reservoir storage capacity further.

A new approach is introduced here to define areas of good injectivity, where acceptable rates can be injected. The product of the permeability, cell height, and ROS is calculated for each grid cell of the simulation model. The results were averaged and plotted for each reservoir unit as shown in Figure 4. The areas with relatively high K*hn (ranges from 120 mD.ft to 450 mD.ft) are marked in white.

To evaluate the reservoir heterogeneity, Dykstra-Parsons description of disparity in vertical permeability is used as shown below. Figure 5 shows for instance, that permeability variation is 0.92 using core data for one well. These results indicate a very high vertical heterogeneity in reservoir permeability.

Full field simulation including the surfactant injection was performed to generate the fluxes at the boundary cells at injection rate 2000 b/d. Sector run was executed by repeating the original conditions used in the FFM (no additional change in the production and injection parameters), the run time was reduced from 5.3 hours to 0.22 hour (13 minutes). The results of these runs are shown in Figure 6. It can be recognized that the predicted pressures by the sector model (dashed lines) are higher than the pressures at the same region in the FFM.

Based on these observations, it was obvious that the boundary should be changed in a manner that reduces the intense change of fluxes across the boundary, and away from the main flow area. Consequently, another regions’ definition was generated as shown in Figure 7. An alternative region definition was generated and determined by observing the maximum sweep of surfactant at a concentration of 1E-20 lb/stb. A reduced run was conducted within a computation run time of 1.25hrs.

A surfactant model was designed using a typical surfactant at a concentration of 3 kg/m3 (0.3 wt%), IFT table used in this model as displayed in Figure 8 (left) which shows an ultra IFT between oil and water at 0.001 mN/m at a maximum surfactant concentration. Surfactant relative permeability is applied within the limitation of capillary number (above the critical). 20% is the minimum Sorc which can be reached at capillary number (Nc≥10−2) was used as a base case. Additional sensitivity has been made assuming low value of Sorc at 10%.

The log10(Nc) implemented in the CDC used in our model ranges between -4 and -2 as shown in Figure 9 (right). Surfactant relative permeability curve exhibited in Figure 9 (left) was designed assuming Sorc value of 20%. Note that brine salinity is not considered in the current simulation model. Therefore, surfactant make-up water is assumed to be fresh water. A proper brine model is recomended to be considered in the upcoming studies.

By plotting the calculated capillary number with the corresponding ROS as shown in Figure 10 (left) which shows a significant improvement in ED by increasing capillary number. However, this occurs at a small volume of the entire sector where only 8% of the swept PV by surfactant has a relatively high capillary number (≥ 10−3). Surfactant flooding showed a limited potential in this pilot due to the large pore volue targeted by the injection (the existing well-spacing is above 2 km).

Fingering phenomenon was observed at injection rates above 1000 b/d. It is well distinguished that low injection rate (500 b/d) could maintain a stable water/surfactant front which will result in a better sweep efficiency. This stable front was estimated at distance of 260 m away from the injector. Considering the number of cells where the surfactant arrives as displayed in Figure 11 (total active cells: 17545) in comparison to where surfactant becomes active (total active cells: 367).
