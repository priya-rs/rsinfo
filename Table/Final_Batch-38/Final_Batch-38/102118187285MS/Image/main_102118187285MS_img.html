Firstly, production rates of some MFHWs are still low after fracturing treatments and the oil production rates for wells #A1 and #A2 are even lower than 1.5 m3/d, shown in Figure 1a. Secondly, production rates decline quickly for some other wells (e.g. wells #A3 and #A4) as shown in Figure 1b. Thirdly, the water-cut may rise to 90% only after two or three years’ water flooding development (e.g. wells #A5 and #A6), shown in Figure 1c, and the water-cut of other MFHWs rapidly increases to 100% (e.g. wells #A7 and #A8) as shown in Figure 1d. Many wells have been shut-in due to high water-cut.

Therefore, it has proven to be challenging because of complex and dynamic behaviors of reservoir and well during production. For instance, formation damage (i.e. fracturing fluid retention) and fracture closure (i.e. proppant broken) will increase the flow resistance both from matrix to fractures and fractures to wellbore in different degrees along horizontal wellbore, and water breakthrough can lead to high watercut rapidly, shown in Figure 2.

Some PLT results also demonstrate this phenomenon in the Ordos tight oil reservoir as shown in Figure 3. We can also find that hydraulic fractures are not uniformly distributed along horizontal wellbore, and the production distribution is nonuniform for these two MFHWs. Some fractures made little contribution to production rates or all fluids from some fractures are water so that the water-cut of the first well is close to 100%. Therefore, we should address this problem to estimate production contribution of individual fracture and diagnose the water-breakthrough sections to improve well performance.

The main objective of using ERT in this paper is to provide a reliable temporal description of the waterflood front propagation around MFHWs resulting from the water flooding. It can reduce the nonuniqueness of well testing interpretation too. Then, the water-breakthrough segments of MFHWs can be estimated by ERT results. We combine the well testing method with ERT to assess production contribution of fractures and water-breakthrough locations for well performance evaluation, and further compare the results with PLT data, as show in Figure 4.

Physical and Mathematical model. To solve the problem in Figure 2, the physical model of an MFHW was developed, shown in Figure. 5. Some assumptions are made to get the solutions.

Li et al. (1996) considered the pressure drop caused by linear flow with uniform flux density along the fracture. Whereas, the flux density along individual fracture is nonuniform since fluids may flow into the fracture from different directions and positions, and fluids will then converge to the horizontal wellbore. He et al. (2016a) considered both nonuniform-flux-density linear flow and radial flow within fractures. We extend this method to deal with asymmetric fractures, in which radial flow and nonuniform-flux-density linear flow are incorporated, seen in Figure 6.

Unstructured perpendicular bisector (PEBI) grid system is applied in the numerical model, shown in Figure 7a. The calculation results between numerical model in Saphir and analytical model in this paper are shown in Figure 7b. It presents a good agreement between them except for the later period of linear flow, which is perhaps caused by the difference of fracture characterization in numerical and analytical well-testing models.

To analyze pressure-transient behavior and flow regimes of an MFHW, we develop the type curve of an MFHW intercepted by multiple nonuniformly distributed and asymmetric fractures with different conductivity and production contribution, as shown in Figure 8. The input parameters are shown in Table 2 and Figure 8.

We choose seven cases to characterize the production contribution of fractures as shown in Figure 9a and Figure 9b. Seven examples are divided into two groups, and type curves are obtained respectively, shown in Figure 9c and Figure 9d. The effects of production distribution are mainly reflected in four regimes (i.e. transitional flow, linear flow, early-radial flow, and biradial flow). The duration of early-radial-flow regime depends on the interference among fractures.

Hydraulic fractures may be nonuniformly distributed along wellbore for field cases since it depends on the stress, properties of rocks, etc. Thus, five cases are selected to describe typical situations of fracture distribution. The type curves are shown in Figure 10. This factor influences linear flow, early-radial flow, and biradial flow. It is also observed that early-radial flow regime disappears due to strong interference among fractures (e.g. xwiD: 0.10-0.15-0.20-0.25-0.30). When fracture spacing become larger (e.g. xwiD: 0.10-0.45-0.50-0.55-0.90, xwiD: 0.10-0.75-0.80-0.85-0.90), early-radial flow gradually become obvious and two horizontal lines can be observed. In conclusion, the period of early-radial flow is dominated by the interference among fractures.

Different fracturing scale and fracture closure may result in unequal conductivity of fractures. We analyze influences of fracture conductivity on pressure response under two conditions. Total fracture conductivity and other parameters keep the same. The wellbore is assumed to be uniformly intercepted by five symmetric factures with equal production and conductivity in the first case. There exist differences between equal and unequal conductivity of fractures as shown in Figure 11a.

Furthermore, we compare transient-pressure behaviors under different total fracture conductivity, shown in Figure 12. It is observed that fracture conductivity is one of the most significant parameters that affect the production performance of MFHWs. The effects of fracture conductivity on pressure behaviors can be ignored when CfD is higher than 50.
