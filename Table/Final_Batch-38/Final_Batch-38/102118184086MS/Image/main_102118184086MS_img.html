Since the South and South-West areas of the RQLF field are still at an early stage of development, it was possible to investigate different well configurations so that an efficient development plan involving chemical EOR is identified. At first a 90m-spaced inverted 5-spot pattern was tested as illustrated on Figure 6.

Before the water breakthrough that is shown in Figure 7, the injection rate is plateauing at around 100 bpd when injecting chemical-free water. After the water breakthrough, from 2018 onwards, the injection rate is smoothly increasing up to 600 bpd because water is recycled. In the reservoir, the sweep is not efficient at all and water preferential paths are observed. A cross-sectional view of water saturation in the simulation grid is presented in Appendix 2.

Polymer dissolution in the injected water increases water viscosity and makes it harder to inject. This is reflected by the decrease in the water injection rate during polymer flooding down to 33 bpd as shown in Figure 8. While the sweep efficiency is increased in the reservoir as reflected by the decrease in the production watercut, the overall effect of the polymer remains unpromising using an inverted 5-spot pattern.

Additional investigation showed that injectivity is not only caused by a relatively low maximum injection pressure, but is additionally a consequence of low fluid outtake. As illustrated in Figure 9, the injection rate follows the liquid production rate. This means that increasing the liquid production rate (outtake) would increase the water injection rate through voidage replacement.

Following the first set of simulation runs using an inverted 5-spot pattern, improved well configurations were envisioned to increase the fluid outtake rate in order to increase the injected volumes through voidage replacement. As shown in Table 2, the final recovery for the waterflood case varies by approximately 1% only with all the studied patterns using vertical wells. Nonetheless, as illustrated on the right of Figure 12, when improved inverted patterns are used less injected water volume is needed to reach the same recovery factor. This effect is relatively more pronounced when an inverted 7-spot pattern is used.

As depicted in Figure 13, by plotting the best polymer flood scenario for each pattern, it is observed that polymer injection decreases the speed of oil recovery during the first years of simulation relative to when polymer-free water is injected. This is due to the decrease in the injection rates caused by the increased viscosity of the water. For the reservoir of interest, the difference between the reservoir and the maximum accepted injection pressures is relatively small (190 psi) and this restricts water injection at high rates especially with dissolved polymer.

Figure 14 shows that, relative to the polymer flood cases that are shown in Figure 13, the surfactantpolymer flood scenarios lead to a less significant decrease in the rate of recovery when compared with the corresponding waterflood base cases. This is due to surfactant presence which increases water mobility and injectivity by modifying the water relative permeability.

The inverted 7-spot pattern results were found to be more promising than those from the inverted 9-spot pattern. Figure 15 presents oil recovery versus cumulative water injection and clearly shows that, relative to waterflooding, polymer and surfactant-polymer injection can significantly increase oil recovery for a given volume of injected water.

The impact of pattern area was investigated for the pattern that resulted in the highest recovery factor which is the inverted 7-spot pattern. Three different spacing values were tested as illustrated in Figure 16. It should be noted that the previously reported results for the 7-spot pattern were based on a spacing of 80 m.

Figures 17 and 18 present efficiency curves to show the relationship of the recovery factor and cumulative water injection for the polymer scenario (at a polymer concentration of 2000 ppm) and the surfactant-polymer scenario (at polymer concentration of 3000 ppm and a surfactant concentration of 2000 ppm) using the three above-mentioned pattern sizes. It can be noticed from Figures 17 and 18 that the pattern size barely impacts the efficiency curves as those curves seem to be exactly superimposed with varying pattern sizes. Nonetheless, the pattern size severely impacts the final recovery factor obtained after 20 years of simulation by influencing injection capacity.
