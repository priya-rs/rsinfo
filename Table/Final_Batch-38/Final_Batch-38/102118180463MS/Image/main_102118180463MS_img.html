A regional depletion planning and comprehensive EOR screening was completed for Alaskan viscous oil (VO) resources, which have in-place volumes exceeding 10 billion barrels (Figure 1). Developed areas are shown in dark blue. With initial production dating from 1985 and a moderate development pace, viscous oil has experienced technology waves in drilling, completion, and EOR capability.

The Schrader Bluff Formation houses viscous oil resources (Figure 2) at depths between 2,000 and 5,000 feet. This formation is shallower than the Alaska light formations, but underlays the heavy oil Ugnu formation. The Schrader Bluff is also known as the West Sak Formation, especially in the Kuparuk River Unit.

Oil viscosities range from 5 cp to 10,000 cp (Figure 3,Table 1). Permafrost typically extends down to approximately 2000 feet so there is a strong temperature impact on oil viscosity at shallow depths. The median oil viscosity is approximately 150 cp for the resource with approximately half of the original oil in place having viscosity lower than this.

Oil quality and distribution is complex, but substantial data reduces risk of prediction. Oil viscosity controls include initial charge quality (typically ~28 API oil gravity), biodegradation (that reduces API gravity), and formation temperature. Bacterial oil biodegradation occurs with increased access to water and with decreasing temperatures at shallower depths, starting at temperatures lower than 80°C. Schrader Bluff oil API gravity and viscosity regionally improves with depth and temperature (Figure 4).

Multiple fault blocks, complex stratigraphy, and fluid fill history exert control on oil viscosity and fluid contacts (Figure 5). Areas filled with water are colored tan in this oil viscosity map.

Lower oil viscosity tranches have been developed preferentially given their higher oil productivity and ultimate recovery using conventional development methods (Figures 6 and 1).

Sands were deposited in a series of several successive shallow marine shoreface environments resulting in multiple commercial horizons. These are known as the O-sands and N-sands in the Prudhoe Bay Unit (PBU). The reservoir exhibits high lateral continuity on an interwell basis. Sediments are generally friable and poorly consolidated. The OA-sand contains one-third of Schrader Bluff oil in place (Figure 7).

The 1985 West Sak pilot and initial viscous oil developments used vertical well waterflood patterns with producer sand control. Although reliable, this strategy had relatively low oil rate (<400 bopd/producer) and low pattern waterflood rates. Producer drilling transitioned to horizontal single or multiple-lateral completions starting in 1998, significantly increasing oil rate (1000 to 3000 bopd/producer) enabling development to restart. Total well count increased markedly through 2010 (Figure 8).

The current generation plan of development (POD) calls for a long multilateral or single lateral (5000’+) horizontal producer with slotted liner completion (Figure 9). Injection is supported by wells with zonal injection control. If few or only one productive horizon exists, the injector can be a single lateral with multiple injection control points along its length (e.g. Nikaitchuq, Abahusayn, 2012).

Sand production is a constant aspect of production with slotted liner horizontal completions often leading to shorter than expected pattern life (Figure 10). Determining the root cause of sand production and the mitigation methods are a critical need for any future EOR program. Healthy waterflood patterns with long life and improved flood rate are key objectives for any EOR process. This geomechanical rock strength related issue then leads to sand control considerations.

MBEs are created when wormholes extending from the producer deep into the formation connect with injection or aquifer water (Figure 11). There are typically short fractures extending from injectors into the formation. When producer wormholes intersect injection or aquifer water, the direct water path to the producer dramatically decreases oil production.

Voidage used in Figure 12 is positive when production exceeds injection and average reservoir pressure declines below original. Conversely, voidage is negative when injection exceeds production and the reservoir experiences fill-up. A “corrected” interwell pressure gradient is a critical parameter in predicting the likely occurrence of MBEs. The correction allows for vertical and horizontal well patterns of different spacing to be analyzed by the same procedure.

One key learning from this study is that global viscous oil waterfloods typically achieve higher injection throughput and ultimate oil recovery than the Schrader Bluff. As shown in Figure 13, global analog waterfloods between 10 to 150 cp fields (small blue circles) typically achieve expected ultimate recovery of 30% at 2.0 hydrocarbon pore volumes injected (HCPVI), with some fields realizing 45% oil recovery at 4.5 HCPVI.

The Schrader Bluff Analog Quilt shown in Figure 16 based on global analogs is categorized by oil viscosity and KH (md-ft). Lower viscosity (Vo) and higher KH are a sweet spot for PF and ASP as enhancements to base waterflood. Injectivity is a key issue for polymer, so polymer and ASP are not applied globally in higher oil viscosity reservoirs. For similar economic reasons, waterflood is thought to be limited to <600 cp in the Schrader Buff. Regional viscosities shown in Figure 5 can be used to estimate which recovery process or processes may be prospective.

Polymer incremental recovery in lower viscosity areas and zones (less than 100 cp) was forecast to be potentially attractive (Figure 17). Solid lines reflect continuous polymer injection while dashed lines reflect a 0.42 HCPV polymer slug followed by water. A polymer concentration that delivered 15 cp fluid was selected since the next step to 30 cp only delivered approximately 1% incremental recovery. Clearly there are economic design optimums for slug size and polymer viscosity to explore, but the team looked only to qualify this process for future consideration.

ASP was also found to be a potentially viable extraction process in lower viscosity areas and zones. As shown in Figure 18, TPM results gave ASP 20% incremental recovery over waterflood at reservoir scale versus Polymer 6% to 10% incremental. Although ASP has significant recovery potential in viscous oil, there are considerable technical and economic challenges that require additional analysis.

Laboratory work began with oil analyses including SARA (saturates, asphaltenes, resins, aromatics), naphthenic acid, total acid number and total base number (TAN, TBN) measurements that suggest the Schrader Bluff is a good ASP candidate. Oil TAN averaged 0.8 mg of KOH/g of oil (Figure 19). The next step was screening P, SP, and ASP chemical formulations; four polymers, six surfactants, and three alkalis were tested.

Polymer screening included viscosity versus polymer concentration (Figure 20). Flopaam 3630 was selected for further consideration.

A type pattern model (TPM) was used to evaluate waterflood recovery with and without sand control and to determine incremental recovery for chemical EOR. The TPM used for this work was provided by ConocoPhillips. It was extracted from an Eclipse full field model (FFM) of the West Sak Core Area, calibrated to actual field performance, and converted to STARS (Figure 21).

Crushed core sand pack floods showed favorable ASP response with incremental oil production and oil recovery exceeding 90% (Figure 22) using 4750ppm Flopaam3630, 0.75% S23-13, 0.25% IOS, 1%IBA, 3.25% Na2CO3). However, the 12 psi/foot pressure drop is unsustainable in the field. Further testing is needed, perhaps using a better co-solvent to decrease microemulsion viscosity. Results are from a 1D sand pack; similar performance was seen in a 2D quarter of a five-spot pattern flood lab test.

Liquid CO2 is an effective solvent regularly used as an environmentally friendly dry cleaning fluid. At first blush, the liquid CO2 oil recovery mechanism is twice as effective as water injection as shown by core flood results in Figure 23. Lab test results show that starting CO2 WAG early as a secondary process does not sacrifice any oil recovery. Since injectivity is actually improved with CO2, there is significantly more incremental oil at any given time versus waiting to run CO2 as a tertiary process.

Typically CO2 incremental oil recovery is not this effective since it is normally a gaseous displacement process. But for the Schrader Bluff’s relatively low temperature conditions, it is a liquid or a high density supercritical fluid and thus far more effective in terms of mobility, density, and extraction (Figure 24).

Mechanistic modelling was performed using a GEM type pattern model (Figure 25). Liquid CO2 cases were run using the sand control simulation TPM with the Orion POD (Figure 9). Production laterals assumed sand control, enabling increased interwell gradient and pattern flood rate. Injectors used waterflood regulating valves by zone so that water was properly distributed by zone to honor voidage. During gas injection, the water valves are swapped out for gas control valves.

Comparison of waterflood and liquid CO2 flood results on the basis of RF versus HCPVI is shown in Figure 26. The reference case for liquid CO2 was sand control using open hole gravel pack (OHGP) and OBa (B-sand) “no thief” zone treatment. Waterflood was used for 5 years prior to the start of liquid CO2 injection, after producing WOR was greater than 1.0.

In concept, liquid CO2 floods in Alaska’s viscous oil could be a major customer for a CO2 supply to capture this EOR benefit. At full development, most CO2 could be recycled within the viscous oil developments and would therefore require a new, dedicated, corrosion resistant viscous oil facility (Figure 27). Tests show that “impure” 95% CO2 / 5% methane does not significantly degrade recovery, thus the facility would not need to generate a pure CO2 product for produced gas re-injection.
