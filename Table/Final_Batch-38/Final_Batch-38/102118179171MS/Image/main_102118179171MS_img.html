This paper builds on previous work done by Griffin and others (2013). A database consisting of completion and production parameters was assembled from the NDIC and Frac Focus websites and from internal completion documentation. The area of interest for this study was confined to the Central Basin, also known as the “Rough Rider” area, within Townships 148-159N and Ranges 97-105W (Figure 1). The bubbles represent 180-day cumulative oil per foot of completed lateral (180-day cumulative oil/ft), and contours in the background represent water cut.

Figure 2 shows the histogram plots of ft/stage (stage spacing), cemented (“0” represents uncemented laterals and “1” represents cemented laterals), number of stages, average proppant concentration (Avg-PPG), percentage of ceramic proppant pumped, mass of proppant pumped in lb/ft, volume of frac fluid pumped in bbl/ft, maximum treatment rate, water cut, gross thickness, oil gravity, Lower Bakken Shale TOC, Upper Bakken Shale TOC, total vertical depth (TVD), and 180-day cumulative oil/ft for the Middle Bakken laterals located in the Rough Rider Area.

Figure 3 is the predicted versus observed 180-day cumulative oil/ft for the multiple regression without interaction terms (left) and the multiple regression with 28 interactions (right). The diagonal black reference line indicates where the observed and predicted values would be equal, while the diagonal red line indicates a linear relationship between the observed and predicted values.

Figure 4 compares the predicted versus observed 180-day cumulative oil/ft of the BIC and AIC chosen models. The BIC and AIC chosen models are very similar to the multiple regression model 2 in terms of the R-squared and training RMSE results. However, the BIC and AIC chosen models are simpler than the multiple regression model 2 since many of the interaction terms have been eliminated without sizeable reduction of the R-squared value and the training RMSE.

Figure 5 (left) shows the predicted versus observed 180-day cumulative oil/ft for the random forest model. This model gives an R-squared of 0.63 and a training RMSE of 1.538 BO/ft. Figure 6 (left) presents the importance of each of the eight predictors for the Middle Bakken data based on the random forest regression. The importance is measured by permutation of the values of a predictor and measuring the percent decrease in RMSE.

For the Three Forks training set, we performed similar data quality control or preprocessing by examining the histograms, scatterplots, bivariate plots, and coefficient correlations among variables in the database. Figure 8 presents the histogram plots for the Three Forks data. Of the 778 wells in the original Three Forks dataset, there were only 444 wells with non-missing values for the predictors included in the analysis.

Figure 9 shows the predicted versus observed 180-day cumulative oil/ft for the multiple regression model 1 without the interaction terms (left) and the multiple regression model 2 with 28 interactions (right). Again, if all of the points lie near the diagonal black reference line, this indicates a good model fit to the training data. The diagonal red line simply indicates a linear relationship between the observed and predicted values.

The multiple regression model 1 consisting of eight predictors results in an R-squared of 0.54 and a training RMSE of 1.745 BO/ft whereas the multiple regression model 2 gives an R-squared of 0.70 with training RMSE of 1.408 BO/ft.
