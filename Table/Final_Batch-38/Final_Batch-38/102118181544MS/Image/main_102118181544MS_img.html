As noted above, a common occurrence is to have a late gas charge into an oil reservoir. Frequently, this gas will rise to the top of the reservoir above the oil. The gas can then diffuse down into the oil column. Figure 5 shows an example of this occurrence.

Figure 6 shows a reservoir that has three adjacent fault blocks where gas charged into oil reservoirs. Each fault block shows entirely different reservoir realizations due to reservoir fluid geodynamic (RFG) processes. [Mullins et al, 2016; Pfeiffer et al, 2016; Achourov et al, 2015] These different RFG processes show a variety of mechanisms that yield asphaltene instability.

Figure 7 shows a scanning electron micrograph (SEM) from a core plug in the tar mat. The asphaltene/tar has the characteristic smooth surface along with cracks that can occur with loss of light ends. [Reeder et al, 2016] There is ample asphaltene in the core; this type of tar mat can be a pressure seal or at least a baffle; thus is important when considering aquifer support. Because the gas diffused to the base of the column, the solvent crude oil became unable to hold much asphaltene, thereby becoming worse.

Figure 8 shows the incipient precipitation that takes place with ‘too much’ asphaltene (solute) added to the crude oil (solvent) at the base of the oil column. Here the concentration is approaching 40% asphaltene in the oil; it is not surprising that the oil cannot dissolve even more asphaltene. The asphaltene is being added by gravity accumulation of nanoparticles towards the base of the oil column.

Figure 9 shows DFA asphaltene and GOR measurements in a field. Most of the field has an equilibrated black oil indicating connectivity. However, Wells A and B show very different fluids at the same subsurface elevation in the oil column. If the conventional density stacking of reservoir fluids applies, then these two wells would appear to be not connected. However, if there is a recent condensate charge in the flank, and a lateral sweep in trap filling, then these two wells could be connected.

As seen in Fig. 15, the extracted asphaltene content in the core plugs does not vary monotonically with height. This is a two phase system, trapped heavy oil and occluding asphaltene deposition. The changing ratio of asphaltene content from core plugs depends on the fraction of these two phases. Tight streaks in this carbonate reservoir contain more asphaltene; below tight streaks there is more trapped oil.

Data from two tar wells are plotted in Fig. 16. All components are normalized by the saturates (or paraffins). This normalization is done because otherwise, the other SARA components decrease as the asphaltenes increase from 30% to 60% obscuring the desired effect. As the %-asphaltene increases in the core plug, the plug contains a greater fraction of deposited asphaltene. Fig. 16 shows that only the normalized asphaltene fraction increases with increasing asphaltene deposition, not the resins and not the aromatics.
