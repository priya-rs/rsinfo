Foam flooding theory (Rossen, Zhou, & Mamun, 1995) (Khatib, Hirasaki, & Falls, 1988) (Alvarez, Rivas, & Rossen, 2001) describes well the rheological behavior of foam in a model porous media, i.e. foam flow in a homogeneous core or sandpack in isothermal conditions under constant back-pressure and in absence of oil. Figure 1 represents a typical sandpack or coreflood setup.

To evaluate foam rheological behavior, different interstitial velocities should be explored. The evolution of apparent viscosity with interstitial velocity at fixed foam quality is presented in Figure 2. We make a distinction between weak foams with moderate to low apparent viscosity, having Newtonian behavior and strong foam which can exhibit high apparent viscosities. Strong foams show an increase in apparent viscosity with interstitial velocity until the critical interstitial velocity vi* is reached. Above vi*, foam has an average shear thinning behavior.

The evolution of apparent viscosity with foam quality at fixed interstitial velocity is presented in Figure 3. Two regimes are described for strong foams: low foam quality regime and high foam quality regime (Alvarez 2001). In the high foam quality regime, insufficient lamellae stabilization leads to continuous gas paths in the porous media. The flow is dominated by coalescence. In the low quality regime, foam flows as gas bubbles separated by liquid lamellae, which are stable toward coalescence and the flow is dominated by bubble trapping and lamellae displacement.

A steam foam pilot was initiated in 1985 after 7 years of steamflood in 4 inverted five-spot patterns, four injectors and nine producers in total (Figure 11). The aim of the pilot was to reduce steam override in this very thick (134 m) reservoir.

The issue of temperature and in particular of the maximum applicable temperature for steam foam is very sensitive. As demonstrated by Table 10 and Figure 14, steam foam has mostly been used below 250°C. Of the tests above 250°C, one (Bachaquero) corresponds to cyclic injections (Robaina & Ziritt, 1988) for which surfactants stability is not as important; one (Cat Canyon) failed maybe due to temperature (Eson & Cook, 1989); and three over 300°C in Shengli correspond to high temperature gas foam with steam rather than to steam foam.

There is relatively little information on how far foam can propagate into the reservoir. Figure 15 reproduced from Mohammadi and Tenzer (Mohammadi & Tenzer, 1990) shows the evolution of foam volume as a function of injected surfactant. According to this interepretation foam can propagate at large distances into the reservoir provided that enough surfactant is injected.
