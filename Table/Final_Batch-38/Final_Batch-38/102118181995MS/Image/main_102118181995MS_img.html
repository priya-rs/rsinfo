The greatest developed reserves are assigned to Urmano-Archinskaya zone (Fig. 1) well studied with exploration drilling, including core sampling and geophysical methods, well logging and well testing. Thematic works in geology research, geochemistry, stress and fractures reconstruction were carried using these data.

 

Pilot production of Archinskoe oil field accounted for these issues. The field was well characterized with 3D seismic with seismic attribute analysis and prediction of seismic attribute "synergetic singularity", or "deconsolidation"; electric and acoustic well imagers (Fig. 3). Pilot production splits up to Paleozoic deposits drilling; upper Jurassic reservoirs development; geological exploration.

Paleozoic geological features within Urmano-Archinskaya zone are determined by massive carbonate deposits with well-developed tectonics, reservoir vugs, pores and fractures storativity and conductivity, as well as gas cap and bottom water influence (Figure 2). The formation is divided into M layer – residual soil consisting of aluminic bauxite-like rocks, and M1 layer - Paleozoice carbonate rocks itself made up of biogenic limestone within Urmanskoe oil field and shallow water shoal facies on Archinskoe oil field, as it has been discovered during palaeo-facial reconstruction (Appendix A-1).

Drilling of Urmanskoe oil field met many drilling problems: oil and gas shows, catastrophic losses of mud return, drill pipe drag and sticking, rock sloughing and well collapse. Urmanskoe and Archinskoe oil fields were drilled with bit set on casing to prevent these failures (Appendix A-2). Drilling-with-casing reduces amount of wells abandoned while drilling significantly, as well as enables underbalanced drilling with full geosteering.

Place for "Fishbone" multilateral well drilling was chosen outside the gas cap and within sufficient net pay thickness zone. The area is surrounded by wells with proven efficiency (Appendix A-3).

The approximate multilateral well drilling diagram is shown in Appendix A-4 (a). Appendix A-4 (b) shows generic completion scheme for the multilateral well. The feature of this technology is possibility for selective treatment and isolation of every branch without entrance.

Expected value of well flow rate was calculated by weighing the probabilities of fracture facing by each amount of braches (Appendix A-5) for this reason.

Maximum liquid flow rate (usually represented by initial well flow rate) showed negligibly small correlation with seismic attributes (Appendix A-8) like acoustic impedance, RMS amplitude, attenuation frequency and "deconsolidation" attribute.

Similar correlation of maximum liquid flow rate and mean azimuth difference, mean dip angle difference, depth under M1 top and mud loss rate (Appendix A-9) revealed weak interconnection of these parameters.

Cross-reference of mud loss rate (Appendix A-10) and seismic attributes confidently selects attenuation frequency parameter. It does not allow predicting loss of return fact, but certainly indicates mud loss rate increase (in logarithmic scale) with this parameter growth.

Prediction of the mud loss rate using various technological and geometrical parameters (Appendix A-11) shows that the depth under M1 top is the most representative parameter. But this fact is associated again with analyzed data selection, namely, horizontal well configuration strategy.
