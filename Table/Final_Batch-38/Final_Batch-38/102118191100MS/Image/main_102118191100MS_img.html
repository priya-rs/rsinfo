The roller cone bit, especially Tungsten Carbide Insert (TCI) removes the formation with crushing and chipping action. For a hard and abrasive formation like in geothermal fields, this type of cutting action is very effective. Comparing to PDC bit shearing action, TCI removes the rock with two steps of action. The first step is indenting and fracturing the rock, and the second step is removing the rock from crater that has generated during the first step (Noviasta et al). These steps are shown in Figure 2.

TCI bit with IADC 537 usually used to drill 9.875" section in Hululais field. This bit type can drill up to 200 – 300 m befored pulled out due to Krevs limitation. To finish 700 – 800 m of 9.875" section, it needs around 3 – 4 bits with around 4 – 5 m/hr of rate of penetration. Some of the bits pulled out of hole with severe dull condition due to the formation impact and abrasiveness. Figure 3 shows the bit condition after running in the hole and performance comparison of this bit.

 

Since the beginning of PDC bit utilization, the bit company improved the cutter quality by changing the grade of the diamond and improving the process of making PDC cutters. Currently, changing the geometry of PDC cutters into conical geometry brings a huge benefit to the drilling process, especially in hard and abrasive drilling condrition (Figure 5).

Conical Diamond Element (CDE) bit has been developed and tested in the laboratory for several years. Doubling the diamond thickness on the diamond table generates two times impact strength and improves the wear resistance by 25 – 30%. CDE bit fails the high compressive strength formation by combining the TCI'[Start Box Here] crushing action and PDC's shearing action, called a "plowing action". A Finite Element Analysis (FEA) shows that the sharp conical geometry helps to concentrate the point loading of the cutter and remove the rocks more efficiently (Figure 6). Compared to PDC cutters, this plowing action of CDE bit requires less force to achieve same fracture generation rate.

The electronic logs from the offset wells, like Gamma Ray, Neutron and Density, and Sonic log are used to quantify the rock percentage depth by depth. Furthermore, the compressional sonic log is used to calculate the formation hardness, measured by unconfined compressive strength (UCS) in kpsi. The UCS value and the formation type then used to calculate the formation impact and hardness (Figure 7). From the RSA software, the section consists of breccia and igneous rock such as tuff and andesite with 18 – 30 kpsi of UCS, heavy impact, and very high abrasiveness.

The bit selector is connected into more than 12,500 wells database to improve the accuracy of the bit selection. For this section, PDC selector in this software recommends applying 12 – 13 blades of PDC cutters (equivalent to 6 – 7 blades with double row cutters), 11 – 16 mm cutters size, and short blade profile to improve the bit durability (Figure 8).

 

 

All bit design has short to medium parabolic blade profile that applicable for hard and abrasive formation (Figure 11). Bit-3 has longer shoulder area that can increase the durability at the shoulder. However, this type of bit is actually more applicable for softer formation. The cutter density at the cone and the shoulder area for Bit-1, 2, and 4 is more balance for Hululais field application.

 

From the ROP comparison, all simulated bits generated similar trends (Figure 13). Bit-3 with 6 blades and 19 mm cutter generated higher ROP compared to other simulated bits. However, as Bit-1 with 7 blades and 16 mm cutters is more stable, this bit is more applicable for this application. Simulation with wider parameter range then performed to provide stable parameter roadmap as a guideline in the field.

Figure 14 shows the drilling parameter roadmap for Bit-1 with 7 blades and 16 mm cutters. This roadmap considers all drillstring component from the bit, motor, M/LWD tools, stabilizers, collars, and the drill pipe to the surface, based on the low vibration threshold. Stable parameter is marked by green colors, while stick/ slip, lateral vibration, and axial vibration consecutively by orange, pink, and blue color. From the parameter roadmap, it is recommended to use WOB below 35 klbs as higher WOB induced stick/slip or torsional vibration to the drilling system.

The objective of this section was to drill the production zone at interval around 1000 m length. The prerun condition of the bit can be seen at Figure 15. The PDC bit was make up with the steerable BHA and run in hole until it reached the bottom hole. Nozzle placement was set to 1.436-in2 TFA (Total Flow Area), expecting a high number of HSI (hydraulic horsepower per-square inch). The drilling parameter applied was based on the roadmap given by the simulation.

The MSE number in HE-3 well ranging between 300-1200 kpsi Compared to the offset wells at the similar depth, the PDC bits produces higher penetration rate with the lower WOB, means efficient mechanical specific energy (MSE) needed through out the run. It showed a higher bit run efficiency. The parameter comparison shown in Figure 16. By using the average WOB around 18.6 klbs, RPM 200, and 700 GPM plus 1800 SCFM aerated water, the average ROP of the PDC is 8.0 m/hr, increased around 60% compared to average ROP or 35% compared to best ROP in TCI bits.

The actual friction factor exceeds 0.5 approximately at the beginning of the section, with significant deviation from the initial roadmap (Figure 17). There is no drag recorded while tripping out, and reaffirmed later on while running the perforated liner without any drag recorded.

Several chipped teeth were found and minor damages were also found on the stinger elements. The bit was reasonably eligible to re-run for the next well. The bit has succesfully giving ± 600 m meterage, thus eliminated about 2 tripping time. Furthermore, it was generated ROP higher than the TCI run used in offset wells. Compared to the offset wells data in Hululais field, PDC bits has give a value creation about US$ 300K – 700K. Moreover, PDC bit were also give another value creation by improving the trajectory control, which means reducing the risk of having a combine load torque and drag that frequently faced by deep drilling (>3000 mMD) in geothermal wells. On the Cost per Foot (CPF) analysis, this bit provides low CPF value, emphasizing the bit efficiency compared to offset wells as can be seen in Figure 19.
