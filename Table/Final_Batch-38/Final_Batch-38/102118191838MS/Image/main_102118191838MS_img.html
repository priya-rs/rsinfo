This is the data source used. However, the Vaca Muerta formation produces hydrocarbons ranging from black oil to dry gas. A fluid distribution map of Vaca Muerta (Dirección General de Estudios 2017; Gutiérrez Schmidt et al. 2014), with class boundaries defined by initial dissolved gas-oil ratios of 20,000, 2,500, 750 and 250 scf/bbl, has been used as a base to classify wells by produced fluid. According to the location of the wells relative to the fluid zones in the map, the wells were classified as black-oil, volatile-oil, gas-condensate, wet-gas, and dry-gas (Fig. 1).

Detailed sequential seismostratigraphic interpretation is considered key in framing the characterization of the Vaca Muerta formation and revealing its systematic lateral and vertical changes. This helped materialize the "Transecta regional Vaca Muerta" project (González et al. 2016), which defined a consented and simple stratigraphic scheme of regional reach (Fig. 2).

The database wells were classified according to their landing zone using "Rosetta" (González Tomassini et. al 2016), which is the consensual stratigraphic equivalence table (Fig. 3). The grouping determined four subclasses: wells landing at Unit 1 (kitchen), wells landing at Unit 2 (organic), wells landing at Unit 3 and Unit 4 (lower prograding), and wells landing at Unit 5 and Unit 6 (upper prograding).

The linear workflow, which is recycled monthly, includes a stage of data loading and consolidation in the unified database, a data preparation instance, and a final step in which the analyst generates the output data (Fig. 5). A detailed step-by-step procedure in the form of a set of instructions for a third person with access to a standard spreadsheet application (Appendix A) was prepared.

The pointsets are displayed in scatter charts, where power log and logarithmic trendline curves are fitted. Our empirical best-fit curve model averages both trendlines. In cases in which the power law exponent is greater than 1, it is replaced by a linear trendform. The modeled cumulative production is calculated, and from it, an average daily production rate is derived. We introduce an arbitrary decline factor that nullifies this rate in 20 years (7,200 effective days) using the cumulative production curve equation (Fig. 6).

The sample includes wells that have been in production for up to 5 years (1,800 effective days), although most of the 150 to 200 wells that contribute to the statistically significant portion of the computed average produced for only less than 1 year (360 effective days) (Fig. 7). The data cloud has a typical open-hand shape with great dispersion.

The overall sample of horizontal wells in Vaca Muerta producing any type of fluid, in any landing zone or location, would accumulate 95,000 BOE, 205,000 BOE, and 290,000 BOE at 1, 3, and 5 years, respectively, for each 1,000 m of stimulated lateral length, according to the modeled type curve, P50. For such effective times, the daily rates would be 190 BOE/D, 150 BOE/D, and 115 BOE/D (Fig. 8). A generatrix algorithm that models the type curve and a data table with the filtered set of significant P50 points are included in the scatter chart. The equation syntax can be pasted as a function in a cell of a standard spreadsheet.

The heterogenity of the overall well sample is revealed by the large difference between P90 and P10 type curves with the mean, as P90 is approximately 2.5 times that of P10 (Fig. 9).

Black-oil wells are the second largest subclass in the database. The sample count in the statistically significant zone ranges from 145 to 50. The dispersion pattern in the data cloud has a bird's tail shape, with an average cumulation of 91,000 BOE in 360 days (Fig. 10).

The median is equal to the average at 360 days, with rates above 180 BOE/D during the first year. A cumulative production of 300,000 BOE was reached after six years (2,160 effective days) (Fig. 11).

The P90 model type curve for black-oil-producing wells accumulated 400,000 BOE in six years, with production rates above 250 BOE/D in the first year, while the P10 type curve showed production rates that fell below 100 BOE/D in the second year (720 effective days) and did not surpass a 300,000-BOE cumulation (Fig. 12).

Wet-oil, gas-condensate, and wet-gas-producing wells are grouped under a single class because of their scarcity (Fig. 13) and appear bimodal. The successive ending of the production data series of shorter-life wells strongly affects the average data. Its modeled type curves were prepared for consistency (Fig. 14 and Fig. 15). The median data points series was more stable on average and was filtered to 180 days. The first year of production showed daily rates above 200 BOE/D. A cumulative production of 500,000 BOE was modeled at nine years (3240 effective days).

The dry-gas well subclass shows a dispersed, but highly symmetrical, data distribution. Significant data point series extend beyond 500 days, and the normalized average cumulative production reached 280,000 BOE in one year (Fig. 16). The type curve shows rates above 450 BOE/D the first year. According to the model, the first cumulative 500,000 BOE will be reached after three years (1,080 effective days) (Fig. 17). In contrast, the P90 dataset rolls that cumulative to one year, with rates above 1,000 BOE/D (Fig. 18).

Black-oil-producing wells located in zone 2a provide a homogeneous sample, showing small dispersion. The average data series reached 600 days, with a cumulative production of 123,000 BOE and a data count in the significant range of approximately 50 wells (Fig. 20).

The modeled type curve indicates daily production rates above 120 BOE/D in the first year (360 effective days) and a cumulative production of 200,000 BOE after six years (2,160 effective days) (Fig. 21).

Black-oil-producing wells in zone 2b comprise a slightly greater and more asymmetrical subclass (Fig. 22), producing at rates above 170 BOE/D, with a cumulative production of 98,000 BOE in the first year and 250,000 BOE after five years (Fig. 23), according to the type curve.

Black-oil-producing wells in zone 2b comprise a slightly greater and more asymmetrical subclass (Fig. 22), producing at rates above 170 BOE/D, with a cumulative production of 98,000 BOE in the first year and 250,000 BOE after five years (Fig. 23), according to the type curve.

After modeling the type curves for black-oil-producing wells in zones 2a and 2b, both could be compared to their cumulative and derived rates (Fig. 24). Wells located in 2b were shown to be approximately 30% more productive than the wells located in 2a. This is merely a quantitative conclusion that is derived by applying the methodology to the data of interest. For a similar sample, an integrated study (Licitra et al. 2015) showed comparable behavior with respect to production.

The wells landing in Unit 1 ("the kitchen") (Fig. 27) had a cumulative production of 215,000, 445,000, and 625,000 BOE at 1, 3, and 5 years, respectively (360, 1,080, and 1,800 effective days), with daily production rates of 405, 295, and 250 BOE/D.

The wells landing in Unit 2 ("organic") (Fig. 28) had a cumulative production of 90,000, 180,000, and 250,000 BOE at 1, 3, and 5 years, respectively, with daily production rates of 160, 105, and 90 BOE/D.

A few wells landed in Units 3 and 4, with a more dispersed data cloud that computed significant values for up to 300 days. According to the modeled type curve (Fig. 29), these wells had a cumulative production of 75,000, 140,000, and 180,000 BOE at 1, 3, and 5 years respectively, with daily rates of 120 BOE/D, 70 BOE/D, and 50 BOE/D.

The wells landing in Unit 5 and Unit 6 ("upper prograding") comprise a very homogeneous sample. The modeled cumulative productions (Fig. 30) are 15,4000 BOE, 290,000 BOE, and 385,000 BOE at 1, 3, and 5 years, respectively, with daily production rates of 260, 170, and 120 BOE/D.

Type curves discriminated by landing zone are compared in Fig. 31. In short approximation, wells landing in the kitchen and upper prograding levels produce at 1.75 and 1.15 times the production, respectively, of the P50 type curve used as reference, while wells landing at the organic and lower prograding levels produce below the reference type curve at 0.71 and 0.55, respectively.
