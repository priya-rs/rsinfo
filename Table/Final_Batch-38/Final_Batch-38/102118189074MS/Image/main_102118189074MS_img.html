The fluid is in a gas phase at initial condition, while liquid is expected to be formed at saturation condition, when reservoir pressure depletes to dew point and increases to its maximum below the dew point, and later revaporizes (retrograde nature), a process known as isothermal revaporization. The result of RFT suggests a compositional grading phenomenon in the reservoir as the measured flash CGR ranges from 188 to 280bbl/MMstb. The measured flash CGR from the upper unit during the well deliverability test (10 years after the well was drilled) aligned favourably with the result of RFT. Figure 1 is the G2X structural depth map and flash CGR profile.

The PVT report for BHS from the deliverability test was used for this study. The CVD Experiment shows a condensate drop out of c.a. 40% at around 4000 psi. G2X PVT was characterized using EoS (P78 Peneloux) lumped into 10 pseudo-components. Attempt was made to capture the observed compositional grading phenomenon in G2X during the fluid characterization as shown in Figure 2. The combined measured mole percent of Nitrogen and Methane (N2+CH4) at the sample depth (9630 ftss) is 69.64% and it compares favourably with the pvt model combined (N2+CH4) mole percent of 69.71% at the same depth.

To account for possible reservoir heterogeneity, ten (10) permeability classes, ranging from lower shoreface to fluvial channel were used to generate relperm/capillary pressure curves assuming a water-wet system. Linear Isoperm has been used as 3-phase model (GW & GO) to account for retrograde condensation phase as the reservoirs are being depleted below the dew point pressure. The flowrates near the well can be high tending to cause viscous stripping. These effects are modelled by allowing the relative permeability curves to be dependent on the capillary numbers. Figures 3 and 4 are the relperm/Pc Curves for G1X and G2X.

The estimated recoverable volumes for Horner field are based on detailed 3D dynamic modelling with reservoir uncertainty management using Design of Experiment. Nine (9) uncertainty parameters were identified, evaluated and their ranges from low to high were defined. They were then screened using high level DoE screening algorithm (Plackett Burman Design). Six (6) uncertainty parameters were progressed in each reservoir to the next level of Experimental Design (Box-Behnken Design). The key uncertainties in Horner field are structure, Net-to-Gross, relative permeability and permeability. The results of parameter screening are shown in Figures 5 & 6.

Six (6) development wells have been found to be optimal in Horner Field; Four (4) wells in G1X with potential offtake rate of 150MMscfd/well and Two (2) development wells in G2X with potential offtake rate of 70MMscfd/well. Figure 8 is the Horner Field Production Forecast with "Hybrid IPSM"

Since gas recycling does not cover G1X, a decision was taken to use the 22 pseu-docomponents model as PX representation to minimize compositional effect, while G2X stand alone dynamic model was converted to full compositional model with component mass rate forecast. For production in G2X, some 50% of the mass flow is formed by the methane, ethane, propane and butane molecule (C1 to C4) while heavy alkenes (C5+C6+C7+…) make up the remaining 50% as shown in Figure 9.

The new "compositional IPSM model" compares favourably with the HybridIPSM model with less than 5% reduction in total production as shown in Figure 10.

In IPSM, the surface rates of the produced subsurface hydrocarbons depend strongly on the processing involved in bringing the reservoir fluids to the surface. At each timestep, IPSM effectively withdraws a mass rate from the reservoir at each producing well and convert these mass rates in to volumetric rates, hence a "surface flash model" is assigned to each well. For compositional model, IPSM uses a separator train as shown in Figure 11.

For optimum injector placement, an in-house algorithm was used to identify potential flooding zone, where the planned injectors could be optimally placed as shown in Figure 12. Well placement optimization sensitivities also indicated that Inj_1 is the most optimally placed injector. Gas re-injection scheme was carried out for eight (8) years. Sensitivities were carried on injector counts to investigate range of incremental condensate recovery. The following scenarios were compared with the Base Case Forecast (No Injection Scenario) to establish the gain.

The Figure 14 is the field production profiles for the three injector scenarios considered. There is no significant difference between 1-Injector scenario and 2-Injector scenario; however, there is about 15%reduction in condensate plateau beyond 2 injectors. Thus, one injector (Inj_1) located in north eastern flank of the field is found to be optimal for G2X gas recycling.

There is an increase in producing CGR plateau period for 1-injector and 2-injector scenarios with respect to the base case (no injection scenario) as reservoir pressure remains above dew point during reinjection phase as shown in Figure 15. However, there is early gas breakthrough in 3-Injector scenario, which shortens its producing CGR plateau. G2X_1 well actually cut-in gas earlier as shown in Figure 16 because of its proximity to the third injector (Inj_3). This explains the reason for shorter condensate plateau period in 3-Injector scenario compared with other two injection scenarios as seen in Figure 13.
