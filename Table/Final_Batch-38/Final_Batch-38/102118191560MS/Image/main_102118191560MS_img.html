Pioneer has collected microseismic data on over a portion of more than 250 horizontal wells, which is one of the largest collections of microseismic data in the industry. When calibrated with other datasets and filtered in a consistent manner, microseismic data has shown to be a reliable indicator of fracture geometry in the Permian Basin shales. A reliable data collection, QC, filtering, and storage process is critical to getting maximum benefit from a large microseismic dataset. We have developed and documented a detailed workflow as summarized by Fig. 4, which ensures our microseismic dataset is evaluated in a consistent and reliable manner.

In addition to a high quality dataset, a multi-disciplinary approach to completion design is key to optimizing completion in a timely and cost-effective manner (Jaripatke et al., 2013 & 2014). High quality visualization tools allow for quick analysis of a specific data set and the display of data from a variety of sources. Fig. 7 shows how microseismic data is integrated in the hydraulic fracture modeling. The Petrophysics-derived log on the left shows some of the important well-log properties, such as gamma ray, volume of clay (Vclay), Poisson's ratio and pore-pressure gradient. As a starting point, the landing depth is adjusted to optimize net hydrocarbon contacted, fracture height and half-length growth based on nearby offset well logs.

Once the model is established, reservoir simulations are performed for production history match and forecast. The newly calibrated fracture model is then used for sensitivity analysis for various possible designs and their impact. Comparisons between decline-curve based EURs and increasing fracture model surface area is shown in Fig. 8. It is important to note that while an increasing trend is observed with increased fracture model surface area, the correlation is also heavily influenced by well spacing and child effects (A "child" well is defined as a well completed after nearby "parent" well(s) produced for a certain amount of time).

As increasing surface area generally requires increased capital spending, well spacing comes into play as shown in Fig. 9. The question is whether we drill fewer wells with larger completions and achieve the same or better results per section than we obtain with more wells and smaller completion designs. The figure demonstrates that for super large jobs, EUR does not degrade if wells are widely spaced (i.e. no interaction between wells). On the other hand, for smaller jobs at the same spacing, we achieve lower EURs and leave untapped hydrocarbons between the wells. As well spacing gets tighter, EURs for larger treatments decrease due to interference.

The process of integrating frac models with spacing and EUR degradation analysis enables us to analyze various combinations of spacing and completion designs and match with field tests as shown in Fig. 10. In the example shown in the figure, three cases are presented. Cases A & C have two wells on each pad, and have same well spacing while case B has three wells with the outside wells spaced the same as the two on pad A & C (that is, Case B has half the well separation than cases A & C).

A typical recent Permian well has a landing depth between 7,000 and 10,500 feet TVD with lateral length in the range of 7,000 and 13,000 feet. The wells are typically drilled and cased with three or four casing strings including 5½" production casing. The reservoir pressures typically vary between 3,500 and 6,000 psi with reservoir temperatures between 140 and 180 degrees Fahrenheit. Typical completion designs are implemented using pump down "Plug and Perf" method with 6,000 – 9,000 psi surface treating pressure. While designing the completion plan, well-interaction considerations must be taken into account, as shown in Fig. 11.

The following factors are considered in order to optimize fracture containment: 1) fluid volume (bbl/cluster), 2) fluid injection rate (bpm/cluster), 3) cluster spacing and stage length and 4) cluster stimulation efficiency (e.g. diversion techniques). Fig. 12 shows the evolution of cluster spacing and stage length during development of our Permian assets to date. Each successive reduction in cluster spacing showed improvement in well performance while containing the hydraulic fracture closer to the wellbore. However, decreasing stage length also comes with increased cost.

The first well performance improvement case involves the evaluation of increased barrel/foot. As shown in Fig. 13, the 50 bbl/ft outperforms the 36/bbl/ft and 20 bbl/ft. Once production performance is established and repeated, corresponding factors were examined in order to determine if changes to future completions were justified. Fluid volume higher than 50 bbl/ft were tested in more recent evaluations and enhanced results were achieved.

The second well performance improvement test involves increased lb/ft. As shown in Fig. 14, production enhancement was observed as lb/ft was systematically increased from 1,400 lb/ft to 3,000 lb/ft. Similar to Case 1, factors were examined and changes were implemented field-wide wherever appropriate.

The third case demonstrates the effect of increased bbl/ft and lb/ft (combinations of Case 1 and Case 2). Fig. 15 shows a large stimulation with 67 bbl/ft and 2,500 lb/ft significantly outperforms older and smaller completions with 36 bbl/ft and 1,400 lb/ft.

The fourth case evaluated the effects of reduced stage length and reduced cluster spacing. Fig. 16 shows the production enhancement achieved while reducing stage length from 360’ to 150’ while simultaneously decreasing cluster spacing from 60’ to 30'. All other completion parameters remained the same between the test pad and the reference pad.
