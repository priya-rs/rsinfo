Geologically, the field belongs to lower cretaceous age and is divided into six distinct anticline layers; for keeping reservoir performance on track each layer is further divided into six sectors as shown in Figure 1. The division of sectors arose out of operational convenience and was not defined by any special characteristics of the layers in the reservoir.

Building on the project objectives, six different tracers were selected for injection through the crestal gas injectors. The target area of investigation was estimated a couple of hunderds square kilometers based on the shallowest oil producers’ locations and the fluid movement predictions as shown in Figure – 2.

Simulation study on gas saturation was conducted to track the gas movement over the period of time, where H2S was introduced into the compositional model as a tracing marker to monitor the gas movement in each reservoir. Time-lapse simulation results were analyzed as shown in Figure – 3 & Figure – 4.

The time-lapse analysis shows that gas moves along the preferential path in the Southwest direction i.e. towards pressure sink area as shown in Figure – 5.

Figure 5a illustrates the tracer breakthrough path ways along with the oil and gas production trends for these wells.

Reservoir complexity could be another reason that would have led to the delayed breakthrough. From the logs (Figure 6) it could clearly be seen that top layers 1, 2 and 3 have higher degree of homogeneity as compared to the bottom three layers i.e. layers 4, 5 and 6. Accordingly, layers 4, 5 and 6 were expected to see a faster breakthrough as compared to the top layers. While we see this in the case of the deepest layer i.e. layer 6 (the only layer that saw a breakthrough), layers 4 and 5 did not see any breakthrough at all.

After the tracer study was carried out and a much higher tracer arrival time was observed. As part of periodic simulation model review, the simulation model for Layer 1 was revalidated whilst incorporating the freshly interpreted reservoir characteristics and updated production trends; information about the additional faults, transmissibilities updates of the existing faulting system and the changes in production trends from closure of wells over the years were incorporated into the model to capture the real scenario.

The deepest layer i.e. the layer 6 was the only layer to have seen a tracer breakthrough. While all the factors like higher heterogeneity, absence of dual phase hydrocarbon and continuous production aided the early arrival of the tracer in this layer, the original simulation match had predicted a comparatively late breakthrough. The comparison is shown in Figure 8.

As it can be seen from the comparative simulation plots and the GOR comparison plot (Figure 9), the old model had predicted a much delayed breakthrough for layer 6 initially. With the additional fine tuning with the aid of tracer response, a comparatively better match could be achieved.

The fine-tuned reservoir model of layer 6 was used to estimate the gas injection split; contrary to the initial assumption of equal distribution of injection across all sectors, the model shows approximately 60% of the gas goes to sector 3 and 4 (Figure 10), which strongly correlates to the total oil offtake from these sectors.
