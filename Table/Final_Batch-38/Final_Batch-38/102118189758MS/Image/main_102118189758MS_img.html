Two sand-rich, thick reservoir intervals of Upper Boscan and Lower Boscan are stratigraphically separated by the Boscan Shale (Figure 1a). These reservoirs are composed of tidal channels and bars deposited in a tidally-influenced fluvial-deltaic environment during the Eocene as part of the Misoa Formation. The reservoir has a south-southwest dip of 2 deg, resulting in the depth varying between ~4,000 ft. in the north to ~9,600 ft. in the south (Figure 1b). This results in variations in pressure, temperature and fluid properties (e.g. oil viscosity) across the field.

These reservoirs are composed of tidal channels and bars deposited in a tidally-influenced fluvial-deltaic environment during the Eocene as part of the Misoa Formation. The reservoir has a south-southwest dip of 2 deg, resulting in the depth varying between ~4,000 ft. in the north to ~9,600 ft. in the south (Figure 1b). This results in variations in pressure, temperature and fluid properties (e.g. oil viscosity) across the field. The average porosity and permeability of the field are 23% and 500 md, respectively. The cross-plot of porosity and permeability are shown in Figure 2.

The large depth variation in the field (4,000 – 9,600 ft.) causes significant change in reservoir temperature and pressure, as shown in Figure 3. Unlike light oil, heavy-oil viscosity is a strong function of temperature (Figure 4a). Additionally, variation in the reservoir pressure is associated with differences in the amount of dissolved gas and the degree of undersaturation and therefore, oil viscosity is also a function of reservoir pressure (Figure 4b).

Drilling started in 1947 with 214 ac/well (1,000 m spacing) using 7-spot hexagonal patterns. In 1956, an infill drilling program was implemented with 71 ac/well (577 m spacing). Although, government regulation required a minimum 600 m spacing for Eocene reservoirs, 577 m was accepted as it was close to the regulation while achieving equidistance between adjacent wells inside the original 1,000 m well spacing (Figure 5a). After 1996, the reservoir was developed only at 577 m spacing with some exceptions due to surface limitations, multi-pad drilling, and deviated wells.

Water injection in the Boscan field was initiated between 1957 and 1963 as water disposal with no fixed configuration (Mendez, et al., 2014). In 1997, four inverted 7-spot patterns were developed as a Water Injection and Pressure Maintenance (WIPM) pilot in the central portion of the field, named Area A (Figure 6). Based on the data analysis of water disposal area, Area A performance and reservoir modeling, the WIPM projects were expanded into Area B (8 patterns in 2004) and Phase 1 (8 patterns in 2007 & 2011). The expansion projects used vertical wells in a pseudo 1-3-1 inverted 7-spot pattern configuration as shown in Figure 5b.

The primary production data analysis quantified the impact of the reservoir depth (oil viscosity and pressure) on initial production (IP) rate, a key well performance indicator. Only early wells, those drilled before the start of infill drilling or waterflooding, were considered for this analysis. The mid-perforation depth of each well was considered its target reservoir depth. Figure 7 displays well IP vs depth, showing a clear trend with higher IPs in deeper reservoirs. An average of initial production rates in deeper areas of the reservoir is more than double that of the shallower area.

Production and injection data from 20 patterns in Area A, Area B, and Phase 1 (~ 150 producers) provided a significant data set to assess the impact of waterflooding (Figure 8). Reservoir pressure maps were also created, combining data from multiple sources to provide additional insights into the field behavior. Pressure measurements across the field included open-hole logging (MDT/XPT), build-up/fall- off tests, and static fluid-level in shut-in wells.

Analysis of production response shows additional recovery of ~3% after the start of waterflood. Figure 9a and Figure 9b show oil recovery versus hydrocarbon pore volume injected (HCPVI) for Area A and individual patterns of Phase 1. After 25+ years of water injection in Area A and 10 years in Phase 1, cumulative water injection is still less than 10% of the hydrocarbon pore volume. The low HCPVI is attributed to the large spacing (577 m) and limited injectivity. Low HCPVI till date also highlight significant recovery potential remaining in the field.

Figure 10 and Figure 11 show the average oil production rate and water cut for four patterns, two in Area A and two in Phase 1, respectively. The plots separate the response of wells located in the first-row and in the second-row from the injector. The key observation is that the second-row producers exhibit similar oil response (peak rates and decline) as the first-row producers after the start of water injection, but significantly lower water cut. It is important to note that due to the adverse mobility, recovery efficiencies are poor, and high water cut is observed even at small HCPVI.

Analysis of field data shows pressure response extending up to the second row producers. This is illustrated in Figure 12 with snapshots of reservoir pressure in Area A in 1996 (before injection), 1999, 2004, and 2007. Pressure after the impact of water injection ranges between 1,850 – 3,000 psi, while nonimpacted area has been depleted to less than 1,300 psi. Localized pressure behavior is observed due to high oil viscosity, and pressure impact beyond two rows of producers is minimal.

A representative symmetry-element sector from the calibrated model (discussed in a subsequent section “Full-field Model Calibration”) was used to evaluate and compare the performance of alternative infill configurations. We use the symmetry element to show the pattern configurations for the current WF configuration (Figure 14a) and two alternative infill configurations: the proposed infills (Figure 14b) and conventional inverted 7-spot (Figure 14c).

The calculation using the representative dynamic model shows that the novel configuration can achieve similar recoveries as the conventional smaller spacing alternatives, but with fewer number of wells and smaller amount of water injected into the reservoir (Figure 15a and Figure 15b). Water cut of the infill producers (between first and second row producers) are smaller than the first-row producers, and therefore pattern level water cut is reduced. Conversely, the poor displacement and sweep efficiencies results into a long tail of production at high water cut (Figure 15c) for conventional infill configuration.

The infills for the water injection area (Area A, Area B, and Phase 1) were designed using the 1-5-1 configuration (in particular, between Area B and Phase 1) with well spacing of 290 m between producers, as described earlier in the “Infill Configuration Selection” section. For the areas where 1-5-1 could not be implemented (e.g., Area A, south of Phase 1, and the boundary patterns), infills were designed between the first and the second row of producers at 290 m spacing. The Infill-project for Area A, Area B and Phase 1 includes 43, 68 and 76 new producers respectively (Figure 16).

The models were matched to the regions (or, patterns) for oil and water production and pressure and not on a well-by-well basis. Oil and water production rates from the history matched models (P10, P50 and P90) against the field data are shown in the Figure 17. These set of calibrated models were subsequently used to evaluate infill potential in the WIPM area of the reservoir.

Phase 1 infill forecast shows incremental oil recovery of 5.8% over base waterflood case after 30% of hydrocarbon pore volume injected into the reservoir (results for P50 case as shown in Figure 18). The final incremental recovery obtained in the full-field model is similar to that achieved using the representative symmetry element model used for selecting the infill configuration. The main difference in results comes in the rate production profile, as the full-field model incorporated more detailed study (drilling schedule, interference with existing wells, regional geologic characteristics, etc.).
