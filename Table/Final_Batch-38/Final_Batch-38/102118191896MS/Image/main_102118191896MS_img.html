One of Australia's most prospective shale gas basins is the Beetaloo Sub-basin in the Northern Territory. The Beetaloo Sub-basin is a Proterozoic rift basin that is in part superimposed upon an older sediment-filled rift basin to the east and north, the McArthur Basin (Falcon, 2011) (Figure 1). Exploration data from the Beetaloo Sub-basin indicate reservoir intervals that contain gas saturated source rocks that are gas mature and laterally continuous over large areas (Close et al., 2016). Two shale targets have been identified within the Sub-basin: the middle Velkerri shale and the lower Kyalla shale. Both formations belong to the Mesoproterozoic Roper Group, making them significantly older than any of the North American shale plays.

Two sets of drill cutting samples from the Amungee NW-1 well (Figure 2) were characterized as part of this study. Set 1 are 10 samples from the lower Kyalla shale, Set 2 are 8 samples from the middle Velkerri B shale. The middle Velkerri B shale samples were recovered from a depth of around 2450 m, while the lower Kyalla shale samples are from a burial depth of around 1300 m. Sample details are in Table 2. The drill cuttings are of different shapes and sizes and were not further sieved to achieve a particular particle size range due to the limited quantity of sample available as to obtain representative measurements (approximately 90 g per sample).

To characterise the gas storage capacity of the shales at in-situ conditions, methane adsorption isotherms were measured using CSIRO Gravimetric Isotherm Rig V3.0 (Figure 3). The rig is specifically designed to measure gas adsorption on porous media, especially shales and coals. It contains four stainless steel pressure vessels, enabling adsorption measurements on up to four samples at a time with pressures up to 30 MPa and temperatures up to 110°C. Each pressure vessel can hold up to 110 cm3 of crushed or powdered sample and sits on a high precision scale, which is able to accurately measure a mass change of 1 mg.

The isotherms measured in the gravimetric rig on middle Velkerri B and lower Kyalla shale samples are shown in Figure 4 and Figure 5 respectively. The agreement between the multiple runs performed for each sample demonstrates that the repeatability of the measurements is very good. The measured adsorption isotherms are well represented by the Langmuir model (Figure 4 and Figure 5).

A comparison between the adsorption behaviour of the middle Velkerri B and the lower Kyalla shale samples highlights that the adsorption capacity of the shallower lower Kyalla shale is significantly less than of the middle Velkerri B shale (Figure 6) - even though the experimental temperature was 40°C lower.

TOC has been found to positively correlate to the methane sorption capacity of shales (Lu et al., 1995; Weniger et al., 2010; Zhang et al., 2012), though for the organic-lean lower Kyalla shale samples there is no correlation between TOC and Langmuir volume (R2 = 0) (Figure 7). Gasaprik et al. (2012) also found no correlation with TOC content for Mesozoic Posidonian, Sleen and Aalburg formation shales and Paleozoic Geverik member shales (TOC: 0.82-10.49%).

Adsorption measurements can be presented using different models or properties (e.g., Gibbs excess sorption, maximum adsorbed amount as a function of the maximum experimental pressure, Langmuir properties). In this study, Langmuir volume is used as a proxy for adsorption capacity to compare the results between different studies. For this, adsorption measurements presented in other studies were converted to Langmuir properties where possible to enable such comparison. The result of this work is presented in Figure 8. It should be noted that the shales are not differentiated with respect to their organic matter origin, but the data sets include marine, lacrustine, continental and transitional shales.

While organic matter is associated with higher adsorption capacities (Merkel et al., 2015), clays and clayrich rocks may also adsorb appreciable volumes of methane (Lu et al., 1995; Ross and Bustin, 2009; Gasparik et al., 2012; Ji et al., 2012). Analysis of the relationship between total clay content and adsorption capacity for the two global shale data sets indicates no trend (Figure 9a, Figure 9c). When Langmuir volume is normalised with respect to TOC there is a positive relationship for the global data set at T = 85-110°C (Figure 9d), but no relationship for the global data set at T = 60-85°C (Figure 9b).

The organic-rich middle Velkerri B shale samples also show no relationship between clay content and adsorption capacity (Figure 10b). The same observations were made by Guo et al. (2017) for Triassic and Jurassic shales from the Kuqa Depression in the Tarim Basin, China (clay: 20–80%; TOC: 0.5–28.5%) and Gasparik et al. (2014) for a data set of Upper Cambrian – Lower Ordovician Alum Shale, Carboniferous (Mississippian- Pennsylvanian) shales, Lower Toarcian Posidonia Shale as well as Barnett, Eagle Ford and Haynesville shales (clay: 0.6-75%, TOC 0.4–14.1%).

To investigate this hypothesis, we filtered the two global shale data sets for samples with a TOC < 2%. Figure 11 indicates that for these samples there is a positive relationship between adsorption capacity and total clay content. This supports Schettler and Parmely's hypothesis and indicates that in low TOC shales clay may be the primary, or at least a significant, control on gas adsorption. This finding also assists in explaining the positive realtionship found for the global data set at T = 85-110°C (Figure 9d): this comparatively small data set contains a large proportion of low TOC samples.

The lower Kyalla shale samples also have considerable quantities of kaolinite (9.8-14.2%), while in the middle Velkerri B samples illite/smectite is present (3.6-6.4%). Figure 12 shows that there is no positive relationship between the different clay minerals and adsorption capacity for the middle Velkerri B samples. However, there is a positive relationship between adsorption capacity and illite/muscovite for the lower Kyalla samples. This may indicate that for the high clay, organic-lean Kyalla shale samples illite/muscovite is the primary control on adsorption.
