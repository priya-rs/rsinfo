The fields of Casabe Alliance are located in the mid-Magdalena River valley in Colombia, South America. Peñas Blancas field is about 10 km from Casabe Sur field and 15 km from Casabe field, as shown in Fig. 1.

Once the pump information was assessed, and considering some other facts as solid production (due to the waterflooding) and an expected low gas production, the PCP pump (shown in Fig. 5) was selected for the Peñas Blancas multiphase pumping station.

PCPs belong to the family of positive displacement pumps. The key components are a rotating rotor within a static stator. As the rotor turns within the stator, it forms cavities between the stator and the eccentrically rotating rotor. These cavities progress continuously in rotating motion along an axial direction (see Fig. 6.

As part of the pump design, the sealing between rotor and stator is not complete; the volumetric losses are controlled by the manufacturer using the right interference fit between rotor and stator. The actual flow rate of a PCP is then influenced by its internal slippage, which is a function of the differential pressure (see Fig. 7) and the interference fit determined by rotor (outside diameter), stator (inside diameter), viscosity of the fluid, turning speed, stator swelling, and medium temperature.

Four different types of elastomers were tested with Peñas Blancas fluid (see Fig. 8), the elastomers were the following polymers: 286, 288 (new technology), 289 (new technology), and the 320; the results demonstrate the ability of elastomers 286 –289 maintain stability over the projection period studied. Additionally, the elastomer 286 had a stable behavior with a minimum weight gain of 1.67% and the elastomer 320 stands out by its low hardness variation of 2.7%.

To control the pump suction pressure, as shown in Fig. 9, a set pressure between 30 and 50 psi is fixed and the speed frequency variator (voltage frequency drive, VFD) will be varied to maintain that pressure.

Chemical injection. Another way to improve the fluid transportation and reduce the high discharge pressure of the multiphase pumps is to add a drag reducing agent (DRA) in the system. A DRA is an “anti-turbulent polymer chemical that, when injected into a pipeline (where the fluid is turbulent) can modify the flow regime by reducing the frictional pressure drop along the pipeline length or serve as a pipeline booster” (Qflo 2014). This solution should be tested in the Peñas Blancas multiphase system, but it must be injected into the discharge pump side, as shown in Fig. 10.
