Grimbeek II field is a multilayer heterogeneous fluvial reservoir producing from Trebol formation which is part of the Comodoro Rivadavia Group (Hechem 1998, Paredes et al. 2007). The project targets three main stratigraphic sequences (Figure 1). The top interval is unconsolidated and had been lost in the majority of the cores. We have been able to run corefloods in the reservoir material from the two bottom intervals (Figure 1) that are more consolidated but still friable (Figure 2).

The reservoir study is one of the key tasks in any EOR project. Historically, most field projects have failed because insufficient understanding of the reservoir. In order to fully understand the reservoir and being able to interpret the results, one should start with a geological-dynamic model, followed by a review of production history, and the execution of some field inter-well tracer tests, transient tests and saturation logging. It is very important to conduct an integrated and detailed reservoir studies through a joint effort of geoscientists/reservoir/production/operations engineers. We constructed two 3D models of a very heterogeneous low net-to-gross fluvial reservoir at two scales (Figure 3 and Figure 4).

At the beginning of the water injection, the field was heavily depleted below bubble point; the pressure was 60% below the initial pressure. We injected water prior to the polymer injection for a period of three years until waterflooding baseline was established at 100 m3/day per injector well. Prior to polymer injection, injectors were evaluated for injection profile (time lapse saturation logging). In the pilot area, there are 10 producers and 4 polymer injector (Figure 7).

Figure 8 shows a permanent installation for water treatment, polymer dispersion and injection. This facility is installed 2 km away of the pilot zone. Notice the water treatment facilities (flotation unit). The polymer processing units and the bulk bag handling systems is shown in Figure 8b.

The results for waterflooding particle swarm optimisation assisted history matching (histograms along the diagonal Figure 9) of the models at different scales revealed the water relative permeability end point was 0.25 to 0.3 for the best reservoir quality facies (Rock Type 5), which is the high permeability unconsolidated channel bar facies in the reservoir simulation models. For the other rock types, the water relative permeability end points were below 0.1.

The waterflooding response (oil rate increasing Figure 11b) seems more related to the water end point relative permeability measured on coreflood in the more consolidated reservoir material <0.1 (Figure 10). After three to four months from the start of polymer injection the injectivity index changed the declining trend indicating that injectivity improved even injecting a fluid with a much more greater viscosity. This means that the effective injection thickness was increasing (profile modification) (Figure 17a, b, c, d).

We calculated water flow velocities across all cell faces for the three simulation models at two reservoir scales (pilot sector model coarse scale, pilot sector model fine scale and full-field model coarse scale). Figure 18a shows the cumulative distribution function of water flow velocities of all cells for each model, the vertical line shows the water velocity of 1 ft/day.

Figure 19 displays the reservoir simulation prediction using polymer resistance factor for the plateau regime and newtonian model for polymer resistance factor in each cell. The simulation underpredict the oil rate however the predictions are accurate, free from numerical instabilities and convergence problems that lead to wrong numerical solutions in many EOR simulations when including shearthinning and shearthickening rheology models and erroneous newtonian viscosity as assuming newtonian viscosity equal to laboratory measurements at 1 ft/day. Note that, just after polymer injection started we increased the production regime in the confined well; the water cut increased immediately and demonstrated the predictivity of the simulation model (Figure 19b). Then, the polymer response was well capture, oil rate raised and water rate declined even when increasing the gross rate.
