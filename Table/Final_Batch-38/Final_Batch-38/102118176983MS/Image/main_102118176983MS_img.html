Figure 1 illustrates the solution of qD for PSS model under constant BHP with different ω and λ values and reD = 50, without skin factor or wellbore storage. The plot clearly demonstrates flow in fracture, transition period, and combined flow. The interporosity model requires more time to deplete the reservoir compared with a homogeneous case (ω = 1). Interporosity mass transfer coefficient λ dominates the flow at later period, while, for early time, tD and ω are in the control. The value of λ will not affect production rate for the homogeneous model.

Figure 2 shows the dimensionless rate vs. dimensionless time for transient slab model. Production from fracture system in early time has the same behavior as the PSS case; however, there is no constant production period at later time. This difference can be used as a quick determination of whether PSS or transient model is applicable.

Figure 3 is the dimensionless rate solution obtained using transient sphere model. The production declines are much slower than the slab model although they have the same parameter values. For different sets of ω and λ, the production rate varies obviously at beginning and then converges together at later period. λ influences the production in early time instead of later period.

Figure 4 shows the effect of reD on dimensionless production rate for transient slab model with a constant ω and λ. It is obvious that with larger reD, more time is needed to deplete reservoir and production rate will be higher. This is consistent with field observation that high SRV yields high flow rate.

Study of well testing has revealed that the characteristic pressure derivative dip in PSS model is not often observed in field data (Figure 5), which means transient interporosity model is more applicable to tight formation than PSS model (Ozkan 2012). For the same reason, this paper will focus on transient model application in production rate-time data.

We used the developed algorithm on Mississippian Lime formation to estimate the formation properties and then use the estimated values in the forward mode to predict its production performance by assuming a constant flowing BHP. Mississippian Lime is a unique major unconventional production formation located in Northern Oklahoma and Southern Kansas (Figure 10). The porosity ranges from 4% - 12% and permeability is typically of the order of 0.01-0.1 md (Grieser and Pinkerton 2013). For each well, about 10 to 15 stages are hydraulic fractured along the horizontal section as shown in Figure 11. The oil from the Mississippian lime has a density of 45°API.
