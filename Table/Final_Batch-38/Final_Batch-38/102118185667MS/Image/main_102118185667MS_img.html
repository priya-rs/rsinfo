Figure 1(a) is a map view of the pilot area for two horizontal steam injection wells ("A" and "B") and 14 observation wells marked as Injector Observation (IO) and Reservoir Observation (RO). Figure 1(b) also shows the schematic of an example well configuration and the installation of the fiber optic capillary tube bundles (FOCTB) clamped to the tubing for steam injection flow profiling. The fiber optic capillary tube has been wrapped helically on the tubing string using mid-joint and over-connection clamps in the horizontal section.

Figure 2 shows a series of workovers performed on the well "A" and "B". From two to five FCDs were used in the five different installation configurations. Three packers were installed in the horizontal section for one of the installations in well "B", Figure 2(i). The FCDs and cup packers were reconfigured to improve steam conformance based upon observed steam flow profiles and injector observation wells.

The intent of this installation was to gain mechanical/operational experience installing fiber optic in horizontal steam injectors before installing a higher-cost, customized, Incoloy 825, fiber optical capillary tube bundle (FOCTB). After installing the fiber and returning well "A" to steam injection, DTS data was successfully acquired during the first 24 hours. The MM fiber was interrogated a week later, and it was unexpected to find the fiber failed at the measured depth of 1847 feet in heel region at the lowest true vertical depth (TVD) of the well path, Figure 3.Figure 3 (c) shows chloride stress corrosion on a piece of capillary tube that was retrieved from 1847 feet in well "A".

The accumulated water was initially fresh at ~400 ppm total dissolved solids (TDS) and ~150 ppm chloride concentration. As the water was continuously boiled off by the high temperature steam inside the tubing, at 380 °F in the heel region, the TDS and chlorides concentration increased to saturation point and eventually deposited as a solid salt scale in the tubing-casing annulus. Figure 4 is a photo of the scale deposited on the outside of the tubing at the lowest TVD location in well "A". It can be clearly seen in Figure 4 that the scale deposition is occurring on the low-side of the wellbore.

A custom FOCTB consisting of a triple wall fiber in metal tube (FIMT) was installed in both Well "A" and Well "B". Figure 2(d) and Figure 2(h) show the schematic view of the well completions in this trial. Per Figure 5 this FOCTB was comprised of an inner 304 stainless steel tube that contained the fibers and hydrogen scavenging gel. The fibers installed in the inner tube were two Multi-Mode (MM), one Single-Mode (SM) and two Fiber Bragg Grating (FBG) arrays of 40 grating each.

Even though the mini-bend failure occurred at an early stage of data acquisition in well "A", both legs of the MM fiber remained functional without excessive signal loss. Good quality DTS and DAS data was acquired in this well for ~3 months after installing the fibers. Figure 6 (a) and (b) show SM and MM fibers power loss change as function of time in well "A".

Well "B" did not experience a mini bend failure. There was also no significant signal loss in well "B" for the first 50 days of steam injection, Figure 6 (c) and (d). However, in well "B" a mechanical failure occurred after 55 days of injection. Figure 7 (a), (b) and (c) show the optical fibers parting in real time over a 22 minute time period. No signal return below 1800 feet is an indication of mechanical failure in the fiber optics system, which was later confirmed when the tubing and FOCTB were retrieved.

The failure point observed in well "B" was similar to the previous 316 stainless steel trial in well "A", with the failure located at the lowest true vertical depth point of the well at the heel. Subsequent recovery of the tubing and FOCTB confirmed that chloride stress corrosion cracking had failed the Incoloy 825 capillary tube and caustic corrosion cracking had failed the L-80 tubing (see Figure 8). Both the L-80 tubing and Incoloy 825 were found to have corrosion related failures at the same location.

Details and evidence of leak free packer installation is provided in the Packer Integrity section of this paper. Figure 9 shows the SM and MM fibers power loss as function of time. As can be seen, after 4 months of steam injection SM fiber starts to degrade. The SM fiber degrades twice as fast as the MM fiber. As Figure 9 shows, after 9 months of steam injection the laser signal loss becomes so great that the DTS data is not reliable any more.

A new flouroelastomer thermal cup packer was installed in the liner top and the packer setting area was cleaned with a scraper and magnet-junk basket run. See Figure 10 for a photo of a new cup packer being penetrated with capillary tubes on the rig floor.

Figure 11 shows a conceptual schematic of two-phase steam flow in the annulus of a horizontal well after steam exits the FCDs. Liquid films and slugs are generated in the bottom of the well due to the vapor and liquid phase segregation. The movement of the liquid or vapor slugs can be detected in the DTS and DAS data by plotting time and frequency domain waterfall plots.

Figure 11 also shows that the fiber optics has been helically wrapped around the tubing. As a result, DTS can show various temperature values corresponding to different locations at the circumference of the tubing. In more detail, Figure 12 shows that depending on the location of the fiber, the tubing temperature, annulus temperature, liner or reservoir temperature can be detected in the DTS data. Although wrapping the fibers around the tubing introduces more complexity to the interpretation of the DTS data, it helps to capture the full cross section of fluid dynamics effects in the wellbore.

One of the methods that has been used to track the liquid and vapor phases in steam injection wells is the heat transfer contrast between the liquid and vapor phases. Figure 13 shows the temperature profiles in well "A" before injection startup and 6 days after installing the fiber in the well. This well had been shut down for the workover. During the shutdown period, quenching water had been pumped to the well for safety reasons. As Figure 13 shows, the quenching water rapidly warms up after installing the fiber and discontinuing pumping the water. The largest amount of temperature rise is seen in the horizontal section where steam injection has occurred.

Using the DTS fluctuation characteristics in the space domain, Figure 14 compares the temperature and temperature gradient in well "A" and well "B". In both wells after 4 hours of data acquisition the steam quality was increased to 95%. As Figure 14 shows, Well "B" has more liquid phase in the lateral than Well "A". In Well "B" the majority of the vapor phase is in the heel zone. Well "A" has more vapor distributed along the lateral. In the toe region of both wells there is more liquid phase as steam loses heat before reaching to the toe. It can also be seen that increasing steam quality at the wellhead of both wells increases the temperature values and the steam vapor phase in the horizontal section.

Figure 15 shows well "A" schematic with the location of flow control devices (FCDs) and the low frequency waterfall plot for DAS data and a snapshot of temperature profile. As can be seen, the first three FCDs show significant amount of continuous acoustic energy at the depth of 2200, 2380, and 2600 feet. The last FCD at 2750 feet, however, has a pattern of high amplitude acoustic energy slugs propagating to the toe of the wellbore. Using the given injection rate and pressure of the well "A", a two-phase flow wellbore model was run.

In addition to the flow regime and heat loss two-phase slugging effects, induced slugs at the wellhead can propagate along the well. In Figure 16, the surface injection rate, quality, and pressure data indicate steam injection surface distribution slugging effect in the selected time period. As the result of slug flow at the surface, the DTS waterfall plot in Figure 17(a) shows slug flow effect in downhole. Steam exiting FCD1 and FCD2 bifurcate and a no-flow zone is generated between FCD1 and FCD2 and steam exiting FCD3 moves toward the toe.

In this horizontal steam injection project, FCD with three nozzle sizes were used to distribute steam. Nozzles of different diameters are installed in flow control device mandrels. Each FCD mandrel has four ports in 90 degree phasing. Figure 18 depicts the wellbore schematics for well "A" and well "B" with four and three FCDs, respectively. In Figure 18, high frequency DAS data was plotted for 60 seconds of data. Using the high frequency band of the DAS data spectrogram, very high amplitude acoustic energy is observed from the three FCDs of well "A", however, the last FCD does not have significant acoustic energy.

In Figure 19, acoustic energy in the vicinity of the first three flow control devices in well "A" are plotted. The power spectral density (PSD) of the DAS data versus frequency is plotted for each channel close to the FCDs. As can be seen, several harmonic frequencies are observed in Figure 19, which is associated with the acoustic characterization of different nozzle sizes. A deconvolution algorithm was applied to the acoustic power of each FCD to find the frequency characterization of each nozzle size. Table 1 shows the harmonic frequencies of three different nozzle sizes that were used in well "A". Similar frequencies were also observed in well "B".

Figure 20 provides another example of using DAS and DTS data for flow diagnostics in a horizontal steam injection well. Figure 20 shows the waterfall plot for 60 second of low frequency band DAS data from well "B" along with the DTS profile. A thermal cup packer has been installed in the liner top to prevent steam flow back to the heel region. It is expected to see a no flow zone above the cup packers if the cup packer provides an adequate sealing.

Figure 21 is in the same well after the top packer has been replaced and the liner cleaned in the packer landing interval. Additional packers have been installed in the horizontal section to provide steam conformance control. The packers were installed at 2227 ft., 2556 ft. and 2665 ft. DAS data for all frequency bands and temperature profile were plotted to observe if the packers were effectively sealing the steam flow.

Figure 22 is the tracking of DTS data during 9 months of injection to monitor the packer integrity. The temperature profiles do not change significantly with time, which indicates packer integrity is being maintained.
