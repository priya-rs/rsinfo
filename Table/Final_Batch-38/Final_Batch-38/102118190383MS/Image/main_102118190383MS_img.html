Different chemical EOR pilot projects were successfully implemented (see Figure 1). Currently, three pilot projects are ongoing: polymer flooding, Thermally Activated Particle (TAP) treatment (Maffeis et al., 2017) and Low salinity waterflooding (Callegaro et al., 2015). Pilots’ outcome will set the foundations for a future EOR full-field application, which foresees a combination of different technologies.

The work herein presented concerns the polymer inter-well pilot project in a selected area of the field. This area was identified for chemical EOR implementation due to good petrophysical properties and geological characterization, moderate recovery factor, low formation temperature, and adeguate well spacing. A depth map of the area is depicted in Figure 2. Extensive reservoir surveillance was established to monitor reservoir performance before and after polymer injection.

In February 2016, after an initial ramp-up phase, continuous polymer injection started at 1000 bbl/day and 1500 ppm of concentration. This corresponds to the target viscosity of 5 cP at Reservoir Conditions (RC), defined to decrease the oil-water mobility ratio below 1 (Braccalenti et al., 2013). Figure 3a reports the Bottomhole Pressure (BHP) and injection rate during the start-up (from 9 to 15 February 2016), while Figure 3b shows the behavior of well parameters during the monitored period.

Its application leads to the plot in Figure 4. We notice that after an initial water baseline, the slope increases during the ramp-up, and eventually stabilizes during polymer injection at 1500 ppm. The plot shows that the injectivity decreases only by a factor of 2 during polymer injection, against a 10-times increase in viscosity.

Before polymer injection, a step-rate injection test with seawater was performed to determine the Fracture Parting Pressure (FPP). Figure 5.a shows the BHP readings during increasing injection rate steps. In Figure 5.b, the stabilized pressures at the end of each individual flow period is plotted versus the injection rate. The FPP is given by the intersection of the two straight lines representing radial and fracture-dominated flow (Singh et al., 1987).

Figure 6 shows the main results of the analysis. Because the perforation radius and the tunnel length are uncertain variables, sensitivities were considered by varying these two parameters. Furthermore, the perforation efficiency, i.e. the area of a perforation open to flow, was considered uncertain too. Assuming a worst-case scenario where the perforation efficiency is 50%, and the shear rate threshold is 60 000 s-1, no evidence of mechanical degradation is detected. Indeed, within common ranges of perforation radius and tunnel length values (perforation radius > 0.31 in and tunnel length > 5.5 in), the estimated equivalent shear rate lies below the assumed upper threshold.

Well test analysis is a fundamental method to estimate reservoir and well properties. It allowed us monitoring the in-situ polymer viscosity, the polymer front advancement, and eventually induced-fractures presence. During pilot start-up, several Fall-Offs (FOs) were recorded; the most relevant ones, highlighted in Figure 7, were selected for pressure transient analysis. Specifically, one FO during water injection and two FOs during polymer injection were considered.

The FO during water injection was used as reference to describe reservoir properties. The selected interpretation model and the match obtained with the observed data is shown in Figure 8. The latter was achieved assuming a homogeneous reservoir; no heterogeneities or geological features were observed within the radius of investigation.

The first FO analyzed for the polymer system was recorded after 2 months of injection. The results obtained show two different radial stabilization: it was assumed that the first Infinite Acting Radial Flow (IARF) was referred to the near-wellbore zone flooded by the polymer, while the outlet zone was still saturated by water. The second FO (recorded after 7 months of polymer injection) shows the slope changing of the derivative after the first stabilization but the second IARF was not reached within the radius of investigation. The transmissibility value (kh/μ) was calculated by matching the FO pressure behavior and the Horner plot. Figure 9 reports the interpretation model and the achieved match with the observed data for the two polymer injection FOs. Table 1 lists the numerical results obtained for the conducted interpretations.

By inspection of both Table 1 and Figure 10, it is noted that the mobility decreased because of the fluid viscosity increased during polymer injection. The effective polymer viscosity acting into the reservoir can be estimated by the transmissibility (kh/μ) ratio between the polymer and the water system. Assuming a water viscosity of 0.5 cP, the resulting in-situ average polymer viscosity is 2.8 cP after 2 months and 5 cP after 7 months. The radial composite system for the polymer interpretation could be considered as the transition from the polymer to the water zone into the reservoir. Transition from polymer to water zone occurs at around 50 m after 2 months and at 230 m after 7 months of injection. Induced-fractures presence during polymer injection is suggested by a negative skin, but the fracture propagation is not evident in the derivative pressure behavior.

A minifrac analysis was performed for fracture diagnostic. Generally, this test is performed before a main fracture stimulation treatment; the intent is to break down the formation to create a short fracture during the injection period, and then observe the closure of the fracture system during the ensuing FO period. The ideal pressure behavior is illustrated in Figure 11 (HIS Marikit Group, 2012).

 

In our context, a main FO period was analyzed. The pressure data were interpreted through specialized plot, such as the SQRT versus time, G-function, and the log-log plot. This allowed us to estimate the closure pressure, i.e. the fluid pressure needed to initiate the re-opening of a fracture, and the breakdown pressure, i.e. the fluid pressure required to initiate a fracture in intact rock. The latter was estimated to be 3000 psi, which is in fair agreement with the FPP evaluation from BHP vs. injection rate plot (3100 psi). From a practical standpoint, the model was used to reproduce the injection history during water and polymer injection. Injection rate and polymer injection concentration are specified, while the BHP is simulated, and compared with down-hole gauge measurements. Figure 14 shows the main simulation results. The model was firstly effectively calibrated during the water injection phase. During polymer injection, it is evident from Figure 14 that if injection in matrix conditions is assumed, the BHP trend is not matched (red curve). In particular, simulated BHP exceeds the downhole measurements (grey dots). In the light of the previous considerations, we decided to explore the possibility to inject in fracturing conditions. A progressive transmissibility improvement within the near-wellbore zone (~1.5 m) was then implemented as a simplified approach for fracture modeling. With this implementation, it is noted that the BHP is finally matched also during the polymer injection phase (black curve).

A conceptual representation of the micro-fracture system during polymer injection is depicted in Figure 15. These are believed to be confined within the near-wellbore zone, as they are not identified as a ½ slope on the pressure derivative plot, but their presence is suggested only by a negative skin.

Model calibration was performed with the data related to the start-up of the polymer injection, with a time frame of 1 month. To further test the quality of the model, a blind test was performed by extending the simulation by 8 months. Figure 16 compares again simulated and observed data; the excellent match reached adds further value to the model.

Figure 17 depicts the in-situ viscosity in the reservoir after 1 (Figure 17.a) and 9 (Figure 17.b) months of polymer injection. The augmented viscosity area extends as the polymer flows deeply inside the reservoir, but the presence of seawater induces dilution that limit the viscosity increase by creating a transition zone of intermediate polymer concentration values. Hence, the viscosity front follows the depth reached by the polymer.

Figure 18 is an enlargement of the near-wellbore zone viscosity distribution during the start-up phase. Within this zone, the viscosity covers a wide range of values. In correspondence of the perforations, the viscosity is low, due to the high shear rate conditions that characterize this area. Moving away from the wellbore, as the polymer enters deeply in the reservoir, it tends to recover its viscosity due to velocity decrease. A transition zone characterized by dilution of polymer concentration due to seawater is then detected. Here the viscosity assumes intermediate values between the seawater and the undisturbed viscosity. The zones where the highest viscosity is found are those under the perforations, identified as stagnant zones where the polymer is stationary.

In the analysed pilot robust reservoir monitoring is ongoing since the pilot start-up date, and preliminary promising EOR effects are arising at pilot producers. Two examples of the production performance analysis are reported in Figure 19 where the remarkable reduction in watercut and the oil rate increase at two monitored producers are evident. Based on these promising results, a potential full field extension is currently under evaluation.
