Located in the southern region of Argentina, a large area known as Patagonia comprises three productive hydrocarbon basins, which include the Neuquina (NQN), Golfo San Jorge (GSJ), and Austral (AUT) basins (Fig. 1). The water sources for human use and consumption in Patagonia are rivers and lakes, which are fed by waters traveling from the mountain-chain melting sector; in the same way, this water supply is obtained from phreatic (groundwater) levels. As a clear example, the first hydrocarbon discovery in Argentina was during the search for water for human consumption in Comodoro Rivadavia (GSJ), in which oil was found by chance at a depth of 540 m in December of 1907.

This section will describe the workflow applied to evaluate a nontraditional water source used as base component in a fracturing fluid (Fig. 2). The main focus of this section is to describe the type of tests performed for gelling systems (linear gel and crosslinked gel).

A hydration test was performed using a rotational viscometer, equipped with a R1B1 rotor-bob couette configuration (Fann-35) (Fig. 3A). Ther interim measurement shear rate of all the tests was 511 1/sec, with the preparation of the linear gels being performed using a constant speed blender-type mixer at 2,000 RPM. The pH of the water samples was adjusted to the desired level using a buffer (weak organic acid or potassium carbonate).

Initial tests were performed with sample 1 to evaluate the performance of a guar-borate fluid that used two crosslinkers: a primary-delayed (XBor-c) and a secondary-instant (XBor-b). Some adjustments to the concentration of these crosslinkers were made to obtain a fluid that met the requirement. Additional tests were performed at two lower temperatures (125°F and 100°F) to improve the breaking profile, thereby changing the crosslinkers and breakers. Information on these tests (A, B, and C) is presented in Table 4 and Fig. 4.

Fig. 8 illustrates the rheology profile for these systems, in which two time intervals were evaluated: an early time (0 to 5 minutes) and an extended time (10 to 60 minutes). In the beginning, Fluids 2 and 3 presented a high viscosity peak, reflected mainly for the action of XBor-b (high-pH instant boron crosslinkers). These fluids have the highest pH of all systems tested. Fluids 1 and 4 presented lower values and in a similar order to the longer time interval. Additionally, these fluids demonstrated a lower pH for all the systems. For the second time interval evaluated, Fluids 1 and 3 showed a lower viscosity (average of 250 cp) than Fluids 2 and 4 for the same gel loading (25 ppt).

The objective for this part of the process was to validate the fluid selected under a variable shear test following the API RP 39 standard. This test was a longer stability test (120 minutes) performed at 140°F, showing a stable viscosity profile without any viscosity degradation/loss for the shear action (Fig. 9).

The target for this stage was to perform a group of break tests at different temperatures to obtain a family of fluid formulations with several breaking times, as shown in Table 10 and Fig. 10. These formulations have been used in most of the treatments for both the conventional treatments (100%crosslinked) and the jobs using the hybrid design in vertical and horizontal wells. It is well established that for low temperatures, an expensive enzyme breaker is often applied. The breaker used for this fluid was an oxidizer, which was combined with a catalyst at 100°F to accelerate the breaking time. This breaker and catalyst did not negatively impact the cost of the fluid system.

Fig. 11 presents a slurry-viscosity graph for Fluid 2, in which three periods of time can be identified. The initial period, from 0 to 60 minutes, is called the elastic transport region. During this time, the fluid showed excellent proppant transport capacity, as the proppant was suspended in the fluid. The settlement process began after this initial period, which was identified over a period of 20 minutes (from the 60- to 80-minute mark) and corresponded to the region of viscous settlement. This breaker action weakened the structure-based fracture fluid. Finally, between 80 and 175 minutes, only one proppant decanted, which shows the lack of system structure by the breaker action at that temperature. These tests demonstrated good proppant transport capacity for the evaluated fluid.

During 2017 and the first four months of 2018, a total of 33 wells (5 horizontal and 28 vertical), with a total of 80 fracture stages, was completed. For these completions, a total of 4.8 million gallons of water were consumed, 4.7 million gallons of which were formation water (Fig. 12). Only six stages consumed fresh water; the remaining 74 stages were performed using formation water from conventional treatments (100% crosslinked). A detailed quality control plan has been executed for each operation to ensure the right properties and performance of the fluid.
