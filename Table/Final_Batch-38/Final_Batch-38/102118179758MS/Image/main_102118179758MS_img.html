The field trial consists of three horizontal injectors with Smart completions to assess long term polymer injectivity and provide information on inflow profile along the horizontal length of the well. The three injectors confine two horizontal producers to the pilot, with two other horizontal producers placed at the boundary of the trial (Figure 1 and Figure 2). The trial has three observation wells to determine horizontal and vertical sweep in the pilot area and, also the polymer losses. The model is built around the pilot and has a total of six producers, three injectors and three observation wells (Figure 1). The term [1]pilot model[1] is used to refer to this model in this paper.

Having defined the variability models, the DoE based Stochastic Uncertainty Management workflow has been carried-out in the SUM tool which is coupled with the in-house simulator. Figure 5 shows our primitive workflow steps that is used initially, which yields 73 runs for the 10 variability models. The SUM tool provides the Root Mean Square Error (RMSE) for each case computed from the observables (pre-defined historical points) and the simulated values.

RMSE is used as the first HM quality indicator or screening criterion. An example is provided in Figure 6 that shows the BSW match of one of the most difficult wells for a set of screened HM realizations output from this DoE iteration. For all the analyses in this study, oil rate, cumulative oil, cumulative water and BSW at different points of time for each well are taken as the observables (including BHP for injectors). The star marks in the plot indicate the observables for the calculation of HM error (RMSE). The band around each observable denotes the tolerance for acceptable HM response.

Sensitivity analyses are done to rank the uncertainty parameters based on their influence on the history match with regards to different observables for each well. This gives an indication of the parameters that would be important, for instance, to match the water breakthrough time in a given well or the cumulative oil in the other well. As an illustration, tornado charts for cumulative oil, BSW and cumulative water at time 01-01-2014 have been shown for one of the wells in Figure 7.

The complete process diagram for the updated iteration of SUM workflow has been shown in the Figure 8. This iteration yields a total of 228 cases against 73 in the previous iteration, out of which 113 cases come alone from the Box-Behnken design and rest additional from the Space Filling design. Pareto charts provided in Figure 9 and Figure 10 show the relative influence of the uncertainty parameters and the cumulative sensitivity curve with regards to all observables (cumulative oil, cumulative water, oil rates, BSW, and BHP) based on all the runs generated from this iteration with the revised inputs and additional workflow steps.

MC (Monte Carlo) and MCMC (Markov Chain Monte Carlo) simulations are important steps in all of our AHM workflow iterations that assist in generating HM cases with low RMSE constituting the set of our best match cases. Since MC and MCMC simulations are done on the proxies generated from the designs, QC of these proxies was done to ensure their validity before it could be used for MC and MCMC simulations (Figure 11).

Based on RMSE, 40 out of 627 HM cases have been screened, which were then manually inspected for match quality of each individual well to yield a final set of 14 realizations with HM errors within acceptable bounds of all HM indicators/criteria. The HM quality of these 14 realizations have been shown been in Figure 12.

Figure 13 summarizes the outcomes of some of the Adjoint runs. Graphs in this figure show the objective function values (HM error) decreasing with iterations in each Adjoint case. The starting points on each curve corresponding to iteration id 0 represent the best matches of the DoE based SUM workflow that were then input to the Adjoint approach. The end points on each curve represent the final history matched realizations from the developed methodology.

Figure 14 shows the updated permeability multiplier maps for one of the representative layers in the model from two different cases of the Adjoint runs – one with two variability models for horizontal and vertical permeability and 10 iterations, and the other with only horizontal permeability as the variability model, but with 15 iterations.

The methodology yields excellent match to the production history of all the wells in the model after the completion of the both stages of AHM with Adjoint method being the final step. Figure 15 to Figure 17 show the HM quality for phase rates, water cut, and phase cumulatives for some of the wells in the pilot area model, obtained from this methodology.
