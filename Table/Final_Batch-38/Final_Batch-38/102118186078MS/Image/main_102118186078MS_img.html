The field was discovered in 1969 with discovery from Well 1-A and followed by subsequent 15+ appraisal wells. The reservoir has been on early production scheme (EPS), followed by a phased development strategy to develop the field (Figure 1).

The static related uncertainty is mainly resulted from the measurement error and lack of information between the wells. The main uncertainty parameters and their respective ranges were identified, selected, and analyzed using Experimental Design to generate a tornado plot which enables the selection of the most influential parameters on the objective function. The main static uncertainties identified from data analysis and interpretations are listed in the Table 1. The applied workflow to capture full spectrum of the static uncertainty is depicted in Figure 2.

There is a large variety of contact observations. Uncertainty in the fluid contacts was established through a cross-disciplinary team effort. The input comprises log-observations, pressure data, and sampling or tested interval. The most likely values of the distributions were estimated using pressure data and oil-water gradients intersection. The shallowest tested oil was expected as low case, and the deepest oil gradient point which is equivalent to the spill point as the optimistic case (Figure 3).

Kriging is used as the gridding algorithm with flexibility of incorporating anisotropy if deemed necessary. The final product will be error envelope map that is zero at well location and gradually increases away from the well. In addition, the error increases towards the flank and towards the area with no well control point (Figure 4).

Saturation height model with utilization of core and logs data was used to provide an accurate water saturation distribution at well scale as well as at reservoir model scale. In order to solve this issue then MICP (mercury injected capillary pressure) - based saturation height functions (modified Leverett J-function) were created for each of the identified RRTs. Sw from log, well production testing data in reservoir interval is used to validate the water saturation of reservoir model. The low case and high cases functions were generated in way that capturing the MICP dataset (Figure 5).

Oil formation volume factor (Bo) uncertainty varies from 1.25 to 1.8 and it changes both laterally and vertically across the field. In addition, it could also be observed that at the same depth the Bo deviation is in the range of +/- 10%. The deviation seen from the dataset was then applied in the uncertainty modelling (Figure 6).

Porosity modeling was generated using effective porosity log and utilized Sequential Gaussian Simulation (SGS) algorithm conditioned to the paleo-depth trend. Multi realizations were conducted by change the seed number and varied the horizontal variogram by +/− 1000 meter and varied the vertical variogram by+/− 3 feet (Figure 7).

The estimated Oil Initially in Place (STOOIP) is ZZZ MMSTB (P50), with P90 and P10 values ranging between 75% and 130% of P50. The resulting volumetric ranges and impact of each uncertainty variables are shown in the Figure 8 below.

A tornado chart (Figure 9) was created to evaluate the impact of each parameter on the Recovery Factor. This was achieved using the 2P Base Case dynamic model, which was simulated with the Phase I and II development scenarios (developed producing and developed non-producing category).

The base case model history-match was checked against the choice of parameters defining the Low and High sensitivity cases. The match data available included: Well by well oil rates, water cuts, GOR, WHP, flowing and static Pressure, MDT profiles, Well saturation profiles derived from open or cased hole logs. The following plots show oil, GOR and %Wcut for the base history matched case, and for the High and Low cases. The parameters defining the Low and High cases are therefore deemed acceptable (Figure 10).
