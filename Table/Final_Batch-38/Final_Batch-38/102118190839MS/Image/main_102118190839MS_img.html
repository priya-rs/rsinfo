The Lebada Field is situated 75 km northeast off the Romanian coastline at Constanta and is comprised of both Lebada East and West accumulations, which are oil and gas prone in the west and oil prone in the east (Fig. 1).

G. Georgiev 2012. Geology and Hydrocarbon Systems in the Western Black Sea . Turkish Journal of Earth Sciences, 21: 723– 754 .

Reservoir geology is a tight mixed carbonate and clastic shelf/slope sequence, with Lebada West being more sand prone and Lebada East more carbonate prone, which correlates with a proximal to distal sediment trend of West to East. The trap (Fig. 2) is mixed stratigraphic and structural that is bound in the north by a regional overthrust fault and stratigraphically pinches out to the west by erosion and facies compaction to the south.

The application of fracturing flow diversion technologies using a degradable diverting agent is a proven cost-effective alternative to mechanical isolation systems. These diverter systems are degradable, self-removing, particulate materials that, when applied in an optimal range of mesh sizing, can create an effective seal (Fig. 3).

The application of a diversion-aided technique has demonstrated improved proppant distribution in openhole completions (Adil et al. 2014). The diverting agent can be pumped in spacers following each proppant cycle, and the particles are sized such that they create a barrier at the mouth of the fracture sufficient to withstand the pressure differentials necessary to divert that fracturing fluid and start a new fracture at another location along the wellbore (Fig. 4).

Team members identified for the project represented broad experience across different discipline specializations, including petrophysics, fracture stimulation engineering, reservoir engineering, and well completions engineering. A workflow of the screening and ranking process was established to obtain best candidates and design for refracture, which required a close collaboration among these different disciplines (Fig. 5).

Well production data were used to define the current well behavior in the field. The “initial production peak” and the “initial decline rate” (Di) relate to initial well behavior and provide evident indication of those wells that have been successfully stimulated under original reservoir conditions (Fig. 6).

Although the initial decline rate has associated uncertainties with determination because of different sections of reservoir being stimulated at different times, it is the main factor in this driver group, since this describes the initial behaviour of the reservoir when the ports were initially opened and therefore assumed to apply for the refracturing. Although SHmax is highly uncertain with conflicting evidence in the Lebada Field, it is a fundamental factor for fracture connectivity in the reservoir. Fig. 7 shows the initial weighting of decision criteria and resulting scores for the relative productivity criteria.

For each well on this project, a production index was computed as a weighted average from multiple defined quality curves, including facies (calculated from mineral contents from the formation), brittleness, and effective porosity. The overall fracture index was then computed as a measure of closure pressure gradient. The product of a good production index and fracture index provided a sweet spot indicator along the wellbore. The overall factor to measure sweet spot potential for refracture treatment was the cumulative refracture sweet spot length (Fig. 8), which was used as one of the decision criteria to understand relative degree of drainage for all wells.

Each decision criteria and driver was given a weight based on the knowledge gathered and biggest impacts on the potential for refracturing (Fig. 7 and Fig. 9). Relative productivity and relative degree of drainage were considered for the initial high grading of wells in a rank. Relative productivity was given a lower weight (40%) than relative degree of drainage (60%) because productivity is related to reservoir properties and production on a field scale whereas degree of drainage contains criteria applicable to refracturing success on a detailed well scale.

A traffic light system was developed for the level of completion risk applied to each well in terms of pilot well selection (Fig. 10). The colour indication is as follows: red=high potential risk, amber=medium potential risk, green=low potential risk. Completion risk is the deciding factor when making a pilot candidate selection; therefore, wells with high potential risk (red) were taken out of the final rank of wells with refracturing potential.

From the resulting scoring and ranking of the top wells, the wells with high potential risk completions (highlighted as red well names) were removed at this stage but were to be reconsidered for future full field refracturing campaigns. The top candidates from this initial rank were carried forward for the third driver ranking—potential for process improvement (Fig. 11).

By understanding the geomechanics and fracture propagation locations, it is possible to select preferred locations for hydrajetting and to plan fracture cycles accordingly (Fig. 12) because it was presumed that this would ultimately contribute to more predicable fracture initiation locations and ultimately an improved production result.

Well #4 (West), located in the southern part of Lebada West Field (Fig. 13), was selected as the pilot well. This well is slanted to a total depth of 3037 m measured depth (MD) [1909 m true vertical depth subsea (TVDSS)] with a final inclination of 83.8° (Fig. 14). Well #4 (West) is an oil well drilled in an Upper Cretaceous formation with an average formation permeability in the range of 0.1 to 2 mD.

This well consisted of a 6-in. open hole with a 4 1/2-in. completion and three open sliding sleeves. The three ports connected the total lateral length of 466 m (Fig. 15 and Table 2). The 7-in. casing shoe is set at 2190 m MD (1790 m TVD), and below this is a 6-in. open hole where the lower completion is installed on the liner hanger at 2138 m MD. A 4 1/2-in., 15.1 ppf N-80 lower completion consists of a toe circulation sub, hydraulic OH packers, and ball activated fracture ports. Packers isolate zones between stages. All three sleeves were left opened during the refracturing campaign.

The equipment on the platform began pumping the repressurization sequence in the days preceding the refracturing operation while the stimulation vessel was being prepared and materials loaded. Seawater together with scale inhibitor, surfactant, biocide, and clay control were continually injected at a rate of 1.0 m3/min into formation. Planned pump shutdowns were performed to record and monitor the ISIP at regular intervals (Fig. 18).

Before beginning short cycles, the PSM fluid was injected with a shut-in to observe the ISIP and leakoff rate. The CBT engineered breakdown sequence was implemented to control fracture growth, support connectivity, and mitigate against excessive pressure losses and restrictions (tortuosity) between the wellbore and formation. Cycles displayed good formation response to the diverting agent because the desired increasing trend of the FGs was achieved (Fig. 19). Fluid loss was reduced to the initial fracture characteristics, and there was a distinct pressure response to the injection of a diverting agent into treatment zones on each of the fracturing cycles.

Pressure diagnostics were performed to track stress magnitude and fluid movement in real time to interpret when injecting into existing or new fractures and the primary fluid exit location along the lateral. Fig. 20 shows the refracturing treatment execution with all four proppant cycles and diversion cycles being completed within a 29-hour period. Each treatment cycle was pumped as per the original design and was displaced to the toe port, except for the last medium stage, which was underdisplaced.

The successful execution operation previously discussed was followed by successful production results (Fig. 21). After a short cleanout period, well production stabilized at levels doubled in comparison to the production before stimulation. Another important indication is an increased flowing wellhead pressure of 40 bar (Fig. 21). Before stimulation, the well was producing by artificial lift; after stimulation, the well is again naturally flowing. Production monitoring continues, and results can be used for potential full field implementation.
