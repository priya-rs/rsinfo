In order to maximize oil production, annular pressure in wells must be minimized to maximize the pressure difference between the reservoir and the well to maximize oil inflow (Sardoff et al 1997). This paper will refer to this produced gas as produced steam. In an attempt to relieve the casing pressure on the individual wellbores, a CVR system has been installed.

The CVR system consists of flowlines off the casing from individual wells which are tied into trunk lines and then feed to vapor recovery traps. The traps separate the liquid (condensate) from the vapor phase. The vapor phase is then sent through cooling fans to allow for separation of any remaining liquids before the gas phase is compressed. Due to well spacing seen in Figure 2, the locations of the individual wells from the compression sites can often exceed a mile in distance.

Historically, spring and summer seasons see decreased CVR system efficiency due to increased ambient air temperature (Figure 3) resulting in less cooling efficiency prior to compression. In order to eliminate operational issues and equipment damage, total gas flow into the CVR system must be decreased. Prior to this project, operations had experienced several process upsets due to the low equipment efficiency.

Once the analytical solution was validated with field measurements and the PSOR report generated daily, the process for trouble shooting CVR pressure increases was changed. An operations culture shift was required. Operators targeted wells by PSOR (Figure 7) instead of relying on tacit knowledge. Operations had to adjust their mindset to proactively shutoff more wells which had a low total oil volume impact rather than a single, high gas producing well that could also be a high oil producing well. The individual well culprit of the upset condition may be left unchanged, but the overall system pressure is managed.

This new process optimizes oil production by targeting low oil producers for CVR shut-in. The analytic solution has successfully prevented CVR maximum pressure shutdowns during high temperature months while, simultaneously optimizing oil production. This field study was successful in lowering the operational temperature by 6 Degrees Fahrenheit (Figure 9) and lowering operational pressure by 0.6 psig (Figure 8).
