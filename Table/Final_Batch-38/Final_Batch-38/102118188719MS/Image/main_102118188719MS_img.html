Fluid contacts were analyzed keeping in mind the possibility of finding different Free Water Level (FWL) across the field. The Free Water Level (FWL) was analyzed based on the OWC from logs and adding capillary pressure displacement pressure (PD) to it. In this approach the minimum and maximum FWL can be found based on the PDmin and PDmax values for each rock type. The two main rock types in the studied reservoir are Rock Type#5 and 6 and hence, PD (in terms of height) associated with these rock types are only used in the FWL analysis. This approach gives the deepest FWL at x876 ft. (Figure 8).

One well located in South-West corner of the field has MDT data which gives the FWL at X782 ft TVDSS. However, to be consistent with the notional contacts it was necessary to shift the pressure points by adding approximately 250 psi to the values. This effectively meant that the pressure readings represented a situation where that area of the field was approximately 250 psi under-pressured from depletion. After the shift to account for depletion, it was found that the pressure points are lined up well with the notional contacts and gradients (Figure 9)

After this exercise, a FWL analysis was carried out in flank wells along the notional FWL and the result shows deeper FWL in SW-W area and gradually shallower FWL towards Eastern Flank of the field proving tilted FWL (Figure 10) as suspected during salinity data analysis.

A 3D porosity model was generated by using a Krigging algorithm without any trend for the hydrocarbon pool area to honour the large amount of well (422 wells) data variation without any imposed trends. Another 3D Krigging model incorporating the seismic trend was generated to represent the geological variation in the flanks. These two 3D porosity models (cubes) were then merged to be used as combined porosity model. For the purpose of investigating alternative models to the combined model workflow, two additional porosity models were generated by SGS simulation (Figure 11).

The numerical simulation model was initialised with the saturation height functions of each RRT, along with the krigged porosity array and the permeability calculated from the porosity-based exponential functions. Checks of the reliability of the saturation model were made against the observed log saturations. Figure 12 below show example saturation matches for wells A, B and C respectively at three different FWL.

The identification of changing fluid contacts across the field required an equilibrium treatment in the simulator to honour these conditions. The selected method used multiple equilibrium regions to capture the fluid contact changes across the reservoir. The first step was to generate an FWL map that honoured the data at each oil rim well and many locations in the gas cap. In particular, the FWL assigned honoured to the possible extent, saturation height function derived Sw to the observed log Sw. Figure 13 below shows the resulting continuous map of FWL across the reservoir.

The variable FWL based saturation modelling resulted in a very good match between the modelled Sw and log derived Sw. The match is shown for three line of section in three different directions across the field (Figure 14). The difference between log Sw and modelled Sw was plotted as histogram showing mean at zero and standard deviation of ±10%, suggesting a reasonable FWL mapping.

A number of history matches achieved quite good high-level matches to observed field performance (Figure 15). However, the individual well matches always experienced a level of variability. The thin oil rim made the history match particularly sensitive to changes in key input parameters, e.g., GOC and FWL.

Throughout the course of the study the understanding of the reservoir physics improved. The 4 primary areas of substantial investigation of the reservoir physics during the history matching included accounting for 1) a tilted FWL, 2) a tilted GOC, 3) more robust saturation modelling and 4) permeability degradation across the FWL boundary and into the aquifer. Figure 16 shows a tornado chart parameter comparison for the history match production for the gas cap and for the oil rim. The charts show that the gas cap gas production history was relatively insensitive to the variations in the history match parameters. However, the oil rim oil production was much more sensitive to the variations in the history match parameters.

The option of “Maintain Pressure” with water injection (option-2) showing around 21% incremental recovery over the field life was selected as the best case redevelopment scenario. In this case, the reservoir pressure is targeted to be maintained at a slightly increased level. The existing oil producers were converted to down dip water injectors. Mid-dip producers and Up-dip injectors are the new proposed wells (Figure 19). An assumption is made that the maximum water injection rate for Up-dip wells is 10 MSTB/D/well and for Down-dip wells is 5 MSTB/D/well. An average of 250 MSTB/D is the required water rate that needs to be injected to maintain the reservoir pressure at the elevated level. The production of 20 MSTB/D in Phase I and Phase II areas can be maintained for 8 years with a total production of 204.0 MMstb at the end of production year 2060.

The option of “Maintain Pressure” with mixed water/N2 injection (option-3) shows around 17.3%incremental recovery over the field life. In this case also, the existing oil producers were converted to down dip nitrogen gas injectors. Mid-dip producers and Up-dip water injectors are the new proposed wells (Figure 20). An assumption is made that the maximum water injection rate for Up-dip wells is 10 MSTB/D/well and the maximum N2 gas injection for Down-dip wells is 10 MMSCF/D/well. An average of 125 MMSCF/D is the required gas rate that needs to be injected to maintain the reservoir pressure at the elevated level.

The key difference between option-2 and option-3 within the Phase I and Phase II redevelopment area is drive mechanism, as option-2 has Down-dip water injectors whereas in option-2 nitrogen gas injectors are placed in Down-dip. In Figure 21, option-2 clearly indicates a longer production plateau for an additional 4 years with an additional cumulative production of 33.5 MMstb. However, water production is more than in option-3 due to more water being injected in both Up-dip and Down-dip wells.

A substantial amount of gas is produced from option-3 as compared to option-2 (Figure 22). This is attributed to breakthrough of nitrogen gas, of which more than 80% of injected nitrogen is reproduced back by oil producers.

For option-2 and option-3, in Phase I area, reservoir pressure is maintained at 2500 psi and 2000 psi respectively (Figure 23). This difference in maintained pressure target is because in option-3, most of the injected nitrogen is reproduced; hence it is less efficient in maintaining target reservoir pressure.
