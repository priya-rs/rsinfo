Where coiled tubing conveyance is concerned, the known disadvantage is the huge, heavy equipment footprint when compared to the smaller footprint of a wireline operation. Conventional coiled tubing equipment has an average weight of 13 Metric Tonnes (MT) but may reach to 20 metric tons (MT) depending on respective job requirements and well conditions. Below in Figure 3 showed the comparison of equipment layout on MDE platform between wireline and CT on the main-deck. As for this equipment footprint and weight, all options are viable with CPOC crane limitation of 13 MT except for E-Coil due to insufficient deck space of CPOC platform to accommodate its additional surface equipment requirement.

Typical rig ups for both coiled tubing and wireline tractor conveyance should be able accommodate the same gun length which is 10 ft section gun in a run. For longer gun length, additional surface equipment is typically necessary, such as an extended (telescopic) jacking frame for coiled tubing and 90ft telescopic mast in wireline tractor conveyance. This option also had been evaluated and resulted maximum of 20 ft section per run able to be deploying in a run. The surface rig up for both CT and wireline are illustrated in the Figure 4.

Owing to the existence of multi-stacked reservoirs and the multiple thin impermeable and water-bearing zones (refer to FIGURE 5: MDE Wells Correlation), perforating the wells in this study requires a relatively accurate depth measurement system. By principle, conventional coiled tubing can provide a measurement of the pipe conveyed into the well but, in a similar fashion to the effect wire stretch has on wireline depth control; factors such as pressure, temperature, pipe deformation, helical buckling etc have quite significant effects on the accuracy of this measurement, varying considerably the actual pipe depth in the well.

Upon deployment (refer to Figure 7 – Deployment Method), the toolstring is divided into two deployment sections. The first deployment toolstring is the gun string with deployment bar connected to the cable head, the second deployment toolstring consists of the remaining toolstring such as correlation and tractor toolstrings. All arming procedures are adhered to prior to connecting the first deployment toolstring or gun assemblies.

On reverse deployment (refer to Figure 8 – Reverse Deployment Method), once the toolstring returns to surface, using the calculated distance from the tool catcher, the deployment bar will be located across the slip and pipe rams. The multistage pressure DBOP rams will be closed and any pressure in top section of PCE will be bled off. Next, an in-flow test will be performed to ensure the sealing integrity of the pipe rams. Then, the lubricator and second deployment toolstring will be disconnected. Following this, the cable head will be re-connected to the deployment bar for reverse deployment of the first deployment toolstring (gun string) by opening the rams, pulling out the tools to tool catcher and closing the X-Mas tree valves.
