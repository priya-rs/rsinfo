The A0201 SAP pilot was a single well pair (A01) pilot in the A02 pad of the Christina Lake Thermal Project (CLTP). A map showing the location of the A0201 well pair relative to the neighboring well pairs and pads is shown in Figure 1. The A0201 well pair is drilled 200 meters south of the A0202 well pair which has been on a butane SAP since 2009. The lateral extent of the A0202 chamber is expected to be quite wide and large. The chambers of the two well pairs are expected to be merged by the end of 2017. The geostatistical profile of A0201 well created at the time of drilling is shown in Figure 2.

The presence of the shale barrier is confirmed by a high gamma ray reading in the producer well log. Figure 7 shows the vertical permeability of the simulation model, with the shale barrier identified with very low porosity and permeability in the simulation model. The exact size, porosity and permeability of this shale barrier are unknown; it was found though that in simulation the production performance of the well pair is greatly affected by the size and permeability of the shale barrier.

Figure 8 shows the history matched oil production rate. An examination of Figure 8 reveals a strikingly accurate match between the A0201 produced oil rates and the oil production rate from the history matched reservoir model. Figure 9 shows the history matched steam injection rate; the steam injection rate from the history matched simulation model is relatively close to the field data though the match is not accurate as the produced oil rate.

Figure 10 and Figure 11 show the results of different parameter sensitivities on the produced oil rates and injected steam rates respectively. Figure 10 shows the produced oil rates are most impacted by the injected solvent viscosity and solubility, as expected. For example, Figure 10 shows that when the solvent viscosity increased from Realization No. 1 to Realization No. 10, the produced oil rate decreased by about 25 m3/day. Figure 10 and Figure 11 also show that both produced oil rates and injected steam rates are sensitive to how much live steam is produced.

Figure 12 and Figure 13 show the history match results of the early-rise rate SAP test. The history match results give the best matched solvent property values and well constraints and should be used as a starting point in future SAP simulations. It was found that the solvent viscosity correlation from lab data gave the best match while the k-value from the optimal history match case was 90% of the value from the lab correlation. The produced live steam rate in the SAP simulations that gave the best match was 85% of the value used in the SAGD simulations.
