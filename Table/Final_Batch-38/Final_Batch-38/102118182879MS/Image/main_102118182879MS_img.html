This paper presents the result of three years of analysis and field testing between the service company and operator. The goal of this project was to address the main issue in southwest Oman, in one of the main clusters, which consists of nine reservoirs in two formations, in seven different fields. Those fields were developed between 1997 and 2002. The drilling process was delayed most of the time because of the technological limitations and high risks presented in the 12 1/4-in. section. Fig. 1 illustrates the catalog of Oman lithostigraphy.

The objective in this area is drilling oil wells in an efficient manner while developing an understanding of how to drill in the Amin formation using the best drilling practices. The Amin formation has wide thickness and is composed of three different layers formed by the Amin Upper, Intra Amin, and Amin Lower, with varying lithology that generally consists of sandstone, conglomerate, shale, and limestone. The 12 1/4-in. section begins at approximately 500 to 600 m and extends to approximately 3800 to 4250 m. Additionally, the compressive rock strength values vary based on depth and thickness. Fig. 2 presents Amin Formation thickness from north to south.

The composition of Amin conglomerate is deposited in a fine grained matrix. Historically, it has been very difficult to drill this type of rock with fixed cutter and roller cone bits. Fig. 3 shows surface samples of the conglomerate presented in the Amin formation.

The bottom part is composed of the Amin, Dhahaban, and Ara Salt formations and was identified as the most challenging interval, where, in the past, eight to eleven roller cone bits were usually necessary to drill the section at poor ROP because of the very hard and abrasive conglomerate in Amin formations where the compressive rock strength reaches 38 KPSI. Table 1 shows the formations tops, thickness, and lithology through the section. Figs. 4 and 5 illustrate the 12 1/4-in. section rock analysis.

The analysis concluded using a fixed cutter with dual row and special layout to help ensure the even load distribution and forces balanced during transit formations drilling to minimize the high cutter wear and breakage, combined with balanced PDC cutter featured with high abrasion, impact, and thermal mechanical integrity resistance. Fig. 6 illustrates the 12 1/4-in. drill bit performance in the area for the offset wells drilled between 2006 and 2011. The gray color represents the runs with roller cone bits and the blue color represents the runs with PDC bits.

One of the main sources affecting the bit performance is the drive system and BHA design. The most efficient way to drill a hard conglomerate is to use a high torque and low speed motor. A 9 5/8-in. motor with 6:7 Lobe–3.5 stage was recommended to use to drill the section, delivering a high torque over 10,000 ft-lb. Fig. 7 (SperryDrill 2009) shows a graph with the operating parameters across the motor.

The success of BHA designs deepens on the ability to model it under real conditions to minimize torque, drag, and bending stress, and predicts critical speeds to reduce harmful drilling vibrations. A force analysis of the BHA was calculated to determinate the forces acting on the bit and the stabilizers using the specific well conditions, weight on bit (WOB), inclination, DLS, formation index, and mud weight.Fig. 8 illustrates the optimized BHA design simulation reducing the bending forces.

Whirl sensitivity analysis was particularly studied to determine the lateral natural frequencies (critical rotary speeds) and mode shapes of the BHA at different values of specific drilling parameters including hole size, WOB, hole inclination, dogleg severity (DLS), formation index, and mud weight. The BHA vibration analysis gave the optimum range of WOB and RPMs to minimize vibration to ensure that adequate energy is transferred to the bit for an optimum bit depth of cut and stability. Fig. 9 illustrates the BHA whirl sensitivity analysis simulation, highlighting in green the optimum run WOB and RPM avoiding critical BHA speeds in blue.

Historically, PDC cutters are laid out on the bit face starting from the bit center following a spiral direction toward the bit gage. Fig. 10 (Chen et al. 2010) depicts an example of a traditional cutting structure layout on bit face and profile for a six-bladed bit Fig. 11 (Chen et al. 2010). At the start of drilling, there can be only one cutter near the nose point engaging with the formation and an initial imbalance force is usually huge (refer to Point A). As the drilling depth increases, peaks of imbalance force occur because only some of the cutters are engaged with the formation.

Multilevel force balance arranges the cutter angularly without changing their radial and axial locations, as shown in Fig. 12 (Chen et al. 2010). In this particular case, for the cutting structure layout designed for Amin, any three consecutive cutters along the bit profile forms a force balance cutter group. The imbalance force generated by these cutters is minimized and the drilling efficiency improves.

The new cutting structure using Multilevel force balance layout reduced significantly the imbalance forces during transient formations compared with the traditional cutting structure as is shown in Fig. 13.

To reduce the high wear produced by drilling the hard rock and abrasive environment in Amin formation, backup cutters were placed in the nose, shoulder and gauge, at a certain depth of cut to be engaged in the bottom part (Amin–Ara Salt formation), as shown in Fig. 14, and not to slow down the ROP in the soft formations in the upper part, and allowing to drill further through the conglomerate.

The PDC cutter technology used is manufactured with revolutionary new press technology. The new cutter series combines improved abrasion resistance and greater thermal stability to withstand the frictional heat generated during the drilling while limiting heat buildup. A new proprietary interface design was used yielding a stronger diamond table that enables the new cutter to withstand abrasion and impact damage, significantly increasing durability drilling Amin. Fig. 15 illustrates the abrasion improvements over the last years.

After a good understanding of the field challenges and the cutting structure necessary, a fixed cutter bit was designed by the new process described using multilevel force balance layout, seven blades, 16 mm cutter size, dual row, and the revolutionary cutter technology described, becoming the benchmark in this application and the undisputed performance leader in the field, drilling not only the Amin conglomerate, but also the previous formations in the upper part from the Natih to the Mahawis Formation. The analysis performed for the field (W-A) helped improve the cutting structure layout in the fixed cutter bits, as shown in Fig. 16. The performance field results are shown in Fig. 17, highlighting in red the runs.

The outstanding performance using fixed cutter bits was reflected in a higher ROP and longer intervals drilled, drilling more than 1000 m at 4m/h, reducing considerably the well drilling time, as shown in Fig. 18. The results clearly indicate that, since the service company implemented in the Amin the new process described, the cutting structure layout, drilling parameters, and BHA were optimized and new cutters developed to increase the abrasion, impact, and thermal mechanical integrity, reducing the number of bits from nine in Well-2 to three bits in Well-18, as shown in Fig. 19.
