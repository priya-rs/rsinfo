Driven by one of the Horizon 2020 supported projects, Drilling in dEep, Super-CRitical AMBient of continental Europe, a suitable cement slurry had to be selected as an integral part of the efforts to drill, evaluate, and complete an ultra-high temperature geothermal well, Venelle-2. The scope of the project meant to drill into the continental-crust with the targeted K-horizon (Fig. 1) represents a transition between old and new, where an aggressive environment with high temperature and pressure from deep fluids is expected.

Cement slurry designs taken under consideration for Phase 1 (Fig. 2) were based on a Portland-silica ready-blend cement with added silica flour used as standard in the respective field, an adjusted Portland- silica blend with a different SiO2 content, and a calcium aluminate phosphate blend. While the first two options are of a common design with known behaviour, the latter has different characteristics when compared to Portland based designs. Calcium aluminate phosphate cement slurries have better resistance to temperature and corrosive environments (such as the expected CO2 content of reservoir fluids) but require special handling during placement operations due to strong adverse effects when contaminated (Dugonjic-Bilic, 2011).

Based on the expected static temperature profile and simulations provided for the 7-in. liner (Fig. 3), cement was cured at 199 °C to simulate placement and then subsequently tested after exposure to 450 °C to test survivability at temperatures similar to what might be experienced when producing from 3000 - 3500 m depths. Due to lack of pressure profile information, 206.8 bar was assumed and used to minimize porosity for curing at 199 °C. At the time of Phase 1, no equipment was available that could maintain pressure similar to what might be present in supercritical conditions. The assumption was made in Phase 1 that survivability could be tested using dry samples. Comparisons would be made after wet curing at 199 °C, drying, and then exposure to 450 °C for 7 days to assess changes in chemical composition, structure, and properties.

To simulate wellbore temperatures, specimens were then placed in a furnace and temperature was increased from ambient to 450°C in 1.5 days (0.21°C/min). After maintaining isothermal conditions at 450°C for 7 days, temperature was decreased back to ambient in also approximately 1.5 days (- 0.21°C/min). (Figs. 4a and 4b)

While the initial evaluation, using a leak-off test (Fig. 11) of the calcium aluminate phosphate cement slurry design, proved to be the right selection the long-term results are outstanding as the exploration well is currently temporarily plugged to evaluate the geothermal reservoir. During milling of set calcium aluminate phosphate cement higher up in the well (due to a forest fire the well site had to be abandoned during a tie-back job), no deviation from the expected was discovered. Set cement behind the casing showed to have the desired mechanical properties in uniform distribution.
