The reservoir consists of three partially overlapping turbidite fans (as shown in Fig. 1).

The reservoir is undersaturated in equilibrium conditions with initial reservoir pressure of 4,533 psia and saturation pressure of 4,500 psia. For the period of history, the Sam Hill oil field (the hypothetical oil field in consideration) consists of five producers and injectors with 2 years of history. The average water cut of the field is ~0% and average initial gas-oil ratio (GOR) of the field is ~230 v/v. The field is typically characterized by low water cut, low GOR, and high oil production rates, as shown in Fig. 2.

Fig. 3. shows the global history match. Overall, the simulated cumulative production for oil and water match that of cumulative history; however, stimulated gas production tends to be slightly higher at the end of year 2002.

In terms of a local match for SH-1, a very small time step is used to accurately match the shut-in period during March 2001. Fig. 4 shows the history match for well SH-1.

For well SH-2, the local grid must be refined to reduce numerical dispersion and match the gas movement more accurately. Porosity around this well seems to be overestimated by the geologist; hence, a pore volume multiplier of 0.7 was used to reduce the pore volume around the well. There could be some gas measurement issue because pressure and liquid production rate matched well locally. Fig. 5 shows the stimulated versus observed data for SH-2.

For SH-3, the aquifer exists to provide reservoir support to the well. Local grid refinement is implemented, and porosity and permeabilty around the well are enhanced to yield better match with the historical observed data. Fig. 6 shows the local match for SH-3.

SH-4 and SH-5 have good local matches after local grid refinement and some minor local multiplier applied to the porosity and permeability around the wells. Figs. 7 and 8 shows the local match for wells SH-4 and SH-5.

Net present value (NPV) may be the most commonly used measure of value that incorporates time value of money. It is the sum of the discounted net cash flow of each period over the life of the project. All eight development scenarios were first evaluated for their net present value for future 20 years, from 2002 to 2022. The top three scenarios with the highest NPV values were selected for economic limit analysis. In this study, vertical wells with injectors, vertical and horizontal wells with injectors, and vertical wells with ESP and injectors have the highest NPV (Fig. 20).

Economic limit analysis enables the economist to predict the time when the project is no longer beneficial. It is useful because it helps the reservoir engineer to plan for future improved recovery or an enhanced recovery scheme. A plot of cumulative before-tax cash flow (BTCF) was plotted against the forecast timeline. The point where the cumulative BTCF starts to decline is the point where the economic limit is reached, and further decision and analysis must be carried out to ensure maximum hydrocarbon recovery is achieved. Fig. 21 shows the general plot of a cumulative BTCF versus forecast timeline plot.

Once the development scenario has been decided, an economic sensitivity analysis on the after taxed cash flow was carried out. In this project, sensitivity variables included operational costs such as injection water, produced water treatment, and other operating expenditures are subject to change of up to 50%. The same applies to production variables such as oil and gas price. The final decision will be based on NPV, recovery factor, economic limit, and after-tax cash-flow sensitivity. Figs. 22–24 show some of the sensitivity analyses for the scenarios.
