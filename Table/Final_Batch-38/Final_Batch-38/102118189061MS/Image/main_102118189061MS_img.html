Net reservoir thickness is generally less than 100 ft. and is expressed as a single loop or multiple loops on seismic. Analysis of reservoir data (logs, cores, and seismic information) indicate that the reservoirs in the field were deposited from lobate and distributive channel deposits to sinuous channel complexes with variable aggradations and/or lateral accretion as illustrated in Figure 1.

The W10 reservoir (Figure 2) is located south of the Dotun structure and overlies the shale above the producing U90 reservoirs. It is a single seismic loop reservoir and mostly below tuning (average net sand in main channel belt ~30ft). The reservoir consists of three NE-SW trending turbidite channel complexes named as Northern-, Central- and Southern- Channel Complexes.

In absence of clear fluid contacts from the well observations, and pressure data from the aquifer, an OWC of 9091 ft. TVDss was used, based on amplitude shut-off analysis on the 2001 seismic, in the original FDP study. Facies distribution was carried out with established Electro-Facies scheme for Dotun reservoirs based on core data and log responses. The facies types included Channel Storey Axis (CSA), Channel Storey Margin (CSM), Inter-Channel Thin Beds (ICTB) along with Mud Rich Thin Beds (MRTB) or shale (Figure 3).

Water Breakthrough happened in the P1 well within six months of bringing the well on stream (Figure 4) and the water cut (WC) increased sharply to 50% within a few weeks. An interference test was conducted between the P1 and W1 wells to check for hydraulic connectivity. However, the pressure pulse from the W1 well did not reach the producer even after 2 days of monitoring, against the expected 1.3 days of transmission lag time based on theoretical calculations. Also, the composition analysis on water samples (Table 1) taken from the P1 well, showed that the produced water properties (salinity) are more consistent with the formation water than with the injected seawater.

As shown in Figure 5, the Iteration 0 model yielded an improved history match specifically in a considerable aquifer influx scenario, but WBT was still roughly 1 year off. Also, it could not adequately replicate the BHP for both the P1 and W1 wells over the history period. This hinted that the model had better reservoir connectivity between the producer-injector pair than actually seen in the wells.

It was inferred that because of the below tuning nature of the seismic and the presence of the aquifer, the reservoir amplitude in the area between the producer well and the aquifer in the East is not representative of the actual net sand distribution. Hence it was decided to carry out manual facies updates in this area based on conceptual models in an iterative manner till reasonable history match is achieved (described in Table 2 and Figure 6). Each realization yielded incrementally better history-matches (in terms of WBT & WC) than previous iteration. Iteration 6 had a better injector performance match, which was obtained by introducing flow barriers (Ibrahimov, 2015) between the producer and injector (Figure 7).

Figure 10 shows the final history matched realization and associated forecast for Iteration 7 post dynamic optimizations.

The study was carried out in Q3'16 based on only 4 months of post WBT production data and forecasted steady WC up to 2025 as pressure support around the producer well was expected to be maintained primarily by the active aquifer and the injector might indeed be redundant. The robustness of the HM exercise has been validated by actual well performance data (post study) from Sep'17 to Jan'17 (Figure 11) as forecasted from simulation. However, more production data and results from upcoming 4D monitor seismic are required for conclusive affirmation of our HM assumptions.
