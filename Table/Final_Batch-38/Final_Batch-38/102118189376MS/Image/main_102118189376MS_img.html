The targeted Layer is located just below the brittle shale formation; it is 10 ft. thickness, limestone, and moderate rock properties (see figure 2).

Group B, targeting layers: Porosity > 6% and <20%; permeability >0.02mD and <100mD, reflecting the variety of matrix-rich and matrix-poor lithofacies, subject to subsequent diagenetic influence (including Dolomitisation) that is typical of inner ramp subtidal-to-intertidal settings (figure 3).

A geological evaluation of the targets location was performed including but not limited to, correlation,(stratigraphics and structural), rock quality prediction (porosity, permeability) and well trajectory design using 3D geological model (see figure 4 and 5). The targeted layer belongs to Group B, which is limestone moderate to poor rock, with net thickness variations from 5 to 9 ft. The plan was to place a 3000 ft.(horizontal) length across target layer A2.

The seismic evaluation doesn’t show any hazards along the well path. The closest fault to TD is located at 300 ft. and the horizontal drain is placed parallel to fault (see figure 6).

At initial stages of changes in well design strategy, a simple conventional dual completion design was run where WLEG for long string was kept above 6″ horizontal drain window depth to use Whipstock as a guide to perform any rigless intervention (stimulation, PLT) as shown in Figure 7.

Furthermore; open hole logs interpretation showed a large wash out observed in the dense formation just above top of targets layers. The feasibility study for window depth, LP and TD was update according to new gathered information. In order to manage Dog Leg Severity (DLS) to a tolerable value for drilling and Geosteering tools, top window (side-track point) was decided to be in the upper part hard dense above the washout zone (see figure2). The side-track was drilled with motor only to pass the high DLS part in dense rock from top window to Landing Point (around 300 ft.) as showed in figure 8.

A big volume of this debris was pushed up inside production tubbing and plugs it, the well ceased to produce. Figure 9 is showing debris of rock recovered and mixture of mud and cement.

The first option was easier and quicker to deploy than the other two options and hence the company decided to run the un-cemented 4 ½″ liner with Swell able packer as quick win solution to avoid any further collapse or blockage as shown in Figure 10.
