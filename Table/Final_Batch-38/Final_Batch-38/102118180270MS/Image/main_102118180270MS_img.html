Unconventional reservoirs have been an overwhelming success for domestic oil production; the Bakken, Eagle Ford, Niobrara and formations in the Permian have dramatically increased production over the last decade. Data has been collected on 7 pilot projects in the Bakken that have been completed over the last ten years. Two of the pilots were performed in the Elm Coulee field on the Montana portion of the Bakken (MBOG, 2015), and five of the pilots were from the North Dakota part of the Bakken (NDIC, 2015). If the rock has more affinity to water than oil, then the rock may suck up the water while expelling the oil. This requires the rock to be water wet. Figure 5 shows the fluid production responses from the water huff-n-puff pilot test. The two injection cycles are shown on the figure, and there does not appear to be any additional oil produced after either cycle; however, an increase in production is observed almost a year after these two cycles.

The earliest type of pilot where the goal was to increase recovery at an offset well occurred in North Dakota and used water as the injectant (Pilot Test #4). Figure 6 shows the general layout of the pilot. There were offsets to the east and west of the injector that were approximately 2300 feet away. There were also wells north and south of the horizontal injector that were about 900 and 1200 feet away from the heel and toe of the injector, respectively.

If anything, the oil rates may have decreased, but this could be due to the high volumes of water choking off some of the oil. Figure 7 shows oil and water production from the east offset well; the west offset well behaved very similarly. The wells to the north and south did not have any increase in oil or water. Oil, water and gas rates for the 4 offset wells along with the production and injection rates for the injector are provided in the Appendix.

The north offset well may be the most intriguing well in the pattern. This well is the closest one to the injection well; the heels of the wells are less than 900 feet apart. Figure 8 has the oil and gas rates from this well. The increase in oil rate during the beginning of 2013 could be explained by a frac-hit from a well to the east of it. The oil increase in mid-2014 could be due to the newly fractured wells to the southwest of the pattern (that are impacting the south and west wells), but those new wells are over 1 mile away, and there are wells between them.

The closest offset well (about 880 feet away) was clearly impacted by the injection well. This offset had a huge increase in water production, but the oil rate did not increase during this time (Figure 9). The breakthrough happened in about one week, and that might have been one reason the injection rates were reduced. In addition, the average tubing pressure on the injection well started to increase, which also might have contributed to the decrease in injection rate.

New tools and interpretation methods are being developed to measure flow responses along horizontal wells. In particular, distributed temperature sensing (DTS) and to a lesser extent, distributed acoustic sensing (DAS) have been successfully used to identify areas of high, low and no flows along wells. Recent papers have shown how it can be effective in evaluating injection processes in low permeability reservoirs with horizontal wells (Foo, et al., 2014; Fitzel, et al., 2015), as it is becoming an established technology (Figure 10).
