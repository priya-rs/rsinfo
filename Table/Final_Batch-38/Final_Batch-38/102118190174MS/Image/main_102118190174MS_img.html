North Slope Alaska is estimated to contain 12 billion barrels of original oil in place of viscous oil in the shallow, late Cretaceous, Schrader Bluff Formation. This resource generally overlies deeper producing fields including two of the biggest fields in North America, Prudhoe Bay and Kuparuk (Figure 1). Alaska North Slope (ANS) viscous oil resource (VO) is one of the largest under-developed accumulations in North America. If even modest recoveries can be achieved (compared to light oil), potential reserves are substantial.

Detailed reviews of ANS VO resources are available (Paskvan 2016, Targac 2005). Of the estimated 12 billion barrels in place, 2.5 billion are being developed with water flooding (Figure 2). Over 150 million barrels oil have been produced to date with estimated ultimate recovery factor (RF) averaging 17% at 0.75 HCPVI at 35 years. This relatively low RF makes Alaska viscous a huge target for improving waterflood and applying enhanced oil recovery methods. Paskvan et. al. (2016) delves into the viscous resource and development history, evolution of well design, key waterflooding challenges, and geomechanical understanding that may meet these challenges.

Play to the engineering and operational strength of BP in managing and improving waterfloods. There exists a continuum of viscosity in VO (Figure 3) ranging from less than 150 cP to more than 2000 cP. Waterflooding is the current development strategy for the lower viscosity barrels, i. e. up to 600 cP, yet WF performance to date has underperformed expectations largely due to soft sand rock failures. The first goal of the RAS is to progress undeveloped high quality viscous oil projects to execute stage. From a technical standpoint, this objective necessitated establishing reliable recovery from waterfloodable viscous oil.

The second RAS objective is to screen VO EOR options over a broad range of viscosities to improve RF by 10%. Alaska fluids range over a continuum of viscosities from light through viscous to heavy. Associated with this are continuums of technical, organizational, scientific and commercial challenges which increase as resource development progresses to heavier, more viscous oils (Figure 4). The optimal extraction technique is not only a function of viscosity, but also original oil in place (OOIP) and rock type. A description of an emerging “Quilt” of different recovery methods applied to different resource tranches is provided in Paskvan 2016.

The third RAS objective is to assess heavy oil (HO) reservoir deliverability and cold extraction techniques through heavy oil pilot operation and artificial lift studies. These topics are beyond the scope of this paper. Heavy oil occurs in the Ugnu Formation at depths from 2000 to 5000 feet (Figures 1 and 5). At-scale development of VO and HO share clear synergies: geography, technologies, facilities, and ownership.

Key challenges to waterflooding Schrader Bluff are well spacing and operational practices that limit HCPVI (Paskvan 2016, Bidinger 1995). Increased flood rate is needed to realize improved oil recovery (IOR), but risks of rock failure through Matrix Bypass Events (MBEs) with increased injection and interwell gradients require improved completions using sand control. Performance of individual areas vary, but SB waterfloods (Figure 8) have an average expected recovery of 17% with 0.75 HCPVI after 35 years of injection (Paskvan 2016). The average waterflood performance of SB is very consistent with global analogs.

Results from a history matched West Sak Type Pattern Model (TPM) (5 spot, 40 acres pattern with vertical wells) provides an estimate of RF by sands (BP and Conoco Phillips internal report). The West Sak area is operated by Conoco Phillips. The West Sak WF area has seen water breakthrough and thus provides more data to match. Figure 9 shows that total waterflood recovery in the 1D area for all the sands is 23% of OOIP at 0.8 HCPVI. The recovery trend is consistent with the original waterflood pilot results and associated pilot forecast.

Figure 10 show recovery by sand. The D sand is well below total recovery with only 14% recovery in 30 years. The B sand appears to be well swept with nearly 35% recovery. B sand with 35% recovery is nearly at 100% water cut by the end 30 years. However, the A and D sands have had very little throughput in that time. This indicates that these zones are poorly swept and would benefit from greater throughput.

According to Beliveau (2009), an analysis of waterflooded reservoirs in Western Canada (Figure11,Figure 12) shows that most viscous oil fields can yield good waterflood recoveries if a few basic tenets are followed including development on reasonably close well spacing when continuity is an issue, appropriate facility design, sufficient throughput of injected water, production to very high watercuts, a VRR close to unity, proper reservoir surveillance, and flexibility in adjusting the waterflood patterns employed are all keys to successful and economic projects.

Slim tube tests were conducted to determine the resistance factors at Schrader Bluff sand thief permeabilities (1 – 1.8 Darcy). A 40’ sandpack is coiled inside an oven with pressure taps every 5’ to measure the pressure at those points (9 pressure tap readings) and differential pressure (dP) over the whole 40’ pack (Figure 14). This slim tube test was conducted for a sandpack with ~2 Darcy permeability using a 5000 ppm BRIGHTWATER™. At the start of the test, TAP is injected into the 40’ sandpack at reservoir temperatures.

Figure 15 shows another slim tube test for a ~1 Darcy sandpack with TAP concentration of 2000 ppm. No increase in pressure was observed even after 100 days indicating the TAP did not pop. For the high permeability 2 Darcy test, slim tube pressure increases were observed though far smaller than expected.

Polymer selection procedure is summarized in Figure 29. For this project, TIORCO polymers including EOR 380, EOR 360 and EOR 80 were screened. As should be expected, for similar concentration, higher molecular weight polymers EOR 380 and EOR 360 give the maximum viscosification compared to lower molecular weight polymer EOR80. However, the molecular weight versus permeability plot (Figure 29) clearly shows that only EOR 80 can effectively propagate through both the high permeability and low permeability sands. Higher molecular weight EOR 380 can effectively propagate through sands with permeabilities greater than 100 mD.

Polymer injection test design is shown in Figure 30. The planned field test could not be conducted due to failure of the vendor’s supplied mixing head (designed to hydrate the dry polymer powder into a uniform aqueous mixture). As mentioned before we could not find a well with only OA/OB completions in a healthy pattern with no MBE history. Well MPI-08 is completed in all the three sands OA, OB and N sands. Our test design included dummying off N sands and pulling out WFRV in both the N sands and the OA sands. Our plan was to set a pressure and rate gauge in the OA sands.

Cell dimensions are 10 feet by 10 feet in x and y directions within the refined area. Figure 31 shows the extent of polymer ring at the end of the test. We wanted to know how far the design polymer has been able to migrate and the polymer adsorption and RRF within this region. This was to ascertain if the design polymer could be injected economically and without a substantial reduction in formation permeability. Figure 31 also shows the expected adsorption, viscosity and RRF for the test design, all important parameters to understand the sensibility of injecting design polymer in average to low permeability Schrader Bluff rock.

Field results thus far indicate additional recovery of 2% to 4% of OOIP above WF recovery from VRWAG. VRWAG also helps lifting well hydraulics, and builds-up reservoir pressure faster than water injection. Figure 33 shows viscosity and oil density reduction for a West Sak oil in a multicomponent laboratory study (Conoco Phillips internal report). Viscosity reduces from an original 60 cP to ~5 cP after 3 contacts of injectant gas (Kuparuk MI-76%C1, 6% C2 and 7+% C3+ hydrocarbons) and West Sak oil (18 API). VRWAG should be considered where a source of VRI is economically available.

Laboratory studies indicate Schrader oil displays classical burning characteristics. Work done at the University of Calgary further showed that SB oil can successfully burn in dry mode, wet mode (co-inject water with air) and post water flood conditions.
