Mode I extension fractures are very common in outcrop maps. Ferrill et al. (2014) outlines the outcrop maps for the Eagle Ford Formation. Fig. 1 shows the west pavement of the mapped outcrop. Bed I, II and III show very well formed fracture networks, which contributes to well production if being hydraulically fractured. Fractures in Bed I is composed of two main fracture sets.

One possible method mimicking how natural fractures are distributed underground is to duplicate the digitized pattern up to the field scale both in the horizontal and vertical directions. Fig. 4 shows a picture of a larger outcrop map, which consists of three patterns along the horizontal direction, and four patterns along the vertical direction. The area of this outcrop map is around 0.5 acre. In total, there are 1584 natural fractures in Fig. 4.

Figs. 11 to 14 show the pressure responses at 0.1 day, 1 day, 42 days, and 1827 days. Fractures play a dominant role in production performance. After 0.1 day’s production (Fig. 12), fractures around the horizontal wellbore are depleted. Because nonuniform fracture apertures are assigned to the fracture network, some fracture gridblocks show the behavior of finite fracture conductivity, and thus much lower pressure values.

After 1 day’s production (Fig. 13), six outcrop map patterns intersected by the horizontal well are depleted significantly, whereas other outcrop map patterns show less pressure decrease. This is because when we duplicated the digitized map to the whole reservoir scale, there might exist some fractures disconnected around the boundaries. One possible solution is to tweak the reservoir boundaries of each pattern a little bit, making sure these fractures cross the boundaries.

Fig. 16 plots cumulative oil production vs. production time. Up to around 7 days (when the well constraint switches from the constant rate to the constant pressure), we observe a linear flow behavior that is cumulative oil increases linearly with production time.

Fig. 17 (a) shows the corresponding plot of production rate vs. time. A common production behaviour from unconventional shale reservoirs is observed, which is a steep decline in the early period followed by a long tail. Fig. 17 (b) shows the zoom-in view around the early period. Note that early flow back of fracturing water was not taken into account in this study. The production rate starts from the highest value (100 STB/ day) and decreases with time.

In this section, the reservoir boundary is increased in order to have more unstimulated area. As seen in Fig. 18 – the gridded model with a large unstimulated reservoir area, there is no fracture in the reservoir region close to four boundaries. For the unstimulated area, we use large gridblocks in order to reduce computational cost.

Fig. 19 and Fig. 20 show pressure graphs at 1 day of production. Due to the existence of large unstimulated regions, matrix-fracture transient flow becomes more dominated after reservoir fluids in fractures are produced. Comparing Fig. 20 with Fig. 13, we observe very similar pressure graphs. Until 1 day, production rate remains 100 STB/days and fractures contribute to the production.

Fig. 25 compares the nonuniform fracture apertures with uniform fracture apertures that are derived from three different averaging approaches. The arithmetic method gives us the best cumulative oil production that is followed by the base case, geometric average, and harmonic average in Fig. 25. Even though we do observe a considerable difference between nonuniform and uniform fracture apertures, the cumulative oil curve of nonuniform fracture apertures is bounded by arithmetic and harmonic averaging approaches.
