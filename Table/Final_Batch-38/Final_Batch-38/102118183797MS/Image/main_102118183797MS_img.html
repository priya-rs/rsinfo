Bahrain Field is an asymmetrical anticline trending in the north-south direction. The field comprises 22 multi-stack carbonate and sandstone reservoirs (Figure 1) with the hydrocarbon content varying from tarry oil in the shallow Aruma to dry gas in the deeper Khuff reservoirs. The geology of the field is complex with a large number of faults occurring in the Wasia group formations, which contain the major oil reservoir of the Bahrain field, the Mauddud reservoir which is the subject of this paper.

Mauddud is a Middle Cretaceous carbonate deposited on a shallow-to-open marine carbonate ramp that is today’s Arabian shelf. Numerous low-relief faults juxtapose Mauddud against overlying Wara and underlying Nahr-Umr, part of the Bahrain zone reservoirs (Figure 1). The average porosity, permeability and thickness are 27%, 25 md and 110’ respectively (Figure 2). The Mauddud reservoir is subdivided into the upper Mauddud A (also called Ba) and lower Mauddud B (also called Bb) intervals.

Crestal gas injection for pressure maintenance has been used in the central area of the Mauddud reservoir since 1938. Open-hole logs have been used to track the expansion of the gas cap and Oil-Cater Contact (OWC) over several decades. The current gas caps in Mauddud A, Mauddud B and OWC are shown in Figure 3. Owing to structural extent and differing Petrophysical characteristics, the gas cap has mainly expanded in the Mauddud A and to a smaller extent in Mauddud B. Since Mauddud A gas cap is now approaching the top of the oil-water transition zone, gas production has increased beyond the capacity of the surface gas handling equipment.

For reservoir analysis, Mauddud has historically been divided into areas based on injection, production and facilities. Recently, the areas have been re-designated (Figure 4) based on gas streamlines generated by a dynamic reservoir model.

Figure 5 shows the current gas handling flow diagram. Injection gas for Mauddud (~500 mmscfd) and other reservoirs is sourced from Khuff and Tatweer compressors. The produced gas (~650 mmscfd) is partly sent to compressors (~330 mmscfpd) for Mauddud reinjection and the balance (~300 mmscfd) along with the BAPCO refinery off-gas goes to BANAGAS, Bahrain National Gas Company plant to recover Propane, Butane and Naphtha.

After 2 years of gas injection, the areas with new injectors showed trends of positive oil CumHI associated with increase in gas CumHI indicating the incremental oil response from increased gas injection. Additionally, water CumHI decreased substantially in those areas. Another observation from this study was the trends of oil and gas cumHI indicated gas streamlines in NW-SE direction which aligns with the understanding of the major faults and fracture networks in the Mauddud reservoir.

A typical procedure of these workovers is to perforate at the Ba/Bb interface and squeeze off all the perforations, and then open a new set of perforations in Bb and Cab reservoirs. The GOR after the workovers often drops by more than an order of magnitude (eg. 70 mscf/stb to 1 mscf/stb) which brings them below the marginal GOR limit and become producible. Shown in Figure 12 is a good example of what can be achieved with an effective gas shut-off workover.

Cement Bond Logs (CBLs) have also been run on these jobs to evaluate the bond before and after the workover. Some of these jobs require multiple cement squeezes in order to get a good bond as seen in Figure 13.

As seen in Figure 14, workovers were done in 2015 (top graph) with very high success rate. The graph is for all the wells which were shut-in and worked over in the year 2015. As can be seen, most of the oil production has been restored to the pre shut-in level with a significant reduction in gas and water production. The 2016 workover program had fewer wells and resulted with only half of them as successful workovers. Reasons of low success rate in 2016 are still under evaluation but are most likely due to the pumping pressure during the acid-wash treatment where it was seen that fracturing pressure was exceeded during stimulation.

The locations of Phases 1 & 2 are illustrated in Figure 15.

Phase 1 started on 29th September 2015 and covered areas A and H to the north (Figure-16) where 5 wells injecting 80 mmscfd of Khuff gas were shut-in. As shown in Figure-17, no incremental decline was observed in the first 2 months of the trial. The area exhibited a drop of around 500 bopd in the 3rd month with an increase in reservoir oil decline rate associated with a decline in gas production and an increase in water cut. The reduction in gas production from the affected area also resulted in opening shut-in wells in other areas.

The Figure 18 shows the bottom hole pressure (BHP) collected at different times in the area. The results indicate higher drop in the east (area A) and the west (area H) compared to the central area. These two pressure sinks identified were further away from the gas injectors and towards the flank of the Mauddud reservoir. As a consequence of this trial, two new gas injector locations have been proposed in these areas.

Completion of the first phase has yielded encouraging results to continue with the VRR program. The second phase started on 18th February 2016 included areas D and E to the south (Figure 19) where 4 injectors were shut-in.

The oil drop in this area was seen within the 2nd month of the trial, as shown in Figure 20. The wells closest to injectors A-0645 and A-0557 showed a drop in gross liquid and gas production soon after the injectors were shut-in. An example of the drop is seen in Figure 21 where the gas and oil decreased and water cut increased, leading to a drop in well head pressure. In this example, the well manifold pressure is also monitored to ensure that no surface facility issue is causing the change in well behavior.
