Over most of the last decade, interest in IOR for unconventionals has been limited to a handful of people in the petroleum industry with specific interest in this topic, but recent reports of success with huff-n-puff gas injection in the Eagle Ford (Thomas, et al., 2016) has prompted a rapidly growing interest over the last couple years. Most of this interest was initiated by a statement in the report that wells could produce 30% - 70% more oil after huff-n-puff gas injection was deployed (Figure 3).

William R. Thomas, Lloyd W. Helms, K. Driggers Timothy, David W. Trice and Gary L. Thomas ; (2016) EOG Resources (EOG) Earnings Call, May 06, 2016.

 

The first pilot was a single isolated well where the nearest offset producers were over 2 miles away. This provided some protection that the injected gas would not migrate to other wells. This pilot started in late 2012 and had three distinct cycles during that year. The oil rates for the well (Figure 5), indicated a positive response to the process. Each cycle increased the production rate to approximately half of the well’s initial rate. Once the oil rate started to drop, another injection cycle was started.

Figure 6 shows the gas oil ratio (GOR) for the Pilot A well using data reported to the TRC. Before injection started, the GOR follows the natural variability around 1000 scf/STB, but once injection began, the GOR drops to a very low value, which is not expected since the fraction of gas should go up, since gas is being injected into the reservoir and produced back from the same well. It is likely reported this way because the operators were assuming that all gas coming back was returned injection gas, but obviously some of this gas was solution gas from the oil being produced.

While the injection and gas production data limitations are troublesome, probably the most important issue is how the production data is allocated and reported. It is reported at the lease level and not at the well level, as it is in most states. Starting in Pilot A, it is clear why this is an issue. When there was only one well in the lease, it is easy to observe how the oil rates were impacted by the huff-n-puff process. Starting in late 2013, however, new wells came online in the same lease and obscured the data for the huff-n-puff injection process (Figure 7).

Figure 8 shows the oil production rates for the two leases, and it is quite easy to see when the injection started for both pilots, as the production rates increase and break from the baseline trend. Much like Pilot A, this indicates a positive outcome from the pilot, and as this is only about half of the wells in the lease, this is even more encouraging.

While the gas rates do not go to zero like in Pilot A, the GOR behavior was unusual for both Pilot B and C (Figure 9). Before the injection started, the GOR varied as expected, but once injection started in 2015, the GOR for both leases remain basically constant, which is not realistic. This is likely to ensure that mineral rights owners and taxes are paid only for the new gas being produced from a well, and does not include the injected gas that is produced back.

Figure 10 shows the cumulative production for the two patterns. The purple dashed line show a projection of what the leases would have done if they had continued to produce on primary production, and the solid green curve shows what the wells actually produced.

This pilot is somewhat different from the other three pilots previously discussed. It is located in La Salle county over 100 miles to the southwest of the other three pilots. Also, all 4 wells in the lease are being injected into at the same time, so it is easier to interpret what is happening. Figure 11 displays the production rates and the cumulative production for this lease. Note that the rate plot is different than previous pilots; since all 4 wells are cyclic injectors, the lease rates were divided by the number of wells to give an average well rate.

Because the data quality is better for this pilot, an attempt is made to predict future production for these wells. Due to the injection/production cycles, it was difficult to fit a decline curve through the data, but a couple different methods were used that ended up giving reasonable results and were similar to each other. A decline curve was fit through the peak production rates, but since the wells are only producing those rates roughly half of the time, the decline curve values were also halved (Figure 12).

Figure 13 shows two different methods to predict the future rates for the huff-n-puff process for Pilot D. The first is simply an extrapolation of the halved peak rate decline curves. The second method tries to mimic the huff-n-puff process by having the well produce at the peak rate for 2 months and then shutting in the wells for two months (to replicate the injection stage). The predicted rates in Figure 13a are only shown the first 4 years, but the models are actually predicted out for 10 years.

Both Pilots E and F were single well pilots. There are four wells in the lease for Pilot E, so again, due to the reporting requirements in Texas, the interpretation becomes difficult (Figure 14). It appears one or maybe two injection periods occur as production rates seem to increase in late 2015 and again in mid 2016; however, since only a quarter of the wells are injecting, the amount of incremental production is hard to discern. There are 61 wells in the lease where Pilot F is located, so there is no possibility to determine the results from this single cyclic injection well pilot.

This project is expected to include 32 wells, so it may be hyperbole to call it a ‘pilot’ as it appears to be the beginning of the large scale field development, but for this paper, it will be called Pilot G. There are 41 wells combined in the three leases, so not all wells will have injection, but most of the wells will be part of the huff-n-puff process. These injection projects have started recently, but they are beginning to show some incremental oil production (Figure 15).
