This project was initiated in the Orinoco Oil Belt, also known as the FAJA. The gathering network studied is located specifically in the Cerro Negro field in the Carabobo area of the Orinoco Oil Belt. This study was developed in Production Block 5 of the Carabobo field (Fig. 2).

A volumetric recombination of two compositions (8°API crude oil plus 30°API diluent) was made to reproduce the composition of the diluent crude (16°API) to be produced and transported. Fig. 3 shows the result of the best match reached; green crosses represent the experimental values and the red line are the results from different sensibilities.

The gathering network scheme is shown in Fig. 4. It consists of four well clusters (A1, A2, A3, and A4) that are flowing to the same merge point (CPF or the outlet boundary pressure).

Fig. 5 shows the granulometric distribution of the production sand used for the model (P 50 = 133 microns).

The amount of sand present in the fluid depends on the production rate, as shown in Fig. 6.

Fig. 7 shows the water dispersion behavior in the flow assurance model that was used.

Fig. 8 shows the minimum fluid velocity that keeps particles in suspension, considering particle sizes of 133 microns and 15% water cut. As can be seen, at high velocities, the sand particles are fully suspended in the liquid phase: the particle volume fraction (blue line) equals the particle volume fraction suspended (black line). The flow rate is high enough to pick up the particles from the pipe bottom and keep them dispersed in the liquid. When the liquid velocity is decreased to 6.15 ft/s, a moving bed is created at the bottom of the pipe.

In Fig. 9, the velocity decreases to 3.7 ft/s, and the stationary bed begins to form. When the velocity reaches 3.7 ft/s, the thickness of the stationary bed height is equal to or greater than the particle diameter used in this model.

A parametric study was performed to study the previous variables with different particles size and percentage of water cut. As can be seen in Fig. 10, with an increase in the size of the particles, the SCV increases.

Fig. 11 shows the critical velocity needed to keep particles in suspension through time. The critical velocity for any particle size will increase with the water cut; however, from with from 5% to 15% water cut, the critical velocity decreases. Besides that, SCV varies with the growth of the field. With field evolution, the water cut will increase; thus, higher critical velocity is required to transport all particles in suspension. After 103 months, water cut decreases (because it reaches 5%). This phenomenon occurs due to the emulsion effects on the fluid viscosity.

The same sensibility study was performed with different grain sizes ranging from 29.9 to 600 microns. The comparison of the DCV for each of the particle sizes shows that increasing the grain size increases this critical velocity (Fig. 12).

Another sensibility study was performed to study the minimum flow rate required to transport different sizes of particles. The minimum flow rate required to transport the particle is reached when the superficial liquid velocity intersects the critical velocity. Above this intersection, sand is entrained. For example, the DCV found was to be 0.2 ft/s for grain size of 133 microns (P50) with water cut of 0%. This was compared against the superficial velocities. The intersection point becomes the cutoff point where sand is transportable. In addition, in Fig. 13, it is possible to verify that liquid flow rate below 12,000 STB/D will result in potential sand deposition in a pipe with inner diameter of 29.438 in. whereas, if the pipe has an inner diameter of 19.124 in., the minimum flow rate is 5,000 STB/D.

Fig. 14 shows the maximum grain size transported in the pipelines that connect the well clusters with the gathering pipeline. In addition, the maximum grain size that can be entrained at the pipe bottom happens when the superficial liquid velocity intersects the critical velocity; above this intersection, sand is entrained. If the water cut is 0%, the maximum size of transported grains is 235, 200, and 100 microns for the A2, A1, and A3 pipeline, respectively; a larger grain size than this diameter will potentially result in sand deposition.

Particle Size of 133.103 Microns. Fig. 19 shows the pipeline condition at 165 days, after the A2 cluster started production. Because of the low production rate, sand particles are deposited; the stationary bed height is shown by the blue line. Note that sand beds are formed in a select portion of the line, as a function of the local liquid velocity.

After 500 days, with the continuous flow rate increment, the superficial velocity overcomes the deposit critical sand deposition, and again the particles start rolling or even remain in suspension, as shown in Fig. 20.

The fluid velocity increases enough to keep particles rolling in the sand bed, but the pressure is affected, as shown in Fig. 21. The pressure begins to fluctuate (red line), showing values that exceed the maximum allowed pressure. This behavior is mainly due to movement of sand in the slugs.

The sand bed was removed out of the A2 pipeline, but deposits of sand will be carried on to the next pipeline with big diameter (superficial velocity below the DCV), as shown in Fig. 22.

The sand settles down and starts to form a stationary sand bed, which creates high pressure losses (as shown in Fig. 23) and, consequently, reduces production. In addition, it can be seen that sand particles are transported in an intermittent way through the pipeline system, and therefore an intermittent pressurization occurs upstream. In this pipeline (T3-4), the sand bed is broken up by the liquid when the fluid velocity reaches the critical velocity, but as the cross-sectional area increases again and the local fluid velocity decreases, the energy is continuously consumed. The active sand particles become less energetic, and they settle at the bottom of the pipe and form a new sand deposit downstream, until they are picked up by another slug.

Because of this process of sand transport in slug flow shown in Fig. 23, the sand received at CPF presents similar behavior, as can be seen in Fig. 24. The total mass of particles received at CPF was 1,600 tons after 500 days of production.

The maximum pressure allowed at well clusters is 200 psig; however, at approximately 313 days, the pressure exceeds this value. Therefore, a remediation operation is needed between days 279 and 313 to avoid a significant increase in the upstream pressure, It is possible to observe in Fig. 25 that the simulations predicted at day 313 a sand volume in the bed of 565 bbl or 210 tons along the entire gathering pipeline (from A2 pipeline connection to CPF). Pipeline pigging is a common method of sand removal, but the pigging program or the frequency is often determined by the pressure available to push sand ahead.

Particle size of 600 Microns. Assuming the system with a grain size of 600 microns, it is possible to verify in Fig. 26 that sand particles start to precipitate along the pipeline (blue line), mainly at the beginning of it.

The sand particles are uniformly distributed along the pipe, as presented in Fig. 27, and then they move upward with the fluid because of the high superficial velocity. There is a distinct moving layer at the top of the bed. In the previous case, the fluid was capable of cleaning the pipe by itself, but the large grains (such as 600 microns) are more difficult to pick up from the pipe bottom.

Under that flow condition, sand deposition increases until the equilibrium condition is reached. Then particles drop out of the fluid to form a stationary bed height and, consequently, decrease the cross-sectional area, but momentarily with the local velocity increment. This phenomenon causes the movement of the particles that were deposited, and a moving bed begins to form at the top of the sand layer that initiates the return to the previous bed thickness, keeping the pressure constant, as shown in Fig. 28.

The last situation is more critical than the previous one because the sand cannot be transported as it was in the previous case during the simulated period. Therefore, pigging operations must be performed in the A2 pipeline. As can be seen in Fig. 29, the pressure reaches 200 psig at approximately 290 days. Thus to avoid this issue, it is necessary to perform a cleaning operation between day 150 and day 290. The sand deposit is 354 bbl (131 tons) along the pipe.
