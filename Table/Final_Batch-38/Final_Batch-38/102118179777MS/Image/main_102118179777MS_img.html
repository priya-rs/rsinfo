Many SPE papers, books and websites 3–9 discuss the physics and application of DTS/DAS. The tool setup and working principles to create DTS/DAS profiles are briefly summarized in Figure 2. The instrument set-up for fiber optics consists of an instrument box which contains the laser source and analyzer. The laser source launches a multiple laser light pulse that lasts for [1]10ns. The laser pulses are directed to the fiber line by means of a directional coupler and the light pulse propagates along the fiber and energizes the glass, lattice structure and molecules/atoms.

The main back-scattered light is at a wavelength of the launched wave and is called the Rayleigh peak which is the strongest back-scattered signal associated to noise and this is used for acoustic data (DAS). The next closest peak is the Brillouin peak and the waves are associated with the lattice vibration but it is very difficult to separate these from main Rayleigh peak. The Raman peak is the weakest peak in the back-scattered light and it is associated to molecular or atomic vibration. It also has a unique temperature dependence which makes it a useful signal for temperature evaluation (DTS). The back- scattered spectrum is shown in Figure 3.

After the fiber installation, a special technique called Optical Time Domain Reflectometry (OTDR) is used to assess the condition of the fiber line. In simple terms this is the measurement of the optical loss in the fiber and it is good practice to run this before, during and after the installation. It used to identify the splicing points, damage and also the end of fiber. DTS analysis was performed for each period during the complete injectivity test as shown in Figure 9. A thermal baseline was first obtained from the pre-injection period (before water and polymers were injected). This step was carried out to identify if there were any events or formation anomalies in temperature that should be taken into consideration before any injection took place.

The baseline showed that there were no major events taking place in the reservoir and no anomalies accept the minor ones associated with the completion. Figure 10 shows the results for this period.

The analyses were then performed for each period and examples are shown in Figures 11 and 12. The analyses were based on selected traces for injection and shut in/warm back (start to medium and end). After Well B has been shut-in, it becomes an observation well measuring temperatures at the top of the formation. If a steam chest is formed it would be represented as steam temperatures (+260°) in Well B which is under static conditions. However, if steam does not rise, or if the steam chest collapses this would be observed by temperatures in Well B not reaching steam temperatures or falling below steam temperatures. Figure 18 represents the temperature response in Well B during its shut-in period (May 2013 onwards). Two key features occur after Well B is shut-in.

As soon as the injection plant was shut-in, significant cooling occurred not only at the toe but throughout the majority of the well. Indeed only the first 100 to 150m of the well remains at steam temperatures, Figure 19. This is the clearest indication, that whilst there is steam injection along the length of the wellbore, the distribution of the injection is uneven with higher amount of steam being diverted to a few higher permeability zones.

Even more dramatic than the temperature collapse in the heel was the cooling recorded in (the shut-in) Well B once all injection stopped. In the first week the entire wellbore cooled to 160°C with the exception of a 50m section that remains at steam temperature. However, in less than a month the temperature in the entire wellbore has collapsed to 160°C. This further highlights the effect of sweep on incremental oil due and the requirement to use flow control in the future, see Figure 20.

The thermal producers, however, were still producing and as such the temperature response was not so drastic, due to the drawdown pulling in any steam left in the reservoir. However, Well C did experience significant cooling in the last half of the well, cooling almost back to reservoir temperature over the course of the two month period, Figure 21.
