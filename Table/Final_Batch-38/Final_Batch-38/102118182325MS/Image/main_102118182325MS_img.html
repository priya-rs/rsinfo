The work of Earlougher (1977) discusses the classical interference analysis approach using the notion of "time lag" in order to determine mean reservoir properties. For an interference pulse test, the time lag is the time taken for the observing well to respond to change in active well behaviour, particularly for the period from when the active well ceases to flow until response in behaviour is observed at the observation well. The concept of time lag is shown as Figure 3, with the phenomena of time lag being the time for an observing well to change from an established trend when active well ceases to produce.

Well flowbacks were simulated from multiple reservoir dynamic models. Each dynamic model had the same flowback profile applied to flowing the active well, being an initial rate of 30 MMScf/d scaling up to 80 MMScf/d at 2% rate every 30 minutes, with a total flow period of 18 hours. The active well was then shut in and pressure pulse propagation to the observation well simulated in the model for a period of 30 days. The gas production profile representing the well unloading for the active well in the simulation model is shown as Figure 4. All other wells in the model are shut-in with down-hole pressure monitored.

The approach for the transient map based numerical modelling method was to extract a representative map from the reservoir interval that both active and observer wells intersected in the Wheatstone reference dynamic model and transfer this map into the transient numerical model. The resulting map shows representative boundaries of net reservoir and variations in the net reservoir thickness. An active well and observer well were then placed per their respective locations in the transient numerical model. The scale for the transient model was set correctly to the distance between the two wells at ~1.6 km. This map based numerical modelling process is shown in Figure 13.

A net thickness constraint was applied to the transient numerical model, adapted from a net thickness map in the reference static model of the main reservoir section between the two wells. This is shown in Figure 14.

The transient map based numerical model was used to match the observed well unloading pulse at the WST-1A observation well from the WST-1C active well flowback profile. The match was determined by varying porosity for amplitude and permeability thickness in the numerical model until a suitable response in the numerical model was aligned to the original signal. The net thickness field was used as a constraint. The transient model match is shown as Figure 15.

The transient numerical model match for the low reservoir pore volume model is shown in Figure 16. This figure shows that a good match of the observed pulse signal is made with the homogeneous reservoir porosity of 15% and reservoir permeability of 1510 md.
