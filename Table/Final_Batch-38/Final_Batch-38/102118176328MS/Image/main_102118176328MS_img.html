The Kerisi field was discovered in 1990 by the Kerisi–1A well and then appraised by 3 further wells. The field covers an area of approximately 18 km2 in Eastern Hub – Block B Offshore PSC Area, South Natuna Sea, Indonesia. It located in Indonesian offshore waters approximately 200 miles NE of Singapore (Fig.1) with approximate water depth of 300 feet.

Kerisi Field is inverted NE – SW four-way dip structures limited by a major reverse fault to the South. It is cut by a series of smaller faults trending SE – NW and NE – SW. A major fault trending SE – NW with displacement up to 500 ft separating the Western and Eastern Kerisi field. Henning et. al15 explained that this major fault is sealing the eastern and western part of the block. Production data later confirmed that there is no interference from wells on each block, thus no transmissibility is present. Hydrocarbon bearing reservoirs in Kerisi field are Upper Gabus (UG), Gabus Zone 3 (Z3), and Lower Gabus (LG) as shown by Figure 2.

Quantitative and qualitative seismic interpretation has been applied in Kerisi. It didn’t show clear channel feature in UG – 1A, UG – 1C, and LG. Meanwhile from seismic amplitude and seismic features extraction in UGM and UG – 1B formation, it showed several channel features which from logs and cuttings were identified as distributary channel and tidal channel as shown by Figure 3.

At the dew pointing system, condensate will be dropped out of the produced gas. The leaner gas is then entering glycol system to dehydrate it from water. Partially stabilized and dewatered oil received from Kerisi CPP will be flowed through a heater to the HP Separator for further processing to meet export Reid Vapor Pressure (RVP) and Basic Sediment and Water (BS&W) specifications. These liquids are pumped to Belanak FPSO, as a sales point, 23 km from KCPP through 16 inch pipeline. Part of the remaining lean gas is diverted for fuel, before compression, for plant and the compressors itself. Compressed gas is separated into three lines, an injection line to KRS-5, gas lift, and a sales line to Belanak FPSO through 16 inch gas pipeline (Fig. 8).

Figure 9 shows a production performance of UGMW with time. Gas injection process can be best evaluated by plotting cumulative voidage against time, as shown at the top of the plot. Cumulative Voidage is the the volume of produced fluid (oil, gas, condensate, and water) minus the volume of gas injected at reservoir condition. When production is higher than injection the slope will be big, indicating high voidage is occurring in the reservoir.

The graphical method plot (Fig. 10) shows a typical classic material balance straight line to estimate oil initially in place (OOIP). The OOIP was estimated within 9% of static models. The reservoir drive plot clearly shows that gas expansion/injection and pore volume expansion are the key production drivers with negligible effects the water influx. At initial condition pore volume expansion is the main force to production, but with time as the fluid is more produced gas injection start to dominate the drive mechanism. Material balance was also used to give an estimate of the current GOC, which is at 3,886 ft or 83 ft below the initial depth.

A water saturation distribution model generated from petrophysics instead of using single water saturation for the whole grid. End point scaling was therefore applied together with the relative permeability model from the Kerisi– 4 core (Figure 11). With high permeability and relatively thin reservoir supported by no sustained water production and no noticeable free water saturation, the capillary pressures were considered to be negligible. Key uncertainties in the updated model were identified as sand extension from seismic, sand-shale ratio and shale elongation from newer channel erosion.

Figure 15 compares the difference of recycling and no–recycling with gas injection of 50 MMSCFD. After injecting constantly at 50 MMSCFD for about 4 years the injection rate will start to drop, which means that the combined Kerisi – Hiu gas source can no longer provide enough volume to be injected. At this time produced gas is solely dedicated to fuel consumption and injection at reduced rates. Therefore, reservoir pressure is starts to decline.

Figure 16 shows produced fluid sales for both scenarios. The upper plot shows gas sales and lower plot shows liquid hydrocarbons, including oil, condensate, and LPG.

As mentioned above, the impact of higher injection rates is to increase reservoir pressure. Higher reservoir pressures enable the economic decision to drill an infill well to increase ultimate recovery, in a structure that has unswept oil (Fig. 17). This well, KRS-12, is being evaluated for drilling on year 9th. This is a horizontal well with horizontal section of 2,830 ft at the depth of 4,016 ftTVDSS, or 19 ft above WOC. The well will be completed with sand exclusion liner to prevent sand production like three other producers.

Figure 18 shows impact of varying the injection rate from 25 to 60 MMSCFD on the production profile of KRS-12. Higher injection rates, (e.g. 60 MMSCFD), result in a higher initial production rate. KRS-12 gas production will add to the to the other gas sources for injection. With fixed gas injection rates, additional offtake from KRS-12 causes the reservoir pressure to drop earlier, which leads to a rapid decrease in production rate. The addition of KRS-12 negatively impacts KRS-6 and KRS-10 production performance. However, there is still an overall incremental production benefit from drilling this well, as shown by Figure 19.
