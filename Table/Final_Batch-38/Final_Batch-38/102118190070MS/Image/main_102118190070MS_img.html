A log of differential stress (Fig. 5A) is extracted along the wellbore from the differential stress map shown in Fig. 1B. The differential stress log is normalized and its distribution along the wellbore is examined. The principle for an engineered completion is to adapt the stage length to the subsurface conditions to treat similar rock throughout a stage, to increase cluster performance. Most companies are currently using the brittleness of the rock along the wellbore to adjust the stage length. Unfortunately, brittleness is often conflated with fracability, and while the brittleness measured at the wellbore plays a major role in the frac initiation, it is the differential stress at the wellbore and beyond that will control the propagation of the hydraulic fracture and its interaction with natural fractures.

Depending on how many standard deviations we are from the average differential stress, the stage length could be reduced to 200 ft for higher differential stress intervals or could be increased to 400ft for lower differential stress as shown in Fig. 5B. The resulting engineered completion is shown in Fig. 6B. With this engineered completion that takes into account the variable differential stress along the wellbore and beyond, the number of stages was increased from 9 to 14 and the approriate treatment is given to each stage. Fig. 7 shows the variation along the wellbore of stage length (blue dots) versus the differential stress (orange curve).

To successfully frac these stages, the amount of sand pumped is adjusted as shown in Fig. 8 where the blue dots represent the stage lengths and the orange curve represents the amount of proppant injected. The shorter stages such as those recommended at the toe where the differential stress is very high will require a smaller amount of proppant as shown in Fig. 8. The result of such engineered completion could be used as input in the 3D frac simulator to evaluate its resulting frac geometry as shown in Fig. 9.

The FMM simulator was applied to the study area and the drainage area around the considered well was estimated across multiple horizontal layers crossed by the inclined wellbore as shown in Fig. 10. The FMM simulator can accurately reproduce the drainage area and the pressure depletion with the advantage of being able to provide such a result in less than one (1) minute of computation time. The resulting pressure depletion can be used as input in the poroelastic geomechanical simulation to study the effect of the new wells drilled next to the producing parent well.
