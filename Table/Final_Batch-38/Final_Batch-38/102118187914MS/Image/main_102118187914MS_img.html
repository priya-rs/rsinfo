Novoportovskoe field was discovered on December 24, 1964, by a gas spouter obtained from exploration well R-50 with a daily production rate of over 1 million cubic meters. Novoportovskoe oil, gas and condensate field is located in the south-east of the peninsula, 250 km north of the town of Nadym, 30km away from the coast of the Gulf of Ob on the Yamal Peninsula and belongs to West Siberian Oil and Gas Province (Fig. 1). Due to logistical, climatic and technological difficulties, a full-scale production drilling commenced only in 2014.

The Jurassic reservoir of Novoportovskoe field is represented by sandstone with interlaid lens-shaped clay bands and limestones. Yu2-6 formation is the most interesting in terms of its development in the Jurassic sequence section. The oil-saturated thickness of the horizon is 40 m (Fig. 2).

The first campaign in multistage hydraulic fracturing started at Novoportovskoe field in 2015. Based on the results, the problem of a breakthrough into the gas horizon as well as a significant water encroachment of the influx was clearly identified. A high gas ratio (more than 1000 m3/t while planned rate is110 m3/t) and water cut of more than 50% (Fig. 3) were received at the majority the wells treated. Such parameters clearly indicate the involvement of gas and water-bearing formations, which leads not only to the reduction of well productivity but also to an additional load onto the field infrastructure (the necessity to utilize gas and water).

Based on the results of the experiments (Fig. 4), the optimum concentration of clay stabilizer was found for the conditions of Novoportovskoe field, which allows avoiding any issues associated with the decrease of the formation porosity and permeability and, consequently, the failure to reach oil production rate after the treatment.

Microseismic monitoring (MSM) was performed at eight stages of hydraulic fracturing in a typical horizontal well. Tonnage of hydraulic fracturing stages varied from 8 to 18 tons in order to compare the fracture geometry achieved with different fracture designs (Fig. 10).

In the fracture profile in Figure 6, there is an obvious variability in the geometry from port to port. At three stages, hydraulic fracture heights greater than in the prepared design were obtained (Fig. 11). It was also noted that at some stages with an equal height in the model and microseismic monitoring, the fracture location against the wellbore was different from the hydraulic fracturing design (Fig. 12).

Based on the MSM results, the fracture propagation along the wellbore (in accordance with the well azimuth) was also proved; such location of the fractures significantly increases the contact area of the fracture with an openhole (Fig. 13).

This study is of a particular importance to implement the hydraulic fracturing project at Novoportovskoe field, as it gives a direct assessment of barriers and obtained fixed fracture geometry. Based on the obtained data for the fracture height and using the data of the bottomhole pressure gage, the hydraulic fracturing model was calibrated (Fig. 14).

Production logging investigations were carried out at a series of wells. Generally, concluding from the study data analysis, the port location in a more shales interval leads to an increased risk of fracture breakthrough into gas/water-bearing intervals. It is also important to note the inevitability of fracture breakthrough into the water-bearing horizon; however, its involvement degree highly depends on the port location (Fig. 15).
