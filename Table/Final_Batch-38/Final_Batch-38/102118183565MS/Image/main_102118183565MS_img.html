Lateral extension of the seven HFU has been confirmed, but lateral heterogeneity within each unit remained a challenge. Besides sand thinning from north to south, porosity showed notable variations in 14 well data, including offset wells (Fig. 6). Therefore, 0D modeling (e.g. MBAL) is not recommended to simulate such cases of reservoir lateral and vertical heterogeneity and multi layered modeling is the only way to present such reservoir conditions.

Reservoir characterization is initialized via well log interpretation and zonation of similar facies across Nezzazat group. Such zonation identified seven reservoir intervals separated by shale barriers that could be traced and correlated through the studied field (Fig. 7). The overall zonation is confirmed by pressure depletion in each zone observed in a well (A6A) drilled 7 years after drilling the first exploratory well (A1)(Fig. 4). In addition, subsequent water injection through well (A6A) showed excellent pressure support to the producing zones in well (A1) (Fig. C2). The identified reservoir units are further investigated using available RFT data through plotting the recorded pressure points of RFT data versus depth (Fig. 4) to identify zonation trends.

Fluid properties and reservoir conditions have been distributed through the static model and dynamic calculations have been accomplished using Nexus 5000.4.10 (Landmark, 2014). The present dynamic model comprises 50*50*94 cell of dimensions (L: 250, W: 250, H: 10 ft.) that simulate flow within seven reservoir units separated by barriers (Fig. 8). Injection in the field adopted dump flooding technique that is simulated with an injection well with constant bottom hole pressure. To avoid numerical model dispersion, local grid refinement is utilized around injector well. The steps applied for building the dynamic model are closely similar to those presented by (Carlson, 2003).

In such circumstance, further permeability enhancement using PLT data are usually needed (Mohamed, 2014). PLT contribution and RFT measurements of reservoir intervals are used as a key parameter of history match. History match is achieved utilizing pore volume multipliers that calculate a dynamic STOIIP capable of providing the measured PLT and RFT data. Numerous pore volume multipliers are attempted until the calculated flow and pressure data match PLT and RFT measurements. The developed fault block communication with adjacent blocks possibility increases the uncertainty in volumetric STOIIP calculations (Lill-Tove W. S., 2002). Relaying only on volumetric STOIIP in complicated structural basins, especially with poor seismic data, could lead to a major uncertainty in structure model, whether for field lateral extension or other blocks juxtaposition (J.S. van Wyk, et. el, 2005). This is the typical conditions prevailing the Gulf of Suez concessions where the thick salt section of South Gharib formation ± 1000 ft. (Fig. D1, Appendix" D") overlying potential hydrocarbon reservoirs greatly deteriorate the seismic data quality.

In some areas where thick salt sections exist, e.g. Gulf of Suez, seismic imaging is so difficult due to strong acoustic impedance and velocity contrasts at the sediment-evaporites interface (Fig. D2, Appendix "D"). In such situations the structure mapping quality is highly governed by the available wells data and in early exploration phases, it is impossible to reliably predict the structure extension and even fault identification is not an easy target. Thus, volumetric STOIIP calculations based on maps derived from poor seismic quality with low well density have enormous uncertainty and cannot be adopted for field development plans.
