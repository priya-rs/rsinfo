ES-MDA is a method based on KF and it is derived directly from ES. To generate better data matches, ES-MDA runs ES methodology multiple times, until reach the defined number of iterations. In order to do so, a weight factor (αi) is added to each iteration. To have a better visualization of the method, Fig. 1 shows a flowchart of the ES-MDA. Following is the explanation of each step.

To define the influence area of the wells in LOC1 approach, I used the influence region of each injector-producer pair according to Maschio and Schiozer (2016) (Fig. 3a), where the different collors represent each individual area. Thus, from this definition, it was possible to create ellipses defining the region of influence of the wells as Fig. 3a shows for the well PROD009.

I applied this methodology to a benchmark case with a known response, called UNISIM-I-H, which is a synthetic oil field based on data from Namorado field at Campos Basin in Brazil (Fig. 4).

Fig. 5 depicts the evolution of krwiro’s pdf and it helps to have a better visualization of the models’ variability. In Fig. 5a, krwiro values are distributed according to the specification shown in Table 1 and the standard deviation was 0.073. Fig. 5b, c and d show a significant reduction of this uncertainty with a standard deviation of 0.006, 0.012 and 0.011, respectively. This great reduction was observed for all scalars uncertainties and it can be explained because these attributes have a single value for the entire model. Thus, localization technique was not used to update them.

Another important question is related to spurious correlation. For the STD case, there was no specific region of influence. Thus, model parameters were updated considering all data available and it caused correlation of non-physical related parameters (spurious correlation). This can be seen through a comparison of the natural logarithm of kx mean images: in regions where there were no or few data to assimilate, the pattern shown in Figs. 6e and g tended to follow the pattern observed in the prior ensemble (Fig 6a, as it is possible to see in the east and northwest part of the model.

Finally, following the application of ES-MDA, it was necessary to conduct the production forecast until the end of the field productive life. Fig 7 shows that all cases (Prior, STD, LOC1 and LOC2) encompassed the reference response for oil cumulative production (Np) and water cumulative production (Wp). The only exception was Np for STD case.

With application of the filter (Fig. 8), it was possible to notice that for STD case, the ensemble converged to the real answer for Wp, but this did not happen for Np, as noticed before. When analyzing LOC1, because only 6.2% of the models were filtered, this small amount contributed to reduce variability of the production curve, which caused the divergence of the answer. Lastly, the curve pattern was the same when compared with Fig. 7 for LOC2 and the filter discarded some models that were far from the reference response, showing that the models converged for the real answer for Np and Wp.
