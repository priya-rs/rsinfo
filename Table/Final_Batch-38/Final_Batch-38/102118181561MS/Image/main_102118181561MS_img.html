Backscattered SEM images (Fig. 6) shown below capture the typical microstructure observed in the Rus, Asmari and Dammam Formations. To a large extent, these samples are crack free and are dominated by equant pores. This is consistent with the linear pressure behavior observed in the majority of porosity and permeability measurements. Composition varies, i.e. the Asmari samples have dolomitic crystallization within existing pores which affects density, porosity and permeability.

Helium porosities were measured on 72 samples as a function of pressure (see Fig. 7); the porosity distributions are plotted by Formation in Fig. 7. Porosities were also derived from NMR and MICP measurements. We used the porosity pressure dependence to measure pore volume compressibility which showed high compressibility at low porosity, probably due to cracks, but almost a constant low value above 3% porosity.

NMR measured porosities on pressure saturated samples and helium porosities are compared in Fig. 8. The results are shown in Fig. 8 along with data published by Straley et al. (1994) for carbonates. The agreement between the two is better at lower porosities, with the NMR porosity being less than the helium values at higher porosities. The best fitting line shows a high correlation coefficient. The Straley et al. (1994) data fall systematically above the 1:1 line suggesting a bulk shift in mineralogy.

In Fig. 9, we compare the porosities dervived from the MICP measurements, ϕHg, made at 60,000 psi injection pressures (red circles) with ϕHe and ϕNMR. While the ϕHg values fall along the trend line established for the ϕHe and ϕNMR, the observed scatter is considerably larger and the corresponding correlation coefficient is lower by ~12%.

For straight capillary tubes, the Kozeny equation offers a simple correlation between permeability and porosity. We measured porosities and permeabilities as a function of confining pressure and report the results at 1500 psi for the Klinkenberg permeabilities in Fig. 10. In general, there is a strong exponential dependence of permeability on porosity with the Asmari and Rus Formations fitting the trend better than the Dammam Formation.

The SDR equation requires log T2 geometric mean and porosity. The measured geometric means are presented in Fig. 13. The Coates-Timur requires either an assumption of a T2 cutoff—typically 100 ms for carbonate—or measured values; we used both. We performed a series of desaturation experiments by centrifuging the samples to an equivalent pressure of 100 psi for 12 hours to establish meaningful T2 cutoff values.

 

The calibrated SDR permeability estimate appears superior to the generic SDR using standard constant values (see Fig. 15) and substantially better than either the calibrated or uncalibrated Coates-Timur estimate for these carbonate samples. Logan et al., (1998) made a similar observation in their carbonate study. They then forced the Coates-Timur permeabilities to match the SDR values to estimate T2 cutoffs from field data.
