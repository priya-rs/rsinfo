Field AB is located in offshore Sarawak and structurally is bounded by two growth faults; one at the north and another one at the south (Figure 1). The first oil production started in 1974. Reservoir sand quality is considered good with average porosity of 30% and average permeability of 300mD. Sand is deposited in shoreface environment and homogeneous throughout the field. Gas cap expansion and strong water drive are two major reservoir producing mechanisms.

Proper job planning and early logistics arrangements were practiced to alleviate the causes of failure of earlier practices. The scope of this paper is to share the best practices in two categories: coring operation and also core analyses in the determination of in-situ water saturation and formation water salinity as shown in Figure 2

Selection of coring bit must minimize mud invasion into the core. Face-discharged core bit was utilized to direct the mud flow away from the core and minimize invasion. Figure 3 shows the type of coring bit used for the job. Due to weakly consolidated nature of the formation, full closure system (FCC) with clamp catcher type was utilized for this job.

Once the core was at surface the laydown cradle was brought to the rig floor via crane. Core was tied at the top, middle and bottom parts to the cradle to minimize flexing or bending during laying down process. Once laid down the actual core recovery was measured. The core was marked every 3ft depth and 6 inch from the top of each 3ft section to be cut. The red and black lines will determine the orientation of the core (top and bottom, see Figure 4).

For Well A the whole core was preserved using liquid nitrogen until temperature reached -20degC. Decision on whether to freeze or not depends on the nature of the rock, consolidated or unconsolidated which is given in the decision tree in Figure 5. The core was placed in stainless steel trough with a closed lid. Liquid nitrogen was then injected through the open compartment of the lid (Figure 6). The critical thing is to ensure no direct contact between the core and liquid nitrogen is allowed during the preservation process.

The core was cut every 3 ft in length from the top. Another 6 in section was cut out of this 3 ft core length for on-site plugging. Cutting was done using both air saw and band saw. The latter provided a smooth cut with less vibration to the core and generated less dust. Figure 7 shows the picture of air saw and band saw.

Plugging was done on non-damaged and least invaded part of the 6 in core (middle section). Plugs were drilled using low pressure air, 10-12 psi, to avoid fluid displacement within the core plug. Figure 8 shows the method used for on-site plugging. The trimmed plugs were labelled properly and then wrapped with 6 layers of saran wrap, 3 layers of aluminum foil and wax-dipped before transporting immediately to the lab in a cool box (Figure 9).

Total of 57 core plugs were drilled, trimmed and preserved in wax at the well-site for immediate transportation to the lab. Three full 6 in core sections were transported together with the drilled plugs. These sections will be plugged later once arrived in the lab. The plugs and cores took 2 days to arrive in the lab from the field location. In the lab, three main analyses were conducted, namely, Sw Dean Stark, Rw micro cell and pore water salinity. Workflow for Sw Dean Stark analysis is given in Figure 10.

A good quality of core was obtained from the operation. CT scan image showed that the core was intact with minimal damage or broken parts. Formation bedding, layers and sedimentological features are fully described and characterized as shown in Figure 11 below.

Rw micro cell, Sw Dean Stark and tracer study results have been tabulated respectively. Rw plot, log evaluation and validation with core results are given in Figure 12.

Rw micro cell and Sw Dean Stark results for the cores plugged at the well-site show a very minimal mud filtrate invasion (less than 5% invasion); while for the cores plugged later in the lab show higher mud filtrate invasion which ranges from 16% to 24%. Figure 13 shows the comparison of mud invasion level between on-site plugs and plugs drilled later in the lab for Sw and Rw measurements
