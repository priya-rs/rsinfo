The concept of surface network is used in this paper to handle the complexity of the well modeling in reservoir simulation. A graph is used to represent the subsurface network, Fig. 1. Laterals of a given well are divided into nodes and links. Each link represents a pipe and or mechanical device such as the ICD, ICV, electric submersible pumps and/or any other mechanical devices. A node represents a connection of two or more links.

A subsurface network is coupled with the grid solution (Dogru et al. 2009) using three methods, namely: explicit, sequential and implicit, Fig. 2. The explicit coupling predicts perf rate at first outer Newton and it does not update after that. Such coupling is easy to implement, but it might be slow and may cause oscillation; however, it may work very well for a large grid block system and low well rate.

As an example, Fig. 3 shows a schematic diagram for two wells distributed among four processors. Processors 1 and 4 will do the computation for the subsurface network for Wells W1 and W2, respectively. At a given Newton iteration, processors 3 and 4 send the boundary grid data to processor 1. Processor 2 sends the boundary grid data to processor 4. Non-blocking inter-processor communication is also implemented to increase the parallel efficiency of the algorithm.

There are three cases studied in this paper. The first one is a sector model for one of the Middle East giant field, Fig. 4. This model is a gas-water system with one vertical well in the middle (zoomed in Fig. 4). The total number of grid cells is about 1 million cells. The total number of simulation years is 30 years. Table 1 shows the statistic of simulation time for both sequential and fully implicit. The number of MPI processors is 10. As is expected, one can notice that the fully implicit coupling took reduced number of iterations and a shorter simulation time.

The second studied case, Fig. 7, is for full field simulation for one of the large fields in the Middle East. Most of the wells in this field are drilled as multilaterals. Total wells in this field number about 500 wells, while 250 of the wells are modeled with a subsurface network. This case is modeled with 34 million active cells and nine compositional components. The field has a total of almost 17 years of history and 15 years of prediction. The total number of MPI processors used to run this model is 3,000, which are more than enough to have one subsurface network per processor.

The SuperLU could not be used to solve for due to technical difficulties. Instead, the Gaussian LU is employed. Figure 8 shows a comparison of the gas-oil ratio (GOR) between history and the simulation for one of the wells modeled using a subsurface network. The result is comparable between them. Normalized oil rate and pressure along one of the laterals for a given well is also shown in Fig. 9. The lateral is horizontal and its length about 3,030 km (10,000 ft). One can notice that the pressure drop is small at the toe due to the low flow rate. On the other hand, at the heal, the pressure drop is high due to the high flow rate. That behavior is expected for a long horizontal lateral (Penmatcha and Aziz 1998).

The last test case is using more complex subsurface networks to model the next generation for the multilateral completion system, Fig. 10. Bouldin et al. (2014) refers to this as an ERC. Abuahmad et al.(2016) and Bouldin et al. (2014) discuss the system in details. The objective in this paper is to do simulation for an ERC system and predict its sweep efficiency. Just briefly, each lateral in a given well is divided into three compartments and each compartment is controlled with infinitely positioned electric flow control valves (FCVs).

When the GOR of a given well is acceding high value, FCVs with the highest GOR will be shut-in. Figure 11 shows the subsurface network's graph for one of the laterals for one of the chosen ERC wells. Note that the flow in compartments between packers is annulus flow, which is connected to the tubing by FCVs.

Two wells of test case 2 were chosen to be an ERC. Figure 12 compares the sweep efficiency between open hole and an ERC. One can notice that sweep efficiency of the well is improved by a large factor especially at the toe. The comparison in the cumulative oil for one of the wells is given in Fig. 13, which shows the next generation completion (ERC) increases of the well recovery factor by more than 2.
