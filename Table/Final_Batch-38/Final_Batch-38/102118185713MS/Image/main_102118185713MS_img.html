Solve the pressure equation for time ti+1 resulting in spatial pressure gradients assumed to be constant over Δti = ti + 1 - ti. Apply Darcy's Law to determine the velocity field for the underlying (static) grid and trace streamlines (Figure 1, left). Streamlines are assumed to be constant over Δti. Note that this is identical to the "Implicit Pressure" step in the traditional IMPES simulation approach.

The model is initialized at a critical water saturation, Swc=0, which along with a non-uniform porosity distribution, gives the initial oil in place map shown in Figure 2 left. Areal permeability is also assumed to vary spatially. Next we run the model under a traditional streamline-based IMPES flow simulation and generate synthetic production and injection profiles for each well, as summarized for the field in Figure 2 bottom. Also included for reference is the flux-pattern map (FPMap) showing the well locations and producer-centered well rate allocation factors (Figure 2 right) at 10 days.

Figure 3 shows the water saturation maps that result from the two computation methods. The saturations as derived from a classic streamline-based flow simulation are shown in the top row; the saturations computed from PatMB are shown in the bottom row. The top row flow simulation results show the expected growth of the injected water as time increases (10, 20, 60 days). The bottom row of images is for the same timesteps and is the effective saturation (from Eq. 3) by applying material balance to the well-pair CVs and the synthetic historical injection/production rates (not shown) for each well. The TOF cutoff ensures that the material balance saturation is only applied to a portion of the bundle volume between each well-pair, which is why the saturation front appears to grow as time increases.

Shown in Figure 4 is a comparison between flow simulation (top row) and PatMB (bottom row) for the final timestep after 1 PVI. The left column images show the oil saturation, while the right column images show the Remaining Oil volume In Place (ROIP) distribution. In comparing the final oil saturations, there are clear differences between the two methods in that the PatMB approach lacks the gridcell-level detail of the flow simulation. However both methods show the trend of higher oil saturations on the west side of the model along with some unswept regions. Comparing the ROIP maps (right column) again reveals differences, but the unswept spots of high ROIP (red areas) are apparent in both methods.

This example contains 48 years of injection/production history with pattern waterflooding beginning 4 years after first production with a total of 18 injectors and 90 producers. The field had well rate adjustments, injector shut-ins, and producer injector conversions, all of which underscore that static, boxed-shape patterns would not work. The original OOIP grid was defined on a 161×63×1 grid cell model and is shown in Figure 5 left.

The main purpose of PatMB is to directly use the historical data to estimate the current fluid distributions. Shown in Figure 6 is the ROIP map and the remaining gas in-place (RGIP) map using the actual historical injection/production rates. As expected, we see areas of low and high ROIP. Recall that the PatMB approach requires no history matching in the traditional sense so long as the reservoir volume and initial conditions are approximately correct. Figure 7 summarize the declining average reservoir pressure through time, from the starting point of 6000 psi. Recall that for PatMB, the pressure map computed using Eq. 5 at each time step is used as the initial pressure condition for Eq. 5 at the next time step, and never relies on a 3D pressure solution. For PatMB, this average pressure is slightly higher than the average pressure from flow simulation (blue line).

There is no mass balance error with the PatMB calculation. As a second comparison for an average pressure calculation, we can ignored Eq. 5 completely and always used the pressure from the 3D pressure solve (always computed for streamline tracing). The result is the black line in Figure 8, which is lower still, is also not mass conservative, nor is it consistent with volumes added or removed from the model. This illustrates the importance of Eq. 5.
