The Petrocedeño field is an onshore extra heavy oil field (8-10 ˚ API) located in the Orinoco Oil belt, Venezuela (Fig. 1) and operated by PDVSA (60%), with the partners TOTAL (30%) and STATOIL (10%). Since 2000 it has been producing crude oil by means of natural depletion through horizontal wells from the shallow reservoirs (230-475 m) made up of unconsolidated sands of the Early to Middle Miocene Oficina Formation. The field covers an area of 399 km2 and over the years a high density of data has been acquired, including more than 1000 wells (deviated or vertical stratigraphic wells and horizontal producer wells), over 6000 ft of core and 3D seismic data.

The productive reservoirs of the Petrocedeño field are of the lower Oficina formation. These Early Miocene sediments are part of a wedge deposited onto Cretaceous or crystalline basement of the eastern Venezuelan fore deep basin (Fig. 2, Martinius et al., 2013). They were deposited in fluvial to tide-dominated deltaic environments filling in the basin. The lower Oficina formation has an operational subdivision of a lower fluvial and an upper transitional/deltaic part.

The first step of the workflow was to create the framework deterministically. Paleogeographic maps focusing on representing the channel belt locations and organization per reservoir unit were made using the sedimentological understanding and interpretation of the stratigraphic wells. These were consequently fine tuned using net-sand maps and information from the horizontal wells (Fig. 6A). The maps for the successive interval are then used to define a 3D parameter in the geocelular model representing the different depositional environments (Fig. 7).

Paleogeographic maps focusing on representing the channel belt locations and organization per reservoir unit were made using the sedimentological understanding and interpretation of the stratigraphic wells. These were consequently fine tuned using net-sand maps and information from the horizontal wells (Fig. 6A). The maps for the successive interval are then used to define a 3D parameter in the geocelular model representing the different depositional environments (Fig. 7).

The 2nd steps of the workflow, was to model the facies associations by means of SIS within the defined depositional environment 3D model (Fig. 8). The main input parameters were the wells with facies association logs, variogram dimensions, and most importantly a probability cube that allowed the representation of vertical and lateral geological trends within each interval.

In order to control the vertical and lateral distribution of the point bar (sand) and mudstone (shale) facies as observed in the wells, a probability cube was created and used as input in the SIS. The first step in creating this probability cube was to implement the vertical facies evolution per reservoir unit, observed at the stratigraphic wells. A vertical proportion curve (VPC) based on the stratigraphic wells (Fig. 9A) were constructed per unit using facies proportion statistics per 5 ft layer. After normalization (Fig. 9B) they were converted into 3D probability cubes, one for shale and one for sand (Fig. 9C).

The second input data included in the probability cube was the potential vertical communication between different reservoir units. At the interface between each reservoir unit, shale thickness maps (Fig. 10A) were created, a result of correlation and kriging of shale thickness data between the stratigraphic wells. These maps provided the information needed, as areas with thin or absent shale barrier are areas of potential communication, whereas areas of thick shale obviously prevent vertical flow.

The resulting probability cube (Fig. 11A) guided the SIS simulation, resulting in a facies association parameter (Fig. 11B) respecting both the vertical evolution and the potential area of inter-unit commu-nication: more sandy facies are present in area of potential vertical communication, and shally facies in area where barrier were identified.

In the 3rd step of the workflow, the facies were modeled using SIS into the existing facies association model (Fig. 12). As the facies association model already included strong lateral and vertical trends, the facies were modeled without additional trend inputs. The facies are computed at the wells using a cut-off. Concerning the variogram settings, the same approach was used as for the modeling of the facies associations: dimension and sinuosity being similar. This enabled the facies to be distributed following the main channel geometries defined by the facies association stage (Fig. 12B).

Analysis of the facies association modelled, showed that the vertical facies evolution was successfully implemented and the desired petrophysical signature achieved. This can be seen by comparing the VPC from well observations (Fig. 13A), the VPC from the model (Fig. 13B) with the vertical evolution shown by the average volume of wet clay per layer from model (Fig. 13C).

The implementations of the shale barrier maps in the same phase can be seen by volume of wet clay average maps of the concerning unit interfaces (Fig. 14B) but are at the same time very much dependent on the shale proportion in the corresponding layers. Impact on the dynamic simulation has yet to be evaluated.

Sand distribution can be independently validated by comparing the net-sand maps per reservoir units from the model (Fig. 15B) with the operational net-sand maps (Fig. 15A). This shows that the main trends are well represented in the model with the expected uncertainty.

The sinuous patterns of the facies model gave the expected result in the petrophysical model. In figure 16 the facies model (Fig. 16A) is compared to the permeability model (Fig. 16A). The high permeability streaks coincide with the good sand locations and follow the same trend as the facies model (Fig. 16). The concept that higher quality sands are deposited along the axis potentially resulting in preferential flow have shown to be implemented.
