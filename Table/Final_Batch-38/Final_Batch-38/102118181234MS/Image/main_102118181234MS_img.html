Operators are continuously improving their well design, drilling, and stimulation techniques, resulting in considerably higher initial production. The shape of the H-Q curve of a centrifugal pump is characteristic of its own stage design and geometry. Fig. 3 shows examples of three different shapes of H-Q curves of 400 Series pumps (4-in OD) for ESP applications. The D3500N pump H-Q curve shows the profile of a continuously decreasing head with increasing flow rate from the zero to the maximum flow rate. Pumps with this shape of H-Q curve (dH/dQ<0) are sometimes referred to as having a stable pump curve.

Finally, the operating point (head and flow rate) is the intersection of the application system curve and the pump H-Q curve at the proposed operating speed as shown in Fig. 4.

Fig. 5 illustrates how a pumping system, comprising a pump with a saddle in its H-Q curve and operating at flow rate QB, can be either statically stable or unstable, depending on the slope of the system curve HA.

The basic principle of static instability is that a pumping system will become unstable if the slope of the pump H-Q is greater than the slope of the system curve, resulting in two or more intersection points such as B and C in the left-hand side of Fig. 6.

Fig. 7 illustrates the perturbation of mass flow (ṁ) and pressure rise (Δp) through the pump during one cycle. The net energy (input or output) is determined as the integral of the product ṁ × Δp in one cycle. CASE I in Fig. 7 shows the result of a momentary disturbance where the pump H-Q has a positive slope and where the mass flow rate and incremental pump pressure are in-phase, resulting in a net positive input of energy and causing dynamic instability.

Several aspects are considered when defining the minimum and maximum limits of the ROR for a pump stage including variability of pump performance and test curves, the net axial thrust, and efficiency. Also, in some cases like the example illustrated in Fig. 8, the lower flow rate limit of the ROR is set based on the shape of the H-Q curve. The example shows the catalog curve for a RC4000 pump stage with its ROR between 3,500 bbl/d and 7000 bbl/d at 60 Hz.

ESP systems driven with a variable speed drive (VSD) enable the operator to expand the operating range of the ESP by increasing or decreasing the operating frequency, and therefore, the pump speed. Fig. 9 shows an example of multi-frequency curves for a pump highlighting the minimum, maximum, and best efficiency point (BEP) rates for each frequency.

The stage geometry was designed and optimized using world-class turbomachinery design tools capable of modeling the complex unsteady flow conditions and validated by extensive testing including multiphase, laser velocimetry, and sand loop tests. As example, Fig. 10 illustrates that the measured profile of the relative velocity field inside a pump impeller measured by laser velocimetry matches very closely with the computed computational fluid dynamics (CFD) model.

This ESP system included an RC4000 pump string with three pumps in tandem and 247 stages in total. The pumps were built in compression type and abrasion resistant configuration for improved reliability when pumping the high content of solids expected. A gas handling device (AGH) and vortex gas separator were also included to improve the performance when pumping the multiphase flow. The ESP string andwellbore diagram are illustrated in Fig. 11.

The production profile illustrated in Fig. 12 is representative of wells in the field. The initial production during this first ESP phase was 4,950 bbl/d of fluid with a PIP of 4200 psi. The production and PIP gradually declined to 1,595 bbl/d and 890 psi, respectively. The water cut decreased from 67% to 54% and the production gas-to-liquid ratio (GLR) increased from 180 to 309 scf/bbl during this period.

Also, since the well flow was gassy dynamic instability as defined by Greitzer (1981) was possible, particularly during the last month of operation when the flow rate decreased below 2,400 bbl/d while running at 50 Hz, as it can be seen in Fig. 14.

It should be mentioned that oscillating parameter trends are not uncommon in ESP systems operating in these challenging unconventional shale plays, regardless of the shape of the pump H-Q curve. Similar trends are present in wells with pumps with stable pump characteristics (dH/dQ <0). The ESP equipment often includes pumps with customized configuration and materials to mitigate corrosion issues. Additionally, the ESPs typically have an external MONEL® coating for corrosion protection. A downhole sensor is installed with the ESP systems to monitor downhole operating parameters including pump intake pressure, fluid and motor temperature, and vibration. Fig. 17 shows the graph for production data taken since the installation. The actual performance with the RC4000 pump greatly exceeded the target production reaching an average production of 6,700 bbl/d after the initial commissioning. As shown in the right hand side of Fig. 16, the operating point at the 66 Hz operating frequency is within the ROR.

The RC4000 pump stage was selected for the application because of its higher head, higher efficiency, and better resistance to erosion due to solids. As illustrated in Fig. 18, the performance curves of the RC4000 pump resulted in a 50% improvement in head per stage and 7.4% improvement in pump efficiency compared to the D4300N pump that was used before.

Because the higher head per stage, the new pump string was shortened by 50%. The previous installations had 201 stages with three pump housings, while the new RC4000 string has only two housings with a total of 138 stages. The components of ESP string installed in the well RCU 41-14 are illustrated in Fig. 19. The operating point of the system is also shown in the pump H-Q curve on the right side of Fig. 19.

Fig. 20 shows the daily production data from the production test records. The average production since the installation of the RC4000 pump is 3,738 bbl/d of fluid, which represents an increase of 11.5% in production for this well. The operation of the current ESP system with the higher efficiency RC4000 pump has reduced the power requirement for operation of the well RCU 41-14. The cost savings in electricity cost are estimated to be approximately 5.5%.
