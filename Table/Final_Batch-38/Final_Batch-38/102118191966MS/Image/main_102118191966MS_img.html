The study area, referred to as the C Block, is located in the middle of Bowen Basin and included the interpretation of 180 wells with geophysical log data which were unevenly distributed in a 3D seismic volume of 51 km2 (Fig. 1-a). The RCM were targeted for this study. They are a middle-rank coal seam gas target with high gas contents and low permeability. The main coal plies in the block are R1 and R2 at the top and R3, R4, R5, R6, R7, R8, R9 and R10 at the middle, with the Girrah seam at the base.

The majority of wells have only been drilled deep enough to intersect the R1 and R2 coal seam, and only a small amount of wells has been drilled through to the Girrah seam (Fig. 1). From the well correlation section (Fig. 2), coal seams R1 and R2 are spatially more continuous with total thickness approximately 5 m. Other coal seams such as R3, R4, R5, R6, R7, R8, R9 and R10 are less continuous with numerous merges and pinch-out features. Several seismic attributes, such as strong amplitude and frequency, have been used to map out the distribution of sandstone channels. Two distinctive channel groups can be distinguished, one coming from the north and one coming from the south (Fig. 2).

The majority of the faults in the C block are high angle normal faults and there are also a number of large reverse faults along the eastern boundary of the study area. The use of coherence slices was found to be a suitable methodology to trace medium and larger faults (Fig. 3). Slices of coherence, max curvature and dip were combined together to detect the small scale faults. The max curvature method was particularly effective in tracing out the smaller faults (Fig. 4). The coherence and dip slices were then used to verify these small scale faults detected by maximum curvature.

Normalization was performed on a number of wells which had density values that exceeded the normal range. The cross plots in Fig. 5 shows that the sonic and density have a strong relationship. The coal cut off value identified from other lithology can be obtained by using the lithology data in the cross plot and histogram figures of P-impedance and density. For coal, the P-impedance cut off value is set to be less than 5*106 kg/m3, and density cut off value is less than 1.8 g/cc (Fig. 5).

The Density inversion was generated using a Multi-attribute Regression Method in HRS software. This meant the P-impedance was used as an external attribute and other attributes generated from seismic data were used as internal attributes. A cross plot analysis of the average error by step-wise regression methods was used to decide the best number of attributes and which of the attributes were to use for the density inversion in Fig. 6.

A cross plot analysis of the average error by step-wise regression methods was used to decide the best number of attributes and which of the attributes were to use for the density inversion in Fig. 6. Additionally, the predicted and actual curves for each well were compared after probabilistic neural network computation to check the reliability of the results (Fig. 7). The resulting density cubes included the overburden zone which was calculated by well data interpolation in consideration of GIS overlays. The velocity model was then calculated by dividing the P-impedance model with the density model.

Analyses were conducted to determine the depth relationship for maximum stress, minimum stress and the mean effective stress. Fig. 8 shows that the maximum, minimum and effective stress increases with depth. Maximum and minimum stress show non-linear positive relationships. These results were used as input for 3D geomechanical modelling.

The 2D stress simulation results are depicted in Fig. 9 as lines showing the principal stress directions and colors revealing the relative magnitudes. In Well L, the simulated principal stress has a northeast-southwest orientation which is consistent with BI data of this well. 2D stress simulation on the R1 and R2 coal seams showed that the direction of principal stress of the study area is predominantly in a north-east to south-west direction. The direction of principal stress can be used to predict the fracture deformation directions.

The density and velocity cube had to be trimmed so that the data extent was from ground level to the base of the Girrah seam. Once the density volume had been trimmed, the overburden pressure could be calculated by integrating the density volume. Fig. 10 shows that Pov is influenced not only by the ground elevation, but also by the monocline structure. The Pov in south area is less than 6 Mpa, but as the depth increases, the pressure exceeds 10 Mpa, maxing out at approximately 13 Mpa.

The relationship between production data, including peak production, average peak gas production, gross in-seam length, net in-seam length, cumulative gas, NTG%, and monthly average peak, has been compared to simulated DHSR. Some of the production attributes had better correlations with DHSR than others. Average peak gas production and monthly average peak production showed good correlation with DHSR (Fig. 14). Average peak gas production decreased rapidly with an increase in DHSR.

Fig. 15 shows that the average peak gas production has a good correlation with depth. Production decreases with an increase in depth. The DHSR increases with depth and therefore the average peak gas shows a strong correlation with DHSR. Fig. 16 shows that once the DHSR exceeds 0.55, the impact on gas production will increase rapidly. This may indicate that the DHSR can indirectly influence the well production stability.
