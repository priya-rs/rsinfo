Since 1982 the field is in production and contains approximately 525 MMSTB STOOIP of 16°API oil. Tambaredjo is located in the marshy coastal area of Suriname, 55 km west of the capital Paramaribo (Figure 1). The pilot which involved 3 injection and 9 production wells, all vertical (Figure 2) has been discussed in several previous papers (Moe Soe Let, Manichand, & Seright, 2012) (Manichand, Moe Soe Let, Gil, Quillien, & Seright, 2013) (Manichand & Seright, 2014).

The reservoir is the T-sand, part of the lower Saramacca member of the Saramacca Formation of Paleocene age (Figure 3). It is composed of vertically stacked sand bodies deposited in a fluvial-estuarine and shallow marine environment and is encountered at depths varying from 900 to 1,400 ft (375-425 m) (Kartoredjo, et al. 2006). The main depositional direction is North-South. The reservoir is dipping towards the north and is limited by an edge aquifer in that direction; to the south the sands are pinching out which constitutes a stratigraphic trap. Several fault systems are present over the field.

S. Toelsie, & P. Goerdajal (2013). Sand Control in Shallow Unconsolidated Sandstone Oil Reservoirs at Staatsolie N.V. Suriname. Paper SPE 165188 presented at the SPE European Formation Damage Conference & Exhibition. The Netherlands, 5– 7 June, Noordwijk.

The T1 sand is the main reservoir and was deposited in braided streams as an incised valley fill; the reservoir is composed of unconsolidated sands, fine to coarse, poorly to well-sorted, of high porosity (33% average) and very high permeability of 3 to over 10 darcies. Reservoir heterogeneity is relatively high due to the depositional environment and the Dykstra-Parsons coefficient is 0.78. Maximum reservoir thickness can reach up to 45 ft (13.5 m) but is less than 25 ft (7.5 m) in the pilot area. Figure 4 shows a type log of the T-sands.

Injection rates varied between 150 – 450 bbl/d/injection well and injected viscosity varied between 45 cp (at the beginning of the injection) to about 125 cp after February 2013 (Figure 5). In total 0.75 PV of polymer solution were injected in pattern 1M101, 0.64 PV in pattern 1N062 and 0.56 PV in pattern 1M052 (Table 1). In order to achieve sufficient injectivity the wells were operated above fracturing pressure; this point will be discussed further below.

As explained in Delamaide et al. (Delamaide, Moe Soe Let, Bhoendie, Jong-APin, & Paidin, 2016), 6 out of the total of 9 wells in the pilot patterns responded favorably to polymer injection and had an increase in oil rate; incremental production was approximately 120,000 bbl as of March 2015. One well from one of the pilot patterns and one well outside the patterns experienced severe water breakthrough and had to be shut-in later on. Some of the wells from outside the pilot patterns also responded favorably. Figure 6 presents a summary of the wells responses.

In most cases polymer injection resulted in an increase in fluid production, suggesting a strong pressure response from the wells. Figure 7 provides an example of that behavior.

This is a very good result in view of the oil viscosity and the heterogeneous geology, however, the result is somewhat biased by pattern 1M052 which performed much better than the other patterns. These results are in line with those obtained in other projects in viscous oil (Delamaide, 2014) (Hernandez, 2015). Figure 8 provides a plot of cumulative oil production and incremental oil in the pilot patterns.

Figure 9 shows the produced polymer concentration for the wells in the pilot; for comparison purposes note that the maximum injected concentration was 2,500 ppm. As can be seen from the figure, produced polymer concentration was low in most wells; in addition, it remained relatively flat after breakthrough except in well 1I25 and seems to have peaked for some wells (1M10, 1N061, 1M051, 1M05 etc.).

Tracer injected in well 1N062 was observed in only 4 wells, none of them being the pilot wells surrounding the injector (Figure 10); the highest tracer concentration was observed in well 1I25 which is the farthest pilot well from the injector. Other concentrations were below 5 ppb.

Tracer injected in well 1M052 was observed in 8 wells (Figure 11) but in most cases at less than 5 ppb (below the reliability level). Concentrations over 5 ppb were only observed in wells 1I25 and 1M04. Breakthrough times varied between 28 days and 1,106 days. Figure 12 shows the highest produced tracer concentrations from the 1M052 injection.

The production of tracer injected in well 1M052 appeared to coincide with changes in oil rate, water-cut and other production elements in both wells 1I25 and 1M04 (Figure 13). This can be interpreted as tracer breakthrough in several distinct layers, immediately preceding the production of corresponding oil or water banks.

The observation of the evolution of water salinity with time in the wells in Figure 2 revealed a relatively similar behavior for most wells; an example is presented in Figure 14. As can be seen in the figure, water salinity increased progressively right from the start of production in the example wells as in most of the wells in the area. Given the fact that the salinity of the formation water is in the 1,000 to 5,000 ppm range compared to 7,500 ppm for the Cretaceous formation water, it seems logical to suspect that the increase in water salinity was due to the influx of Cretaceous water into the reservoir and subsequent mixing with formation water.

A simple ratio calculation on this basis allowed to establish the fraction of produced water originating from the Cretaceous formation (Figure 15). The results suggest that the contribution of the Cretaceous to the water production is in general less than 40% of the total. The rest of the produced water is formation water released by compaction.

Using the values of TDS for the injected water and the produced water before breakthrough, it was possible to calculate the percentage of produced water originating from the injection wells; the plot corresponding to 1N06 is shown in Figure 16. As can be seen in the figure, the breakthrough was rather sharp and the percentage of injected water in the production increased rapidly.

It was also possible to calculate the total injected water produced using this data. The corresponding plot comparing injected and produced water provided in Figure 17 shows that only 20% of the injected water was produced.

A Jordan plot over the pilot period (Figure 18) suggests that total fluid production is only approximately 50% of total injected fluid. Even taking reservoir expansion into account, this suggests that a significant portion of the fluid could be leaving the patterns. The fact that fluid was leaving the pattern area was expected due to the unconfined nature of the pilot patterns and was corroborated by the response to injection observed in wells far outside the pilot patterns such as wells 1M16 and 1M25 (Figure 6). As mentioned by Delamaide et al. (Delamaide, Moe Soe Let, Bhoendie, Jong-A-Pin, & Paidin, 2016) it had been decided not to adjust pumping rates during the pilot and as a result the wells were not optimally pumped-off. This probably resulted in driving some of the fluids away from the pilot's producers.

The evolution of static pressure in the pilot area between 1991 and the present day is presented in Figure 19; there is a fair amount of scatter in the data, however, the trend is unmistakable. Initial reservoir pressure in the pilot area was approximately 421 psi and average reservoir pressure at the beginning of the pilot was approximately 181 psi. Between the start of production and the time when the pilot started in September 2008, a total of 1,217 Mbbl of fluid were produced (757 Mbbl of oil and 460 Mbbl of water); net withdrawal (taking into account the water influx from the Cretaceous and assuming all this water was produced) was thus 1,217 – 163 = 1,054 Mbbl. This net withdrawal resulted in a pressure decline of approximately 240 psi which corresponds to 0.228 psi/Mbbl.

Polymer production was calculated on a well-by well basis and is shown in Figure 20 together with produced injection water. Polymer production was calculated by interpolating the measurements of produced concentration and may thus be subject to error. As can be seen from the figure, while the volume of injected water produced increases with time, the percentage of produced polymer appears to remain stationary from the end of 2012, after seeing a first inflexion (downwards) in late 2011. This appears to approximately coincide with the first increase in injected polymer viscosity from 45 cp to 85 cp which took place in November 2011 and the second increase from 85 cp to 125 cp which occurred in February 2013.

Similarly, Figure 21 shows a plot of polymer production vs. cumulative polymer injected with and without well 1I25. Total polymer injected was estimated based on measured injected viscosity as well as polymer consumption. As can be seen from the figure, the increases in injected polymer viscosity seem to coincide with breaks in the slope of cumulative polymer production. Figure 9 shows that well 1I25 was producing high concentration of polymer and exhibiting an increase in produced concentration, contrary to most of the other wells in the pilot. For that reason, Figure 21 also shows a plot of cumulative polymer produced for the wells in the pilot except 1I25. The trend that was apparent in the first curve is even more obvious on this plot.

The main difficulty of the process was to determine for each pattern exactly how much of the incremental oil had to be attributed to which injection well; for instance, some wells in pattern 1N062 may have responded to injection in well 1M101 thus the incremental oil should be attributed to 1M101 instead of 1N062. Figure 22 represents the PUF for each pattern and Figure 23 is a summary of the pilot results including the PUF which takes into account the wells surrounding the pilot. Note that the PUF for the pilot is significantly higher than the PUF of each individual pattern because it includes the response of all the wells in the pattern, whereas the PUF for individual patterns includes only the response of the pattern wells due to the pattern injector.

During the course of the pilot, well 1N11 had to be shut-in due to high water-cut and high polymer concentration (Figure 24); breakthrough occurred in less than 3 months. In addition, well 1N01 which is outside of the pilot patterns (Figure 2) also experienced a sudden breakthrough and produced high polymer concentration, in fact much higher than most wells in the pilot (Figure 9). It was initially suspected that these events were linked to hydraulic fractures, however, after careful review, nothing allowed to attribute these behaviors to hydraulic fractures rather than to reservoir heterogeneity.
