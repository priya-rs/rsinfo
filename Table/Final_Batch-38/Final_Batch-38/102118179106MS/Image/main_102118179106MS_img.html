Wattenberg field of the Denver Julesburg basin, Colorado as shown in Figure 1 (Sonnenburg, 2002) isone of the largest overpressured, basin-centered oil and gas fields within the Rockies in terms of total proved reserves, areal extent, and number of wells drilled. Discovered in 1970 by the drilling of a vertical well into the Dakota “J” sandstone, Wattenberg continues to be one of the most active fields currently in the country, despite almost continuous oil and gas development throughout the last 45 years.

The 5-well study pad is located in the Wattenberg Field, Weld County, Colorado. Five horizontal wells named I, J, K, L and M respectively were drilled in this pad oriented from north [hee< to south [toe] as shown in Figure 2.

As shown in Figure 2, fault throws encountered were on the order of ~50 feet. The primary target for these five wells is the Niobrara “C” chalk bench as shown in Figure 3 which is a type log from an offset vertical well. Wells I and L were landed in and stayed in the lower “C” chalk bench for most of the lateral but had a toe-up design in the second half of the well, ending in the lower “B” chalk bench. Wells J and M were planned in the lower “C” chalk bench and stayed there flat throughout the entire lateral. The Well-K was drilled in the middle “C” chalk bench and stayed flat in the middle of the chalk until the graben where it contacted the “B” chalk before ending up in the lower part of the “B” marl.

Eventhough Wells I and L were drilled similarly, Well-K and Well-L were chosen for this optimization study because they were next to each other and Well-I had sleeves while both Well-K and Well-L utilized perf-n-plug completion methodology. Figure 4 depicts the Niobrara structural cross section along the Well-K directional survey. It clearly shows the well landing in the middle “C” chalk bench before being faulted into the “B” chalk and then back into the upper “C” chalk and lower “B” marl.

Figure 5 shows the Niobrara structural cross section along the Well-L directional survey. The well follows a toe-up design with the heel landing in the lower “C” chalk followed by faulting into the “B” chalk and then back into the “B” marl before ending up in the “B” chalk again. The well trajectories and where they landed need to be accounted for when comparing production.

Coiled tubing deployed fiber also provides an alternative for deviated/horizontal laterals when compared to wireline deployed fiber-optics. The rigidity of coiled tubing also provides protection to the fiber-optic cable when deployed. The coiled tubing deployed fiber-optics provides DTS and DAS data along the length of the coil or the wellbore once deployed in a well. This technology can also be utilized to evaluate acid treatments, observe inter-well communication during treatments, leak detection and finally as a production log. Memory mode pressure and temperature point gauges can also be run as a part of the BHA of coiled tubing, to gain better understanding of flowing pressure. Most of the current fiber-optics driven diagnostics applications currently target DTS, which is used to determine the fracturing fluid distribution by identifying the cooling and warming patterns post injection (Nitika et al.,). It is difficult to assess the quantity of fluid being placed in individual perforation clusters within a stage of Plug-n-perf type completions during injection (Sierra et al.).

Analyzing warm-back DTS data can provide essential information into the near-wellbore fracture initiation points which in turn would be helpful in determining cluster efficiency of the various stages. Figure 14 describes the various fiber-optics sensing techniques that are currently utilized. The techniques range from a single point sensor (providing temperature and pressure point measurement at the end of the fiber optic cable) to an array of point-sensor measurements for strain and temperature, to a fully distributed sensing setup where the entire length of the fiber becomes the sensors.

Thus, when deployed in wellbores either via coiled tubing or permanent installation, fiber-optics provides a unique method of surveying the entire wellbore at the same time. Fiber-optics technology utilizes analysis of backscattered light from the fiber glass. Figure 15 shows the various components of the backscattered light. Temperature, Acoustics and Strain information can be inferred by analyzing these components of the backscattered light. The intensity of the Raman component of the anti-stokes band is temperature dependent, and stokes band is non-temperature dependent.
