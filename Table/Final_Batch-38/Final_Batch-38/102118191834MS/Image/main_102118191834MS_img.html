The following workflow, see fig#1 was developed to determine optimal cluster and well spacing. Each step is explained through out the document. Figures, charts and results in documents are examples.

In shale reservoirs flow regimens stay in transient flow for a long period of time due to the low permeability which ranges in the Nano Darcies figures. This aspect makes very difficult to estimate recoveries in early times. However, diagnostic plot- Linear Flow Specialized –Normalized pressure vs. square root of time (fig#2) can be used as a completion effectiveness tool if limited variation in reservoir properties7 is a characteriistic of the selected Area. Diagnostic Plot is a good indicator of completion effectiveness based on early well performance data and also quantifies total connected fracture area and square root of time of SRV permeability.

The second step is to validate the results of a robust numerical simulator (in this case Intersect) versus a commercial simplified numerical simulator. A simplified earth model was used taking into account the models' strengths and weaknesses of commercial simplified numerical simulator, showed in fig#6.

A homogeneous reservoir without natural fractures was proposed to be numerically simulated by drilling a defined number of horizontal wells. Fig. #7 describes two extreme cases for reservoir simulation. Case 1: minimum amount of horizontal wells (4), spaced 1000ft, and case 5: maximum amount of horizontal wells (8), spaced 500ft. The other 3 cases are represented in Table# 4. Completion designs were assumed at 3 clusters per stage assuming cluster spacing of 60, 80 &100ft.

Simulation results presented in fig# 8 shows that the simplified numerical simulator EUR's compare favorably with the more robust numerical simulator.

One of weaknesses presented in the use of simplified numerical simulator is that the fracture cannot overlap due to simulator limitations (see weekness in fig# 6). In order to incorporate the mentioned limitation in the analysis, fracture overlap was incorporated (fig 9) in the simulation in order to evaluate its impact on well performance.

The third step is to analyze the impact on well and cluster spacing of a specific economic indicator to all reservoir, geology and completions parameters that affect the numerical simulation run results and their economic indicators. Fig# 10 shows all parameters that were identified as uncertainties and tested. The tornado chart displayed in fig #10 is an example that shows each of the uncertainty impacts on a particular scenario NPV.

Step number 4 requires running selected production forecasts using simplified numerical simulator based on the selected uncertainties identified in step 3. The following decision tree (fig 11) was developed to understand the impact of each uncertainty on cluster and well spacing. The decision node #1 and 2 includes well spacing and cluster spacing cases while the probabilistic node (2, 3, 4, 5) includes the P10, P50 and P90 values identified in step 3. The P10 and P90 cases are represented as 25% Chance of occurrence which indicates normal distribution in all identified uncertainties.

Deterministic economic analysis are performed for the selected cases assuming P50 values for all the uncertainties selected in step #3. Examples of value drivers could be DPIR (discount profit invetment ratio) & NPV(Net present value), EUR/1000ft (Estimated ultimate recovery per 1000ft) and first year production. Results indicated in Fig 15 shows an example of NPV and DPI for each well and cluster spacing scenario and reserves increase from a particular scenario. It is clear that the optimum well and cluster spacing is a function of the value measure.

Probabilistic Analysis are performed using Decision Analysis (DAT) technique. The intent is to see the impact of each main uncertainty selected in step 3 on the selected value drivers. Sensitivity of value driver to parameter probability are the results showed in charts Fig 16 and 17 where value drivers are sensible (change decision) to the parameter selected Fig 16 and not sensible (no change in decision) Fig 17.

Currently the most direct method of monitoring stimulation of rocks during hydraulic fracturing. Data is used to map/characterize fracture growth (azimuth, length, height, etc.), and in some cases to understand source/failure mechanisms. Mapping of microseismic events in real time can provide useful information on fracture growth and completions effectiveness – other operators have used microseismic monitoring in real time to change completions design on the fly and well spacing due to overlapping (see fig 23). Microseismic monitoring can be conducted downhole (using tools in horizontal and/or vertical borehole), from surface, or using a combination of both surface and downhole.
