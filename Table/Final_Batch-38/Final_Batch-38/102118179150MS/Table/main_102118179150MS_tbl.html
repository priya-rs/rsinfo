Fracture complexity here refers to fracture planes at multiple orientations that are dilating and accepting fluid, as opposed to the planar complexity observed in cored fractures (Figs. 2 and 4). Fig. 18 shows an illustration of various forms of complexity (Warpinski et al. 2009); the lower two cases would be considered complex if the orthogonal fractures dilate. Complexity is a difficult trait to assess because of the potential to be artificially created or obscured by uncertainty in the microseismic measurements. Such complexity was already discussed relative to azimuth (Table 1) measurements in Australia (Johnson et al. 2010) and at Jonah field (Wolhart et al. 2006; Malone et al. 2009).
