An experiment in the Piceance basin of Colorado was the site of a core-through of a hydraulic fracture that had been microseismically monitored a few years earlier (Warpinski et al. 1993). The microseismic data at that time were not particularly accurate because of technology limitations (e.g., single receiver systems), but the core fracture locations were approximately coincident with the microseismic distribu-tion, as shown in Fig. 1. In this case, there were two fracture intervals observed in a deviated-well core across the zone that was fractured, and these were spaced approximately 50 ft apart, so it is difficult to attach any uncertainty of the azimuth relative to the microseismic data.

The use of surface tiltmeters to determine fracture azimuth is well known (Wright 1998a). A combined microseismic and surface tiltmeter project in the San Juan basin of New Mexico (Estrada et al. 2009) provided a detailed comparison of microseismic monitoring of four fracture stages in each of two wells, with tiltmeter monitoring on those two wells and four others. Fig. 8 shows a comparison of the results for the two sets of data. For these planar fractures in sandstone reservoirs, the azimuths from both the tiltmeters and the microseismic data are consistent, with agreement within a few degrees, which is well within any expected uncertainty bounds for measurements at depth.

Fig. 15 shows a composite set of data for two small water-calibration treatments that were conducted to evaluate stress conditions and general fracture behavior. These injections were 100 and 210 bbl, respectively, and both were pumped at 10 bbl/min. Shown clockwise in the figure, from the top left are a side view of the microseismic data from the two injections (red and blue symbols), a plan view of the microseismic data, also showing the location of monitor wells, a microseismic depth histogram, the tiltmeter data, and the finite element model match of the data.

A similar data set is shown in Fig. 16 for two identical minifracs that were conducted in the same interval several months later. The minifracs used 400 bbl of a 40-lbm linear gel pumped at 20 bbl/min. Again, the interpreted tiltmeter height agrees well with the microseismic data. It should be noted that there are a few microseismic outliers, but this is not unexpected because there (a) might be uncertainty in the microseismic locations, (b) might be stress effects above and below the actual fracture, or (c) might be isolated places where the fracture actually does have greater height but the tiltmeters only monitor the average height.

The Mounds drill cuttings injection experiment, discussed previously, was an interesting validation test of microseismic fracture height. It consisted of the microseismic data, downhole tiltmeter data, RA tracers, and, most importantly, cored wells to detect the fractures. Fig. 17 shows a composite side view of the various data (Moschovidis et al. 2000).

Fracture complexity here refers to fracture planes at multiple orientations that are dilating and accepting fluid, as opposed to the planar complexity observed in cored fractures (Figs. 2 and 4). Fig. 18 shows an illustration of various forms of complexity (Warpinski et al. 2009); the lower two cases would be considered complex if the orthogonal fractures dilate. Complexity is a difficult trait to assess because of the potential to be artificially created or obscured by uncertainty in the microseismic measurements. Such complexity was already discussed relative to azimuth (Table 1) measurements in Australia (Johnson et al. 2010) and at Jonah field (Wolhart et al. 2006; Malone et al. 2009).

The analysis (Astakhov et al. 2012) breaks the reservoir layer into grid blocks and inverts the surface tilt response to determine the best fit of the fracturing behavior in all of the reservoir blocks. In this example, the magenta-colored grid blocks show horizontal fractures, the cyan grid blocks show vertical fractures (with the azimuth indicated by the darker lines), and the mixed color blocks have both horizontal and vertical components.
