The DataLogger was designed and implemented to be as simple and small as possible by choosing small chip-based accelerometers, a microcontroller, memory chip, and batteries. The final design version contains only three chips, one lithium battery (CR1025), and two capacitors. Figure 4 shows the configuration of the DataLogger and a bench testing implementation inside an appropriately sized shell.

The software for the DataLogger consists of two parts: Control software written into program memory inside the microcontroller; and data processing software, which runs on a workstation or laptop PC to process recorded data and compute the path/position by integrating the recorded accelerations. Chip level processes are illustrated in Figure 5; from top to bottom: a battery is attached to the DataLogger, the microprocessor initializes, the accelerometer initializes, the flash memory initializes, an interrupt is set that pauses writing until a change in orientation is measured by the accelerometer, when movement is registered the microprocessor reads data from the accelerometer and writes it to the flash memory.

The Viper Drill can be deployed on cased or open-hole completed wells.

The Viper Drill can be deployed on cased or open-hole completed wells. On cased wells, a casing milling bit is first used to form a hole in the casing; then the coiled tubing is pulled and the drill string is attached for the formation drilling process. The Viper Drill can drill out at 90° from 5.5 in. or larger casing, and at higher angles from smaller casing. This allows its use in the majority of mature stripper wells in the USA. The Viper Drill then creates a hole in the pay zone by utilizing a rotating, mechanical drill bit on the end of the Viper Drill string (Figure 9).

Early in the workover of the well, problems with the surface equipment arose. While the casing and cement drilling string performed flawlessly, some unexpected issues arose with the formation drilling-string and probe tool-strings. For example, on the first run of the formation drilling tool the drilling head was lost in the lateral due to stick-slips. The project also experienced problems with the initial probe that conveyed the sensor (Figure 10).

The probe was designed to use a jet hose and while the probe had been successfully tested at bench scale, in the field, the crew had problems getting it to transition through the tight “J-path” of the downhole whipstock. A new sensor probe (Figure 11) was designed to deploy on the end of the Viper Drill string and ran successfully on all subsequent downhole probe runs.

After the initial set of field tests at the Barbie#3, a second generation DataLogger was developed for the Federal 00 #3 test, in part due to much longer trip times in the deeper well, and also due to continuing development of chips used in the design of the DataLogger which also allowed the inclusion of a gyroscope and compass to the data stream. Figure 12 shows the schematic of the new DataLogger designed with the upgraded Main Processor (MPU-9250), accelerometer (PICLF1825) and larger flash memory (IS25LP128).

The new DataLogger has three layers: A battery (CR1632 140mAh), the MPU-9250 Breakout, and a small PCB with the MCU and accelerometer chips. The system is constructed in the lab, with the exception of the battery, which is soldered on immediately before deployment. Figure 13 shows images of a DataLogger used in field tests at the Federal 00 #003.
