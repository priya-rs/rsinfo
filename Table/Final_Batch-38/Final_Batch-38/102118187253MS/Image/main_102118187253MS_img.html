The Permian Basin (Figure 1) continues to be the most prolific oil producing region in the nation (EIA, 2017). Continued advancements in hydraulic fracturing techniques, horizontal drilling and advanced diagnostic tools have greatly increased development in unconventional reservoirs. The previous work, SPE 175535 (Parker et al., 2015) discussed traditional fracture optimization methods and design variables including fluid and proppant selection and fracture conductivity enhancement to promote well performance improvements and introduced stage size, cluster spacing and perforation efficiency design methods.

The engineered design methodology discussed resulted in significant improvements to well performance, allowed for a systematic method to validate parameters and provided the basis-of-design for benchmarking well performance. Key parameters, engineering tools and methods are necessary to evaluate completion efficiency and hydrocarbon productivity of the reservoir in order to design optimal fracture treatments in unconventional reservoirs (Meyer et al., 2013). Figure 2 shows the locations of Wells A-F and offset technology Well G in Reeves County, Texas, used in this study.

Core data from Well G (vertical) was used to calibrate and enhance the confidence of the log-derived reservoir characteristic used for natural fracture spacing estimates, fluid leakoff calculations, mechanical rock properties and in-situ stress calculations necessary for DFN fracture modeling. Minor adjustments were required to calibrate with DFIT results conducted in the toe of Well B. The core points and the calibrated logs used in the fracture modeling are presented in Figure 3 showing favorable agreement with the inputs.

Applied Post-Job Analytics (APJA) is a post-job engineering based analysis and methodology designed to determine the number of contributing transverse fractures (clusters) in horizontal wellbores, fracturing pressure components and the fracture characteristic half-lengths per stage. Table 3 presents the APJA cluster performance results for Wells A-F. Although the industry usually communicates in terms of cluster efficiency, a more useful metric is the number of fractures per 1,000 ft and the equivalent cluster spacing between open clusters. All three metrics should be considered. Figures 4 and 5 show the APJA cluster analysis results for Well D.

DFN pressure matching with updated and calibrated inputs was performed for Well D, Stage 9 which was completed with 25 stages, 5 clusters per stage and 200 ft stage spacing. The surface and bottomhole pressure match is shown in Figure 9.

Total created fracture height is approximately 525 ft on average. This is supported by the nearby microseismic data for Well A (Figure 10) but in the previous work the upper microseismic events were ignored because it did not match the initial assumptions and the fracture height was limited to 225 ft based on the data cloud. Additionally, the job design for Well D was double that of Well A, so additional fracture height would be expected.

Several authors (Lehman et. al., (2016), Friedrich and Milliken (2013), Zakhour et al., (2015), and Rodriguez (2017)) have shown similar (or greater) fracture heights in the Delaware and Midland basins and further work by the authors support 525 ft of fracture height. Solutions were determined for the apparent net pressure, true width-opening pressure, mid-field fracture complexity, complex back stress, created length, maximum proppant length (plug displacement), likely proppant length (settled bank) and settled bank height. Likely proppant length can be considered propped length when compared to the DFN model and effective length when compared to the PHM and RTA. The authors find the methodology very useful to identify and bound solutions such as the number of contributing clusters, effective fracture half-length, propped fracture height and permeability. From this analysis (Figure 15), the permeability, number of clusters, propped fracture height and propped half-length is most likely 386 nD, 78, 130 ft and 358 ft respectively. The permeability ties well to core values, the cluster efficiency is similar to APJA and OFT/CFT analysis and the fracture geometry parameters correlate well to the DFN modeling, PHA, and APJA.

Figure 17 shows the forecast of cumulative 10 year EUR performance with different job designs for Wells A-F. It is an important part of the completion optimization process to understand the resulting trends in production performance, with the changes in fracture characteristics, based on the job design and number of contributing clusters. The results indicate that larger job designs result in increased production as supported by field data. This is also seen in Figure 18, the Average Productivity Ratio, that larger jobs increase productivity.
