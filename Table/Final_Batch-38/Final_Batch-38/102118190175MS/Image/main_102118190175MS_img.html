The Southern Upper Captain Sandstone (SUCS) reservoir lies approximately 1km South-East of the Captain Well Protection Platform (WPP), in Figure 1.

The SUCS can easily be differentiated from the underlying LCS by a slight lower gamma ray profile (lower clay content), higher resistivity and different heavy mineral families. The two reservoirs are either separated by the debrites deposits and/or the Mid Captain Shale (Figure 2). Production data (e.g pressure and PLT information) shows that LCS and SUCS reservoirs have limited communication.

As a check on decline curve results, the theory developed by Koval (1963) was modified for use in immiscible floods. In 1963 Koval proposed a measure of heterogeneity for unstable miscible displacements; however, if the flow is segregated, the theory can be applied to immiscible displacements as well. There is some evidence from recent logging that flow in Captain can be treated as segregated. Figure 13 below shows water saturation along the length for an injection well in the Upper Captain Sand; one can see the saturation profile is essentially binary; oil is either swept or unswept with displacing fluid contact, with a very small transition in between. This field data supports that using segregated flow conditions is reasonable.

Polymer has been injected continuously as plotted schematically in Figure 15. We estimate polymer flood swept volume by treating the produced polymer history as a continuous tracer and applying an analytic (complementary error function) solution to 1-D transport. This 1-D solution is a reasonable assumption because the wells are horizontal with uniform injection/production (observed in PLT data) and geologic studies of the SUCS reservoir describe it is very homogeneous and able to be represented with a low Lorenz coefficient (Lc) = 0.37. The C-D equation assumes 1-D flow with a single source and sink, and full recovery of the injected fluids. There is an abundance of evidence there is flow from outside the pilot zone. For example, for the majority of the polymer flood the C43 injection / C47 production was relatively balanced; however, there is a substantial departure from this in Q2 2013 (Figure 16). This dilution effect must be captured to estimate swept volume and accurately determine oil production from the pilot.

If VRR is not maintained at unity, it is clear all produced fluids cannot be from C43. As a first pass at accounting for the dilution effect, we multiply the polymer concentration obtained from Equation 4 by a 7-day averaged VRR. The resulting swept volume is 7.6 MMSTB and represents the volume of the reservoir contacted by the injected polymer, or Vp*(1-So). Taking So at 0.26 (the tangent to the polymer-oil P50 fractional flow curve), and accounting for the polymer inaccessible pore volume and adsorption (‘D4’, -0.05 PV) gives a total swept pore volume of 9.8 MMSTB or 4.7 MMSTB incremental to the WF.

Therefore, the pilot oil production is calculated as qo*VRR where qo is the C47 oil rate. This implies the water cut is the same in either production stream. Given the maturity of the flood (>1 PV injected), this assumption is considered acceptable.
