The Williston basin, one of the major resources of oil and gas in the United States, covers more than 300,000 square miles in area and includes Saskatchewan and Manitoba in Canada and Montana and South Dakota in US (Fig. 1). The Bakken formation and Three Forks formation, located in the subsurface of the Williston basin, are the two the main produced formations. The Bakken formation comprises three members: the upper shale, the middle dolomite, and the lower shale.

The maximum thickness of the Bakken formation is about 140 ft, whereas for Three Forks formation, maximum thickness is about 270 ft (Tran et al. 2011). The increased activity since 2007 is the result of advances in horizontal drilling and multistage hydraulic fracturing. Due to the downturn of the oil industry, the total rig count dropped to about 25 in May 2016, as shown in Fig. 2.

The Bakken petroleum system lies within the oil window of the vast Williston Basin. As of today, numerous operators are actively developing the Bakken and Three Forks formations, pursuing a variety of drilling and completion strategies. Fig. 3 shows the production history of Bakken wells up to 2013. Before 2000, most Bakken wells had marginal economic success partially due to extremely low matrix permeability (0.0001 to 0.01 md) as well as failed attempts to exploit many localized natural factures with a vertical well.

The Three Forks formation is another important part of the Bakken petroleum system. Source rock maturity is considered a primary control on the extent of Three Forks production across the basin (Sonnenberg 2015). Poor production exists outside the maturity boundary. The level of source rock maturity can be determined via source rock analysis or resistivity of shales. Fig. 6 illustrates areas of source rock maturity based on resistivity measurements in the Upper and Lower Bakken shales. Three Forks production appears to coincide with areas of high resistivity, which suggests good source rock quality.

Lateral length and completion strategy are two other parameters that the operators have experimented with. Fig. 8a shows that in the Bakken, lateral length over the past decade has ranged from 1,000 to more than 10,000 ft. As longer laterals are drilled in the Bakken, operators have started to shift from an openhole completion using ball-activated sliding sleeves (OHSS) to cemented-casing plug-and-perforation (CCPP) demonstrated by Fig. 8b. This is mainly driven by the need for greater flexibility in increasingly longer laterals with clusters close together, and therefore greater wellbore stability must be considered (Lu et al. 2018a).

Using nodal analysis, natural flow, ESP, and JP are modeled. It is important to consider that during production, inflow and outflow, the main components of nodal analysis, change over time. Inflow changes in the reservoir pressure and productivity index occur over time, and those changes are fed to the model using the generated LUT. In the same way, outflow is governed by the type of artificial lift system installed, changes in PVT properties, and tubinghead pressure. This case study considers constant water cut and GOR, making outflow fully governed by the specific artificial lift system and tubinghead pressure.
