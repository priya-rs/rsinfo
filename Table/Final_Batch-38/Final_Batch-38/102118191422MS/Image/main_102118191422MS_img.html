As an example, when the operational practices of Eagle Ford operators in La Salle County are compared, their practices are widely varying. Fig. 1 shows pounds of proppant pumped per gallon of fracturing fluid (lbm/gal) by the various operators in the area and there is wide range of variations. There is hence a need for consistently understanding the current practices and reducing the inefficiency through reservoir modeling and characterization of the fracture geometry through advanced slurry transport models.

Just as the operators juggle the treatment design schedule, completion plan for number of stages, cluster spacing, amount of proppant per gallon of fluid, etc. to find the perfect recipe for improving production, proppant types pumped across different basins are also greatly varying (Fig. 2).

The test cases for sensitivity were run on a well completed in the Eagle Ford formation. Discovered only in 2008, the Eagle Ford is one of the youngest plays in the United States. It has prominent development across various counties by various operators. The US Energy Information Administration reported 1,387 thousand barrels/day production in June 2018 and 6,867 million cubic feet/day of gas per day. The productivity has been on decline since 2011; however, with the improving prices in the most recent times, the high drilling and completion activity in the region has picked up as drilled but uncompleted (DUC) wells are being completed and newer wells are being drilled. The legacy production change from the Eagle Ford has been shown in Fig. 3 (EIA).

Deposited in a low-energy anoxic marine shelf environment, the Upper Eagle Ford was created during a regressive phase and the Lower Eagle Ford was deposited during a transgressional period. The study area for analyzing the sensitivity is a high production gas well located in the southern half of the Eagle Ford basin. The gas to oil ratio (GOR) trend in the Eagle Ford play generally corresponds to the depth of the hydrocarbons being accessed. In the eastern half of the play, deeper wells (up to 15,000 feet) to the south have higher initial GORs, or a relatively greater share of natural gas, whereas the shallower wells in the north have lower initial GORs, or a relatively greater share of oil (Fig. 4) (Coleman 2015).

Following Velikanov et al. 2018. fracture geometry is calculated using pseudo 3D (P3D) model (Adachi et al. 2007) based on 1D moving grid along the fracture length. The width profiles for the grid cells are shown schematically for a fracture half-wing in Fig. 5.

The simulation results for the base case are shown in Fig. 6a and Fig. 7. Due to the low viscosity of slickwater, proppant particles settled rapidly and formed a bank at the bottom of the fracture before fracture closure. However, in real hydraulic fractures the settling may be slowed down due to the presence of laminations and wall roughness/morphology, which is currently not considered in the simulator.

Fig. 9 shows proppant distributions obtained for the additional designs. The case with sweeps has longer fracture half-length compared to the base case and more even proppant distribution along fracture length. The case with the straight ramp has similar fracture length and less proppant in fracture tip compared to the base case. In terms of production, the described cases gave similar production results, within 10% difference (Fig. 10) with a minor advantage of the case with sweeps. The simulation results presented in Fig. 22 confirm that highly viscous treatment fluids provide good transport of sands with different mesh size. The differences between production curves for different cases shown in Fig. 22d appear due to proppant conductivity properties.

For low-viscosity fluids, the sweeps lead to creation of alternating propped and unpropped zones like those obtained in channel fracturing technology, but at a larger scale (see Fig. 23). When these channels are open, they result in good production, as shown in Fig. 24.
