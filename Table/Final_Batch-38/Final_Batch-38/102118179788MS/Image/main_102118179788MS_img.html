In this paper we analyze one of three sequential SWCTTs, by means of reservoir simulation and assisted history matching. The SWCTTs were performed on a selected well in a mature on-shore field in West Africa (Figure 1). The EOR efficiency with low salinity water and surfactant injection was evaluated and compared to sea water flood. These tests and interpretation of the tests using an analytical approach was presented by Callegaro et al. (2014). Here we focus on the second one of these tests in our assisted history matching.

Water-oil relative permeability curves were set to typical curves measured in the field (Figure 2). For these simulations, relative permeability is not critical, as the tracer simulation starts after the well has been injecting large amounts of water. The near-well reservoir zone can therefore be assumed to be at residual oil saturation (i.e. no mobile oil exist). Oil saturations were therefore set to a constant value, which is one of the parameters in the matching process. The tracer transport thus occur in a setting with fixed oil and water saturations.

To optimize the simulations the number of gridblocks should be minimized without reducing the numerical accuracy. Numerical smearing can be a significant problem for SWCTT simulations (Huseby et al. 2012).In such cases it is important to use a grid that reduces smearing enough that it does not compromise the investigation of physical dispersion or mixing in the problem. As shown in Figure 3, a cylindrical grid with two blocks in the vertical direction, representing the two main layers (in addition to an inactive layer separating these two) was used.
