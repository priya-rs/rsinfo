Methanol is produced from synthesis gas, which can be obtained from steam reforming of natural gas or from gasification of coal or biomass. Today the most common feedstock of methanol for DME production is natural gas while methanol from coal is not economical because three to four times higher capital cost than natural gas to DME and vast CO2 treatment cost are required. Conversion of natural gas into DME comprises two processes: conversion of natural gas into methanol followed by conversion of methanol into DME (Figure 5).

The yield of natural gas to DME conversion is high. According to the Industry standard in methanol, 850 KTPA (Kilo Ton per Annum) of plant capacity, which require 90 MMSCFD of natural gas as feedstock, is most economical and such amount of methanol could produce 600 KTPA. DME production will require the supply of water for steam reforming and for utility (Figure 6).

Production of DME from natural gas may be carried out near the gas field and ship the DME to the major DME markets. Other option of DME production scheme is to convert natural gas into methanol near the gas field, ship the methanol to a location near to the DME major market where it is then converted into DME (Figure 7). This study will investigate the two DME production schemes.

Indonesia is endowed with significant amount of natural gas reserves. As of January 2011, the total of Indonesia gas reserves is 157 TSCF, comprises of 108 TSCF proven reserves and 49 TSCF potential reserves (probable/possible reserves). These reserves are spread in many different regions of the country (see Figure 8). It is also shown in the Figure the potency (resources) of coal bed methane (CBM), which is considered as new type of energy source in Indonesia.

Indonesian gas reserves are exploited to produce LNG for export, to satisfy domestic gas demand and for export as piped gas (to Singapore). Between year 2000 to 2011, Indonesian gas production varies between 6.9 BSCFD (2.5 TCF/year) to 8.2 BSCFD (3 TSCF/year). Figure 9 shows the development of gas utilization during 2000 – 2011 for different gas consumers. One can see from the figure that LNG export accounts for nearly one-half of the national gas utilization in 2011.

Gas price setting basically is resulted from “business-to-business” negotiation between producers and gas buyer. However, the formal/official decision is in the government hand.
