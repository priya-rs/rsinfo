Case one. The sandstone reservoir in M Field consists of three flow units with average permeability varying from 50 mD to > 500 mD. Well A had been producing solely from the low-permeability top pay sand. By comparison, Well B produced from all the three pay sands (Fig. 3).

The rapid production decline in Well A was the response of pressure depletion. Based on the above analysis, the water injector was recompleted adopting a single-string adjustable eccentric water distributor. The technology enhanced water injection to the top flow unit while constraining injection to the lower two flow units. Both Well A and Well B responded to the injection adjustment immediately with a significant rise in production rates (Fig. 4). By May 2006, production rates of Well A and Well B had reached 566 BOPD and 725 BOPD, respectively, a significant increase from the pre-zonal water injection rates of 116 BOPD and 471 BOPD.

Case two. An inverted nine-spot well pattern produced oil from a multi-layer, low-medium permeability sandstone reservoir (Fig. 5). The heterogeneous reservoir is characterized by low permeability in the upper interval and medium permeability in the lower interval. Commingled water injection was performed during early stage pressure maintenance. Well B contained a STOIIP of 1.791 MMBO. With the water cut increasing from 35% to 60%, the production rate dropped sharply from 410 BOPD to 194 BOPD within one year (Fig. 6a).

A water injection optimization campaign was launched in 2009. The injection-production pattern was modified from line cutting to peripheral line injection, consisting of four producers and five injectors (Fig.7). The injection-production well spacing increased from 250 m to 450 m.

The new well pattern led to change in water injection direction and helped expand sweep volume to further recover inter-well remaining oil and attic oil. The water injection optimization activity resulted in a significant increase in production rate from 110 BOPD to 194 BOPD and a slight drop of water cut from 97.9% to 96.7% (Fig. 8). Average production rate per well increased from 28 BOPD to 49 BOPD. Ultimate recovery factor increased by 10% from 58% to 68% (Wang, 2011).

In the example shown in Fig.9, the distributary channel reservoir presents a great lateral permeability contrast between channel and inter-channel sands. Injected water mainly swept oil along high-permeability channel sand, causing the oil well in channel sandbody to have high water cut and low oil production. The injected water had been ineffectively cycling from injector to producer. Instead, the producers in the interchannel sand were bypassed. Water plugging workovers were implemented in Well Z4-7 and Well Z6-6 to optimize sweep efficiency.

The multi-layer, heterogeneous SII9-10 reservoir comprises seven sub-units. Among them, the two upper sub-units have low permeability of 160 mD and 290 mD, respectively; while the lower sub-units possess high permeability ranging from 1400 mD to 3500 mD (Fig. 10a). At the beginning of the study, the reservoir had recovered 32.2% STOIIP, was producing at 246 BOPD from 16 active producers with a water cut of 96.4%. Its ER was estimated as 36.3%. The significant permeability contrast between SII9 and SII10 results in substantial difference in recovery between the two reservoirs, which is 38.4% of STOIIP for the SII10 reservoir and 22.6% of STOIIP for the SII9 reservoir.

In SII10 reservoir unit, the injection-production spacings slightly expanded from 300/260 m to 380/300 m (Fig. 10c); while the voidage replacement ratio increased from 0.57 to 1.3. Production performance showed that within 10 months since the injection-production unit subdivision, the SII10 unit had its pressure restored from 1958 psi to 2161 psi, production rate rose from 187 BOPD to 223 BOPD and water cut reduced from 97% to 96.7% (Fig. 11). The SII10 was expected to achieve an incremental recovery by 9% STOIIP from 40% to 49% (Wang, 2011).

Cyclic water injection was adopted at a scheme of six-month half period. Within each individual cycle, water was injected into oil pay groups of SII, PIUpper in the first-half period with that of SIII, PILower suspended. Conversely, in the second-half period, water was injected into oil pay groups of SIII, PILower, with that of SII, PIUpper suspended (Fig. 12).

The pilot cyclic water injection successfully alleviated oil rate decline and water cut rise (Fig. 13). Water cut dropped from 90.4% to a minimum of 88.4% and oil rate increased from 2111 BOPD to a maximum of 2160 BOPD in cyclic period one. Compared with the forecasted performances of conventional waterflooding through reservoir simulation, oil rate by cyclic water injection is generally higher than that by conventional water flooding. Estimated EURs by Type A water-drive performance curve indicate that cyclic water injection increased recoverable reserves by 1.84% from 51.7 MMBO to 52.7 MMBO.
