The Kita-Akita oil field is one of the satellite fields of main Yabase Oil Fields (Hirai et al., 1990), which developed from the early 1900s and has produced 5.8 million bbls of oil and 1.4 billion scf of natural gas cumulatively (equivalent to 36.5 million bbls of oil and 51.8 billion scf of natural gas), located in Akita region, Northern Japan (Fig. 1). The satellite field (approximately 3 × 3 km2) produced from 1957 to 1973 and all produced wells were plugged and abandoned. However, the past study shows the recovery rate is approximately a few %. A task force was organized and a series of programs including the data acquisition (e.g., Fig. 2A and 2B) and the feasibility study were conducted to test the sub-commercial field redevelopment.

To select a best completion and stimulation candidate from multiple methods, we constructed a score matrix as shown in Fig. 4, where vertical and lateral axes show the type of well and each type of completion and well stimulation methods, respectively. Each box corresponding to the type of well and type of completion and stimulation has sub-boxes divided into four categories, namely, maturity, applicability, productivity, and economics. Each sub-box will be filled up with scores (1 through 4, for example).

Fig. 5 illustrates the workflow. Once the resevoir model is calibrated with production history in static and dynamic modeling, geomechanical modeling was conducted to create a 3D MEM (Mechanical Earth Model) to provide stress field information for wellbore stability analysis and hydraulic fracturing simulation. The 3D MEM was calibrated with a 1D MEM of the pilot-hole, which was constructed from log-based geomechanical data and tied to a set of laboratory rock mechanics tests, wellbore images and drilling events. The calibrated stress field is utilized in our stimulation and completion screening study afterwards.

The reservoir model is formed as the sector model (1.5 km to 2km) focusing the Kita-Akita channel belt. The grid size was set up in 20 m × 20 m with proportional 60 layers on approximately the 40 m reservoir interval. Three rock types in the formation are formed; sand and fine sand as reservoir, and mud as non-reservoir. To address the uncertainty of highly heterogeneous sandstone distribution, the Sequential Indicator Simulation (SIS) and the Multiple-Point Statistics (MPS) simulation (e.g., Strebelle et al., 2002) methods were applied (Fig. 6A). Permeabilities of sand and fine sand facies were calibrated with well data showing two orders of magnitude difference between them (Fig. 7).

Permeabilities of sand and fine sand facies were calibrated with well data showing two orders of magnitude difference between them (Fig. 7).

Multiple realizations formed by each MPS scienario were screened with history matching of the past production data of the Kita-Akita field (Fig. 8) and the single realization was chosen for the each MPS model (Figs 6&7). The SIS model exhibits highly discontinuous distribution (Fig. 6A, right). As a result, a higher reservoir Net-to-Gross ratio was needed to match the historical production data, relative to the actual well data. Therefore, the SIS model was considered to be inconsistent. The history-matched realizations from the two MPS cases demonstrated distinctive reservoir distributions. The channel and the sheet models from the MPS show vertically and laterally connected distributions, respectively (Fig. 7).

The subsurface regions surrounding the reservoir is of 6 × 4.5 × 4.5 km, forming the computational volume included in the 3DMEM and shown in Fig. 9. Based on a seismic interpretation of a set of 2D lines, two faults and the main stratigraphic seismic horizon from a ground level below the reservoir of the target were incorporated. Based on previous studies mapping the stress field at a regional scale, the long side of the model aligned with the direction of maximum tectonic strain. The direction is approximately N135°E. From outcrop analogs and initial 1D geomechanics modeling results, fracture height containment was a concern from the apparent lack of vertical stress barriers.

The input properties such as Young's modulus, Poisson's ratio, bulk density, Biot's coefficient, unconfined compressive strength, and friction angle were populated along layers using log-derived properties in a pilot hole. The geomechanical properties in the reservoir region were distributed by allowing for dependency to reservoir model facies (sand, fine sand, and shale). The correlation between facies and geomechanical properties were estimated from extensive laboratory tests on the pilot hole. Geomechanical properties along the pilot hole are shown in Fig. 10. Curves in pink and bars in colors indicate the values of log and model, respectively.

The magnitude of the overburden stress, the maximum and minimum horizontal stresses are shown in Fig. 11. Regarding comparison of the magnitude of the stress, the stress regime changes from the reverse faulting regime to strike-slip faulting regime with depth. In the reservoir section, the strike-slip faulting regime was interpreted. The contrast in the magnitude of Shmin between the reservoir section and the intervals above and below the reservoir is not significant. Considering the AOI shown in Fig. 15 (left), we attempted a brute force optimization by sampling all possible infill well locations in Fig. 16 (left). To avoid drilling collision with plugged and abandoned producers, 50 meter radius of the investigation was avoided to set up a new well. All sandstone connected zone of the created infill wells are then perforated and reservoir simulation was performed. After all well production profiles of each infill well location are sorted, the best infill location is selected shown in Fig. 16 (right).

Fig. 17 shows the mud weight window in the reservoir section for wells to NE-SW (left) and NW-SE (right) directions. Here, the lower limit of the window is the higher value between pore pressure and mud weight to prevent any borehole breakout to minimize the risk due to wellbore instability from drilling a well to landing completion assembly. As shown in the figure, the mud weight window of a well along the NE-SW direction is narrower than one of a well along the NW-SE direction. Since there are some sporadic intervals in which more than 1.2 SG mud weight is required as the lower limit, the mud weight window in the NE-SW direction is narrower, with range from 1.37 to 1.47 SG.

Considering the Kita-Akita thin layered reservoir, two types of deviated wells were then defined and compared. One is highly deviated well which enable to penetrate each layer efficiently. Another is fully horizontal penetration along with a specific layer target. Fig. 18 illustrates two lateral placement and reservoir contact. It is expected that highly deviated well (Fig 17, left) which is designed to penetrate all sand connected layers will have better performance. Similarly, the other two laterals are also drilled at a deeper depth. The mainbore is cased by 7" casing thus a reservoir fluid is produced only from the four horizontal laterals. Fig. 20 shows a FEM mesh created for the radial drilling completion. The model is made of 10 node tetrahedral elements with approximately 3 million nodes.

In this study, it is also assumed that the reservoir permeability is 10 md (isotropic) and the fluid viscosity and formation volume factor are 1 cp and 1.0 rb/stb, respectively. A steady-state flow simulation result is presented in Fig. 21. The pressure contour plot clearly shows that the four laterals effectively drains the reservoir fluid deep from the formation. In this case, the productivity index for the radial drilling well is estimated to be 3.87 stbpd/psi.

Fig. 22 shows skin factors for radial drilling completions calculated by the Karakas and Tariq's perforation semianalytical skin model (Karakas and Tariq, 1991). For comparison, a skin value estimated by the 3D FEM model is also plotted for the effective lateral spacing, hlat of 32.8 ft (10 m). As shown in the figure, both analytical and numerical models match very well. This comparison ensures that radial drilling skin factors can be accurately predicted by the Karakas and Tariq's model. It should be noted that in the above calculations, the horizontal laterals are assumed to have infinite conductivity (i.e., no frictional pressure drop inside the lateral).

In this study, three RD settings are considered. Fig. 23 shows the patterns where radial drilling completions are designed in a vertical well identified in infill well placement section. Each RD lateral is 100 m long with the 2"-OD. To be conservative, these radial drilling cases are completed without perforation in non-RD penetrated sand layers thus a production flow is only from radial laterals. Four laterals of single RD system is placed in a best layer, whereas 45 degree angular and dual RD system are expected to contact more sandface.

The technology comprises MST subs that houses three small-diameter (hole size OD: 013 mm; bit size OD: 8 mm), high-strength tubes called needles up to approximately 35 ft (10 m). Each needle has a drill bit on the formation end and a turbine on the casing end as shown in Fig. 25. The laterals are drilled simultaneously form the openhole liner in a relatively short pumping operation (Torvund et al, 2016). If a formation is limestone, acid jetting is mostly used and four needles are allowed in each sub.

Following the previous radial drilling performance evaluation, a similar FEM modeling study was done for fishbone drilling. For the fishbone case, it is assumed that the 5.5"-diameter fishbone liner system is installed in a 8.5"-diameter horizontal borehole. In the 80 ft lateral section, two subs are installed thus sub spacing assumes to be 40 ft. Each fishbone sub has three 32.8'-length needles with 120 deg. phasing. The effective diameter of the holes created by the drilling from the needle end is assumed to be 0.512". The fishbone subs are oriented in an alternative way. Fig. 26 shows a FEM mesh created for the fishbone liner completion (approximately 0.5 million nodes) used in this study.

Also we assume no frictional pressure drop in the needle annuli between the drilled hole and the fishbone needle. The drainage pattern estimated from the FEM model is presented in Fig. 27. The productivity index for the fishbone well is estimated to be 0.55 stbpd/psi per 80 ft horizontal well length (i.e., 2 subs). If the filter cake of the mainbore is completely cleaned up, it can contribute to inflow form the reservoir. In this case, the productivity index estimated from the simulation model becomes 0.59 stbpd/psi per 80 ft horizontal well length.

Fig. 28 shows skin factors for fishbone completions calculated by the Karakas and Tariq's model. For comparison, skin values estimated by the 3D FEM model is also plotted for 120 deg phasing cases. The numerical model calculates skin values of −1.7 and −2.4 for needle only and needle + mainbore cases, respectively. These skin values are slightly better than one predicted by the Karakas and Tariq's model (S =−1.44), especially for the needle + mainbore case. The additional inflow from the mainbore causes higher well productivity. But for practical use, the Karakas and Tariq's skin factor model can be used for rough skin estimations of fishbone completions.

Using that information, we firstly looked back the past treated hydraulic fracturing job in created 1D MEM based on goemchanical properties taken by the pilot-hole program. Fig. 31 illustrates fracture propagation result after closure. Since the job size in 1960s were much smaller proppant volume than today (Table 4), dimensionless conductivity was calculated almost zero. It is expected that a larger volume fracturing jobs would enhance well performance under the current sitation.

For a sand, dehaydration impact is larger due to higher permeability thus fracture tip (approximately 0.5 inch) become dry zone. In this area, fracuturing polymer is clogged and the pressure is not propagated. For a shale, due to lower permeability, fluid loss is small enough, thereore, dehydration zone is smaller (0.1 to 0.2 inch). In our sensitivity trial in 1D MEM, increasing the fracture toughness results in less fracture length and higher fracture height as shown in Fig 32.

Another unique setting in this reservoir is that 3D MEM initialization was performed after the reservoir pressure depleted from past producers, which may cause depletion induced stress in the reservoir as shown in Fig. 33. It may contribute to build enough barriers to contain a hydraulic fracture propagation. The result of the 3D MEM initialization, however, Shmin change was confirmed only 10 to 15 bar. Therefore, enough barrier was not generated from the depletion effect, therefore height growth issue should be considered for a fracturing design.

As seen in Fig. 34, the propped height is more contained in the same layer depth in 1D MEM when compared to the same fracture simulation running on the 3D MEM. This is because the vertical stress stays the same at any horizontal location. The simulations also suggest opposite fracture extention directions between these two models. In the 3D MEM case, higher stress zones exist down dip of fracture, and this works as a barrier of the fracture propagation in that direction.

Two hyfraulic fracture simulation cases are defined in a vertical and a horizontal well. A single stage hydraulic fracture was designed in the vertical well. Aslo 5 stages, 100 m spacing fracturing was designed in the horizontal well oriented in Shmin direction for the comparison. The resultant hydraulic fracture propagation by the Planar 3D model in a 3D MEM is shown in Fig. 35.

Final screening summary is summarized in Fig. 39 and Table 5. Each maturity, applicability, productivity, and economics are normalized based on cased and perforated vertical well performance. In the study, multistage hydraulic fracturing treatment is still the most brute way to maximize a contact area and improve recovery, however, fracturing treatment cost worsen the economics.
