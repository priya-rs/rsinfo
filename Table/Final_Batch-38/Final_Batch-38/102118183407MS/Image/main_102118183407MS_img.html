In order to evaluate the possibility of reservoir description by single EOS model, the lateral and vertical variation of major reservoir fluid properties have been analyzed (figure 1,figure 2). No significant variation of bubble-point pressure, solution gas-oil ratio and reservoir fluid density were observed neither laterally (figure 2), nor vertically (figure 1). Based on mentioned above, one EOS fluid model could be used for description of the overall reservoir.

Weights of parameters from PVT experiments were varied on each regression step to get the best agreement of modeled outcomes with experimental results (figure 3). All major PVT properties are well predicted from final composition for the whole range of pressure: bubblepoint pressure, oil and gas densities, gas to oil ratio, oil and gas FVF.

Last step of EOS tuning was the oil viscosity tuning. CSP tuning was used and good agreement with experimental data for the whole range of pressures (under reservoir temperature) was achieved as shown on figure 4.

Phase diagram of Reservoir A fluid is shown on figure 5. Similar workflow has been applied for the composition from reservoir fluids sampled from Well-7 and Well-8. Not all QC have been passed by these compositions and that is why they have not been selected for the reservoir characterization.

In addition, variation of fluid properties with depth inside Reservoir A column has been simulated based on the derived EOS. No significant variation with depth supposed to be observed (figure 6). This prediction is in line with observations across the whole oil column from all wells (figure 1).

As it is shown on figure 7 (Conventional PVT analysis from Well-5 used as an example), the derived EOS could reproduce all PVT experiment results from other well with fairly good accuracy. This is clearly validating derived model for future reservoir characterization and use in numerical reservoir simulator.

Multireservoir model (figure 8b) was coupled with surface production and water/ gas injection networks (figure 8a). Surface network is solving together with reservoir grids on each step of simulation process. Surface pipelines, seprartors and other elements are represented in this network.

Relative volumes of gas injection in Reservoir A and Reservoir B in one of field development scenario are presented on figure 9. There is a clear dependency of oil production from Reservoir B and injected gas volumes. In 2028-2031 the oil production from Reservoir B is dropping due to the reduction of gas injection. In Reservoir A the main displacement mechanism is water injection, so the idea behind using WAG is stabilizing the injection front and dumping additional gas.

The WAG process in top layer of Reservoir A simulation model is shown on figure 10. On the figure 10a the difference in water saturation is presented from the start of WAG process to the end. Water saturation around injection wells is increasing with time, but due to the gravity effect the front is not reaching oil producers in the upper part of reservoir. Gas saturation evolution from the beginning of WAG injection to the end is shown on figure 10b. In contrast to water, injected gas is reaching oil producers in the top part of Reservoir A.

6 wells are using as WAG injectors (water and gas injectiton cycles) on 2 WHTs, and 9 wells – in case of 3 WHTs (figure 11). Base case is assuming only water injection in injectors (WAG reserves are calculated relative to water injection only case). Different durations of WAG cycle were investigated: 2 months, 1 year, 2 years. It was noticed, that the shorter the cycle duration, the better the shape of the flooding front and higher WAG effect.

Gas injection in 3 gas inejctiors located in the crestal part of Reservoir B in shown on figure 12. 10 oil producers are situated on the perimeter. The main displacement mechanism is moving oil from gas injectors to oil producers. At the end of field development, as it is shown on figure 12c, the gas front is reaching all oil producers. Gas saturation is plotted on top layer of Reservoir B.

Initial average reservoir pressure in Reservoir B is above MMP (figure 13a) and remains above it during few years of reservoir development. Assuming that reservoir pressure in the viscinity of gas injectors is higher, the duration of miscible flood could be even longer. Based on this, we can conclude, that the gas injection in Reservoir B could sufficiently impove recovery. Also targeting of reservoir pressure is needed during reservoir development process. Additional reserves associated with gas injection project could be evaluated as 15,5 MMSTB (figure 13b).
