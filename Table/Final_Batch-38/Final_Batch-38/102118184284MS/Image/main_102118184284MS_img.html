The main concern about pressure communication between stacked reservoirs is that it not only affects the management of these fields/reservoirs [Ozkaya 2007],[SayedAkram 2012] it may affect potential well recoveries. The field investigated for this study is one of the large fields in the Nigeria Delta region. It is comprised of two stacked sandstone reservoirs: Reservoir X1 and Reservoir Y1 having approximately same hydrocarbon contacts but separated by shale about 8-50ft thick (Fig 1). Reservoir Y1 is unproduced while Reservoir X1 has been on production for over 40 years. The reservoir properties are given in Table 1.

Fig 2 shows reservoir production history of Reservoir X1. Production peaked in June 2005 at 4339 BOPD and 0% BS&W. Cumulative production from this reservoir ~ 8.0 MMSTBO (December 2012), representing 24% of the STOOIP. The average initial pressure of the reservoir X was 3675 psig. Current reservoir pressure is 3202 psig; representing 13% decline after 38 years and after producing over 8 MMBO. A strong water drive is indicated by this lack of pressure decline, rise of the OWC and the lack of movement in the GOC. Fig X shows the historical drive strength of reservoir X1.

The Y1 reservoir is characterized by extensive good quality sands in the upper section with reasonable degree of consolidation relative to the shallow Meji sands. The upper section is interpreted to be made up of laterally extensive shore face sands while the lower section is likely made of a mix of distal lower shore face/offshore transition zone facies; both sections being of the Miocene-Pliocene age. The average reservoir properties such as net-to-gross and porosity within the upper member are very favorable. Fig 5 shows the top structure map of the Y1 sand.

Similar to the underlying Y1 reservoir, the X1 reservoir also has a good sand development that is extensive across the field. The sand within the hydrocarbon column of the reservoir is a clean middle- to upper shoreface delta front deposit, although there are some patches of silty lower shoreface to shelf sediments in the reservoir to the west and south. The average reservoir properties are also very favorable. The X1 sand is Middle Miocene in age. Fig 6 shows the top structure map of the X 1 sand.
