La Cira Infantas (LCI) field is a mature oil field located in the Middle Magdalena Valley Basin in northern Colombia as seen in Figure 1. LCI field was discovered in 1918 and has been operating for 100 years. Primary depletion began in 1918 until 1959 when the first water flooding process started.

In 2005, Occidental de Colombia and Ecopetrol initiated a joint venture of a new redeveloped water flooding process for LCI field. The redevelopment is currently ongoing for 13 years. The water flooding project was implemented in the C-sands of the Mugrosa formation. The Mugrosa formation, as seen in Figure 2, is the main producer of hydrocarbon in La Cira Infantas field and is defined as an interbedding of sandstones, shales and siltstones.

Prior to the redevelopment, the first approach to the water flooding process spanned for 46 years. The two phases of the water flooding process differentiate from each other by distinct recovery approaches and completion methods. As depicted in Figure 3, the first water flooding process was based on line drive arrangements, 30-acre to 40-acre patterns, 5-spot to 7-spot inverted patterns and single string completions for injector wells. Since the redevelopment, the field has expanded to 400 patterns and 1,000 active producer wells, 95% of which are under water flooding process.

For a water flood process, it is essential to monitor the behavior of the recovery factor after the water injection begins. For surveillance purposes, it is preferred to graph the recovery factor of the secondary process vs. flood maturity (removing the primary recovery factor if present in the field) as seen in Figure 5.

The graph of Sec RF vs. DWI (Figure 6) is generally the first diagnostic plot used in the dimensionless methodology in order to quickly rank the performance of the flood process. The graph is complemented with other dimensionless variables represented as the patterns size, pattern color or pattern shape to assist in the overall performance diagnosis.

The RF vs. DWI plot is one of the most basic plots but also one of the most important in the surveillance process. It allows the comparison of individual pattern's current recovery factor versus the expected ideal recovery from a prototype at a specific maturity.

The second plot concentrates on the evaluation of the current flood efficiency. It is important to keep in mind that the efficiency is expected to change as the flood process matures. As indicated by the continuous line in Figure 8 (prototype reference line), the utilization has a maximum value at the beginning of the injection process during the fill up process. When water displaces oil towards producer wells, the utilization decreases rapidly reaching a minimum value right before water breakthrough. After water breakthrough, water cut will gradually increase, likewise increasing the utilization, and the water flood efficiency will decrease.

The Utilization vs. Injection Throughput plot combines the current water food efficiency and the current speed of the flood process. Ideally, the target is to have an excellent performance represented by low utilization with the highest injection rate possible. The position of each pattern in this plot represents an opportunity to increase injection rate when the flood is performing efficiently or on the other hand an opportunity to improve pattern efficiency by controlling injection rate where needed.

The purpose of the current injection throughput vs. injection throughput of 12 months ago is the identification of injectivity rate changes and its effect on the pattern's flood efficiency. In this case, the continuous black line corresponds to the "no change" condition. Any symbol located above the reference line corresponds to patterns where processing rate increased whereas patterns below the reference line corresponds to patterns were injection rate decreased.

The IWR vs. Water Utilization plot shows the relationship of current IWR with patterns utilization. Similarly to what is observed on Plot # 3 or Figure 9, the relationship between IWR values and utilization tends to be linear and direct. The effect of high IWR values, or overbalanced condition, is more significant on patterns efficiency compared to having underbalanced conditions or IWR < 1. Very high IWR values are commonly observed when injection is lost outside of the pattern, which refers to a portion of the injected volume not being effective, increasing utilization.

The OPR vs. WPR plot is very important to identify the sectors of the field where the process has been efficient or inefficient. The plot uses the variables expressed in Equation 9 and Equation 10 and it is subdivided in four quadrants, by two lines when OPR and WPR are equal to 1.

If injection profile data is available, vertical injection distribution over time can be constructed. This information is vital in multilayer reservoirs under a water flooding process. Besides monitoring vertical efficiency, the vertical injection distribution allows to estimate water flood maturity or DWI for each zone, and therefore, allows the identification of swept, poorly swept or non-swept zones.

The Mallet A Unit is located in the Slaughter Field in the Hockley County, West Texas (Figure 14). The field has been water flooded since 1960's in the San Andres formation.

The field has experienced several development campaigns and currently it has a set of patterns that range from 20-acre 5-spot normal patterns to 40-acre chicken wire patterns as seen in Figure 15.

Figure 16 shows the difference in recovery efficiency of the two injection processes, comparing the recovery factor vs. cumulative water injection curve. Patterns with selective injection only (green lines) have consistently better recovery slopes than patterns with no selectivity (blue lines) which correspond to a previous injection process.

The same plot also shows that the recovery slope in the blue lines change at certain point of maturity as seen in Figure 17. When the same patterns are plotted versus time (instead of DWI), the slope changes occur almost at the same value in the X-axis, when selective string was incorporated.

With this information, injection rate, DWI and injection throughput are all calculated per unit layer for each injector and pattern of the LCI field. Figure 18 shows an example of the Bar Charts created with injection data at the unit level. In this case, the DWI value observed in the upper sands indicates high maturity which also implies very high water saturation. On the contrary, the lowermost sands have low DWI which implies the necessity of increasing injection in order to accelerate oil recovery.

When combining the variables of DWI vs. Injection Throughput at the unit level, injection increase or injection control requirements can be rapidly identified. In Figure 19, the background map represents DWI, where the green colors indicate low values of DWI and the blue colors represent high values of DWI. The injection throughput is superposed as bubbles and the color is associated with the magnitude of the processing rate.

Figure 20 shows an example of the compiled cumulative volumes injected for one selected area of LCI field. Light blue bubbles represent current active injectors and dark blue bubbles represent inactive injector wells from the previous water flood process. The size of the bubbles represent the cumulative water injected. The sand units with highest cumulative volumes of water injection may represent swept zones; sand units with high current water saturation and low remanent reserves.

A reserves evaluation by unit layer, considering a balance of the previous injected fluids and current injected fluids as well as the produced fluids, is necessary in order to define the volume of remanent reserves. Figure 21 portrays an example of the property maps with cumulative injected volumes. The warmer colors represent the best quality rock whereas the colder colors represent less quality rock. It can be noted than in the black contours regions there are high cumulative volumes of injected water with corresponding high quality rock.

This will minimize the contribution of high water saturation layers and thus permit the production contribution from lower water saturation sand units. On the other hand, if there are low cumulative water injected volumes sector, it is recommended to increase water injection rate in order to recover expected production volumes and improve flood performance. LCI field has patterns with low vertical efficiency and patterns with low areal efficiency contributing to a low volumetric efficiency and ultimately a lower than expected recovery factor. The low areal efficiency is represented by the presence of thief zones in specific sand units affecting only one producer from a pattern area as seen in Figure 24. The channeling phenomenon hinders the drainage of a larger volume area in the pattern and therefore remanent reserves recovery is limited and delayed over time.

If a pattern is overbalanced, it is important to identify opportunities that will increase the current extraction in producer wells. As shown in Figure 26, the area on the left represents current water cut for producer wells and the color of the injector well represents current utilization for that pattern. The area on the right represents current dynamic fluid levels on the producer wells. High dynamic fluid levels in producer wells and corresponding low utilization values in a pattern represent an opportunity to increase fluid extraction.

In patterns where workover jobs have been executed in producer wells it is important to validate that there is injection support in the corresponding sand units in order to see the expected response of incremental oil production. This variable allows identifying opportunities to increase injection rate and to improve the injection support in these new sands. A status map of the executed jobs in LCI field is used to identify patterns with an opportunity to increase injection rate and injectors with an opportunity to perforate new intervals.
