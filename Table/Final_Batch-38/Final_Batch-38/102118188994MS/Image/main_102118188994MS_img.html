The Tengiz Field is a unique, super-giant, carbonate oilfield of Devonian to Carboniferous age located in the Pricaspian Basin of the Republic of Kazakhstan along the northeastern margin of the Caspian Sea (Fig. 1). The field was discovered in 1979 and production commenced in 1991. Since 1993, the field has been operated by TCO, a joint venture company comprising of Chevron, ExxonMobil Kazakhstan Ventures Inc., KazMunaiGas and LukArco.

The Tengiz field has two extensive and complex gathering systems feeding two separate plants. The Base system feeds the KTL plant (original facility) and the new generation gathering system (NGGS) feeds the second generation plant (SGP) which was started up in 2007. Each system has its own network of flowlines, gathering lines, manifolds, and trunklines with some flexibility to transfer production between the two systems. Details of the Base and NGGS systems have been accurately modeled in IPM model. A screenshot of the short term IPM surface network is provided in Fig. 2.

When the choke gets plugged, the effective area for flow is reduced and results in a higher dP across the choke. The new choke curve will deviate from the original choke curve when there is choke plugging. The change can be identified by engineer at the early stages of choke plugging. Fig. 7 illustrates a choke curve for all wells with CC-40 chokes.
