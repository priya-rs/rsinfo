Flow control devices (FCDs) and isolation devices (packers) are commonly installed downhole to assist with improvement of steam conformance along the horizontal section in both SAGD and mobile heavy oil applications. A typical tubing-deployed FCD configuration in a horizontal steam injector is depicted in Fig.1 (a). However, the use of FCDs and packers does not necessarily assure acceptable steam conformance. Steam conformance and flow profiles in horizontal steam injectors can be affected by many factors which include: reservoir rock and fluid properties, well geometry, completion architecture, heat transfer mechanisms, flow regimes, and completion damages.

An overview of the steam injection flow profiling methodology is provided in Fig. 3 as a flowchart. This six step method results in computing the injected steam flow profiles for each wellbore segment associated with a material balance region. The computational flow profiling algorithms are based on a multi-physics approach that can be applied to broad variety of flowing conditions, surveillance system configurations, well architectures, and conformance control designs as discussed in the previous section. While the examples in this paper are focused on tubing-deployed fiber optics, the methodology and algorithms can be applied to other deployment configurations such as liner or instrument tube fiber optics with minor modifications.

In this research, an FCD on 3-1/2 inch tubing was installed inside 7 inch casing in the HSITF. A capillary tubing bundle with DTS and DAS was clamped with a helical geometry to the 3-1/2 inch tubing in the annulus. This installation had identical geometries to the tubing and FCDs installed in the two experimental injectors. Fig. 5(a) shows a schematic of the HSITF and Fig. 5(b) depicts the test section of the HSITF with the fiber optic installation. A three-level, design of experiment (DOE) test matrix was executed to characterize DTS and DAS responses for steam flow behavior for common operating conditions. The DOE test matrix included testing at various steam qualities (e.g., 40%, 60% and 80%), different tubing pressures (e.g., 400, 500, and 600 psi), different steam flow rates ranging from 1000 to 1800 BSPD-CWE, and flow directions both cocurrent and counter-current in the annulus.

While the HSITF results are useful for characterizing DTS and DAS responses, this procedural step is optional. The results of the HSITF steam flow characterization are used for flow diagnostic interpretation and pattern recognition of steam flow with DTS and DAS. The DOE flow characterization is very useful for distinguishing cocurrent and counter-current flow in horizontal steam injectors based on the underlying patterns of the DTS and DAS data as illustrated in Fig 6.

Assuming the warm-back or cool-down process is dominated by heat conduction, a numerical heat conduction model (cylindrical axisymmetric geometry) can estimate the rate of cooling or warming based on the initial temperature differential between the wellbore fluid and the surrounding rock temperature. The higher temperature differential during the cool-down period and the lower temperature differential during the warm-back period indicate less thermal mass storage in the rock and slower temperature change. Fig. 8 provides an example of cool-down and warm-back model results for various temperature differentials between the wellbore and surrounding reservoir.

Fig. 9 shows the baseline temperature profiles at 0, 5 and 6 days after installation of the fiber on the tubing of well "A". Before putting the well on injection, the quenching water warms back slowly due to the heat transfer from surrounding fluid and rock and reaches a stable temperature after 6 days. Fig. 9 shows that toe region is warmer than the rest of the well indicating that steam has been injected predominately to the toe.

During steam injection start up, transient flow and heat transfer behavior can be observed in the DTS and DAS surveillance data. At the beginning of the injection, cold liquid flows in the surface lines initially and creates a fluid temperature differential effect as a result steam displacing cold water in the surface lines. Fig. 10(a) shows the temperature ramp-up at 5 different points above the packer and Fig. 10(b) shows the temperature ramp-up at 5 different points below the packer in the horizontal section during start up. The temperature ramp-up above the packer has a concave shape characteristic of conduction dominated heat transfer.

This confirms again that more steam had been injected into the toe of well “A” because it maintains a higher relative temperature. Well "A" has been shut down on several occasions during injection while DTS data was acquired. In Fig. 14(a) a semi-log plot of early transient shut-in temperature data at several points along the wellbore from heel to the toe are depicted. The temperature data in region (X) has been convectively affected with rapid fluid movement once the injection was stopped. Region (Y) is a conduction dominated period when the wellbore fluid cools-down slowly due to heat exchange with the surrounding formation.

In Fig. 15(a) the shut-in temperature profiles in the lateral section of well "A" after ~10 days are provided. In Fig. 15(b) the temperature decline rates for five wellbore segments are provided. Convective fluid movement in the wellbore is assumed to diminish after a few days and then the temperature declines linearly due to conduction. The inverse of the temperature decline rate is an indication of the thermal mass around the wellbore. Using the slope of the temperature decline rate in late transient period, the steam injection flow profile along the wellbore can be estimated.

Steam flow releases acoustic energy through different components of the completion architecture (i.e. pipe, nozzles, slots in the slotted liner). In Fig. 16 a spectrogram analysis of the acoustic surveillance data in the lateral section (2000 – 2880 ft) for 2 minute time windows is provided. Eight different frequency bands are summarized from 0.3 Hz up to 3 kHz. As can be seen in Fig. 16, acoustic energy with a wide frequency range and high amplitudes are generated at the discharge point for the first three FCDs. The FCD frequency is most distinctly observed in higher frequencies (band 8th) at ~3 kHz. Plotting the PSD of band eight shows that the last FCD near the toe has dissimilar performance to the first three FCDs.

Similar analysis for well "B" is shown in Fig. 17. In well "B" the majority of acoustic energy occurs in the heel region, indicating steam is injected more to the heel than toe region. The acoustic energy in band 3 also indicates that acoustic energy propagates preferentially upstream of the FCDs and toward the heel. In contrast to well "A", a sudden temperature drop around the FCDs does not occur in well "B", which indicates more liquid presence in this well and counter-current flow to heel direction.

Fig. 19(a) and (b) show the up-going, down-going speed of sound and bulk fluid velocity calculation from the Doppler shift algorithm. The generated speed of sound is several orders of magnitude larger than water or steam speed of sound velocities and is very close to the speed of sound in metal (i.e. steel tubing). Even though clear up-going and down-going speed of sound was identified, this algorithm did not provide reasonable velocities for a steam injection flow profile. The calculated bulk velocity does yield an acceptable result near the wellhead. In Fig. 19(b) the red circle is the bulk fluid velocity in the tubing at the surface calculated from the injection rate, pressure and steam quality, which is in good agreement with the calculated velocity from the Doppler shift algorithm.
