Modelling the effects of all contributing factors of a system would normally be ideal, however data availability, budgeting limitations and time contraints typically prevent this. A screening experiment is an economic method to assessing the importance of the factors on oil recovery. In this case, it is desired to obtain a general understanding of the fluid and reservoir properties of the Chicontepec Basin, therefore the well completion properties were selectively screened resulting in a process as shown in Figure 3.

Using the input parameters, a nonlinear numerical model was used to simulate production profiles and oil recovery volumes for each of the runs in Table 2. The nonlinear model represents a horizontally fractured well as pictured in Figure 4.

The five factors with the smallest individual p-Value (after selectively removing well completion factors) were determined to have the most significance on the oil recovery. Figure 5 portrays the reservoir system model with the key factors (hn, γ,A,pR, and ϕ, the controlled well completion parameters (nf and L), the uncontrollable reservoir/fluid properties (R, k, SW, and TR), and the reservoir system output (Npo) for a model of the Chicontepec Basin reservoir system.

After the screening experiment is completed and the key factors have been identified, another experi-mental design is created. A Central Composite Design combines two-level fractional factorial points, center points (midrange), and axial as shown in Figure 6.

An initial analysis of the data is conducted by use of the quartile plot (Figure 7a) and lognormal probability plot (Figure 7b). This analysis indicates a lognormal distribution of the output (NPO) and the the outputs remain withing the 95% confidence interval throughout most of the distribution. A more formalanalysis is completed by a stepwise regression of all main effects and two-factor interactions with the p-Value as the threshold set to a 0.05.
