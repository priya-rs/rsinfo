The method parameterizes the well-level relative error in simulated production from the model versus observed production. The workflow utilizes this data, known as well-level modifiers, to alter subsurface properties. This has been achieved with a semi-automated workflow to make the process efficient and repeatable, as well as to enable engineering judgement to be incorporated in the history-matching process. A summary of key history-matching workflow steps is displayed in Figure 1.

A sensitivity analysis study of history-matching property adjustments and impacted production parameters are shown in Figure 2 which had been tested while on Gas Rate Control (refer Section 1.2). For example, if the intended impact is on the early-time Water-Gas Ratio (WGR), the properties that should be adjusted are porosity and gas saturation, after which the adjustment of transmissibility multiplier can also be investigated if necessary. The property adjustments must always be supported with engineering judgement.

Once the subsurface property has been altered using the grid calculator functionality in the proprietary modelling software, a new simulation case is run with the resultant altered subsurface grid, which is termed as an ‘Iteration’. The desired effect is to improve history-match (HM) quality at the well-level, iteratively, until an adequate well and field-level HM is achieved. For example, Figure 3 displays the resulting property modifier on the porosity grid using local well-level modifiers, completing an ‘iteration’ on porosity to improve the mismatch on water production data.

 

As shown in Figure 5, if the simulated water exceeds the observed water (i.e. modifier > 1), the inverse of this modifier (which will then make the multiplier < 1) can be used to reduce the porosity at the well location to reduce the simulated water to match the observed production.

Figure 6 displays well-level history-matches of varying quality, with decreasing match quality from top to bottom, for three example wells. In this example, the model is controlled on gas rate (0% mismatch on gas in all three wells) with water being the matched phase. Absolute mismatch percentages for water are shown for each well. Note that red and blue dots represent observed data for gas and water respectively, while red and blue lines represent simulated data for the same phase.

Additionally, a spatial map of well-level water match quality (on gas rate control) for a Surat field example in Figure 7 can focus attention on specific areas where further property adjustments is required to improve the overall history-match quality. The supporting histogram in Figure 8 can aid the comparison of history-match quality statistics between simulation cases (or "Iterations").
