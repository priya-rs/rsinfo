The SRVP is installed downhole inside a concentric tubing string and is powered by injecting a high pressure fluid. The injected (power) fluid causes a signal rod to extend/retract within a pilot-operated shuttle (engine) valve (Fig. 2). At the conclusion of the signal rod’s stroke, indentations in the signal rod cause the engine valve to shift from a “Home” to a “Forward” position or vice versa. The engine valve’s inlet/discharge flow paths are reversed in the Home/Forward configurations, allowing power fluid to be routed to the appropriate side of a power piston.

Fig. 3 diagrams the basic operating principles of the downhole SRVP. Fig. 3a describes the beginning of the “extend” stroke, with the engine valve in the Home position. Injected power fluid is routed through the engine valve to the top (left) of the power piston, forcing it to travel down (left-to-right). This movement exhausts spent power fluid around the power rod, back through the engine valve, and up the insert string by production tubing annulus.

Fig. 4 describes the operating envelopes for Type I/II AL methods and the SRVP in terms of gas production rate (Mscf/D) versus produced liquids (BFPD) at lifting depths of 8,000 and 12,000 ft-true vertical depth (TVD). A well with production characteristics towards the top left of the Fig. 4 graphs would be considered a “dry” well, while one with characteristics toward the lower right would be considered a “wet” well.

Fig. 5 shows daily (Mscf/D) and cumulative (MMscf) gas production versus time for a typical 3 Bcf tight gas well with a 30-year producing life (see Baihly et al. 2010, 2015 for other tight/shale gas examples). The reservoir pressure and producing rate of the well decrease when reserves are processed, causing the production rate to fall below the critical rate of 400 Mscf/D at approximately 5 years.

The SRVP was piloted in a Western Colorado Piceance Basin (Fig. 7) well for deliquification operations. The objective was to pump fluids from a deep setting depth and later retrieve and redeploy the SRVP without a workover rig. This section will describe the pilot program, including the well characteristics, completion design, surface pumping skid, deployment and retrieval operations, performance and teardowns, learnings, and forward plans.

Table 3 summarizes the pilot well characteristics. The tight-gas Mesaverde was the producing formation. The Mesaverde is composed of interbedded, thinly-layered sands that can require many hydraulically fractured zones for processing (Adeyeye et al. 2013; Juranek et al. 2010; Matheney et al. 2013). A typical well in the field has several thousand feet of perforations and a deviated or “S”-shaped geometry. The pilot well’s geometry is diagrammed in Fig. 8.

Fig. 9 diagrams the pilot well’s completion. The production casing was 4.5 in. OD, 15.1 lbm/ft (3.826 in. ID), P110 grade to surface. The production tubing was 2.875 in. OD, 6.5 lbm/ft (2.441 in. ID), N80 grade with reduced-OD couplings because of the relatively small ID of the production casing. The 2.875-in. OD tubing was chosen to allow installation of a 2.063-in. OD, 3.25 lbm/ft (1.751 in. ID), L80 grade, flush-joint insert string that could accommodate either 238- or 278-series SRVPs because both models could use the same OD circulating seals.

Fig. 10 shows photos of the power fluid surface pumping skid, 2.063 in. OD stick pipe insert string, SRVP BHA with sealing cups and strainer, SRVP during a bucket test, and wellhead configuration.

Fig. 11 describes the injection fluid flow paths. Power fluid is injected through the upper left wing valve and master valve into the insert string for deployment and operation (Fig. 11a). Returning power and pumped fluids are produced up the insert string by production tubing annulus and out of the lower right wing valve. Gas is produced up the production tubing by production casing annulus and out of the casing valve (not shown).

It was initially thought that each SRVP well would require an individual lubricator. However, it was found that the lubricator was only needed for deployment/retrieval operations and thus could be moved around a field as applicable. Fig. 12 shows a photo of the lubricator in place on the pilot well. A detailed diagram of the custom lubricator can be found in Romer et al. (2016).

A brushed plunger was left on top of the SRVP to reduce the chance of debris falling around its top. Slickline (SL) magnet runs were performed after the plunger was removed to clean debris from the tubing. SRVP 1 was reverse-circulated with a pump truck at up to 0.75 bbl/min (10 gal/min) to nearly 2,100 ft-TVD in 1.5 hours, then tagged and pulled with SL. Pumping pressures encountered during SRVP 1 retrieval are plotted in Fig. 13.

The third and final SRVP was deployed hydraulically with the surface pumping skid in 5 hours. The injection rates and pressures measured during deployment are plotted in Fig. 14. There is a clear indication that the SRVP seated when the injection pressure increased from nearly 400 psi for circulation to 1,100 psi for operation near the 5 hour mark.

SRVP 1 inspection found that the engine valve, circulation valve, and water section were plugged with debris (Fig. 17). The debris appeared to include thread cuttings and corrosion-related products (e.g., an Xray diffraction analysis indicated FeCO3, likely from entrained CO2).

SRVP 2 inspection found that the inlet side of the engine valve section was completely plugged with primarily quartz debris (Fig. 18). The debris appeared to have arrived in a large, catastrophic influx because the power and water sections were clear—the source was never identified. SRVP 2 components were again operational following cleaning.

The SRVP 3 teardown uncovered some debris in the engine valve and some large material (suspected to be seal springs) that had entered the water section intake (Fig. 19). The combination of debris and water section blockage/wear likely resulted in reduced efficiency near the end of the pumping period. The SRVP components were operational following cleaning of the engine valve. The SRVP 3 engine valve was the same as was run in SRVP 1.
