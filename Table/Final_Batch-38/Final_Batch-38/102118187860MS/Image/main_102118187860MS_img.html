The initial production rates of gas wells at the Cenomanian deposit were 1-1.5 million m3/day. Presently, due to the decrease in reservoir pressure, the actual average gas production rate for gas wells is 90,000 m3/day (Fig. 1). Among the current operational problems are the low wellhead pressures, as well as water and sand production. A proper understanding of the reasons for uneven drainage of gas volumes and distribution of reservoir pressure is also important for their management in order to efficiently solve the well operation problems.

Fig. 6 shows a map of the current GWC levels for the analyzed area of the reservoir. In the process of development, gas production from the central (dome) zone predetermined the general elevation of the GWC. Thus, maximum movement of the GWC is associated with the central zone of the GPF-1 and GPF-4 area, which is characterized by the maximum initial gas-saturated thicknesses.

In Fig. 7 a, b according to WLIR-2013 and in Fig. 8 a, b according to WLIR-2016 a map of the average permeability values above and below the current (for 2016) GWC is presented. It can be seen on the maps that the local high permeability zones in the center of the dome (near wells 106, 107) are confined to the top part of the reservoir, so that GWC movement didn't influence much the average permeability of these zones in the gas-saturated region. On the periphery, the picture is somewhat different. The rising water occupies the regions within the initial GWC contour and the intervals above the initial GWC. For the gas-saturated part on the periphery, this means the loss of intervals with a relatively high permeability, with their commingling to the water-saturated zone.

General configuration of the increased hydraulic conductivity zones according to WLIR-2013 and WLIR-2016 has a similar appearance and is largely controlled by current gas-saturated thicknesses in the dome part of the site (see Fig. 11 below).

A later period of pressure build-up is associated with a general redistribution of gas volumes within the stopped area and between it and the peripheral zones. To assess this effect, Fig. 12 shows a similar map of the rates of pressure increase (also in atm per day) for the third "quarter" from the moment of well stopping.

The rate of pressure increase over a long period from the well stopping, as shown in Fig. 12, is indirectly interrelated with the inhomogeinity of the GWC advancement (see Fig. 6). Perhaps this fact reflects a greater effect of bottom water on pressure build-up in zones with improved vertical connection of the reservoir. At the same time, correlation with the current gas-saturated thickness (Fig. 13) is not evident.

To confirm what has been said, we present the results of flow simulations with a simplified sector model of a typical zone of the deposit. The general view of the model is shown in Fig. 14.

As an example, Fig. 15 shows simulation results of the homogeneous model for two simulation cases that differ by the lateral-to-vertical permeability ratio. The operating modes of well clusters and the dynamics of gas production in the cases are the same. Differences are noticable in the calculated dynamics of water production. Apparently, the vertical permeability fundamentally affects the bottom water inflow dynamics. Moreover, even the minimum value of the lateral-to-vertical permeability of 10 is much higher the average value in the cell columns of the FSM (about 3). At the same time, the calculated rates of bottom water advancement towards gas wells are significantly higher than those observed according to field data. The same tendencies are evident in the simulations on the heterogeneous (stochastic) model.

A joint analysis of the map of current gas-saturated thicknesses (Fig. 13) and the geologo-statistic profile (Fig. 16) made it possible to make a forecast of trends in the future changes of the average permeability within the gas-saturated zone.
