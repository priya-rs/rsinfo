Over time, some of the water injecters and oil producers were converted to WAG injection. Most of the pool is pressure supported by fluid injection. Figure 1 shows the map for the field location and areas of the waterflood and CO2 EOR schemes. By the end of 2015, there were 751 wells, of which 304 were active.

The productive interval found at a depth of approximately 1,400m is the Midale Carbonate, which is overlain and underlain by impervious anhydrite beds. The 20m thick productive interval is subdivided into an upper dolomitic unit (Marly) and a lower limestone unit (Vuggy). Figure 2 shows the reservoir characteristics.

The Vuggy Intershoal facies primarily consist of lower porosity peloidal and bioclastic packstones. The Vuggy Shoal section contains high-permeability peloidal grainstones. Vuggy dominated performance is characterized by faster flood-front breakthrough, lower oil rates and higher watercuts. Naturally fractured systems are suspected in the NESW direction as presented in Figure 3.

Figure 4 shows a schematic the placements of the pool wells, which are mainly perpendicular to the natural fracture orientation.

Figure 5 shows the production response from the beginning of the waterflood. In addition, one objective with the waterflood is to increase the average reservoir pressure. The initial pool pressure was 14,900 kPa, and the CO2 miscibility pressure is estimated between 14,000 and 16,000 kPa.

The EOR scheme currently consists of 25 CO2 injectors and 138 oil wells (active). The CO2 injected is a mixture of supply volumes plus the recycle/produced gas, which is composed of recycled CO2 and produced solution gas. CO2 injection is directed at the highest performing patterns. Figure 6 shows the production response associated with the CO2 injection. As with water, oil production and gas oil ratio (GOR) increased shortly after initiating CO2 injection.

These efforts are exhibiting success by reducing oil declines, sometimes even increasing oil production, while reducing costs associated with the amount of injection and associated costs. Figure 7 shows the Midale production history and response after implementing such practices, and it is believed that further improvements are possible by utilizing flow control technology.

For passive ICDs a crossflow nozzle (CFN), as shown in Figure 8, in its standard version with two opposing throats, was chosen over a standard nozzle (single throat) because it significantly reduces the velocity of the fluid exiting the nozzle during production, thereby reducing the risk of erosion-corrosion on surfaces directly downstream of the nozzle.

The AICV technology consists of a valve that is actuated by the pressure drop response of the fluid through a laminar and turbulent flow restrictor in series [1, 2, 3]; Figure 9 illustrates the principle. The main flow enters a conduit, A, at inlet (wellbore) pressure P1. A minor pilot flow, parallel to the main flow, is passing through the laminar flow element where a pressure drop occurs, and the pressure in chamber is reduced to P2.

Figure 10 shows drawings of the AICV in open and closed positions. The thin blue lines present the pilot flow path, the thickest blue arrows presents the inlet of the main flow to the valve, and the two horizontal arrows show the outlet of the main flow to the base pipe. The thin blue vertical arrow presents the outlet of the pilot flow.

The force balance around the piston is controlling the piston position, as shown in Figure 11. The force, F1, on the upper part of the piston (p1·A1) is acting downwards and the force F2 below the piston (p2·A2) is acting upwards. Ffric is a friction force, which will work against the direction of fluid flow.

The specification of flow rates are based on the reservoir and fluid properties. A prototype AICV was tested with relevant fluids’ properties, and Figure 12 shows the AICV performance curves for oil (1.2 and 2.7 cP), water and CO2. As can be seen from the figure, the prototype AICV will close for CO2 and water and keep open for oil. When open, the AICV behaves like a passive ICD with an equivalent flow restriction (orifice) area of about 20 mm^2 per valve (See Equation 1 above).

Premium port [4] is a unique downhole 3-position (2-port) full bore valve that can be either closed or opened to one of two ICD settings or ICD types, as dictated by the position of a short sleeve as shown in Figure 13 (A 2-position or 1-port version is also available). The sleeve, shown with ICD1 open, can be shifted to either the closed position or to ICD2 position using special shifting tools run on jointed pipe, coil tubing, or wireline tractor.

The sleeve, as shown in Figure 14, is designed to minimize the risk of sticking over time, caused primarily by surface deposition which plagues conventional sliding sleeves. The sleeve length and stroke are very short. The metallurgy on sliding surfaces is specially treated to minimize surface deposition. Scrapers are installed to scrape deposition off of surfaces when shifted. A special seal technology (not o-rings) is used to achieve high pressure differential ratings in the closed position. The detent rings are used to adjust the amount of shifting force that is desired for surface readout.

For this project the standard 3ICI valve had to be customized in order to accommodate the AICV, which is significantly larger than nozzle ICDs. The assembly had to be small enough on the OD to fit inside 5.5" casing (4.89" drift ID). And the sub had to be short because the zones with thin. Hence, two 3ICI valves were integrated into one sub to allow 4 different ICD types or settings, as shown in Figure 15.

The 3ICI valves can be switched to different positions by means of a unique shifting tool which is run on coiled tubing, conventional tubing or drill string [4]. Figure 16 shows the standard tool, but for this project a custom tool was designed because of the inside diamater required. Any valve can be controlled selectively and shifted various times during one trip.

Midale oil producers are normally cased-hole verticals or open-hole horizontals, which are completed commingled across the different horizons, as shown in Figure 17. Over time, producers significantly short circuit with the injectors and are shut in due to uneconomic high water cut (> 99.5%) and/or huge GOR (mainly CO2 gas), which leaves behind significant oil.

Figure 18 demonstrates potential shortcut pathways, via natural fractures, for the injected fluids (CO2 and/or Water), from a WAG injector towards a horizontal oil wellbore, and then towards the vertical oil producer via one of the present horizons with higher K*h (=perm × zone height). It is this vertical oil well that was selected for the ICD field trial.

This vertical trial well had previously produced 160 MBbl of Oil and 1.9 MMBbl of Water (averaged cumulative water cut: 95%). But it was declared uneconomic and shut-in in 2014 because of high water cut (> 99.5%) and elevated GOR (> 220,000 scf/Bbl, mainly CO2), as shown in Figure 19.

Figure 20 shows a good CBL (Slim Cement Mapping Tool or SCMT) for the selected trial well, which ensures the required hydraulic isolation behind casing. The wavy shape of the zebra pattern with the delay presented on the VDL and the low CBL amplitude across and above the perfs to the depth of 1400m infers reasonable isolation.

Figure 21 shows the results for the 3 zones of the trial well. The RST tool was run through tubing. The RST utilized sigma and carbon-oxygen measurement to process oil saturation because the play is under both water flood and CO2 gas injection, where hydrogen-based tools can’t be used. The RST C/O oil curve shows the presence of a pocket of oil (denoted by the green shaded horizontal parabola) across M2 and M3 formaiton.

The trial well candidate was re-opened, tested and PLT’d in 2015 to establish a base-line for current conditions and for designing the ICD completion. The average ratios were: 0.4% oil cut (OC), 99.6% water cut (WC), 32,000 scf/bbl gas oil ratio (GOR), with rates up to 2,050 blpd, 10 bopd and 346 Mscfd, as shown in Table 2 and Figure 22. Moreover, the water cut and GOR performance versus fluid rate are shown in Figure 23 and Figure 24.

In addition, PLT was performed over three days in August 2015. Several passes were executed using Dual DEFT tool, followed by additional run with DEFT/GHOST PLT tools. This PLT (Figure 25) showed that the lowest horizon (M3) was highly swept by water (during which the WAG injector was injecting water); furthermore, it also revealed the best oil cut presence within the second horizon (M2), as summarized in Table 3.

By varying the wellhead pressure (WHP), accurate Inflow Performance Relationship (IPR) curves were generated for the no-ICD case for each zone from predicted oil, water and gas rates for different wellhead pressures. The next step was to perform similar hydraulic simulations using packers to compartmentalize the three zones and using passive ICDs to separately control the inflow from each zone. Figure 26 shows the two modelling configurations.

It was decided to make the nozzle setting equal for each zone because for future wells’ PLT data will likely not be available to optimize the setting on each zone, because of expense. Figure 27 shows wellhead liquid rate versus wellhead pressure for different nozzle settings (i.e., cross section area of nozzle throat). Note how the curves fall off steeper for WHPs below 3500 psi indicating that not much is gained by lowering WHP below this value.

 

Figure 29 shows the oil production rate versus throat area for each zone as well as well total. M2 has the lowest watercut which helps explain the highest oil production rate. Also shown (black curve) is the water-oil ratio which is minimized at 25.6 mm^2. Although minimizing water-oil ratio minimizes the cost of handling water production on surface, it is not necessarily the most economical setting since the oil rates are still rising sharply with increasing nozzle throat area.

Figure 30 shows wellhead pressure versus predicted liquid rate for 103.2 mm^2 nozzle throat area per zone. Ignoring zone M1, which produces much less liquid than zones M2 and M3, note that the passive ICD setting causes less liquid to produce from M3 because it’s choked back, but produces much more liquid from M2 because M3 is significantly choked back, thereby allowing M2 to produce more. The difference increases as WHP decreases.

Figure 31 shows predicted wellhead pressure versus stock tank oil rate for 103.2 mm^2 nozzle throat area per zone. Note that the passive ICD setting causes less oil to produce from M3 because it’s significantly choked back, but produces much more oil from M2 because it’s less choked back. The difference increases as WHP decreases. Hence ICDs produce more oil by choking zones that have both high flow rate and high watercut.

Note that both valves can be opened for each zone giving a third nozzle ICD setting. The three types of nozzles are shown in Figure 32. Note that the SFN nozzle is a standard flow nozzle (SFN), not a crossflow nozzle, because we could not get enough open flow area through the CFN to satisfy the test objectives.
