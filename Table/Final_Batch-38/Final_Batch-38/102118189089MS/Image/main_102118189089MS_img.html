By definition, the GOC is a bounding surface in a reservoir above which predominantly gas occurs and below which predominantly oil occurs (Ahmed, 2007). In other words, it is the depth at which oil and gas phases are in equilibrium within a continuous geological (reservoir) column. Essentially, GOC reflects gradation in oil and gas properties over depth. In theory, it is possible to estimate this transition depth by subjecting the PVT data from either the gas or oil leg of the reservoir to well-established thermodynamic analysis (Fig. 1).

Fig. 2 is a typical workflow for PVT characterisation and CGS. Fig. 3 depicts a typical output from a CGS work. By definition, the depth of intersection of the resulting gas dew-pressure and oil bubble-pressure lines is the GOC. However, to be realistic, the estimated GOC should fall within known vertical limits of hydrocarbon accumulation in a continuous reservoir column. In most cases, these structural limits are defined by the reservoir crest (updip) and either a hydrocarbon-water contact or structural spill point (downdip).

In this paper, two classes of Niger-Delta case studies are examined. These are (i) saturated reservoirs that have their GOC established by well penetrations, and (ii) undersaturated reservoirs proven by well penetrations. For simplicity, we present three examples of each class of case studies, highlighting the robustness and practicality of the CGS technique. A total of six reservoirs are discussed, comprising reservoirs A-C for class-I, and D-F for class-II (Figs. 4 and 5).
