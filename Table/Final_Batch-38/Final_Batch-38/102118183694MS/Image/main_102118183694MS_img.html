The first step in the minifrac analysis process is to determine the ISIP by extrapolating the leakoff curve back to the point of shut in. Various plots are used to analyze the leakoff trends and slope changes to pinpoint the exact pressure at which the fracture closes (i.e., closure stress). The plots used primarily during this analysis are the G-function and square root of time plot. These are included for reference in Figs. 9 and 10.

Leakoff was affected by moderate pressure-dependent leakoff (PDL), with a coefficient in the range of 0.005 to 0.007. A fissure opening (natural fracture dilation) was identified at 3,074 psi of downhole pressure. With the total value of developed net pressure at 471 psi (Fig. 11), leakoff during the main treatment would be severely affected by natural fractures.

The information obtained from the minifrac analysis was used to validate and calibrate the simulation model by matching the simulated pressure falloff data vs. the actual observed decline data. Pressure matching (Fig. 12) involves calibrating the formation process zone stress and leakoff (i.e., permeability, wall building coefficient) properties entered into the fracture model to obtain a better representation of the formation.

The pad stage was commenced by bullheading the treatment down the fracturing string at the designed rate of 22 bbl/min (Tables 6 and 7). Pad stage pressure stabilized at 5,200 psi with a little upward trend indicating fracture development in both length and height (Fig. 13). The sand slug entered the formation accompanied by a +150 psi surface treating pressure response with subsequent pressure reduction. Surface treating pressure was stabilized at 4,700 psi before the proppant stages began.

Post-treatment simulation matching indicated that the pad stage did indeed propagate a fracture across the entire interval of interest. According to the post-job pressure match (Fig. 14), the final fracture geometry was approximately 20% smaller than the planned length, with the fracture height reaching 74 m and extending up to 829 m true vertical depth (TVD), which could allow communication with the wet sands located at 820 m (Fig. 15).

When the fracturing operation was completed, fracturing fluids were flowed back through the fracturing string into the pit. One week was neccessary to reduce the bottomhole pressure (BHP) (Fig. 16), ensure the well could be killed, and retrieve the fracturing string.

A cased-hole string was run and shot at a depth of 942 m measured depth (MD) on 7-in. production casing to provide the baseline signal for microseismic signal calibration. In addition, perforations were created (875 to 882 m MD) for fracture initiation on the fracturing target to calibrate the baseline signal. It was planned to capture microseismic events during the minifrac and main fracturing treatment (Figs. 17 through 19).
