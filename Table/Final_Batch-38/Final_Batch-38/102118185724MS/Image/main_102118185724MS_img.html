Production-based methods include the use of rate-transient analysis (RTA) techniques, in which models for simulating fluid flow are applied in inverse mode to extract reservoir and hydraulic fracture properties from flow rates and flowing pressures (Clarkson 2013). Historically RTA methods have been applied primarily to production data obtained after the initial "cleanup" period (ideally after 6-12 months of production) following hydraulic fracture stimulation (e.g. online or long-term production data); recently, practitioners have attempted to apply these techniques to flowback data, which often is initially dominated by hydraulic fracture fluid production, after which reservoir fluid production becomes dominant (Clarkson et al. 2014). The conceptual model used in the DDA method is illustrated in Fig. 2. Each fracturing stage is assumed to be comprised of a primary hydraulic fracture (PHF), which is the conductive induced fracture assumed to contain the majority of the proppant, and an adjacent reservoir region that is comprised of either: 1) reservoir that has had its permeability enhanced due to (for example) natural fracture reactivation during the stimulation treatment (enhanced fracture region or EFR) or 2) a non-stimulated reservoir region where the native-state reservoir properties exist (non-stimulated region or NSR).

Prior to performing the frac modeling operation, the fracture geometry for the studied MFHW was first assessed from microseismic data (Fig. 4). The data suggest that the stimulation treatment (details of which are withheld to preserve operator confidentiality) resulted in low-complexity (mostly planar) fractures with a consistent NE-SW orientation. This information justified the choice of a simple planar hydraulic fracture model for estimating PHF properties.

Using the fracture treatment data, and the model inputs provided in Table 2, the Valko HF2D frac design spreadsheet was used to estimate fracture length and width as a function of pumping time (Fig. 5). The fracture half-length at the end of pumping is estimated to be ~ 450 ft, and fracture width ~ 0.12 inches. Frac height is assumed to be equal to net pay thickness. Independent fracture modeling performed by a service provider using the Gohfer software (not shown) resulted in propped fracture half-lengths consistent with those determined using the Valko model. The resulting frac geometry is assumed to correspond to that for the PHF region.

The procedure for modeling fracture propagation and leakoff discussed in the Theory and Methods section was then applied to the field case. Using the estimated bottomhole pressures during the fracture treatment (Fig. 6a), the leakoff rate during fracture propagation was first calculated using the DDA model. For this purpose, fracture propagation during the 30 minute pump time was constrained by the HF2D fracture model (Fig. 5), and the DDA model used to calculate leakoff from the PHF to the NSR (Fig. 6b). Reservoir properties were adopted from the flowback modeling of Clarkson et al. (2016b) (Table 3).

At the end of fracture propagation, assuming a static fracture length consistent with that derived from the frac model (450 ft), the DDA model was then used calculate frac fluid leakoff to the NSR over the 12-day shut-in period prior to flowback. As noted in the Theory and Methods section, the current version of the DDA model uses fracture pressure as a constraint; however, the pressure in the fracture during the shut-in pressure was not available and therefore was extrapolated from the last bottomhole pressure obtained from the frac treatment (6082 psi) to the frac pressure (5000 psi) used at the start of the flowback history-match by Clarkson et al. (2016b) (Fig. 7a).
