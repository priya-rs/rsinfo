The Golfo San Jorge Basin (CGSJ), located in Central Patagonia (Figure 1), can be defined as an intracratonic Basin, extensional, elongated in an E-W direction, bounded by the Macizo Nord Patagónico to the North and the Deseado Region to the south. The main sedimentary fill of the basin was produced during the end of the rifting stages, in the late Jurassic, and in the sag stages in the Cretaceous Period, and finished with an Atlantic Tertiary marine transgression.

The study area is located within the Field Los Perales - Las Mesetas (LP-LM), on the northern flank of the Santa Cruz Province, about 45 km northwest of Las Heras city. The selected study zone contemplates an area of 2300 hectares. Figure 2 shows Los Perales Field and the study zone. There are 260 wells drilled in this area.

Low sinuosity fluvial systems, mud flats and shallow lakes have been observed. The geometry of these bodies corresponds to lobes and sheets with important volcaniclastic participation. Vertical wells cross this formation at the range of 900 to 1400 MBBP. It consist of multilayer reservoirs of 1 to 12 layers per production level, with an average of 4 levels per well. The productive layers correspond to lenses and filled canal. The thicknesses vary between 1 to 12 meters (average 3 to 4 m). The width of the channels varies between 600 and 1500 meters. Figure 3 shows the stratigraphic sequence of the region. Figure 4 shows a layer corresponding to one of the channels identified.

Water injection began in 2002 (Figure 11). This storical inyection was loaded to the simulator too. This information is the basis for running the first history run. Once the reservoir level adjustment was achieved (Figure 15), the well-to-well adjustment was continued. Following the same methodology of the adjustment at field level, but now located in the areas bordering each well. Well was worked well to try to adjust its history of production of water and oil. It must be taken into account that each well has on average between 5 and 15 productive levels. Each of them hydraulically independent of each other.

Figures. 16a, 16b and 16c shows good and regular adjustments per well. For this report only three wells are presented. Two considered with a good fit in oil and water. Anotherone, where the adjustment is not so good and requires more work. It is possible to see how in all cases of good fit the product curve of the simulation represents very well the peaks of production. In the case of regular adjustments it is seen that the problem is saturation. The total liquid fits well, but not water and oil. Where one is abundant, the other is scarce, compensating for the volumes. This should be layered by differences in initial saturations.

As mentiones before, due to the size of the static model, the number of active cells and the number of years of production history, it was decided to work the dynamic model with Streamlines. The traditional dynamic simulation, to run the 22 years of history, demanded 12 hours. The model running with flow lines only 2.5 hours. For this reason, all historical adjustment runs were performed with this methodology (Figure 17).

Moreover, the modeling presented in this paper will allow us to determine non-swept zones with initial saturation (Figure 18), wellbore production, and deficiencies in sweeping efficiency. Points that are not possible to be analyzed with the current way of studying. Having achieved the historical adjustment encourages us to continue advancing with this methodology.
