The crude oil has an in-situ oil viscosity of 9-22 cP, with live oil wax appearance temperature (WAT) approximately 6°>C lower than reservoir temperature of 65°>C. The oil also has a high pour point of 40-45°>C. Figure 1 shows the depth structure and type log of Mangala field.

Incorporating all the learnings from detailed laboratory evaluations and the successful polymer flood pilot, the full-field polymer flood started in November 2014. To integrate the EOR scheme with the ongoing water injection, the ramp-up strategy was to down-space each of the inverted 9-spot water-flood patterns (450 m spacing) of the upper Fatehgarh group (FM1 and FM2) to five spot polymer flood patterns (250 m spacing) by drilling additional injectors and replacement producers (at the location of previous injectors) (Figure 2).

The polymer/water mixture is allowed to hydrate in a series of tanks. Hydration tanks provide two hours of maturation time. Polymer mixing tanks and hydration tanks are kept under nitrogen blankets. Hydrated polymer is filtered using a pair of filters arranged in series (100 µ and 20 µ filters respectively). Filtered polymer (the mother solution) is pumped to individual well pads via five headers. Figure 3 shows a flow diagram of polymer solution from the CPF to individual wells.

The oil production rate of the central FM1 area, where a higher volume of polymer has been injected to date, has nearly doubled in the last six months. The performance diagnostic curves for active patterns show that for each fraction of pore volume injection, the recovery has increased, thus demonstrating the sweep iwmprovement. During the polymer pilot in this field, an average injectivity of 3-5 bpd/psi was measured during the water injection phase and a corresponding injectivity of 1 bpd/psi was observed during polymer phase. For the sake of simplicity of demonstration, a central pattern (Pattern-102) of FM-1 (Figure 8) was picked for discussion in the following sections of the paper. The performance of the polymer flood in other areas of the field has been very similar to the selected pattern.

It is very important for modelling purposes that injection tubing head pressure (ITHP) and injection rate be stable. The injection rate measurement can be crosschecked from the surface and simulated rate match from the spinner survey analysis. Figure 9 shows the match of M-182 injection bottom-hole pressure. With an established injection rate of 3,900 Bpd (surface rate matched with simulated rate) at an ITHP of 410 psi (stable during spinner survey), viscosity of the polymer solution was adjusted to match the recorded injection bottom-hole pressure of 1828.4 psi.

Fall-off surveys carried out in another well after injecting polymer showed reduction in oil saturation around the well when compared with the fall-off tests carried out during the water injection period. The radial flow region in the polymer injection case is at a higher pressure than the previous baseline case. Similar behavior was also observed in another well, MEOR6, during the pilot injection phase. Figure 10 shows the pressure fall-off survey of M-178 indicating sweep improvement around the injector with time.

Numerical simulation techniques were also used in order to establish in-situ viscosity estimates. For this purpose, an FM-3 layer polymer injector was used in order to avoid inherent uncertainities with fall-off interpretation in multi-layered system. As already discussed, the FM-3 layer is relatively homogeneous with little variation in permeability and thickness. As part of regular surveillance, fall-off tests were done with water injection and polymer injection. These injection bottom-hole pressures were matched in a commercial simulator, both for water injection and polymer injection. This again confirmed that the insitu viscosity of polymer solution is in the range of 15-20 cP for a 2,500 ppm polymer solution. Figure 11 shows the results from actual fall-off tests and from the numerical simulator.

These saturation logs have also been used previously in infill well drilling campaigns in order to optimize well completion zones (Pranay et.al IPTC 17236). The injection rate for the well is stabilized at two different rates, and for each rate, a spinner log is run across the entire injection interval to record simultaneous profiles of down-hole flow rates and injection pressures. The data are then used to estimate the IPR corresponding to each contributing zone. A selective inflow performance test (SIP) was conducted in M-180 in Feb 2015. Two zones are contributing to injection as shown in Figure 14. A spinner survey was conducted at two rates of 4,300 Bpd and 2,700 Bpd. Using the data for each zone (i.e 3302 Bpd at 2031 Psi and 1794 Bpdat 1936 Psi for Zone A, and 1075 Bpd at 2091 Psi and 932 Bpd at 1992 Psi for Zone B) the IPR for each contributing zone can be drawn.

Various chemical stimulation techniques were used to target the damage mechanism due to water quality; these included acid wash, solvent treatment, and even surfactant treatment in a few wells. The improvement in injectivity after each stimulation activity was evident, but the sustenanace was not for as long as desired. One of the injectors, M-250 was re-perforated and then flowed back; this has resulted in a very good improvement in injectivity and is still under observation. Figure 15 is the Halls plot of M-250 which shows improved well injectivity post re-perforation and flow back.

The integration of seismic data interpretations, surveillance data, and geological understanding of the areas between the polymer injectors and offset producers has helped the operator take corrective/remedial action for some underperforming producers, significantly improving the polymer flood performance. One of the example cases is for M-128 and is shown in Figure 16. M-128 is a flank producer in FM1. The well was producing at a low oil rate at very high watercut during the waterflood.

The production performance of producers that are relatively close to polymer injectors has shown very good improvement in terms of increasing oil rates and declining water cuts. The mobilization of additional oil by polymer bank over water flood is also visible in time-lapse fall-off tests and saturation monitoring surveys. The saturation monitoring surveys have shown re-saturation in parts of the reservoirs due to sweep improvement from polymer flooding. Polymer flooding has also helped in achieving improved conformance as compared to the waterflood in parts of the field, thereby improving injection performance. The oil production rate within the area that has had the maximum amount of polymer flooding (central FM1) to date has increased by nearly double in the last quarter, and it is still on an increasing trend, as shown in Figure 17.

For FM1, the oil production is increasing even though the number of active producers has decreased as pre-producers have been converted to polymer injectors. Water cut is on a declining trend, demonstrating the improved production performance of FM1 producers. Current performance of FM1 is shown in Figure 18.

Various diagnostic plots are prepared regularly to analyze the production efficiency of each pattern in the polymer flood. Figure 20 shows two diagnostic plots for M-102 pattern. The first one is “water cut versus cumulative oil production from the pattern” and the second one is “average oil rate of the pattern versus cumulative metric tonnage of polymer injected in the pattern”. Both the plots show improved performance of M-102 pattern with polymer flooding. Incase any pattern does not have the impact at the expected level, each producer and injector of that pattern is individually analysed, taking into consideration all the surveillance, geological, geophysical, or otherdata, to make recommendations that will improve the performance of that particular pattern.

Even if the overall production performance of any pattern is better than predicted, it is not always true that all producers or injectors of that particular pattern are performing optimally. Thus, it is always important to monitor the performance of each producer and injector of any pattern. Figure 21 shows the production performance of a few producers of M-102 pattern. Although the overall production performance of M-102 pattern is still improving during the polymer flood, it was observed that performance of M-112 (Figure 21) has started showing a declining trend.
