For the simulation model, horizontal heterogeneity was incorporated by assuming there is better quality rock in the SW region and poorer rock in the NE region. These heterogeneous rock characteristics were taken into account in the geo-model by using the Sequential Gaussian Geostatistical method. The average geological properties used in the simulation model were obtained from literature (Kurtoglu, 2013; Sonnenberg et al, 2009; Alexandre et al, 2011) and are presented in Table 1. For the gas injection cases (N2 and CO2 injection), slim tube tests were performed using a lab-scale simulation model to determine the Minimum Miscibility Pressure (MMP). For CO2, the MMP was determined to be 21 mPa, which is very close to what other studies have found for Bakken oil (Adekunle and Hoffman 2014). For N2, an MMP could not be found even when injecting at pressures above 50 mPa. This is also consistent with the observations made in other studies (Adekunle and Hoffman, 2014). The optimal CO2 flood case yielded an NPV of $47 M, exceeding both nitrogen injection and natural depletion. For this model, the improved recovery due to oil swelling from miscibility contributed significantly leading to an NPV 68% higher than the immiscible case. Further to the above parameters there were 6 other operating inputs that were changed during the optimization and the results can be seen in Table 11. Unique to a cyclic process is the optimization of the duration of each period in a cycle. Because the optimal cycle phase length may change with time, a different set of criteria was used for cycle switching as opposed to specifying a length of time. The injection phase was considered complete when the average pressure around a well reached a maximum value and the production phase was ended when the oil rate dropped below a minimum.
