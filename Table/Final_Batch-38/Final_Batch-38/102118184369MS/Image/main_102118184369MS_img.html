The study approach involves the integration of knowledge from the various subsurface disciplines in creating a synthesized understanding and a representative description of the reservoir. Interdisciplinary feedbacks were provided at each of the project milestone to ensure consistency of interpretations with geological, geophysical and reservoir engineering concepts. The summary of the methodology is shown in Figure 1 to reflect the integration approach

Fault interpretation was done on the dipline (inline, Figure 3) because as seen on semblance slice (Figure 2), the faults strike in the E-W direction. Four intra-reservoir faults were identified. The major one is a synthetic fault running almost across the structure (Marked F-F’= in Figure 1).

Fault interpretation was done on the dipline (inline, Figure 3) because as seen on semblance slice (Figure 2), the faults strike in the E-W direction. Four intra-reservoir faults were identified. The major one is a synthetic fault running almost across the structure (Marked F-F’= in Figure 1).

Facies identification was based on log motif analysis because core data were not available. Paleo-bathymetry data in MAR-01A was used to determine the gross EOD as shallow marine, and the sub environment was identified as a shoreface. This lead to the inference and description of the EOD as a channelized shoreface as illustrated Figure 4;

A vital need for integration was in the horizon interpretation, which relies strongly on picking the right event on seismic, the geologic understanding of the regional framework helped in constraining the interpreted loop. As shown in Figure 4, H1 and H2 were identified from the sequence stratigraphic framework, and were also resolvable on seismic, this offered some validation to the well tie and increased confidence in picking the MOT event as shown in Figure 5.

The time maps were generated from the interpretations and depth converted with available checkshot data. The MOT reservoir has only five well penetrations and no reliable check shot data, hence there is uncertainty in structure away from well penetrations. Two different velocity modelling techniques (Polynomial and VoK) were adopted in order to constrain the resulting depth uncertainty. Consequently, the derived depth uncertainty was +/-31.5ft. To manage this uncertainty, low and high case top structure maps were generated (Figure 6).

The estimated petrophysical logs were used to populate the reservoir using statistical algorithms for both the facies (SIS) and property modelling (GIS) due to data paucity. The generated 3D property models were constrained by the 3D facies model in distribution of the properties away from well position as shown in figure 7;

A representative saturation model should capture the effect of rock quality variability and preserve this effect while modeling other saturation functions to achieve consistent modeling. A log based saturation height model (Figure 8) was generated due to non availability of capillary pressure data. Best practice has pointed out that the use of a nearby field SHF gives quicker match, this was deployed and the obtain SHF was generated with an acceptable global error of 6%.

A cross plot of the porosity and permeability log in the objective wells reflect three characteristic flow units (as shown in Figure 9) and this is consistent with the number of facies identified by the geologist, howere, a fit-for-purpose singular poro-perm was developed to respect the dominating flow unit.

The capillary pressure curves were generated with a QC-tool to examine the constructed model for its validity in terms of established reservoir physics. Figure 10, shows the reduction of the Swi, transition interval, and the Pce as the permeability improves.

The reservoir fluid property was characterized by the use of the black oil correlations, but the question of which correlation is most suitable was answered by testing the correlation in nearby fields with available PVT data. The workflow for this process is shown in Figure 11.

For this study a 1:1 upscaling was performed because the available computing capacity can support full scale modeling. A QC plot of the voxel and upscaled properties were made to check for consistency, as shown in Figure 12, the two plots perfectly overlay confirming the consistency.

The model was hydrostatically initialized, on a block by block basis, using the initial pressure at the original fluid contacts defined for the reservoir, the fluid PVT data and the saturation height functions derived from the petrophysical data, and the volume in place for the respective cases were obtained as shown in Figure 13. This is usually a quality checking step that confirms that the dynamic model resulting from the upscalling exercise do not vary significantly from the static volumes. Usually a 5% variation is acceptable as this may be attributed to the differences in the algorithm used in the static and dynamic domains.
