Areal sweep efficiency is defined as the fraction of the total area of the oil reservoir that has been contacted by water. It is usually delineated as EA. The vertical sweep efficiency (delineated as EV) on the other hand is the fraction of the reservoir thickness contacted by water. EA and EV are better depicted as shown in figure 2 below.

Although no BHP was acquired during the short period of production, there are two strong indications of good aquifer support. Firstly, the well when closed-in (for flowline failure- see Appendix 3) was still producing at a BSW of 78 %. This is indicative of a good aquifer support. Secondly pressure depletion analysis of all other produced/producing reservoirs in the field indicates < 10 % pressure depletion. These outcomes indicate a very high Ee.

Forty-Nine (49) Bottom Hole Pressures (BHPs) have been acquired over the 40-year period of production from all the wells. These pressures, acquired from the different wells, when plotted over time (see Appendix 4) clearly indicate rapid pressure equilibration and fluid connectivity. This dynamic observation strongly supports the initial static observation on reservoir connectivity strongly indicting a very high EC.

The implication of this is that oil can be produced even at very high BSW which indeed was the case (see Appendix 4). supporting a high Ee. The case of TANA F8000N reservoir presents a very unique and interesting case. The reservoir is a small one with was a STOIIP of ~ 13 MMstb. Consequently, it was only developed with 1 well which was located close to the crest of the structure (Appendix 10 & 11). Production was brief starting in 20001 and ending in 2004 (about 4 years- see production profile in Appendix 1). The well was horizontal (with a horizontal leg of ~1650 ft) and as such had a good area contact with the oil. This definitely limited coning possibility thus encouraging both EA and EV.
