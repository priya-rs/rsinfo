More complex situations were clarified as well, such as the one reported in Fig. 9. In this case, the analysis showed a communication through faults between reservoirs separated by shales, highlighted by a strong depletion before the production start-up.

The results highlighted that the communication between wells completed on dynamically separated levels (Zone IIIA and IV in Fig.10), was possible through a complex system of faults by sands superposition.

In Fig. 11, the connections between injectors and producers (highlighted by red areas) show a good communication in the producing zone both in the Northern and Southern areas.

Fig. 12 shows the salinity behavior in a group of wells, which experienced partialization during the production life. In particular, to reduce the water production, the lower part of the interval (zone II1) was excluded in some wells, based on the assumption of the advancement of a bottom aquifer. Salinity values showed a drastic reduction as soon as the production came only from the upper zone (II), in agreement with the presence of a water injector dedicated to zone II.

Anomalies in wells zonation is another outcome of conceptual models. The example reported in Fig. 13 shows that well 4 was directly entering in a formation named “Lower”, without crossing the shallower “Upper” formation, in strong disagreement with the surrounding wells (left side of Fig.13).

The low salinity values showed that well 4 is clearly producing injected water, like the other close wells of the area (Fig. 14). Since the open injector is completed only in the “Upper” zone, the analysis confirmed that well 4 should be open in “Upper” zone as well, and the zonation was accordingly updated in the new geological model (Fig. 13 - right).

Based on the analysis of water breakthrough evidences, a preliminary conceptual model was set-up, showing aquifer arrival from both SW and E directions (Fig. 15a).
