The basic principle of WFR is from old Watermann patent form the 50’s. One piston is moveable and the other one is fixed as shown in Figure 3 [RAJ11] with the size variable depending on the flow desired to be injected in that certain layer so the regulator is calibrated by predetermined flow rate.

Each layer or zone is packed off to prevent any fluids within that zone of the wellbore invading another zone. An injection nozzle is located within each section. Several companies currently sell these valves based on the same principle. Figure4 and 5 show some valves from one of these providers, normally the service include the valves and the tool for the instalation in the mandrel.

Step Rate Injectivity Test (SRIT) was done in two zones before installing the regulators. The aim of this test is to determine Formation Parting Pressure (FPP) so as to create fractures and channels prior to installing WFR valves. Figure 9 shows four plots, in the left side, the pressure and rate for the pump and in the right side the well-known SRIT plot for each layer.

The first implementation of WFR in Kuwait has proved the concept of improving vertical injection profile by enabling higher injection rates into layers that never received water before and regulating the distribution into all layers. As most of the water injected into the well (more than 90%) was traversing through one zone only before the job, the vertical distribution of injected water due to WFR approach resulted in choking the flow through this layer to about 60% and improve the flow through the remaining uncontacted layers like summirized in Table 1 and Figure 10.
