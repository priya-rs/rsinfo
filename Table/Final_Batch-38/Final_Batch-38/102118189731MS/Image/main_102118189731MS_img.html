From a fluids perspective, the MBA Fatehgarh oils do have some unusual properties that lead to a few development challenges (Table 1,Figure 1). The oils are lacustrine-sourced and thus very waxy, with average viscosities in the main oil columns of 7-30 cP.

Bhagyam is a compact 5.5km2 field with ~500 mmb Stoiip (Table 2). Figure 2 shows FB1 top structure, Figure 3 shows a typical well log, Figure 4 shows FB1 net pay and Figure 5 shows a dip cross-section. The reservoirs have been developed with a total of 112 producers and 41 injectors via edge linedrive waterfloods. Although FB-1 (~70% of Stoiip) and FB-3 (~20% of Stoiip) contain most of the Bhagyam oil resource, each Fatehgarh member has dedicated production and injection wells, so there is no commingling between members. Also note that almost half of the Stoiip lies in the higher viscosity BOZ.

Early infill drilling programs increased total well count to 153 (vs. 81 planned), with an average well spacing of 200250m (10-15 acres/well) in the oil column, but production (Figure 6) remained below targeted levels. Field performance continued to surprise on the downside, and modeling remained a significant challenge as regular model updates with numerous local modifications were required to achieve ‘history matches’. This suggested a fundamental issue with the flow dynamics in the reservoir as compared to the model.

With time, it became apparent that water movement was more widespread than could be explained by discrete, scattered high permeability streaks. A key indicator of more diffuse/widespread water movement came from saturation monitoring logs (Figure 8) in a few wells, which showed increased Sw in most sands; in contrast with a reservoir model populated with a few high permeability streaks. Selective flow tests in several wells further bolstered these observations.

Figure 9 shows Bhagyam MDT data overlaid on the core poro-perm plot. On the right side of the plot, early oil-leg MDT data are shown in red, with later water-leg points in yellow. A startling trend emerges: water-leg points correlate nicely with core data while oil-leg points are substantially lower (by up to an order of magnitude). Obviously, there is something impacting in situ permeability estimates from the oil column but not from the (single-phase) aquifer zone.

The new permeability model was then blind-tested against core permeabilities; this exercise yielded the excellent matches shown in Figure 10.

Analyses suggested that the koih/µo calculated from well tests was ~40% of that predicted from the combination of the absolute permeability model and standard relative permeability models. Figure 11 shows that although log-based permeabilities matched well-test permeabilities in the water-leg where a single phase existed, a multiplier of ~0.45 was required on the absolute permeability modeled in the oil- leg to match corresponding well-test permeabilities.

For perspective, Figure 12 shows simplistic spherical representations of a pore body for strongly-wetted systems at different saturations (waterflood advancing from left to right). Our eyes have difficulty judging volumes associated with concentric shells: since they are related to the cube of a characteristic dimension (in this case, radius), we tend to overestimate the volume associated with the innermost shell and significantly underestimate the volume of the outer shell.

The thin barely-noticeable blue rim on the outside of Figure 13 (left) shows Bhagyam’s Swi~5%. The black rim is the prune-skin wax saturation, Swax=15%, while the red volume comprises the remaining (liquid) oil saturation, Soi=80%. Figure 13 (right) shows the fluid distribution post-waterflood, with the typical Fatehgarh remaining oil saturation (ROS, not Sorw) of ~50% comprised of Swax=15% and liquid So=35%.

Typical Fatehgarh waterflood imbibition relative permeabilities in Figure 14 show schematically how a small prune-skin wax saturation could significantly reduce initial effective (liquid) oil permeability. The oil saturation Soi~95% attained under drainage would typically result in an initial oil relative permeability kroiDrain>90% (close to a single-phase relative permeability of 100%).

The Bhagyam history match was based on liquid rate control, as water is the dominant flowing phase. Results were excellent compared to previous model versions (Figure 15). With virtually no modifications changes to the static model, field and regional watercut matches were excellent, and a large number of wells were reasonably matched on the first attempt. A limited number of baffles were modeled where appropriate, based on pressure data and seismic evidence.
