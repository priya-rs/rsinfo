The Case 1 field structure is a faulted collapsed crest roll-over anticline, elongated in an east-west direction and bounded to the north and south by 2 major south-hading synthetic growth faults (Figure 1).

The stacked reservoir sequences in the field are interpreted to consist of tidally influenced shore face packages intersected by channel sand bodies in a clastic coastal depositional environment. The hydro-carbon accumulations are mainly in fault/dip closures. There are 25 hydrocarbon-bearing sands A-B- & C - sand packages) between the depths of 5500 and 11050 ftss. The sands are laterally continuous and correlatable across the field based on well penetration (See Figure 2 showing Case 1 field correlation panel and Figure 3 a type log).

The Case 1 Reservoir A structure is an elongated east-west trending faulted rollover anticline, bounded to the north and south by two major growth faults (Figure 4).

The Reservoir A is about 200 feet thick and interpreted as a deltaic deposit comprising of amalgamated channel bodies that have eroded the pre-existing shore face deposits. Shore face relics were preserved in few wells. From available sidewall sample descriptions and wireline log data, the reservoir is made up of fine to coarse grained sands, and it exhibits retrograding and aggrading stacking patterns. The Reservoir A package is further subdivided into three flow units comprising two sand units separated by an extensive shale unit (Figure 5).

To capture the uncertainty in facies, the standard deviations of facies proportions from all the wells were calculated. These standard deviations were applied to the base case facies portions to generate the low and high case facies models. To model the Low and High case of NTG, low and high case NTG curves were used. Similar to the base case NTG modelling a facies based NTG value assignment was done for Channel, USF, MSF and Shale facies, while for the Heteroliths facies NTG was modelled using the upscaled well log along with defined variogram inputs using SGS algorithm. The first SPDC well predictive MBAL model was built in 2008 in Reservoir A incorporating at well level the interpretation (Figure 11a) of the MRT data acquired from the wells in this reservoir.

The model (Figure 11b) was initialized with static volumetric and performance-based GIIP of 1.6Tscf based on limited production and pressure data. The model predicted this reservoir would go off 450 MMScf/d plateau in 2010 and recent production performance confirmed the robustness of the plateau prediction (Figures 20 and 21). Also using contact monitoring plot on a stick diagram Figure 11b, the model predicted imminent water breakthrough in well 28 and 23 and recent production performance has confirmed the robustness of this prediction.

However model predicted early end of life due to very restrictive constraints, narrow dynamic uncertainties applied and under-estimated GIIP, Figure 11c.

Later production performance led to relaxation of those constrains and dynamic uncertainties leading to improve recovery factor range from 67 – 80% as shown in multi-scenario P/Z versus Gp analysis for this moderately water drive reservoir, Figure 11d. As we reached the upper limit of the multi-scenario analysis RF range with yearly tendency of producing against negative reserves, this gave rise to a more comprehensive integrated reservoir review study based on ample performance data available in 2014.

GIIP was re-estimated from straight-line material balance analysis technique of Havlena and Odeh confirming higher Low, Base and High GIIP 1.9, 2.04 and 2.14 Tscf respectively from static volumetric review Figure 12.

Subsequently uncertainty analysis was carried out to identify the key variables based on their impact on gas ultimate recovery as shown in Figure 13.

Potential communication in the aquifer leg between Reservoir A and A2100N or a break in the shale layer separating both reservoirs is thought to be the most plausible explanation for the significant rise in PGWC seen in the central part of the field (Figure 14). The produced gas water contact is one of the history match parameters as it was measured in 6 wells in Case 1 Reservoir A reservoir (4 Saturation logs-Case 1 09,11,14,17 and 2 newly drilled wells Case1 -30 and 31), Figure 14b. The logs from these wells show a significant rise in wells 17, 31, 9 within 1-2kms from Case 1_28 which was shut-in in 2013 for excessive water production.

With the key subsurface uncertainties identified, an experimental design algorithm was used to generate a sufficient number of runs that met the history match criteria. The output is a group of runs which all match the historical data to within 10% but yield a spreads in the final ultimate recovery as shown in Figure 15 These runs were in turn used to generate a response surface/proxy, Figure 16.
