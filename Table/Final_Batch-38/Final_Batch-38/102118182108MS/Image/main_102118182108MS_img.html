A bar chart is constructed to perform more meaningful analyses (see Fig. 5). It is clear that inlet pressure has to be higher at small diameters and it should decrease by increasing the internal diameter. Trend line shown in Fig. 5 clearly describes this fact. Controversially, liquid holdup is at minimum degree with minimum pipe internal diameter and then, it rises as the diameter increases. Therefore, the optimal pipe diameter should represent a trade-off between both minimum inlet pressure and minimum holdup. Pipes with diameters 24, 26 and 28 inches led to low inlet pressures with a much lower liquid holdup than the initial gas pipeline configuration.

Sensitivity analysis was performed to observe the difference in the intensity of liquid holdups between 24, 26 and 28 inches pipes. Figures 6, 7, 8 present the comparison of diameters by intensity of holdups. Thus, a 28-inch pipeline requires the lowest inlet pressure, but it has significantly higher liquid holdup compared with a 24-inch pipeline. Pressure drop comparison between 24-, 26- and 28-inch pipes does not show significant differences.
