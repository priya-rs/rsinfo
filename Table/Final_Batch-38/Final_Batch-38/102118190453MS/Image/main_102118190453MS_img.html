It is well established in the surfactant science literature that surfactants in water, having hydrophobic and hydrophilic (literally water hating and water loving) parts in the same molecule, tend to self-aggregate at or above the critical micelle concentration (CMC) to minimise the overall free energy of the system. As a result, a surfactant transitions through several highly ordered phases as its concentration is increased [10-13]. These structures range from individual spherical micelles, on the nanometer scale, to packed rod-like structures which are much larger, see Figure 1.

Figure 2 shows a gel diagram example for a blend of surfactants, a 3:1 blend of a C12,13- 7PO-sulfate and a C20-24 internal olefin sulfonate, in water. The nature of the blend, whether it is visually a liquid, a flowable gel (discerned by tipping the container to see it flow), or a solid gel, can be seen by making up different concentrations of the blend at temperatures between 0°C and 90°C. For this system at ambient temperature (20°C), a liquid is present <37% AM (easy to handle), a flowable gel 37-52% AM (more difficult to handle), a solid gel 52-65% AM (practically impossible to handle) and a flowable gel phase is re-established >65%AM.

As reference points to compare the viscosity behavior of surfactant concentrates, Figure 3 shows the viscosity as a function of shear rate of two common foods, liquid honey (a viscous liquid) and smooth peanut butter (a paste). Liquid honey is a useful reference for surfactant concentrates as it is a high viscosity liquid that is still pourable at ambient temperature (20-25°C), its viscosity being around 10,000 cP. In contrast, peanut butter is not pourable and its viscosity climbs to 100,000 cP at a shear rate of 10 s-1. Liquid honey is largely a Newtonian liquid3 whereas peanut butter is non-Newtonian. Shear rate dependency of viscosity of a surfactant concentrate is important as it determines how it should be handled in facilities, for mixing and diluting, and what equipment should be used.

Figures 4a, 5a, and 6a show the viscosity with shear rate and temperature of three low AM (20-30%AM) surfactants, C20-24 IOS, C15-18 IOS and the AAS C12,13 -13PO-sulfate. All three concentrates are viscous liquids with viscosities <10,000 cP at 10 s-1 shear rate at ambient temperature, less than liquid honey. This illustrates why low AM concentrates are often supplied for small volume projects, single-well tests and small multi-well pilots [2, 3], as their beneficial handling behavior in smaller project facilities tends to outweigh the benefit of supplying a high AM concentrate with a lower shipping cost.

An example of an engineering approach being successfully applied to a paste-like HAM concentrate is for a 62% AM IOS surfactant blend supplied to a multi-well pilot in West Siberia, Russia, the scope of the pilot being described elsewhere [14]. Figure 7 shows the three steps to supply and mix/dilute the paste. In Step 1 the surfactant is manufactured hot (at >60 °C) and transported in 20 ton insulated tanktrucks to a drumming location to package the product (still hot and pourable) into 55-gallon (~180 kg) drums.

Mixing of the delivered surfactant concentrate in the large storage tank of a project facilities is highly advisable (and the norm) as it tends to physically separate over time. I t is important that the concentrate is fully homogeneous before it is diluted and mixed with other components. A visual, qualitative aid showing how to select the mixing equipment appropriate to the surfactant concentrate’s viscosity is shown in Figure 8.

An additional benefit of mixed surfactant systems, such as high%AM AAS/IOS blends, is that they typically result in lower viscosities than the standalone high AM IOS parent as they take on the more fluid behavior of the AAS component [5]. Figure 9 illustrates this with the viscosity data at 20 and 60°C for a 73% AM AAS/IOS blend consisting of a 3/1 mixture of C12-13 -7PO-sulfate / C20-24 IOS. The high AM blend at 20°C has a viscosity <10,000 cP across the 1 to 100 s-1 shear rate range which can be compared to the HAM C20-24 IOS component (Figure 4b) which has a viscosity >100,000 cP at 10 s-1 at 20°C.
