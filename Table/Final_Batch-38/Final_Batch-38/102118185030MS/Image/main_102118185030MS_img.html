The slip-corrected profile (N2) permeability values estimated for the Bakken slabbed core at the ‘in-situ’ stress conditions range between 1.1×10−3 and 4.7×10−1 mD (Fig. 3). High resolution profile permeability provides a more accurate portrayal of the vertical heterogeneities within the reservoir (Unit A; Fig. 3) and reveals two distinct permeability trends. As indicated before (Cronkwright et al., 2016), this data can be used to identify net pay cut offs and more accurately predict the distribution of producible hydrocarbons.

Because the measurement resolution cannot be reflected in the vertical grid resolution of the simulation model, these properties were averaged and scaled up to the grid resolution (~3 ft). Knowing that the initial pressure in the Viewfield Bakken is around 2600 psi, which is well above the oil saturation (bubble point) pressure of 1480 psi, the only remaining parameter to evaluate is the initial water saturation. To capture the physics of the fluid flow within the fracture pathways, logarithmically spaced local grid refinement is used. With this arrangement, the middle fine grid designated for the fracture plane is assigned a high permeability and the grid sizes increase on both sides of the fracture plane symmetrically. Fig. 7 presents the final results of the history match at the field level (8 wells combined). Even under the unusual and complex arrangement of fluids in this reservoir (i.e. overlying aquifer instead of the typical configuration of bottom water), an acceptable match within a reasonable number of iterations was accomplished. Further scrutiny of Fig. 7 reveals that the simulated water production rate between days 600 and 800 (Fig. 7-b) is less than the actual production rates.

For relative permeability data, Corey’s correlation is utilized for both the matrix and hydraulic fractures to construct the initial curves, and thereafter, the end-points and exponents are tuned until the mismatch error becomes fairly small and stable. The final determined two-phase curves of oil-water and gas-oil systems for the matrix and the hydraulic fractures are shown in Fig. 8.

Fig. 9 displays the final history match results for well “B”, which is converted into a water injector after 450 days of production (Fig. 9-d). Water injection begins when the pressure near the well region has reached a low value of 1450 psi (close to oil bubble point pressure). However, the well bottom-hole pressure starts to build up immediately and rather quickly hits the maximum constraint pressure of 3,200 psi. This signifies the dominant effect of low matrix permeability on the water front advancement when the fracture density is sub-optimal in the offset producers.

Fig. 10-a illustrates the model-predicted long-term performance of primary recovery in this reservoir. To generate the graph, no water injection conversion is assumed to occur and all eight wells continue to act as producers. Fig. 10-b, in contrast, shows the oil production performance of the water-flood scheme if the current operation continues. The difference in recovery factor for the two scenarios is 11% (15% for primary versus 26% for waterflood). There is also a considerable difference in well productivity levels.

Acknowledging the fact that the fractures in this section of the Bakken are already in communication with the top aquifer, it would be instructive to investigate the situation where this does not occur. Fig. 11 compares these two cases. For primary recovery (Fig. 11-a), communication with the aquifer makes a significant difference in short-term and long-term performance, where a 5% difference in recovery factor would be observed over the first 5 years. The detrimental effect of water encroachment from the aquifer would be less on the waterflood scenario (Fig. 11-b).

In both cases, continuous injection and WAG scenarios are considered. Similar injection volumes at reservoir conditions were used in the gas flooding and waterflooding cases. A WAG ratio of one, with alternating period of 6 months was considered. Production performance of these four operations are shown in Fig. 12. Oil recovery factors are approximately 21% and 29% for continuous immiscible and miscible gas injection scenarios, respectively. These values increase to 25% and 34% when the operations switch to WAG.

In miscible gas flooding, maximum miscibility advantages are realized when the average reservoir pressure is maintained above the MMP. In continuous injection, after CO2 breakthrough, reservoir pressure drops below the MMP and thereafter the net CO2 utilization factor increases dramatically. As shown in Fig. 13,with an optimized WAG operation (after adjustment of its relevant parameters such as slug size and WAG ratio), it is possible to keep the average reservoir pressure above the MMP line and maximize the recovery.

As mentioned earlier, and depicted in Fig. 14, gravity segregation can eventually lead to the escape of injected gas (CO2) into the top aquifer. Accurate quantification of the leakage parameters (amount and rate of leakage) requires supplementary geological modelling of the Lodgepole, which is beyond the scope of this study. However, upon characterization of the leakage, modification of the operations can be considered for co-optimization of oil recovery and CO2 storage within the reservoir and the top aquifer. This will be explored in future work.
