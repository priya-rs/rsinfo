The reservoir consists of fluvio-deltaic sands that were deposited along a SW-NE direction While, the reservoir package is correlatable as a whole, individual sands seen in wells cannot be correlated across the field due to the inherent heterogeneity in a fluvial reservoir. While individual sand bodies are discontinuous, lateral continuity is established by amalgamation of these sand bodies. A discontinuous shale layer subdivides the reservoir package in to the upper and lower sand zones (Figure 1). This shale is not seen at all the wells, and is likely to be a result of subsequent erosion by channel bodies rather than non-deposition. These gaps in the shale layer allow vertical communication across the reservoir.

 

 

 

Additionally, it is important to understand whether the well is in the pseudo steady state or not for validity of RTA. To establish this, a diagnostic plot of pwf v/s q is used (Figure 4). If the trend, as indicated in the figure (marked with red lines) is observed, RTA can be conducted for that well.

Analysis of the facies proportions seen at the wells showed areas with high proportion of channel sands. These wells were grouped into ‘fairways’ oriented along the regional depositional trend (Figure 5, red dotted lines indicate fairways). Wells that lie within the geologically determined ‘fairways’ coincide with the well-connected wells identified from the RTA analysis. This gave us more confidence in the concept of ‘fairways’ and a clearer understanding of the preferential movement of water in the reservoir.

The first step towards understanding the depositional setting was to analyze the log data, as indicated by Fig. 6. A facies identification scheme was developed by qualitative study of the gamma ray, neutron-density, resistivity and sonic data.

Saturation logs and PLTs are crucial for understanding the vertical movement of water. Among the data sets available is a PLT from a recent well and 3 other saturation logs (Figure 7). The flowing PLT data indicated that the bottom most layer produces water even though the zones above produce the maximum oil. The saturation data from other wells (that had broken water) also indicated that water was approaching from the bottom. The lower sand zone is better in terms of properties – thicker with more amalgamation of channels – resulting in more lateral connectivity.

Given this understanding (from available PLTs), that water is coming in from the bottom, it becomes important to check the depth of all well perforations to identify wells under threat, represented in Figure 8.

 

The concept here is a fairway system, indicated by Fig 10, where water is moving in from the bottom sand zone. In different scenarios, different analytical techniques would come in handy for validating the established concept and we propose that the merits and validity of those techniques be assessed on the reservoir and fluid system.

Stiles calculations (modified for an aquifer system) show that the timing for Well A and Well B are fairly consistent (difference in time of around 3 months) with the actual timing of water breakthrough in these wells. The results for both wells are depicted in Figure 11. Post this initial testing for Well A and Well B, Stiles’ conceptual model was calibrated to predict WBT timing for other wells. Figure 12 shows these prediction results.

Figure 13 shows the three fairway realizations modelled for the upper sand zone (top figure) and the lower sand zone (bottom figure) that resulted in a good history match on WBT timing. The coloured bands are the fairways while the grey background represents the non-fairway region. The colours have no relevance to the properties of the fairways and have been merely used here to identify the different fairways. Fairways in the lower sand zone are wider and better connected as compared to the upper sand zone (Figure 13). This is evident in the well logs, where thicker channel sands are seen in the lower sand zone, interpreted to be a result of amalgamation of several channels.

Additionally, in order to bring the water breakthrough match closer to the actual water breakthrough timing observed and within the match tolerance criteria, permeability multipliers were introduced to create a contrast in the stochastically populated permeabilities between fairways and non-fairway regions. Subsequently, these realizations were used to predict fluid recovery and water breakthrough risks in addition to validating the results from analytical studies. Table 3 shows the summary of history match quality on water breakthrough timing and Figure 14 a, b shows the water breakthrough match for two wells in the production period and Figure 15 shows water production forecast from the dynamic model.
