Performance Summary of Active Wells (Figure 1): Maps and trends of oil, gas, and water production; well vintage and grouping analyses, by layer and block. These analytics provide a quick, automated framing analysis of reservoir trends.

Offline Well Analysis (Figure 2): Special emphasis is given to wells currently offline, which often present the quickest and easiest opportunities to raise production. The cause of each well’s shutin is characterized based on trends in water cut (WCUT), gas/oil ratio (GOR), and pressure (e.g. a rapid increase in WCUT is flagged as a well watering-out). These analytics will lead to specific opportunities to restore production from offline wells.

Decline Curve Analysis (Figure 3): The accuracy of automated decline curve analysis is improved through automated event detection, wherein trend breakpoints are detected mathematically (Castineira et al., 2014). These breakpoints may correspond to specific well events such as a choke change or workover; a field event, such as a change in backpressure; or a downhole event, such as a change in flow regime or drive mechanism. Any of these causes will necessitate a discreet decline curve fit, which is necessary for both future forecasting of each well, and for development of type curves to be applied for that well type or lift mechanism. Remaining reserves are estimated by well, and trends in EUR and PDP are identified by layer and block.

Reservoir Contact Analysis (Figure 4): Analytics and statistics describing reservoir contact are performed for all wells over time, by zone, accounting for well type and lift mechanism. This analysis combines data from the completions, formation tops, and production/injection databases to automatically characterize the completion contact on a layer basis. Metrics such as contact intensity (ft/MMstb), defined as cumulative length of contact in a layer divided by its oil in place, allow missed opportunities to be identified for future targeting.

Flow Unit Allocation (Figure 5): The flow unit allocation module estimates the production and injection for each formation, in each well, at each month, allowing a granular perspective in performance by formation and by well, which is used in many other modules of this methodology. Stochastic Material Balance (Figure 6):): Based on the possible range of each input, a single and multi-tank material balance (Matringe et al., 2015) history match is performed stochastically, providing a range of likely outcomes and forecasts. Instantaneous and cumulative drive indices are described for each layer and block, with associated scenarios of oil in place and aquifer strength.

Fractional Flow Analysis and Heterogeneity Index (Figure 7): The oil, gas, and water fractional flow for all wells is provided over time, with estimated source of water breakthrough provided when available. This is based on each well’s monthly production, PVT curves, and estimated reservoir pressure at each timestep. Sweep Efficiency Estimation (Figure 8): Areal sweep, vertical sweep, and displacement efficiency are quantified using analytical correlations. Total sweep efficiency is estimated as a function of pore volumes of water injected, or pore volumes swept through aquifer influx.

This tool relies on a customized implementation of dynamic time warping (Sakoe and Chiba, 1978), which was originally developed to find the optimal time alignment between two time series. The algorithm was customized to take as input vetted formation top picks (i.e. manually validated/picked by a geologist) in a few key wells called pivot wells. The algorithm then proceeds to correlate these vetted interpretations from the pivot wells to their corresponding dependent wells (Figure 9). Pivot wells are critical to this tool because they provide key control data points that can assist the algorithm in areas of significant geological change due to faulting, stratigraphic pinchouts, or lateral changes in facies.

Given the corrected logs provided by the petrophysicist, and the validated/corrected stratigraphic correlation discussed above, a semi-automated workflow using Petrel™ enables the streamlined construction of a 2.5D geological model (Figure 10). These workflows allow the incorporation of hard data points from logs as well as generic depositional trends specific to each zone (e.g. major fluvial/channel orientations) to quickly create fit-for-purpose models and maps. Despite the fact that 2.5D models have lower vertical resolution (i.e. one vertical cell per stratigraphic zone) compared to conventional 3D models, they capture the same lateral trends for any given zone and have the added benefit of being relatively easy to maintain and quality check.

First, a missed net pay log is created in every well using an algorithm that incorporates the most recent completion data together with a net pay log reflecting various petrophysical cutoffs (e.g. porosity, permeability, water saturation, etc.). The algorithm correlates perforation intervals to vertical net pay blocks so as to identify missed pay intervals that are not in communication (disconnected) from existing perforations due to vertical flow barriers (Figure 11).

Matrix-dominated systems: The algorithm estimates a drainage radius for every well/zone by relating the completed hydrocarbon pore volume thickness (HCPVT: estimated from the logs) to the allocated EUR in the completed zone. The algorithm also calculates a drainage index (DI) for every completed zone, which is defined as the ratio of the completed HCPVT to the original HCPVT. In other words, small DIs reflect areas where only thin sand layers have been accessed/drained and therefore have more oil remaining in either overlying or underlying sand packages. Figure 12 shows examples of zone-specific drainage index grids that combine information from both the drainage radius and drainage index.

The training set used to train the model consists of past completion intervals, and the training attributes are derived/calculated specifically over these intervals. For instance, a permeability training attribute is averaged from log values extracted specifically from the completion interval. Figure 13 shows an example model’s performance during training, validation, and testing phases. Once the model is trained, the testing dataset is collected by deriving the testing attributes specifically over the target missed net pay intervals. Table 1 shows various training attributes together with their corresponding testing attributes. While neural networks can incorporate relationships between several types of attributes, their reliability greatly depends on the size of the training dataset.

For every opportunity, the analog well set is defined as the set of wells producing from the opportunity’s target zone, and located within the spatial neighborhood (i.e. within radius R or part of N closest points), and within the temporal neighborhood (i.e. started producing within the last M years). Note that the neighborhood parameters R, N, and M are user-defined. A P10 and P90 IP/EUR is extracted from the analog well set. The P50 is calculated by interpolating the IPs/EURs of the analog wells based on weights that reflect their spatial and temporal proximity. To calculate confidence, the predicted IP/EUR from the previous step is compared to the P10, P50, and P90 IP/EUR of the analog well set as shown in Figure 14.

An economic model is prepared for all producing wells in the field, quantifying the profitability of each well at current commodity prices. This process is depicted is Figure 15, which plots the monthly income (revenue minus OPEX) of each well on the left axis, and the breakeven oil price for each well on the right axis. According to this analysis, 13 wells currently producing may have been uneconomic at the price of $43/barrel. These wells have been recommended for testing and possible shut-in or cycling, and were added to the list of available wells in the production uplift and water shut-off categories. The average water cut of these wells is 97%.

Fifty-one active wells and thirteen inactive wells were identified with opportunities to raise production through wellbore rejuvenation, usually through optimization of artificial lift or improved stimulation practices. A summary of these results is shown in Figure 16, while the specific opportunities in one example well are summarized in Figure 17.
