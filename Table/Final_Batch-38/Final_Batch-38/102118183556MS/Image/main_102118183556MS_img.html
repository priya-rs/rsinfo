El-Morgan Field (Figure 01) was discovered by the drilling of the El-Tor-1 well in February, 1965. Two oil productive zones were found. Subsequently, these two zones were identified as the Belayim formation and Kareem formation (Figure 02). The drilling of second and third wells confirmed the commercial viability of the oil discovery. Production operation commenced on April 12, 1967. The Kareem (Figure 04) is medium-coarse grained, poorly sorted arkosic sands inter-bedded with laminated shales. Normal grading is common, and individual sand bodies sometimes show coarse erosive bases, resulting in sand stacking and good vertical communication. Sands are sheet-like and no evidence is seen for channelization. The Kareem likely consists of a series of overlapping lobes that were strongly influenced by rift-bathymetry.

High production rates from Kareem reservoir resulted in a rapid pressure decline (Figure 05) which caused a rapid increase in Gas-Oil Ratio (GOR) due to the high bubble-point pressure as well. This reservoir performance indicated that the underlying aquifer was not active enough to support production operations at high rates. As a result, GUPCO initiated evaluating the possibility of Water Injection in late 1970 to increase reservoir pressure and improve the production rate.

From 1975 through 1990, many wells had been drilled in Kareem reservoir with high withdrawal rate which in turn needs higher pressure support. That's why in mid-1990, Waterflooding strategy is changed; Infill-injectors were started to be used in addition to the peripheral injectors. This strategy resulted in a better Waterflooding efficiency for both displacing and sweeping the oil. It is noteworthy that the infill injectors were placed carefully in a manner helping in minimizing the bypassed oil. Figure (06) shows the wells status during that period.

October field is located in northern part of Gulf of Suez (Figure 19). It was discovered in 1970 with estimated STOOIP 2.38 Billion STBO, It is producing mainly from four formations (Nubia, Nezzazat, Nukhul and Asl) via around 100 wells. October's peak production hit 180,000 BOPD, and current production rate is around 23,000 BOPD.

Nubia formation is a high quality reservoir in terms of rock permeability. It is composed of four zones; TZ, MN, M1 and M2 (Figure 20). Their average permeability (air permeability from core plugs) is 100-300 mD, 200-500 mD, 500-1500 mD, 500-1000 mD respectively. That discrepancy in zones' permeabilities led to obvious vertical heterogeneity across Nubia zones.

After fifteen years of production, Nubia pressure hovered around the bubble point pressure. That's why Waterflooding started in 1996 to support the high offtake from Nubia wells by injecting in three wells (Figure 21) at that time (H11, G9 and G14). One of these injectors was dedicated to M2 zone only (G14) however the other two injectors were injecting in entire Nubia zones simultaneously.

One of the most common problems in dealing with Waterflooding is presence of high Permeability layers causing the water flood-front to be unsteady (or fingers out) (Figures 13 & 31). That's why Thermally-Activated Particles (or Polymers) TAP is used to somehow plug this high perm layer, and helps in directing the water into the unswept layers (Figures 31 & 35).

Normally these particles are injected in liquid phase with injected water, and then they get cross-linked when exposed to certain temperature for a certain period of time, and plug pore- throats of the flooded reservoir part (Figures 32 & 33). This technique is known commercially as BrightWater®.

In appraising LoSal™ technique, GUPCO conducted many core analyses on different reservoirs' samples in GoS to know what downside, most-likely and Upside cases are. In addition to that, we've also conducted SWCTT on the well M170-253 in Kareem reservoir, and results showed a reduction in (Sor) of 2 saturation units (SU). And at final, we came up with the prize range we'll use (Figure 36).

In general, LoSal™ showed an improvement in recovery factor (Figures 36 & 37) ranging from 21%from core-flood tests, up to 54% from single-well chemical tracer tests (SWCTT).

Normally High Salinity Waterflooding increases the "Adhesion Force" between the crude oil molecule and the surface (bounded oil).

Our first recommendation is to use SWOT Matrix technique in planning any job, study or project. SWOT matrix includes four sections: Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities and Threats.
