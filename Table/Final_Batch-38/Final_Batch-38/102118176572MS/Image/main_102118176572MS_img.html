The principle scheme of oil field development effectiveness using block-factor analysis instrument is given on Figure 1.

The division of oil field into blocks is done to simplify the analysis process and to define the most problem areas of the field that are needed in top priority operations to improve the effectiveness of the development system. Lithological and facies model (figure 2) can be used as a base for the division of oil field into blocks. Therefore, in the process of division the geological properties of formation are taken into account, and the collector within the bounds of block is presented by the similar geological characteristics.

Model displacement curve describes the process of oil displacement in layered heterogeneous formation. It is a function of displacement efficiency, variation efficiency, mobility ratio, initial and current watercut. By default fluid watercut is forecasted by modified model curve of El-Khatib [1] (Figure 3) based on after historical adaptation parameters: coefficient of Dykstra-Parsons (VDP) [2], residual oil saturation (Sor), relative water permeability (kwor) at the point of residual oil saturation and initial watercut (WC0).

Figure 4 shows the example of the factor analysis results for one block during 12 months. Each of the 12 lines of the histogram shows the value of oil production changing according to the first (base) month due to each possible factor. Dot line shows the oil production losses due to the natural (proved) watercut growth by the typical curve.

Further the static and dynamic points of “problemacity” for each block are summing up. Generalized chart of the blocks “problematicity” points assignment is provided on the figure 5.

The data of real exploitation is compared with theoretical (“ideal”) curve of displacement for each block (figure 6). The comparison of absolute deviation of watercut real value from the expected value is provided. The trend of real watercut during the last half year exploitation is estimated. In this case the most problematic sections of the field (according “Watercut” factor) are the blocks with a real watercut value significantly (more than 10%) higher than expected watercut value and the rate of watercut increase exceeds the ideal curve rate of increase.

Therefore the most problematic blocks according the “reservoir pressure” criteria are the blocks with reservoir pressure significantly lower than primary and proceeding to decrease pressure. Blocks with the reservoir pressure much higher than primary pressure also are in the group of problematic blocks with the I-st rank as these blocks have high possibility of overcompensation. The result of the block rating is “light-signal maps” (figure 7) based on the monthly data. These maps are simple and evident mean for diagnosis of field negative factors and further decision making.
