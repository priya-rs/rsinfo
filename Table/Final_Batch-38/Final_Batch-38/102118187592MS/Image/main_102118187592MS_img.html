KOC is fully engaged in standardization of its Reservoir Management Best Practices (RMBP), to meet industry-leading standards of excellence over onshore oil and gas fields in Kuwait (Figure 1). An executive-level Steering Committee is responsible for implementation oversight of workflow standards, policies and guidelines to ensure the enhancement and standardization of RMBP.

The framework for the implementation of best practices includes a Steering Committee chaired by the manager of Reservoir Management and includes field development-related managers from assets and support groups (Figure 2). Namely, KOC Field Development, Exploration, Research & Technology, Planning, and Well Surveillance managers. The Steering Committee provides leadership and governance authority for adopting the Reservoir Management Best Practices and has the ultimate responsibility for implementation of policies, best practices, standards, guidelines and workflows.

The six PIP clusters are shown in Figure 3. Each PIP was chartered to adopt, standardize, and implement best practice guidelines, processes, and workflows to close identified gaps in specific areas.

The six PIPs are linked in a way that naturally maximizes the optimization of RM. Figure 4 illustrates the linkage among the six PIPs with reservoir management being the linchpin (i.e., the part that is so vital it forms the center spoke for the other PIPs). Data governance and knowledge management are foundational and upon which everything builds. Starting with PIP #3, well surveillance is a way to mitigate risks to the production forecast and EUR.

As part of the stewardship process, due diligence reviews are scheduled regularly to focus on a specific aspect of the complex hydrocarbon production process (Figure 6). The processes have different timelines and require stewardship reviews at different intervals. KOC Reserves Management has some of these processes already in place. Overseeing the process cycle are due diligence activities that ensure that the process is being followed and constraints observed. Elements of this step are Exploration, Reserves, Capital Projects, and Reservoir Management Annual Reviews.

Each reservoir is unique with its own challenges and pre-existing conditions, which change over time. Establishing uniform best practices for waterflood management makes sense only if the best practices are general in nature rather than detailed and prescriptive. Each individual reservoir should have its own best practices. Figure 7 illustrates how the water-cut trend changed after applying waterflood management best practices.
