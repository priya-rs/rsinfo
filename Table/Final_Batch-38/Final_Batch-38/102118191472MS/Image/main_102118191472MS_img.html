For this work, the full-field model was converted to a compositional model using the Equation of State (EOS) model provided by the operator. A sector model subsuming the pilot location was generated from the calibrated full-field model as shown in Fig. 1(a). In this step, we retain the native vertical and areal grid resolutions in the pilot model, as well as the calibrated pore volume multipliers obtained from the full-field history matching (Olalotiti-Lawal et al., 2017).

We follow a two-step hierarchical approach for the calibration of the pilot model (Yin et al., 2011) as described in the general workflow schematic in Fig. 2. In the first step, we focus on a global update of spatial distribution of the model permeability field and the relative permeability functions. We apply the Grid Connectivity Transform (GCT) which enables spatial updates of the permeability field using a small set of basis functions (Bhark et al., 2011). In the second step, we fine-tune the pilot model permeability field by matching CO2 arrival times at the producers using the streamline-based Generalized Travel Time Inversion (GTTI) algorithm (Cheng et al., 2005; Cheng et al., 2004; He et al., 2002).

We first integrate the multiphase production and pressure data in the pilot model by updating the three-phase relative permeability functions and absolute permeability distribution. For model permeability field updates, a set of orthogonal basis functions Φi obtained by an eigen-decompostion of the grid connectivity Laplacian is utilized. A few of these basis functions are shown in Fig. 3.

A plot of the data misfit reduction obtained from GA in the global model calibration step is shown in Fig. 4(a). Although, the 'best' model is selected as the one with the least data misfit at the end of the 20 generations, we also provide a boxplot of the normalized distribution of the parameters in the 80 members of the population in the last generation in Fig. 4(b). Water endpoint relative permeability krwE turned out to have the most reduction in the spread with clear bias toward the maximum value (close to 1.0).

The results show a close agreement with the initial relative permeability function, which was obtained from the full-field model calibration (Olalotiti-Lawal et al., 2017). In fact, looking at the comparison of the relative permeability functions between initial and updated model in Figs. 5(a) and (b), it turns out that the oil-water relative permeability functions are retained from the full-field model, while the gas-oil functions are modified. It can be recalled that the field data integrated into the full-field model was predominantly based on a two-phase oil-water system. This justifies the preservation of the oil-water relative permeability function.

It is expected that a significant portion of injected CO2 is trapped in the subsurface and the proportion recovered provides an indication of the quality of sweep in the subsurface (Melzer, 2012). A field-wide comparison of CO2 recovery between the initial and updated models is shown in Fig. 13. An early CO2 breakthrough is obtained from the initial model response and hence, CO2 recovery is overestimated.

Next we compared the model response with gas saturation logs at two separate observation wells. The locations of the observation wells O1 and O2 relative to the other wells in the pilot pattern are shown in Figs. 14(a) and (b). In each of these figures, the observed data is compared with the gas saturation profiles at the wells. The wireline measurements serve as proxy for reservoir saturation logs (sigma responses) so that time variations in the in the measurements at specific zones down the observation well indicate gas invasion through that zone. Looking at Fig. 14(a), CO2 is expected to flow into Zone 2 of the reservoir at the location of O1 according to the observed data.

In Figs. 15 (a) and (b), visual comparisons of the CO2 plume between the initial and updated models at the beginning and the end of CO2 injection are provided. In all the plots presented, the plume shape was obtained at a threshold value of 0.3 CO2 model fraction. The preferential flow of CO2 towards PRD4 earlier discussed can be visually confirmed in Figs. 15 (a). As discussed in previous sub-sections, the CO2 fingering in the initial model resulted in earlier CO2 breakthrough and hence, unfavorable CO2 sweep compared to the updated model response. Also at the end of CO2 injection, as shown in Figs. 15 (b), CO2 never flowed towards PRD3 due to the poor hydraulic connectivity with INJ in the initial model.

The Lorentz curve shown in Fig. 16(a) shows a plot of the flow capacity F as a function of the storage capacity Φ. The 45-degree line typically mimics a piston-like displacement and the geometric area enclosed by this line and the Lorentz curve gives the Lorentz coefficient LC. Large values of LC indicate higher flow heterogeneity and therefore, less tracer sweep efficiency. A reduction of LC from 0.75 in the initial model to 0.64 in the updated model, with a corresponding 20% increase in tracer sweep efficiency (Fig. 16(b)) was deduced at from the analyses. The updated model shows much better flow connectivity compared to the initial model.

A CO2 WAG sensitivity and optimization study was conducted using the calibrated model. We apply a combination of Design of Experiments (DoE) based surrogate models to exhaustively search the parameter space that describe a region of feasibility of the problem. The workflow schematic is presented in Fig. 17.

Tornado charts of the parameter sensitivities on the two objective functions considered are presented in Fig. 18. As expected, CO2PVI shows a positive trend with OilRF but an opposite trend with CO2UF. Higher values of CO2PVI causes more mobilization of residual oil. Going by the definition in Eq. 20, higher OilRF in turn, causes the desired reduction in CO2UF. From Fig. 18, calculated sensitivities for WAGRATIO appear to disagree with common field observations.

In the construction of the proxy models, all the 4 dimensionless parameters were included. First 100 parameter combinations were generated using a Latin-Hypercube sampling in the parameter space. These parameter sets were evaluated using a commercial compositional finite difference simulator to calculate the OilRF and CO2UF objective functions. Plots of these objective functions with respect to time and HCPVI are provided in Figs. 19. To generate a surrogate model, a kriged response surface was then computed independently for the two objective functions.

We considered four types of correlation functions including linear, exponential, Gaussian and cubic functions in the construction of the proxy models (Lophaven et al., 2002). These proxy models were validated with 50 blind parameter combinations sampled within the bounds of the parameter space. These results of the proxy model validation are presented in Fig. 20. These figures show crossplots of numerical simulation responses versus proxy model predictions of both OilRF and CO2UF for all correlations functions. Clearly, the constructed proxy models shows good agreement with numerical simulation results for the blind parameter combinations. The proxy model based on the linear correlation function resulted in the best fit and therefore, was chosen for the CO2 WAG optimization.

A scatter plot of 20,000 sampled parameter combinations evaluated using the proxy model is presented in the objective function space in Fig. 21. Based on the preference of the operator, we select an optimal, intermediate and poor designs that correspond to 90, 50, and 10 percentiles of two objectives as shown in the figure. The design parameters for these three solutions are provided in Tables 3.

The schedules obtained for the three selected solutions were repeated for another two years for a total duration of 4 years for the CO2 WAG operations. The results obtained are shown in Table 4. We also provide the injection and production rate profile comparisons for the three cases in Fig. 22 and a visualization of the CO2 sweep in the three cases in Fig. 23. Clearly, these figures show good agreement with the numerical results. As shown in the figures, better CO2 sweep was achieved in the optimal case compared to other cases.

Finally we examine the value of the model calibration on field recovery estimation. Here we seek to estimate the comparative CO2 EOR performance between an uncalibrated reservoir model and a calibrated one. For this task, we conducted numerical simulations using optimized schedules based on the initial and updated pilot models. From the plot of OilRF as a function of time is provided in Fig. 24, it can be noticed that production forecasts from the initial model significantly underestimates the CO2 EOR performance in the field. Although not shown, cumulative injection volumes and well BHPs were kept consistent between the two scenarios. We provide an intuitive visualization comparing the CO2 sweep between the initial and updated models in Fig. 25.
