In the case of tight reservoirs, applying the CM can be particularly challenging because wells may be stimulated (e.g., fracturing, acid wash/squeezing) frequently, which changes their productivity index. Thus, choosing a time interval (time window), t0 < t < tmax, to perform connectivity analysis without violating this core assumption becomes tricky (Fig. 1) for large areas containing many wells

Performing an IWC evaluation over a window area (Figure 2) where there is a flux imbalance however raises a problem, because the CMP is based on material balance. Hence, when the total rate of injectors is significantly different from the total rate of producers, we should add a term to the model to account for the imbalance.

In a balanced injection/production case, the CMP-PW model can accommodate a reasonable range of fluid-rock system compressibilities caused by a local injection volume imbalance. To demonstrate this, we added a barrier between I01 and P01 in the semi-homogeneous 5 × 4 model (Figure 20). In this low permeability model, adding the barrier creates a local imbalance in that portion of the model.

Plots of APW-producer vs τproducer show that pseudo well connectivity increases with an increasing time constant for 8*10-6 1/psi ≤ Ct ≤ 1*10-5 1/psi(Figure 21). Within the specific compressibility range, we observe the relation between APW-producer vs Tproducer appears and the slope of the relation decreases as the system becomes more compressible.

In a balanced system, for the applicable range of compressibility, the CMP-PW pseudo well accounting for the fluid left in the low permeability model distributes it to the producers with preference given to the wells with longer time constants (e.g., P02 and P01). Sample injection allocation factors are shown with the connectivity map for the case with ct = 8*10-6 1/psi (Figure 22).

In this work, we used the CM-PW to evaluate interwell connectivity in two areas from the East Pembina Field (Figure 32). The field was under primary depletion until 1967, when waterflooding began. It produces 37° API oil and the average permeability of the producing zone is about 0.7 md (IHS Accumap, 2014).

In the first window area (Figure 33), there are 4 producers and one injector. We have chosen three time windows to study the effects of well treatments on interwell connectivity (Figure 34). We obtained a very good match between CMP-PW predicted and measured rates for all the three windows (Table 7).

The analysis of the first time window shows that the normalized connectivities between well pairs I62-P224 and I62-P219 are large (Figure 35). Since the pseudo well connectivity is directed towards the conglomerate bearing zone in the east, we infer that the conglomerate bearing zone contributes to the connectivities in this window, especially to the production of P219 and P184.

Core data from unfractured wells P225 and P184 also suggest the presence of conglomerate in the east, since the flow capacity (permeability-thickness product, kh) of P184 is 2 ½ times that of well P225. A plot of λ’ vs kmaxh shows the well P184 having higher kmaxh than P225 possesses a strong positive connection with the injector whereas P225 has a negative λ’ (Figure 36).

P225 is the only well having λ’ < 0. It appears the operator may have identified this issue. The well was acid squeezed twice and fractured with 8.5 tons of 10/20 sand after the first window (AER Database). Connectivity analysis in the second time window (Figure 37) indicates the connectivity of P225 increased by 0.16 (i.e., Δλ’ = λ’win2 – λ’ win1 = 0.16), and the connectivity of I62 with other producers either decreased (I62 – P184) or remained the same (I62 – P219).

The 2nd window period IWC analysis reveals that, after the well treatments at other producers, injection support from I62 towards P184 diminished. Perhaps the operator was also aware of this problem. In 1986, P184 was fractured with 27 tons of 20/40 sand (AER Database). The connectivities in the window area are again changed (Figure 38).

A plot of Δ λ’ vs proppant tonnage for the treatments of wells P225 and P184 shows a direct proportion (Figure 39), where we have added the point at the origin on the basis that zero proppant should leave the connectivity unchanged.

Prior to the first time window, all 4 producers were fractured (Figure 40). The CMP-PW analysis (Figure 41) reveals that the largest connectivities are oriented in the NE-SW direction, which is consistent with the direction of maximum horizontal principal stress in the region (Bell et al., 1994).

Before the second time window, P145 was fractured again, although the reasons are unclear because the CMP-PW analysis suggests this well was supported by both I37 and I40. After the P145 refracture, connectivity with I37 and I40 decreased and P127 is the only well having positive normalized connectivity (Figure 42).

The second period analysis shows that P145 does not benefit from the nearby injectors. P145 was fractured for the third time prior to the third window period (Figure 40). Re-fracturing P145 does not appear to have been effective (Figure 43); I40 decreased connectivity with the surrounding producers.

It appears that injector induced fractures controlled the connectivity distribution of I40 during window 1 (Figure 44). Having the steepest slope of the plot and the largest connectivities oriented in the direction of larger horizontal stress in the first time window suggests there may be an injector induced fracture. When the slope of the plot decreases in the second time window, we observe that the connection with P145 was lost.
