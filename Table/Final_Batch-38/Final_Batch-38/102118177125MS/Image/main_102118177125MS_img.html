Upon continued transgression and rising sea level, the tidal deposits give way to more marine sedimentation, which is generally composed of more massive, often structureless, sand rich sediments of the Upper T with variable and poorly sorted grain sizes because intense marine bioturbation has reworked mud into the sandy layers. The sand is frequently cemented with carbonate due to dissolution of shelly material. Of importance in the Upper T sands is the presence of glauconite of variable intensity but which can constitute up to 40% of the rock.

Despite the presence of light gravity oil in the Upper and Middle T sands from mud gas readings, oil staining in drill cuttings, and the mixed results of the small number of production tests conducted, the lack of potential as judged from conventional petrophysical methods, made any focused attention to the T sands a low priority. About 5 years ago, a more concerted effort on testing the Upper T sandstone began. Figure 5 shows how bed thickness variations influence log measurements. The thicknesses of shale beds in green are constant, and the thicknesses of the sands beds in white are varied. It can be clearly seen that as the sand layer thickness decreases, the values of the resistivity, PEF, gamma ray, density, and neutron curves are suppressed.

In Figure 6 below, are shown the vertical resolution and radial depth of investigation characteristics for several well logs. There are significant differences for the volume of investigation between deep-sensing resistivity logs and density logs, which causes them to respond differently to oil bearing layers, especially when those layers are thinner than the vertical resolution of both sets of logs.

As can be seen in Figure 7, the presence of sand / shale laminations less than 1 foot is very high, and in spite of the sand being oil saturated in the upper photo, the resitivity only measures average values across the interval.

In Figure 8, is displayed a comparison between two wells where Chorongo Este 1 has a greater presence of laminations and as such the resistivity is more affected. Both wells have a comparable concentration of glauconite.

Based on the core descriptions, we can see that in the Middle T is a greater presence of intercalations with less than two feet in thickness, which are not clearly reflected in the gamma ray curve, as can be seen in Figure 9.

In Figure 10, it can be clearly seen how log values of RHOB vs. PHIN change across the different T sandstone zones of Mariann Norte 1. The Lower T zone in red shows values confined to quite a small range and concentrated in the sandstone–limestone interval as would be expected in a massive, homogeneous reservoir.

Figure 11 shows the core / log relationship from Mariann Norte 1. Despite the suggestion of intercalations in the GR and porosity logs in the Upper T, laminations are not visible in the cores as shaly interbeds.

Figure 12, shows the spectral gamma ray data over the cored interval of the Mariann Norte 1 well and provides additional evidence of the heterogeneity of the rock.

As can be seen in Figure 13 of the Mariann Norte core, the presence of oil saturation doesn’t exist over the entire interval of the Upper and Middle T sandstone.

Glauconite variably affects different well logs and must be taken into account when doing petrophysical analyses. In the case of GR curves, in Figure 14, high values of gamma ray can be seen corresponding to sandy intervals (no shaly content) and also high concentration of glauconite, showing clearly the influence that glauconite can have. In this case, the GR would not meet the Vshale pay cutoff if the sand was oil saturated and hydrocarbon content would be underestimated.
