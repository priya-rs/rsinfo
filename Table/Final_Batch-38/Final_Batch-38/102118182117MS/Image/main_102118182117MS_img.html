The Salym group of fields is located in Khanty-Mansi autonomous district of the Russian Federation (Fig. 1). The deposits are at an advanced stage of development and require measures to increase oil recovery and reduce water production. The primary development is the Cherkashinskaya suite, which includes oil-saturated reservoirs AS11.1, AS11.2 and AS11.3. Well was perforated in intervals of AS11.2, and AS11.3 reservoirs (Fig. 2). A complicating factor for hydraulic fracturing in these formations is the proximity of a thick and highly permeable aquifer AS10 (Fig. 2), which, if penetrated by a hydraulic fracture, can lead to a significant increase in fluid rate and water production, and the expected increase in oil production might not be realized. This creates an additional load on the oil treatment facility. In this case, correct simulation and fracturing without penetrating highly permeable aquifers is important. Modern software simulators model fracture geometry correctly but require calibration and validation by various methods.

Openhole logging allowed reservoir properties to be determined, including an average porosity of 19%and an average permeability of 30 md. The well was drilled and put into production in January 2009. Two water injection wells (from the east and west sides) were also drilled for pressure support purposes, with water injection starting in January 2009. Before fracturing, the treatment fluid rate was 112 m3/day, and the water cut was 92%; the high water content resulted from injection water breakthrough from the injection wells (Fig. 3).

Fig. 4 shows the flow measurement results (2015): integral and differential inflow profiles for water (blue) and oil (red). It can be observed that only the top two perforation intervals function, and the production is proportional to the reservoir permeabilities (K-merge curve).

Fig. 5 shows the results of logging: cementing quality assessment, CBL wave pattern, slowness of P-, S-, and St-waves, slowness of the fast and slow S-waves (cross-dipole sensor data), Young's modulus, Poisson's ratio, and anisotropy.

Fig. 6 shows sigma curves for the proximal sigma of formation near detector (SFND) and the distal sigma of formation far detector (SFFD) probes, as well as the hydrogen content curve thermal neutron porosity (TRNP) in rocks beyond the borehole. In the first stage, the curves are obtained by three logging techniques for the well without hydraulic fracturing. These curves characterize undisturbed medium and are a point of reference for data obtained from logging after hydraulic fracturing.

The initial designs in F3D (Fig. 7) and P3D (Fig. 8) models were prepared using logging data interpretation based on standard logging data and model calibration by fracturing treatments performed on neighboring wells. Calculation of hydraulic fracturing parameters was performed in various simulators, but for correct comparison, only data from one software tool is discussed here. It should be noted that simulations from other P3D simulators showed similar fracture parameters for the given treatment.

Before the major fracturing treatment, testing was performed using a minifrac (Fig. 9) to assess the treatment parameters. During the minifrac, sand (1 ton of 100-mesh) and proppant (3 tons of NRT 16/20-mesh) was pumped to clean the perforation zone. Analysis of the minifrac showed a fluid efficiency of 27%and final bottomhole shut-in pressure of 318 bar, which corresponded to the shut-in gradient of 0.138 bar/m.

The net pressure was 34 bar. After analyzing the minifrac, the decision was made to perform the main fracturing treatment per the initial design. During the main fracturing stage (Fig. 10), a buffer of 24 m3 (24.3%) of crosslinked 30-lbm gel and 36 tons of NRT proppant (16.5 tons of 16/20-mesh and 19.5 tons of 12/18-mesh) was pumped. The maximum proppant concentration was 1,000 kg/m3, while more than half of the proppant was pumped at greater than 800 kg/m3. The job was pumped as per design which also implied zero underflush volume for temperature logging immediately after the primary treatment.

The tubing and packer were lowered into the well to a depth of 2500 m; downhole pressure and temperature gauges were installed. Temperature logging was also prescribed, with the well resting first for 5 days. The temperature gradient was 3.9°C per 100 m, and the target reservoir temperature was approximately 87°C. Fig. 11 shows a summary of temperature logs produced before the fracturing treatment.

After the temperature log was recorded, the well was stimulated by installing coiled tubing, and nitrided fluid was injected for 10 hours. The tubing and packer were then extracted, and a cross-dipole sonic log and DBTDT were performed. Fig. 12 compares acoustic waves before hydraulic fracturing (black curves) and after hydraulic fracturing (green curves). The first block shows P-wave characteristics with a monopole transmitter, the second shows S-wave characteristics with a monopole transmitter, and the third shows transverse waves with a dipole transmitter.

The last logging performed after the injection of neutron-absorbing proppant was DBTDT (Fig. 13). Pressure values were compared, and the model was calibrated after fracturing by using downhole pressure gauge data and sonic log data (Fig. 14). Using the adjusted values of Young's modulus and Poisson's ratio in simulation (Fig. 5) changed the geometry of the hydraulic fracture. A comparison of pressures in the F3D simulator showed the fracture breakthrough into the aquifer (Fig. 15). Tellingly, the fracture did not develop in the entire water-bearing interval and was limited to the middle of the highly permeable formation. This kind of fracture growth resulted from the different geomechanical properties of rocks in the upper and lower parts of the water-saturated horizon. The denser rock and high permeability of the upper part of the aquifer stopped further growth of fracture height.

Fig. 16 shows a comparison of fracture height determined by acoustic and neutron logging. The difference between the total height of the fracture and the height filled with proppant was approximately 2 m, indicating agreement. It can be assumed that the acoustic method also registers the hydraulic fracture where rock discontinuity was created, but proppant did not secure this part of it. It is possible that the proppant secured the edges of the fracture, but its concentration was so small that it was not registered by DBTDT.

Temperature logging (Fig. 11) performed after the main fracture does not correlate with the fracture model in the simulator (Fig. 17), nor was it supported by DBTDT or the cross-dipole sonic log. In the lower perforation range (2595 to 2597 m), proppant was not recorded, although the temperature logging during the minifrac presented a weak response in the given interval, and the simulation showed fracture growth in this area. Fracture initiation occurred through the upper, highly permeable perforation interval, and its development moved primarily upward, to a lower-stress zone. It was observed that the hydraulic fracture broke into the aquifer, and an increase of water volume in the well was detected.

The start of well operations after fracturing showed an increase in fluid flow rates from 112 to 650 m3/day, with oil production increased from 8 to 60 m3/day (Fig. 18). The watercut was 92%. Production analysis confirmed that the fracture penetrated the high-permeability AS10 aquifer.
