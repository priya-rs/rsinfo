Field production facilities comprise a single unmanned installation (NUI), which is connected by a 13.4-km pipeline to the Markham J6A facilities (Fig. 1). After metering on J6A, the gas is exported to Den Helder in the Netherlands for conditioning and blending. Based on results from the A sand infill well, the operator objective was to assess the potential to drill additional development wells in the A sand reservoir unit, which contains ~50% of the total field gas initially-in-place (GIIP).

The Westphalian formation consists of fluvio-deltaic sediments that contain active and abandoned distributary channels interbedded with associated overbank and levee deposits. Based on core and production data, the basal A sandstones are generally of poorer quality than the overlying B, C, and D sandstone reservoirs (Fig. 2). However, the undepleted nature of the sandstones makes them an attractive target for (fracture stimulated) infill production wells.

The completion design (Fig. 3) was for a platform well that could be hydraulically fractured and cleaned up, with the installation of a downhole pressure/temperature gauge (DHPG) to provide accurate data for both the stimulation treatment and long-term reservoir management, as detailed in previous papers (Langford et al. 2014). A tubing hanger was used while fracturing, and the treatment was performedthrough the permanent completion string, which included a subsurface safety valve (SSSV).

Interval 5 was perforated using a 3 1/8-in., orientated, 0 to 180°, 4-spf multiflow 3106 TCP gun. The gun change was required because the final fracture location was through a double liner, 7-in. 32-lbm/ft and 4 1/2-in. 15.1-lbm/ft liners, as shown in Fig. 4.

The planned strategy was to use a 7-ft perforation length in a 10-ft gun carrier loaded with 4 spf at 0/180° gravity orientated phasing (Fig. 5). The planned charges to be run were a big-shot-based RDX charge, providing a large entrance hole to reduce the risk of premature bridging and screenout at the perforations. These are the same perforation charges run on the two most recent perforated stimulated wells by Centrica at the time; Kew (49/4c-7Y) and Ensign ED (48/14A-7Y) (Langford et al. 2014).

The azimuth of the 49/10A-G6z wellbore (Fig. 6) was 19.5° (NNE), with the maximum stress direction and therefore the azimuth of the fractures running 310° (NW-SE), almost perpendicular to the wellbore.

The reservoir thicknesses that were used in the pseudo-three-dimensional (P3D) fracturing simulator are shown in Fig. 7 (overleaf). The expected thickness of the A sandstone at Fracture 1 was 37 ft, whereas the thickness of the A sandstone increased to 140 ft at the location of Fracture 5 at the heel of the horizontal wellbore.

A detailed analysis of the time required to place the planned fractures (Fig. 11) showed that the operating time with the stimulation vessel within the 500-m zone of the platform was 5.35 days. The sailing time between the platform and the port in IJmuiden in the Netherlands was 6.68 days, while the standby time in the port was recorded at 14.97 days.

G-function analysis performed after each diagnostic test sequence showed that the before closure analysis (BCA) could be used to estimate permeability, which appeared to correlate well with the log-derived permeability (Fig. 12). The obtained G-function permeabilities were used as a basis for modelling the resultant fracture geometries.
