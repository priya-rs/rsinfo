Passive satellite systems measure the radiation from the sun that is reflected to the satellite off the Earth to record information about the Earth, whereas active satellite systems, such as radar, send a signal to the Earth s surface from the satellite and measure the signal that is reflected from the Earth. As the satellite passes over the Earth its antenna sends out a radar pulse with wavelengths of 3 to 24 cm depending on the satellite (Fig. 1).

At the same time the satellite records the return pulse that is reflected back. The opacity of the atmosphere is close to zero to these wavelengths. During processing, the phase of the return pulse is compared to the phase of the pulse from an earlier pass of the satellite (Fig. 2).

The figure below shows how we derive deformation from a difference in phase. If the Earth’s surface subsides, the emitted radar signal has to travel a further distance to reach the surface. This results in an extra fraction of the radar wave being reflected and recorded, known as the phase difference (shown in red). The recorded images are 2 D arrays of pixels with each pixel having data about phase and amplitude of the return signal (Fig. 3).

The phase differences are typically displayed initially as Interferograms that are color-scaled by radians of the wavelength (Fig. 4). However, because InSAR measures change in wave phase, a step called: phase unwrapping "needs to be done in the processing to account for any phase ambiguity. This is because only relative differences are determined and the integer number of wavelengths that the signal has travelled back and forth is unknown.

The radar pulses are not affected by visible light or low levels of cloud cover so acquisition is not limited to days that are sunny and cloud-free (Fig. 5). However, large amounts of atmospheric moisture slow the microwave energy, causing noise affecting the ground deformation signal.

The quality and amount of radar signal reflected back to the satellite depends upon the reflection characteristics of the ground (Fig, 6).

If the dominant reflections within a resolution cell are consistent enough throughout the measurement timespan, they can be used as discrete InSAR measurement points and are called Persistent Scatterers (PS). Buildings, roads, outcrops, and radar reflectors at GPS-defined locations are often used as PS points. Coherent and incoherent changes can be detected over time and interpreted using the Persistent Scatterer InSAR (PS-InSAR) technique. This uses a stack of slave radar images that are co-registered to a master radar image that has good geometry, high coherence, and low atmospheric effects (Fig. 7).

InSAR satellites are on polar, sun-synchronous orbits so, as the Earth rotates from west to east beneath them, they cover the same exact swath or strip of the Earth’s surface along the path azimuth at regular intervals of time that are unique to each satellite. (Fig. 8).

These are made by subtracting the phase data of the current revisit from the phase data of the previous revisit (see Fig. 4). They are initially scaled in radians with each fringe (one 'rainbow') corresponding to positive or negative deformation in the line of sight, relative to the satellites’s operating wavelength (Fig, 9).

The range of vertical deformation also has to be scaled positive (uplift) and negative (subsidence) using stable areas in the peripheral buffer zones as zero baselines. Time lapse profile plots can also be made for key areas in the AOI (Fig. 10).

The balancing is generally done per pattern of wells or by groups of patterns. If the VRR is over 1 then the reservoir may swell or injection might move out of zone. If the VRR is below 1 then the reservoir will lose drive energy and, especially for fluidsupported reservoirs like diatomite, may compact (Fig. 11).

The poster child for demonstrating the application and value of InSAR is the giant Belridge oil field in the southwest part of the San Joaquin valley, California (Fig. 12). It is thought to be the most heavily published field in the world because of its variety of complex reservoirs and assortment of problems caused by the Diatomite reservoirs of the Miocene Monterey formation.

Although the average oil saturation is a low 40-60, the porosity ranges from 55 % to 70 % so the amount of oil stored in given volume (e.g., an acre/ft) is very large (Allan, 2010). Matrix permeability is around 0.1 to 2.0 md – fairly typical for the grain and particle size (Fig. 13). However, the diatomite is fluid-supported and highly compressible (about 300 microsips compressibility) so it easily compacts or swells if reservoir pressure is not maintained by balancing injection to production (de Rouffignac, 1995).

Although the changes in ground elevation are muted and smoothed compared to the changes in the reservoir, the ground surface moves up or down by amounts detectable with InSAR as small vertical elevation changes covering large areas. Our typical pixel density of about 20,000 points per sq. km (50,000$ points per sq. mile) clearly indicates the areas where the surface has gone up or down between the 11 -day InSA R revisits (Fig. 14).

Although this paper focusses on the Belridge field, we acquire InSAR data in California for other fields in the southem San J oaquin and Santa Maria basins. Both basins have many fields where ground deformation is observed to pulse as reservoirs are drained and the VVR varies from the ideal case of 1. However, like Belridge, the fields tend to show general long-tem trends of surface subsidence due to reservoir compaction (Fig. 15).

Our client’s strategy to manage the waterflood operation of the Diatomite reservoir is injector-based. At any one time they have between 80 and 100 water injectors that need to provide pressure support for 250 to 300 producers. Every 11 days they get an updated InSAR deformation map that tells them how effectively they have balanced the Voidage Replacement Ratio in the past 11 days (Fig. 16).

They review injectors in sets of 5 to 15 and adjust injection rates in areas where the InSAR deformation map shows uplift or subsidence. The result is that they are operating the waterflood efficiently and since late 2015 no well casing damage has been assigned to compaction and there have been no surface expressions (Fig. 17).

At the Belridge field significant horizontal displacement accompanies long-term reservoir compaction and the resulting vertical ground subsidence. Effectively, compression is happening on the flanks of the subsidence bowl. The ratio of horizontal to vertical movement is about 1 to 3, a ratio we have seen in other fields as well (Fig. 18).

Shearing in the subsurface causes many problems. At first, shearing will bend a well's casing and tubing so that downhole tools cannot pass below the obstruction. Eventually the shearing will completely cut through and displace the casing and tubing (Fig, 19). Along the way mechanical integrity will be lost.

The field also has many wells with compromised wellbore integrity that can provide a pathway for reservoir fluids to move upwards towards the ground surface (Fig, 20).

The result: since this service was initiated, the customer reports that subsidence-related well casing damage has all but stopped and three surface expressions have been prevented. In one episode, we detected a massive blister with a diameter of 400 m (1,300 ft) and uplift of 2 cm (0.8_), triggering an alert (Fig. 21).

The giant Groningen gas field is at the northern end of the Netherlands and is operated by NAM, a joint venture company of Shell and ExxonMobil. Original gas-in-place was about 2.8 TCM (100 T CF) and the field has produced over 1.7 TCM (60 BCF). The discovery well for the field was drilled in 1959 but the size of the field was not realized until 1963 (Fig. 22).

The field produces from the 180 m (600 ft) thick Permian Rotliegend Slochteren sandstone at approximately 3,000 m (9,850 ft) and from the overlying shaley Ten Boer member which is capped by the Zechstein evaporites (Fig. 23).

The gas production has greatly reduced pore pressure in the reservoir and the sandstone reservoirs have compacted unevenly due to very different production rates in different parts of the field. (The center of the field is the most depleted, the flanks are less depleted.) In turn, the ground level has subsided in a bowl-shaped way over the extent of the field and the areas around it that is a muted reflection of the underlying compaction (Fig. 24).

Almost all earthquakes associated with oil and gas production elsewhere in the world are caused by increases in pore pressure. Groningen is unusual in that the earthquakes are caused by pressure depletion and this consequence is not well understood. The result is that seismicity is hard to model and to predict (Fig. 25).

Shell Canada has a group of horizontal paired wellbores in the Peace River area of Alberta that are being actively monitored by InSAR (Fig. 26).

All that is needed is acquisition of InSAR data at a time frequency that matches the injection/production cyclicity. In 2015, Shell compared ground deformation data from InSAR with changes in seismic travel time using SeisMovieù which uses buried sets of continuously recording seismic sources and receivers to get a 4D view of the reservoir (Przybysz-J arnut, 2015) (Fig. 27).
