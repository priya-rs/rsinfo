Table 1 summarizes the oil recovery factor (recovered oil volume % of pilot area Original Oil in Place [OOIP]) and Pore Volume Injected (PVI) of water and gas before and after the pilot initiation. In the early water injection phase before pilot initiation, 7.2% PV of water was injected and 6.5% OOIP was recovered. During the SWAG pilot, a total 13.2% PV of injection fluid (9.7% PV of water and 3.5% PV of lean gas) was injected and 10% OOIP was recovered. If a linear relationship between the water PVI and oil recovery is assumed, the oil recovery with 13.2% PV of water only injection would be 11.9% which is higher than the 10% oil recovery by 13.2% PV of water and gas injection. This suggests that immiscible lean gas injection is inferior compared to the water injection and EOR benefit by lean gas injection would not be expected.

Through the sensitivity and optimization studies, several WAG injection schemes were evaluated to optimize oil recovery and minimize the gas usage. The optimized WAG injection scheme, a "Tapered" WAG, modifies the duration of gas injection over time with longer gas injection cycles initially which are progressively reduced with subsequent WAG cycles as illustrated in Table 2 and Fig. 6. In addition, line drive injection with 500 m well spacing was identified as an optimal well configuration for the miscible WAG (Kohata et al. 2017).

Table 4 summarizes the compositions of the injection fluids; lean HC gas was used in the SWAG pilot and rich HC gas was used for the miscible WAG. The Minimum Miscible Pressure (MMP) of each gas was estimated by slimtube tests as 5000 psi for the lean HC gas and 3770 psi for the rich HC gas (Kohata et al., 2017). These results indicate the rich HC gas will achieve the miscibility whereas the lean HC gas will not achieve the miscibility under reservoir conditions where the average reservoir pressure is approximately 3800-4000 psi. Fig. 13-(d) shows the simulation results with lean HC gas and rich HC gas.
