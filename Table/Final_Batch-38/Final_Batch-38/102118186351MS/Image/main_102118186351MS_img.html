Based on the country-wide EOR screening study, Raudhatain Zubair (RAZU) was identified as a potential ASP (Alkaline Surfactant Polymer) EOR target. The Raudhatain field is located in North Kuwait and it consists of multiple stacked sand stone reservoirs (Figure 1). This field was discovered in 1955 and has been on continuous production since 1960 (Abdul Azim et. al., 2006). The RAZU formation is the deepest of three major oil producing reservoirs in the large dome anticline (Nemcsok et. al., 2006).

Aerially the reservoir was divided into 6 sectors as shown in Figure 2 below. A semi-quantitative approach was chosen to rank each of the 6 areas for suitability of pilot execution. The selection criteria incorporated a detailed look at STOIIP and remaining oil for EOR, waterflood maturity and sweep efficiency, oil production and water cut, formation properties, sample availability for laboratory calibration, representativeness of area for full-field application, well density, faults and compartmentalization, surface facilities congestion and surface facilities readiness to receive ASP produced oil. Due to the higher density of faults and higher surface footprint in the southern and western quadrants of the field, the decision was taken to select the pilot location in Areas 1 and 2 of Figure 2 below.

The Raudhatain Zubair field is highly dissected by many faults (over 36 main faults). Most of the faults are concentrated on the southern and western quadrants of the field as shown in Figure 3 below. In general, very few faults are connected. However, this doesn’t eliminate the risk of compartmentalization with respect to the sub seismic faults since the stratigraphic sequence contains high density of shale streaks. A small fault throw can disconnect the sands and create baffles due to juxtaposition or shale smearing along the fault surface.

Fluid movements in the reservoir are impacted greatly by relative permeabilities. Available SCAL data as shown in Figure 4 below was reviewed and compared with the relative permeabilities from the previously history-matched KOC Eclipse model. Due to the limited Relative Permeability SCAL data available from RAZU UZSD, Shell proprietary tool “RELATE” was used to generate relative permeability ranges based on Shell’s analog sandstone database for similar formations in Middle East region. However, in the end the decision was made to stay with measured SCAL data from the field.

Large volume blocks (volume modifiers) were attached to the edge of the model and the volume of these blocks was adjusted so that the pressure decline in the model was consistent with the range of average pressure decline observed in the field (Shell Generation 1 Model Report). It was expected that during the pilot injection period, the injector and producers of the pilot would dominate the pressure profile in the pattern. This was verified on running the sector model at the injection and production rates specified for the pilot. This method reduced the effort that it would have taken to get a complete history match for the simulation model to set the pressure boundary conditions. The current average pressure of Upper Zubair Sand in Areas 1 and 2 of UZSD Zubair 46 channel (Z46CH) area is estimated to be around 3625 psia. This is based on the available Static BHP and Repeat Formation Tester (RFT) data. As shown in Figure 7 below, the annual pressure drop in the pilot area historically ranged from 5.2 psia/year to 39.3 psia/year. Based on this understanding, the sector model was initialized at 3625 psi and boundary conditions were applied such that average pressure drop per year was stable between 5.2 and 39.3 psia/year as shown in Figure 7 below. A sensitivity analysis was performed to evaluate impact of different pressure decline rates on the model which showed minimal impact of pressure decline on incremental recovery from ASP.

Surfactant propagation is a key issue in assessing performance of an ASP project and in it can be anticipated that the larger the pattern size the less efficient is the surfactant propagation. Minimizing the time to evaluate pilot performance became a driver in for the project. Different pilot pattern sizes were evaluated to assess the effect of heterogeneity on performance. The oil desaturation and oil recovery for the pilot pattern sizes evaluated is shown in Figure 16 below. For a 0.5-1.5 Acre inverted 5-spot pattern, average oil saturation of the pattern is reduced to less than 10% within one year after 0.3 PV ASP injection followed by polymer chase injection. In the larger pattern spacing (i.e. 7.5 Acre pattern) average oil saturation reduces to only 20% over a similar period.

For purpose of this modeling work, ratio of cumulative oil to cumulative water injected after start of ASP injection was used as a proxy to oil chemical ratio. Oil chemical ratio for various pattern sizes is shown in Figure 17 below. As expected, it can be observed that smaller the pattern size, the faster the incremental gain in oil recovery from the chemical injection.

The regular versus inverted five spot patterns were evaluated for the ASP pilot design. The inverted 5-spot pattern consists of 4 producers surrounding 1 injector in the middle whereas the regular 5-spot has 4 injectors surrounding 1 central producer as shown in Figure 18 below.

The oil desaturation comparison for the two pattern types is shown in Figure 19 below, depicting average oil saturation reducing to 10% for the regular 5-spot pattern in comparison to 6% in inverted 5 spot in similar time duration with similar volumes and rates of fluid injected. Balancing total injection and production is necessary to maintain voidage in a pilot with an unconfined pattern. (As opposed to a confined inverted 5 spot pattern where production is four times that of injection due to no flow across the pattern boundaries).

To understand the efficiency of pattern types, Oil chemical ratios for regular and inverted 5-spot patterns were compared as shown Figure 20 below. It can be observed that the Inverted 5-spot pattern is more efficient in mobilizing the oil from the pilot area to the producers compared to regular 5-spot pattern for similar volumes and rates of fluid injected.

This can be explained further by comparison of the oil desaturation after ASP slug injection for both pattern types as shown in Figure 21 below. The inverted 5-spot pattern shows higher desaturation than the regular 5-spot pattern in the pilot pattern area, mainly due to injection lost to area outside the pilot for the regular 5-spot pattern.

Injection rate or injection volume per year is a measure of the speed at which pattern area is being swept. The range of injection/production rates that was evaluated for the pilot pattern was 500 bpd to 5000 bpd with 1600 bpd as reference case (Voidage Replacement Ratio, VRR=1). For low injection rate of 500 bpd, oil desaturation is very slow. For the high injection rate of 5000 bpd, oil saturation is quite rapid reducing to almost 5% within 1 year after ASP slug was injected. Injecting too much polymer will not continue to improve the displacement efficiency for a given pore volume injected. Figure 24 below shows that there is a noticeable reduction in remaining oil saturation on increasing the viscosity of the slug from 1 cP to 2 cP. However, the upside going from 2 cP to 6 cP is not very significant and there is no upside in going from 6 cP to 10 cP so far as lowering the remaining oil saturation in the pattern is concerned. This would support a decision to begin designing the insitu polymer slug viscosity close to 2 cP.

The cumulative oil chemical ratio improves when polymer viscosity is increased, as shown in Figure 25 below. However, as discussed above, this comes at the cost of an injectivity decrease when the injection fluid is more viscous. Thus, designing for an ASP injection slug viscosity in the range of 2-6 cP is recommended.

In the reference case, pore volume of injected ASP slug is 0.3 PV. This chemical slug size was varied from a low case of approximately 0.1 PV to the high case of 0.6 PV. The oil desaturation for the different cases is illustrated in Figure 26 below and is consistent with general experience in Enhanced Oil Recovery.

The oil desaturation and oil chemical ratios for a small ASP slug size and a large ASP slug size are compared in Figure 27 below.

The chemical concentration of the ASP slug is specified relative to the optimal concentration used in the reference case. The surfactant concentration is varied from a value that is one tenth of the reference case to a value that is ten times that of the reference case. The lower the chemical concentration or under optimal, the higher the interfacial tension, which results in a lower mobilized water flood residual oil. This is the reason for the significant downside in oil recovery when less surfactant is injected in the ASP slug. The oil desaturation as a function of surfactant concentration injected and the corresponding oil/chemical ratio is as shown in the Figures 28 and 29 respectively below.
