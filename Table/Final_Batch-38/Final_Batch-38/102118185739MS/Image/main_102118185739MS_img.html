As a result of plate collision, the Pacific Plate plunges under the North American Plate almost directly under Cook Inlet. All the petroleum basins examined here are on the North American plate with the North Slope being furthest from the subduction zone, and Cook Inlet Being right on top of it (Figure 2). This subduction zone has led to high numbers of earthquakes, resulting from deformation in this area (Finzel, Flesch, and Ridgway 2011). The Cook Inlet is along the pacific plate boundary as shown in Figure 3 between the Kenai and Chugach mountains. The area has had large amounts of tidal, alluvial and glaciation-fed deposition (Mankemthong and Pavlis 2013, Swenson 1997, Saltus 2011). The basin includes five Miocene formations: the Sterling, Beluga, Tyonek, Hemlock, and West Foreland formations, which are part of a northeast anticline that follows the inlet northeast from the North Pacific Ocean. The formations are non-marine sandstones, siltstones, claystones and shale, and thin toward the edges of the basin (Mankemthong and Pavlis 2013, Swenson 1997).

Fourteen wells were chosen from different fields across 3 major basins in Alaska. These were the Colville Basin (North Slope), Nenana Basin, and the Cook Inlet Basin. Figure 4 shows the location of all of the wells that were chosen for this study.

As expected, most of the fields followed trend lines of between 0.9 psi/ft. and 1 psi/ft. at the deeper depths, with more variance in the shallower depths. All the wells analyzed are placed on the Figure 5 to easily see trends in vertical stress and pressure gradient.

Figure 6 presents the pressure gradients of all the wells sampled. It shows that as the depth of the wells increase the spread of the data decreases.

From this data, MATLAB was used again to generate models to show how the pressure gradients change through Alaska. The first figure is a snapshot of Alaska at a depth of 6000 feet. This figure shows that aside from the OCS and Kugrua wells, there appears to be a very slight North to South trend of increasing overburden. This trend is shown in illustrated in Figure 7.

This trend can further be developed by examining overburden gradients at 4 distinct depths as shown in Figure 8. Once again this shows that aside from the OCS and Kugrua wells, there is a slight North to South trend of increasing overburden.

Authors believe that this increase in vertical stress is due to the collision of the Pacific and North American Plates. As seen from Figure 9, there have been several earthquakes recorded in Alaska along this subduction zone. This subduction zone is suspected to be resulted in stress in the formations resulting in higher vertical stresses. For variances in the data, this is attributed to the high deformation zones that exist on the North American plate. High deformation zones can cause fractures which will decrease formation density and change vertical stress.
