It is observed from Fig. 8 that many MS events from Stage 5 are generated around Stage 4, so we can conclude that liquid from the Stage 5 reinitiated a lot of MS in the area already stimulated by the Stage 4. The liquid is constantly trying to find the path of least resistance, so interpretation of MS events may help in identification of inefficient pad fluid leakoff. Possible losses of pad fluid preferably into the area stimulated by previous stages may also be a result of fluid migration through of highly conductive axial fracture (orange line in Fig. 8).

The pressure dependent leakoff was calculated for the range of treating pressures from 7,000 psi to 11,000 psi and for various h/a0. The calculation results for the case of h/a0=0.4are presented at semi-log plot in Fig. 9, together with the best fit exponential curve. From the fitting equation, the pressure dependent leakoff coefficient (PDL) is 0.00047 psi−1, which is close to one we used before MS data was available for analysis – 0.0005 psi−1.

Despite reasonable results of geomechanical modeling indicating 100% cluster efficiency for all tested stages, we ran test cases with variations of cluster designs. To show the importance of stress shadowing and engineering design, we left all pumping parameters the same except we artificially closed clusters #1 and #3 for Stages 3-14. This change resulted in more liquid and proppant available for each of three open clusters, more vertically symmetrical reservoir stimulation, better proppant placement laterally, and more conductive axial fractures (Fig. 13).
