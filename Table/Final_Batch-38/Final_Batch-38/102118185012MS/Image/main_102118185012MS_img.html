The oil bearing sands are divided into three main reservoirs (Lower, Middle and Upper) separated by maximum flooding surfaces (MFS) (Figure 1). The main reservoir intervals are further split into multiple stratigraphic intervals (Figure 1) for mapping and well planning. Siltstones, lignites and shales bound the sands vertically and shales thicker than 10’ are considered as effective seals for hydrocarbons.

The Lower reservoir is the deepest and the most prolific reservoir in the field and is the focus of this work. Figure 2 shows a south-west (SW) north-east (NE) cross section, with well logs from four wells in the Lower reservoir. The four well locations are superimposed on the net pay map of AMV-125, the deepest stratigraphic interval (Figure 2). The top interval of the Lower reservoir is AMV-155, which is overlain by a maximum flooding surface (MFS) that constitutes a regional seal across the field.

Heavy and extra heavy oil viscosity is a strong function of temperature, unlike light oil. Figure 3 shows a typical dependence of Huyapari dead oil on temperature. The significant depth variation in this field across the large operational area causes significant change in reservoir temperature (Tankersley and Waite, 2003) and hence, significant variation in oil viscosity at in-situ conditions. Representation of accurate reservoir temperature and associated oil viscosity is critical to obtain a reliable production forecast.

The field has been developed using long laterals, drilled from multiwell drilling pads (Figure 4). Length of the horizontal laterals ranges from 4000 to 8000 ft., with an average of about 6000 ft. Some of these established world record for length of slotted liners (Acharya et al. 2004) in the past.

As noted, LWD measurements were used to steer the well during drilling in order to maintain well trajectory in pay zone, but not all of the horizontal section was drilled in reservoir quality rock due to uncertainty in channel location. These wells are completed with 7” slotted liner. The LWD information in the completed section was used to calculate the effective well length (Figure 5).

Most of the data shows that the effective well length is higher than 60% of the drilled lateral section with an average value of about 80% of the lateral section (Figure 6). Less than 5% of the wells have effective well length less than 60% of its drilled lateral length.

Allocated well production data was used to calculate IP. The oil rate averaged over a 30-day period (discounting non-producing days) was considered as IP (Figure 7a). IP estimated by averaging provided a better assessment of sustained rate than allocated calendar rate values. Well test data were used to benchmark production data so as to have improved confidence in production data allocation (Figure 7b). In general, the allocation factor was very high (95-97%) for the data in this study.

Due to uncertainty in local geology, only a part of the well contacts reservoir quality sand and effectively contributes to the fluid production. Figure 6 shows that on average the effective well length is 80% of the drilled lateral length for this reservoir. Figure 8 captures the scatter of IP vs. effective well lengths (calculated per the criteria outlined in Table 2) of wells completed in AMV-125 sand of the Lower reservoir. Wells with higher effective length showed better performance (higher IP) than those with lower effective length. Similar relationships were also found for other sands in the Lower reservoir.

The IP shows a strong and clear trend with depth (Figures 9a and 9b), with IPs increasing with increasing reservoir depth. This is caused by lower oil viscosity (due to higher temperature) and higher pressure in deeper reservoirs. As mentioned earlier, the field has a large depth variation and a geothermal gradient causes the reservoir temperature to increase with depth. The viscosity variations with temperature and reservoir depth are shown in Figure 3.

A study using a single horizontal well (Figure 10) with uniform reservoir properties (mid properties from Table 3) shows that coarse-scale models do not capture the pressure depletion and gas saturation change observed at the fine scale. Typical grid sizes used in the previous studies used coarse-scale dimensions of 328 ft. × 328 ft. in areal (x and y) and 10 ft. in vertical (z) directions. Figure 11 shows that the coarse grid blocks model significantly under-predict gas production rate and over-predict oil production rate and cumulative recovery.

Using a uniform property model at an effective well spacing of 300 meter and ranges of reservoir properties, we identified reservoir properties which have largest impact on the well performance. Tornado diagrams (Figure 12) generated at different times (5 and 35 years) of depletion helped to quantify the impact of different reservoir properties on production behavior. Results at 5 years capture early production behavior, whereas those at 35 years represent ultimate recovery.

We consider two adjacent pads with 19 wells (Figure 13a and b) to illustrate the impact of heterogeneity on well performance. Wells in the same and adjacent pads are included, as key fluid properties such as oil-viscosity, GOR and bubble-point are similar. Also, because all wells were placed on production at nearly the same time, the initial reservoir pressures are also similar (except a small variation caused by reservoir depth).

The dynamic reservoir simulation model shows that local heterogeneity results in a wide range of fluid production rates for these wells. The forecasted range of performances from the simulation model (Figure 14a) was comparable to that observed in the field (see Figure 9). However, the individual well forecasts (using base model without any calibration) did not generally match the actual performances. On the other hand, forecast on a group of wells (Figure 14b) or at a pad level compares very well with field data. In other words, pad-level or a group of well-level forecasts are more reliable than well by well forecast because of local heterogeneity, which may not be precisely captured in the geologic model.

In the area under consideration, the geostatistical model honored well log data (Figure 15) of high shale volume (VShale) at the target stratigraphic interval top, but areas away from wells showed low VShale and high permeability regions (Figure 16). Connectivity through these patchy areas resulted in pressure communication between the vertically stacked flow units during production; as a result, forecasted cumulative oil production for the target sand were affected by ~10%. Model calibration to production data helped to incorporate a regional barrier between the vertically stacked intervals, making them separate flow units with no vertical communication.

Although, frictional pressure drop is an important consideration in the horizontal well design, heterogeneity largely dictates inflow along lateral. The oil inflow profiles are generally non-uniform (Jha et al., 2009) and oil rates calculated in the flow simulation models were higher in the areas of higher permeability (Figure 17). Some portions of the well did not contribute because of locally poor quality reservoir. These non-contributing zones can occur anywhere along the well. In this well, the percentage of non-contributing zone (~20% for this well) calculated from the model is comparable with the average non-effective well length (as % of drilled length), calculated using the cut-offs presented in Table 1.

Figure 18 (stock chart) shows that the smaller well spacing results in accelerated oil production and generally a small incremental recovery. Further, the effect of smaller spacing on acceleration is more pronounced in thicker and shallower reservoirs. In thicker reservoirs (having the same well spacing), OOIP is higher and therefore, incremental production in real volumes shall be proportionately higher even for similar incremental in percentage recovery. Figure 20a and b show reservoir pressure at a midway point between two horizontal producers (for 16 sets of horizontal well pairs) at different times for the heterogeneous model (shown in Figure 13a). Figure 20 clearly shows that with time, significant reservoir pressure reduction occurs at the midway point. Further, it shows that at any given time, there is a large variation in pressure depletion. This is caused by varying degree of interwell reservoir connectivity in different parts of the reservoir because of local heterogeneity. Figure 20 shows that, on average, midway pressures reduce by about 50% in ~ 8 years from the start of production.

Figure 21 shows the field data for infill wells drilled after eight years of production. These infills were drilled midway between the existing wells. The initial oil production rates (IPs) of two infill wells are much smaller than the IP of existing wells. The field data essentially corroborates the observations made from numerical study that the late time infills’ performance will be much worse than the existing wells IP due to lower reservoir pressure and higher oil viscosity compared to the initial condition. In fact, the current performance existing wells may be used as an analog for intermediate to late time infill well IP estimate.
