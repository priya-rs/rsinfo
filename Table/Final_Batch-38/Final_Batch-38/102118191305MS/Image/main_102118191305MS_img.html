The tapering function typically achieves its maximum value 1 when dist* (A, B) = 0 (i.e., when A and B are identical) and monotonically decreases as the normalized distance dist* (A, B) increases. In a practical implementation of distance-based localization, one may assume that the shape of the area of model variables that are correlated to a given observation element is elliptical (Emerick and Reynolds, 2011a). However, the major and minor axes of the ellipse may not overlap the axes of the coordinate system used in the reservoir models, as illustrated in Figure 1.

Norne is an offshore oil field in the Norwegian Sea, operated by Statoil ASA, Eni Norge AS and Petoro. The Norne field has five primary formations (from top to bottom): Garn, Not, Ile, Tofte and Tilje. Oil is mainly found in the Ile and Tofte formations, whereas gas in the Garn formation. The full Norne field reservoir model is obtained from the Norne database provided by the Center for Integrated Operations at NTNU (http://www.ipt.ntnu.no/~norne), and it consists of four segments: C, D, E and G (see Figure 2).

For illustration, Figure 3 shows a map of distance-based tapering coefficients c^h_ks × c^v_ks distributed over the active reservoir gridblocks on Layer 10 of the reservoir models. These tapering coefficients are computed with respect to all production rates (WGPRH, WOPRH and WWPRH) from well B-2H. To reduce the complexities in implementing distance-based localization, the same map in Figure 3 will be applied to "localize" the impacts of all WGPRH, WOPRH and WWPRH data from well B-2H (at different time instances) on the updates of petro-physical parameters on Layer 10 of the reservoir model.

Figure 4 shows the maps of correlations between PERMX (or PORO) and WGPR (or WOPR) data from well B-2H at the time of Day 41, distributed over active reservoir gridblocks on Layer 10. As one can see from the sub-figures in Figure 4, the correlation maps with respect to the combinations of different types of petro-physical parameters and production data appear distinct from each other. Thus, in principle, the localization scheme should take these differences into account.

As in Figure 4,Figure 5 reports similar correlation maps, but for simulated WGPR (or WOPR) data from well B-2H at the time of Day 2460 instead. Comparing Figures 4 and 5, one can see that the correlation maps also appear different for the same pairs of petro-physical parameters and production data at different time instants. As a result, the localization scheme should also take the time-lapse effect into account.

Figures 6 and 7 indicate the maps of correlation-based tapering coefficients, obtained by applying Eq.(27) to the correlation maps in Figures 4 and 5, respectively. As one can see, these tapering maps are different from each other, meaning that they are adaptive to different combinations of petro-physical parameters and production data at different time instants, in contrast to the non-adaptive tapering map in Figure 3 from distance-based localization.

Figures 11 reports the mean (top) and STD (bottom) of PERMX fields distributed over the active gridblocks on Layer 10, with respect to the initial ensemble (first column), the final history-matched ensemble with distance-based localization (second column), and the final history-matched ensemble with correlation-based localization (third column), respectively. In comparison to the mean (or STD) map of the initial ensemble, there are significant changes in the mean (or STD) maps of the final history matched ensembles.

In contrast, in history matching with correlation-based localization, the spatial distributions of the tapering coefficients are not confined to be unimodal, whereas relatively large tapering coefficients can be found along the edges of the area with active gridblocks, as demonstrated in Figures 6 and 7. As a result, the petro-physical parameters on the active gridblocks along these edges have more changes (hence smaller ensemble spreads), in comparison to the same petro-physical parameters with distance-based localization. Similar phenomena can also be observed in the mean and STD maps of PORO fields, as indicated in Figure 12.

Figure 13 shows the boxplots of the ensembles of the MULTFLT parameter for fault C-08 (top) and the KRG parameter for the Tilje formation (Layers 19 – 22) (bottom), obtained at different iteration steps without localization (first column) and with correlation-based (second column) localization, respectively. As can be seen in the figure, in this particular case, ensemble collapse did not seem to take place for these two parameters when there is no localization, although localization can indeed help retain higher spreads in the final ensembles.

As aforementioned, in the current case study, we have reserved 2680 production data for cross validation. Here, cross validation is carried out by comparing the corresponding simulated production rates of the final history-matched ensembles to these 2680 observed data. Figure 14 shows the boxplots of data mismatch between the production data for cross validation and the corresponding simulated data from the final ensembles with distance- and correlation-based localization, respectively. In this case, on average, correlation-based localization also leads to lower data mismatch than distance-based localization.
