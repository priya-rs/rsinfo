An integrated workflow for primary and thermal probabilistic forecast is schematically illustrated in Figure 2. The workflow comprises of three sequential segments to assess the full-field (FF) potential. The first segment of the workflow selects three realizations from a suite of full-field geostatistical realizations. The three selected realizations account for variations in reservoir geology and represent low, mid, and high scenarios of reservoir geology respectively. Additionally, well locations were selected separately for each realization based on surface constraints and well selection algorithm (described below).

The cumulative oil production (OPC) at 50 years from the simulation runs were used to compare the outcomes of various scenarios and rank them. Three perfor-mance curves representing low, mid & high full-field primary production forecast are selected based on cumulative oil production after 50 years. The output from this step was the full-field rate profiles of different phases (oil, water and gas), estimated recovery values, number of producers and well-scheduling using Approach-1 development plan. Figure 3 shows the oil production profiles with the initial ramp up reaching to the target full-field plateau and then decline. Oil recoveries for three cases were 2.4%, 2.8%and 3.8% on the full-field basis OOIP.

Following the full-field primary DOE simulations, we developed the Pareto charts of OPC under primary at 25 years (Figure 4) and 50 years (Figure 5). It ranks the uncertain parameters in order of their impact. Rig availability came out to be the most important parameters affecting OPC in the time-frames of 25 and 50 years. Number of rigs (and hence number of wells drilled) has a very large impact on the field performance.

The next and most important step for primary production forecasting is to compare the simulation results with analog data. The analog field was an XHO field with similar geology, but at a greater depth (and therefore, with lower oil viscosity and higher reservoir pressure) and developed using horizontal wells of similar horizontal lengths. Figure 6 compares the cumulative distribution of initial production rates and estimated ultimate recovery at the individual well level in the low, mid & high cases with the analog field data.

First of all, the thermal development targets are identified. Cut-offs on minimum reservoir thickness and maximum thickness of barriers-to-steam were imposed to map thermal geobodies for each FFM. Figure 7 shows a plan-view of geobodies after imposition of cut-offs (thermal targets) along with the associated primary producers in one of the target reservoirs. The area shown in the figure is considered amenable for thermal recovery.

After the simulation, we also created the Pareto chart of DNOPC under thermal production (Figure 8) to rank the uncertain parameters in order of their impact. Geology (a key factor controlling OOIP) came out as the most important parameter affecting the thermal performance.

Monte Carlo simulations were performed using the proxy. Several Monte Carlo runs were made by randomly picking values of uncertain parameters from their specified distribution and estimating DNOPC using the proxy. Thus, after Monte Carlo simulation, we obtain a distribution (cumulative distribution function, CDF) of DNOPC. Three different points on this CDF corresponding to cumulative probabilities of 10 –50 –90 respectively represent low, mid and high performance profiles and called thermal type wells. Figure 9 shows the oil-production rate profile, steam-injection rate profile and cumulative steam oil ratio (Cum SOR) for the three selected type wells.

Figure 10 is a schematic diagram showing wells for thermal development. The well spacing for PP was 500 meter. For thermal development scenario, we used reduced well spacing by placing three equally spaced horizontal infills between existing PP producers. This reduces the interwell spacing to 125 meter. The additional producers at each end of AOI serve as guard wells. These wells protect the inner thermal wells from cold oil-influx from the buffer zone.

Figure 11 shows a plan view of the reservoir simulation grid used for the thermal performance study. The thermal sector includes the area of interest (AOI) and the surrounding buffer zone. The AOI is 2500ft. long and 2050ft. wide in the aerial direction. We used tartan grids in the x-y plain. Tartan grid has fine grid size (12.5m × 12.5m) in the AOI to accurately capture the impact of important phenomena occurring in that region.

Figure 12 shows the pressure depletion in the three geological sectors using primary production for five years. Pressure depletions in the three sectors are quite different. Primary production only can deplete the Sector-2 reservoirs to target pressure of 450 psi before five years. However, target pressure in Sector-1 is achieved only at five years, whereas Sector-3 (thickest) is not depleted to the target pressure during this period.

Figure 13 shows the geological sector cross sections for three sectors at the end of five years. These cross-sections show local pressure depletion at the end of each year for five years (i.e. at the end of 1, 2, 3, 4, and 5 years). The shale baffles obstruct the flow of oil from the upper regions of the reservoir to the primary producers. Therefore, the presence of baffles reduces the rate of pressure depletion in the upper regions (Figure 13c); higher pressure depletion occurs in the regions below the baffles. The overall impact of shale baffles is to slow down average pressure depletion in the AOI.

Figure 14 shows the impact of CSS on pressure depletion for the three geological sectors. In Sector-1, CSS accelerates the pressure depletion to some extent. The difference in average pressures in the AOI is about 150 psi during initial cycles and it reduces to about 50 psi towards the end of five years (Fig. 14(a)). The difference in pressure depletions is even less pronounced for Sector-2 (Fig. 14(b)). In this case, comparable pressure depletion is achieved with primary production because of good reservoir connec-tivity and smaller thickness.

Figure 17 illustrates the importance of maintaining an appropriate differential pressure. In the first case, the differential pressure is 4 psi. This is sufficient to produce all the heated liquid. As a result, there is only a small liquid level build-up above the producer. The steam chamber develops nicely and the reservoir is drained in an efficient manner (Fig. 17(a)). However, in the second case (Fig. 17(b)), the differential pressure is much smaller (~0 psi).

A positive differential pressure is important for efficient drainage of the steam chamber. However, pressure drops in a CSI-HIHP well-pair (producer and injector) can cause a condition, when theoretically, pressure in portions of the injector may be lower than that in the producer. Figure 18 schematically illustrates impact of wellbore pressure drops in CSI-HIHP configuration.

Figure 19 shows the producer and injector pressure profiles along their lengths with no pressure drop in the injector. The injector pressure was kept constant along its length to explicitly study impact of pressure drop in producer only. However, producer pressure can drop from toe of the well towards its heel depending on the magnitude of pressure drop. For a typical 6.25” liner diameter, the pressure drop in the wellbore is only 1 psi.

Figure 20 shows a comparison of oil inflow profile, cumulative oil production and steam-oil-ratio for the three cases. As evident, because of small pressure drop in case of standard size liner diameter (6.25”) the impact of pressure drop in producer on thermal performance is minimal. It compares very closely with the no friction case. We also illustrate the case, when the liner diameter was reduced to 3.12”.

As described above, in the development lifecycle of many heavy-oil reservoirs, thermal enhanced oil recovery (EOR) method is preceded by primary production. Long producers (~ 5000 ft. or longer) is typically used during primary production of heavy oil reservoirs. The current art for thermal development would require shorter injectors and producers to be drilled after primary production, which could make thermal recovery commercially non-economic. The proposed well configuration of two shorter injectors per producer (Figure 23) would significantly improve economics of such projects.
