Depending on the solid concentration, the product undergoes secondary treatment i.e. thickners for higher solid concentration and Activated Sludge Basin for lower solid concentration. The thickened sludge enters digester followed by centrifuge stage where the product is dried and then sent for land application. The simplicity of the slurry injection technique allows the slurry to be fed into the injection well after the initial processing step (i.e. filtering/screening large chunks of solids, plastic etc) irrespective of the treatment stage it is in during the wastewater treatment process as shown in Figure 1. This is cost effective as the slurry does not have to go through each stage in the treatment process to produce dry biosolids.

It is also estimated that by applying the slurry injection technique, the city of LA has the capacity to eliminate nearly 600,000 metric tons of CO2 equivalent emissions each year, along with over 2,000,000 truck miles each year. Moreover, no significant seismic activity was recorded during injection operations at this site as shown in Figure 2, which demonstrates that biosolids injection does not create any significant change in the seismicity of the area.

This is similar to the diageneses process that naturally deposited organic layers undergo over time after deposition. For example, as demonstrated at the biosolids injection facility in Los Angeles, upon exposure to the high temperature (~125°F) of the deep geologic formation, biosolids decompose into CO2, CH4 and other non-volatile residual solids (Bruno et al., 2000). The CH4 will be trapped in the formation to be available for production and beneficial use depending on the need and economic justification. Additionally, due to high formation pressure, the CO2 is partially preferentially dissolved into the natural formation brine, with the remainder acting like a liquid in supercritical state. This results in both a separation of the CO2 from the CH4 and a sequestration of the CO2 in the formation (Bruno et al., 2005). Figure 3 shows the schematic of a biosolids injection process.

The geology in northern coastal area of Gulf of Mexico, specially focusing in the Houston area is almost flat. The slurry injection study is conducted using the well data from the well located ~25 miles from the targeted area, but the analysis can be used for different areas of the Houston due to its less complicated geological history. The comparison of Gamma Ray logs across different areas of Houston shows similar lithological trends (with well C in the targeted area and well D in the area of study) as shown in Figure 6.

According to the Texas Administrative Code SECTION 331.42, the Area of Review (AoR) (except those wells authorized to inject only nonhazardous desalination concentrate or nonhazardous drinking water treatment residuals) is an area determined by a radius of 2 miles from the proposed or existing wellbore, or the area within the Cone of Influence (CoI), whichever is greater. The area around the injection well within which increased injection zone pressures caused by injection of wastes would be sufficient to drive fluids into a USDW is the cone of influence (Texas Administrative Code §331.2). Figure 10 shows the cost comparison between slurry injection and other alternate technologies for biosolids management. Based on previously conducted interviews, the operating costs for the drying system at Houston's 69th Street Plant are about $317 per dry ton and can be reduced to approximately $180 per dry ton processed by implementing slurry injection technology. Figure 11 shows cumulative savings of $90MM in a comparison plot of annual costs just for drying expenses and savings over a 25-year plan for the city of Houston. Further costs may be able to be reduced by intercepting the sludge at earlier stages.
