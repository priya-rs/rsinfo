The Wolfcamp Shale formation is a hydrocarbon-producing zone in the Permian Basin (Figure 1). Located in West Texas and extending into the southeast portion of New Mexico, it is primarily a basinal structure. Covering the Midland, Delaware and Central Platform sub-basins (Scotia Howard Heil, 2014; Schwartz et al., 2015), it varies in thickness depending on where it is located, with some areas exceeding 2,000 ft.

Case 1 is an example of frac fluid (Table 1) and hydrocarbon (Table 2) tracers being utilized to show the effects of existing faults intersecting a wellbore. There was little indication during the hydraulic fracture stimulation that traced segments 7 and 8 were treated differently than the surrounding segments. Post-frac flowback/production fluid tracer analyses indicated that these zones were not contributing into the wellbore flow stream (Figure 2).

The lessons learned resulted in abandoning stages across known faults that resulted in zero or minimal flowback/production (Figure 3 and Figure 4). Some effects are still observed in this example as shown in Table 3 specifically in traced segments 18 to 15 and 14 to 13 which are on either side of the known fault. After approximately two weeks, flowback/production was finally achieved from these intervals once the wellbore bottomhole pressures were reduced. This type of study led to lower completion costs by bypassing zones that were unproductive.

Case 3 examines the lateral drainage pattern when the wellbore was drilled into different portions of the Wolfcamp structure. The heel, approximately 40% of the lateral, was more limestone-rich and transitioned into a more dominantly shale-rich zone (Figure 5). Hydrocarbon fluid tracers were used to evaluate oil producing regions. Additionally, proppant tracers were utilized to evaluate near-wellbore proppant placement. The lessons learned from this type of study led to better placement of the laterals in the more productive interval across the Wolfcamp. The increased awareness of drilling the lateral across the same structure helps facilitate frac stage containment and allows for uniform flowback across the entire lateral. Figure 7 shows the results of the lessons learned. Once a more uniform propped fracture character was achieved, the production became more evenly distributed across the entire lateral (Figure 8).

Case 4 examines the amount of proppant fracture communication between three individual wells in different vertical sections of the Wolfcamp. The four-panel image (Figure 9) helps illustrate the extent of communication. Each wellbore is highlighted to show the unique tracer that was injected in that wellbore (Red is Iridium, Yellow is Scandium and Blue is Antimony). Panel A is a composite image of all the proppant tracers detected in each wellbore. As is evident from the image, all three proppant tracers were observed in all three wellbores. Multiple tracer types in a single wellbore indicate that proppant has intersected an offset well during some part of the stimulation treatment.

ase 5 examines the amount of frac fluid communication between two hydraulic fractured horizontal wellbores that were zipper fracture completed and the effects of two preexisting vertical wells. The vertical wells had been producing for approximately two years prior to stimulating the two horizontal wells. Each horizontal well utilized 11 unique frac fluid tracers (CFTs). Produced fluid samples were collected from all four wells and analyzed to determine the magnitude of the offset well fluid communication. Figure 10 shows a top down view of all four wells and their location in relation to each other. Well 2 and Well 3 are the horizontal wells. Well 1 and Well 4 are the preexisting vertical wells. Shown across each horizontal well are 11 traced colored segments.

The production for all wells in the Wolfcamp did in fact increase over time (Figure 11), implying that changes to the frac programs were successful in producing more hydrocarbon. As costs for these wells certainly decreased over the 5 years of this study, the value of these improvements are only amplified.

Along with the increase of production for all types of wells in both basins in the Wolfcamp, the proficiency of operators to drill and complete these wells improved. The percent proppant coverage had a slight increase in both basins (Figure 12), which is a good measure of the proppant behind pipe and indicates the success of the frac program. This increase implies that companies were more likely to refine their completion techniques over time to cause this increase in coverage, which should lead to an increase in hydrocarbon production.

Completion programs for each well are critical for the ultimate fate of the well, but there is also much to learn about how these wells are produced and clean up over the early stages of production. To visualize this, the water profile effectiveness (WPE), a measure of how evenly the frac fluid cleans up from the well, was used. A scale of 1 to 10 is utilized with 10 being the best a well could achieve. Figure 13 shows the typical upward trend observed when plotting an individual company's wells over the lifetime of the program.

An additional number of metrics were surveyed for each basin in the Wolfcamp, and several trends were apparent. While both basins saw a marked increase in lateral length drilled (Figure 14), the lateral lengths were generally longer in the shallower Midland sub-basin and shorter in the deeper Delaware sub-basin.

Both basins predominantly included completion plans with an average of 1,200 pounds per foot of lateral, though the Midland Basin had a handful of recent wells that pushed the amount above 2,000 pounds per foot (Figure 15). Again, the amount of proppant per foot increased over the lifetime of the basin, possibly due to increases in the ability to deliver that much proppant as well as the hope that more proppant would increase production.

There was a marked difference in the number of frac stages for wells in each basin (Figure 16), with the Midland Basin again having more due to the necessity to contact as much rock as possible with the fractures. As technologies and proficiencies progressed, the stage length was also increased in an attempt to increase production.
