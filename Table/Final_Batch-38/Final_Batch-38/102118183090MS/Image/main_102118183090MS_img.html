The Weyburn-Midale field is located in southeast Saskatchewan, Canada (Fig. 1). It was discovered in 1953 and is one of the largest medium-sour oil reservoirs in Canada. Waterflooding began in 1964, followed by horizontal drilling in the 1990s (Protti, 2005). Although, these measures helped production, an enhanced oil recovery (EOR) process was implemented using CO2 to revert long-term production decline and to demonstrate the large-scale geologic storage of CO2 (Ansarizadeh et al. 2015).

Vertical producers are normally casedhole whereas the horizontals are openhole, mainly completed for commingled production, controlled by the wellhead valve. A candidate well that incorporated different horizons and reflected the complex reservoir conditions was selected. The vertical candidate chosen serves the purpose since it penetrates the main horizons of the Marly formation (M1, M2 and M3), as shown in Fig. 2, and is in communication with the offset WAG injector.

CO2 properties have been extensively studied by different authors. Ansarizadeh et al. (2015) represented the CO2 phases, as shown in Fig. 3. Phase boundary lines (blue) define the areas in which each CO2 phase exists. At the triple point, all three phases (solid, liquid, and vapor CO2) coexist in thermodynamic equilibrium.

M. Ansarizadeh, K. Dodds, O. Gurpinar et al. 2015. Carbon Dioxide-Challenges and Opportunities . Oilfield Review 27 (2): 36– 50 .

The subject well is then operating around the CO2 super-critical conditions as displayed in Fig. 4. Due to the sensitive behavior of the production fluid, the well model, and corresponding ICD design, used a compositional fluid model.

Because of the composition of the Midale fluid (high CO2 content and hydrocarbon from methane through C30+,), the expected behavior should be similar to the phase envelope proposed by Orr et al (1981), as shown in Fig. 5, in which normal alkanes C14 and heavier show liquid-liquid (LL) immiscibility, but the LL critical locus and the liquid-vapor (LV) critical locus merge into a continuous critical curve.

F. M. Orr, A. D. Yu, and C. L. Lien 1981. Phase Behavior of CO2 and Crude Oil in Low- Temperature Reservoirs. SPE J . 21 (4): 480– 492 . SPE-8813-PA. http://dx.doi.org/10.2118/8813-PA.

For the characterization of the Midale well fluid, a proprietary software was used, and the Peng-Robinson cubic EOS with the Peneloux volume shift option was the equation that provided the best match for the two criteria, as displayed by the phase envelope behavior shown in Fig. 6 and the fluid property match in Fig. 7. In addition to this EOS, the GERG 2008 natural gas model was used to estimate vapor densities (Kunz et al. 2007).

The selected Peng-Robinson EOS has the advantage of reproducing the liquid-vapor boundary much better than any other cubic EOS. Furthermore, Peng-Robinson EOS has been used to represent CO2-H2O mutual solubility from 25° to 350°C and up to 1000 bar (Spycher et al. 2003), which is the within the operational range of the subject well. Fig. 9 represents the phase envelope of the fluid, calculated by the EOS, considering the effect of the water (including them water dew and hydrate lines).

As previously mentioned, normally all wells are completed for commingled production with one packer placed above of the top of the Midale’s M1 horizon, as shown in Fig. 10. As communication is established between the injectors and the producers, water cut increases because of short-circuiting of injection fluids from injectors to producers via high permeability streaks such as fractures, leaving behind significant unswept oil.

All completion components and tubing are coated with a commercial tubular coating to deter corrosion due to the corrosive nature of the CO2 and presence of H2S in varying percentages. For reference, a simplified half-sectional view of the commingled completion is shown in Fig. 11.

The pressure and liquid rate values from the production tests were input for the inflow model of the three horizons, M1, M2, and M3. With a multipoint IPR, a productivity index was obtained for each reservoir, considering their independent water cut and GOR, recalibrating the compositional fluid model per completion to match the PLT data. The outflow and inflow models were tested in the nodal analysis, as shown in Fig. 12. The hydraulic model matched the PLT data within 0.4%.

Because the three zones were tightly stacked with 1.5m thickness and a 1m stringer between each of the zones, it was crucial to place the segregating packers accurately in the 1m between zones while accommodating two nozzle configurations per zone across the perforations (Fig. 13). The Multiport completion had different possible positions, allowing combinations of ICDs and AICVs, as discussed in more detail in this section.

To achieve the requirement for the multiport completion’s ICD design, a unique adaptable downhole three-position fullbore valve is deployed with cup packers for isolation. This device can be either closed or opened to one of two ICD settings or ICD types, by moving a short sleeve, as shown in Fig. 14. The sleeve, shown with ICD1 open, can be shifted to either the closed position or to ICD2 using special shifting tools run on jointed pipe, coiled tubing, or wireline tractor.

The sleeve, as shown in Fig. 15, is designed to minimize the risk of sticking over time, caused primarily by the surface deposition that plagues conventional sliding sleeves. The sleeve length and stroke are very short. The metallurgy on sliding surfaces is specially treated to minimize surface deposition. Scrapers are there to scrape deposition off surfaces when shifted.

The assembly had to have a small-enough outside diameter (OD) to fit inside 5.5-in. casing (4.89-in. drift inside diameter, ID), and the sub had to be short because the zones are thin. Hence, two devices were integrated into one assembly by modifying the design to include a midway locating sub, allowing four different ICD types or settings to be selected individually, as shown in Fig. 16.

Fig. 17 shows the standard tool, but for this project, a custom tool was designed because of the small valve size required. Any valve can be controlled selectively and shifted various times during one trip.

The core completion accessories included a series of nipples as contingency to isolate below the top of the M3 horizon and above the Marly formations. Sliding sleeves were also incorporated to allow for circulation path and to avoid excessive uplift/drag on the cup packers while running in a live well. Fig. 18 shows the final completion assembly as run in hole to depth. Space-out was confirmed using a casing collar locator (CCL) and gamma ray log.

Based on the requirements of the optimization, a MINLP solver was selected in the software to obtain the solution for this maximization problem. This gradient-based solver implements the MINLP method by Bonami et al. (2005). In the example in Fig. 19, MINLP solver is used with varying ICD nozzle sizes to maximize the objective function.

The hydraulic model of the well also provided the benchmark from which numerous scenarios were investigated. Various ICD settings and WHPs were analyzed to understand the well performance, as shown in Fig. 20, and ICD drawdown. WHPs below 3500 KPa are not investigated because the curves fall sharply with a steep slope.

Fig. 21 shows the oil production rate versus the ICD throat area for each zone and the well total at the cutoff WHP of 3500 KPa. M2 has the lowest water cut, which helps explain the highest oil production rate. Also shown (black curve) is the WOR, which is minimized at 25.6 mm2. Although minimizing WOR minimizes the cost of handling water production on surface, it is not necessarily the most economically sound setting since the oil rates are still rising sharply with increasing nozzle throat area. The oil rate curves flatten out above ~100 mm2.

Next, the chosen nozzle size of 103.2 mm2, is simulated against WHP and liquid rate. Fig. 22 shows the reduction of total liquid rate per zone when completed with ICD nozzle size of 103.2 mm2. M3 shows a high reduction in rate for the same ICD size due to the high prolific nature of M3 and its much higher AOFP. With the production of M3 curbed, and because now the production is not commingled with the packer isolation in place, M2 produces more.

The well was tested in this mode by first starting at low production rates (below the design rates), then gradually increasing the rate towards the production rate range required by design. Under this specific mode, significant improvement was noted. Fig. 23 shows the results.

The comparison shows how the well ratios improved as the oil cut increased from 0.4% to 1.3%, water cut decreased from 99.6% to 98.7% (Fig. 24), and GOR decreased from 32,000 scf/bbl to 10,500 scf/bbl (Fig. 25). This improvement allowed increasing oil rate from 5 BOPD to 45 BOPD, which translates into an improvement of 810% on oil rate.
