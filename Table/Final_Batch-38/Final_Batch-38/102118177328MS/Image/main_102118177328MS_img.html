ACG structure is located in the offshore of Azerbaijan part of the South Caspian Basin (Figure 1). The structure is an elongate anticline with 3 main accumulations (Figure 2): 1). Shallow Water Gunashli (SWG) is in the northwest operated by SOCAR and on production since 1980; Deep Water Gunashli (DWG) extended from SWG to the east and became a part of ACG field operated by AIOC with production start-up in 2008; 2). Chirag situated in the centre of the structure that has been on production since 1997; 3). Azeri is in the south east which is in production since 2005 (Wethington et al., 2002).

ACG structure is located in the offshore of Azerbaijan part of the South Caspian Basin (Figure 1). The structure is an elongate anticline with 3 main accumulations (Figure 2): 1). Shallow Water Gunashli (SWG) is in the northwest operated by SOCAR and on production since 1980; Deep Water Gunashli (DWG) extended from SWG to the east and became a part of ACG field operated by AIOC with production start-up in 2008; 2). Chirag situated in the centre of the structure that has been on production since 1997; 3). Azeri is in the south east which is in production since 2005 (Wethington et al., 2002).

The main producing interval in Azeri field is a Fasila reservoir that has been subdivided into 5 sublayers (Fasila A,B,C,D, & E) based on the first appraisal well stratigraphy. A stratigraphic section of Fasila reservoir from one of ACG wells shown on Figure 3 illustrates high net-to-gross (NTG) Fasila B and Fasila D sublayers separated by lower NTG Fasila A, C and Fasila E layers.

The main objective of AzC1 was support of the north flank (NF) producers. However, first couple years of injection was not giving the expected results and showing increase of the pressure just around the well in spite of high injection rate (Figure 4). After February 2007 with dropping reservoir pressure in this well pressure maintains in down-deep NF producers was observed (Figure 5).

No gas injectors (GI) were drilled and completed on the east part of Azeri field. However, well performance data along with pressure and later gas tracer data suggested Central Azeri (CA) gas injectors were infact also impacting the North Flank (NF) of East Azeri. The Bottom Hole Temperature (BHT) data from 4 EA producers closest to the CA sector showed decrease in BHT temperature of those wells nearest the gas injector once GI started-up (Figure 6)(Hutchinson et al., 2007). It was noted that the injection rates into the closest water injectors and producers had remained relatively stable with no change in choke size. This observation was confirmed by gas tracer data analysed later and was modelled based on existing reservoir model (Mammadli et al., 2014).

Tracers injected in water injectors within Azeri field have also been detected in some of the up-dip producers and along with pressure data provide information about reservoir connectivity in particular areas. So, different types of tracers injected in two NF West Azeri (WA) injectors (one of mostly Fasila B and other mostly Fasila D injectors) have been detected in three up-dip producers and suggested that different source of water (mainly B in two of them and mainly D in third one (Figure 7). Produced water chemistry analysis completed for these producers several times also suggested presence of injected water in production (Figure 8). However, they could not provide information about exact source of the water.

In spite of that, only Fasila D injector tracers have been found in one of the wells. The latest Production Loggging Tool (PLT) results suggested water production is coming from Fasila B as well (Figure 9).

AzC2 one of SF CA Pereriv BCD producer that has been producing with a low GOR for a long period of time in spite of high GOR observed in the rest of SF producers (Figure 10 point a). This well is in production since 2007. A pressure decline in this well has been observed since 2013, however there was noticed a constant increase of the background BHT (Figure 10 point b).

The dip map constructed for this area suggested obvious dip changes straight up-dip from the well location (Figure 11) that can be interpreted as a baffle.

This information was incorporated into reservoir model and helped improve the history match of the area. Presence of a baffle between AzW9 NF WA producer and rest of WA NF producers located to the east from this well has been observed from the BHG (Figure 12) and Geotap© pressure data. BHT data from AzW9 well and wells AzW10 and AzW11, shown in Figure 12, suggest no communication between these three wells. There were no pressure drop observed in AzW9 well with AzW10 start-up, instead there was pressure stabilization and even some increase of BHP observed in that well with other producers start ups. This observation has been confirmed with RFT and Geotap© pressure obtained from AzW10 and AzW9A wells drilled in proximity to AzW9 (Figure 13).
