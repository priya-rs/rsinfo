Figure 2 shows a normal frac stage on the left that was pumped according to the design schedule, and shows on the right a stage that was pumped with an unplanned sweep as a result of observed challenges with the treating pressure response. When the latter occurred, attempts were made to reduce the volume of water needed for sweeping the wellbore that which would be of utmost importance in areas of water availability limitations which has become even a bigger strain with the recent increases in frac design and proppant volumes.

Figure 3 presents an example of a resulting near wellbore screenout. In this case, a dirty sweep which comprises of cutting the proppant in the slurry to minimal concentrations instead of a pumping a full wellbore of a clean sweep to aid in water consumption management, was pumped to reduce the encountered pressure challenges. However, it appears that the dirty sweep did not allow a full wellbore and perforation cleanout that which has ultimately caused the near wellbore screenout to occur potentially as a result of the perforation clusters locking up.

The distribution of unplanned sweeps is depicted in Figure 4. 60% of the stages were pumped without the need for unplanned sweeps while the remaining 40% were pumped with anywhere between 1 and 3 sweeps total depending on the number of times the stage encountered pressure challenges to avoid screenouts.

An in-house algorithm was developed using Gamma Ray logs measured while drilling, drilling rate of penetration and Young's modulus calculated from seismic to create a rock typing methodology along the wellbore of the study wells. Eleven distinct rock facies were determined and three well types were established. Figure 5 (up) below shows the rock facies distribution in the studied wellbores and Figure 5 (down) shows a cross plot of Young's Modulus and Gamma Ray highlighting clearly the three distinct well types.

Sonic velocities for both compressional and shear components were calibrated using triaxial core testing data conducted in-situ horizontal effective stress (Sh'-confining stress) of about 2,900 psi and closure pressure measurements from a diagnostic fracture injection test (see figure 18). The initial geomechanical model was built around a cored well. To reduce the uncertainty in anisotropic mechanical properties the model was extended to other wells in the area.

Multiple iterations in pore pressure gradients were applied by calibrating the derived stresses to the mini-frac's gathered closure stress public data and by performing pressure history matching (PHM). Based on log signatures and Dean's producible history, pore pressure ramp from sub hydrostatic to over pressure is created from 10ft above Dean to Wolfcamp. Figure 19 shows the pore pressure ramp and calculated minimum horizontal stress.

 

Fracture parent well modeling is critical to understanding the changes required in operational activities (Cherian et al., 2015). A model was created with a fine grid numerical planar simulator using the petrophysics and geomechanics models described as inputs to match the fracture treatment reports obtained from the field. Figure 21 shows the completed pressure history match with the end of job instantaneous shut in pressure matched. Figure shows the fracture geometries simulated for each cluster.

 

The number of perforations and discharge coefficient were modified as described in figure 12 to match the observed surface net pressures. Ugueto et al. (2016) demonstrated this by using DAS and DTS measurements to determine the number of clusters open during a fracturing treatment to evaluate perforation cluster efficiency. For this study, it is assumed that the toe-ward perforation cluster plugs off first and subsequent pressure increases are because of the next most dominant fracture simulated.
