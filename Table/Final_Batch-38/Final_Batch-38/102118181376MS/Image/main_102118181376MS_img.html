The petroliferous Barmer Rift Basin is a narrow NNW-SSE graben structure as shown in Figure 01, extending towards north of the prolific Cambay Basin. The northern part of the basin has thick high quality fluvial sandstone reservoirs in the Fatehgarh Formation that overlies the pre-rift basement of Lower Cretaceous. The gross thickness of the Fatehgarh Formation increases in the footwall portions from south to north in the Down-to-the-West (DTTW) fault system. A similar thickness trend is observed in the un-conformably overlying Palaeocene-Eocene Barmer Hill Formation.

The Barmer Hill Formation is divided into different members/units as shown in open hole log tracks in Figure 02, based on lithological characteristics (Chowdhury et. al., 2011). In many areas, the formation contains high total organic carbon throughout, with particularly significant organic rich intervals near the base. In the lake settings, particularly in northern part of the basin, upper Barmer Hill is comprised mainly of laminated porcellanite. This unit is 50-250 meters thick and is known to be hydrocarbon bearing within structural closures.

The laminations in the upper unit consist of diagenetic micro-quartz, silt-sized quartz grains with high porosity (25-35%) and moderate permeability (0.1-4mD), organic rich and minor aluminosilicate clay. Figure 03 shows that these laminations are very pronounced at every resolution formed as a result of hemipelagic sedimentation within lacustrine environment.

Multiple zones of formation breakout were observed from image logs in vertical wells (Guha et. al., 2013, Chowdhury et.al, 2011). The directions of the main boundary faults in the Mangala field are generally coincident with the SHmax. No shear anisotropy is observed from sonic logs as seen in Figure 04 but the magnitudes of minimum and maximum horizontal stress differ based upon measured Shmin and estimated Shmax.

Figure 06 contains the fall off analysis for the injections in BH-08 ad BH-09. The interpretations indicate that the closure gradient for BH-09 (0.58 psi/ft) is significantly lower than the 0.62 psi/ft gradient estimated for BH-08. This confirmed the microseismic observations that BH-09 is not serving as a barrier for the fracture containment. The general understanding of non-pay sections to act as stress barriers for fracture height containment is ruled out for fracturing key upper BH units i.e BH-10 & BH-08.

Figure 07 (from El Rabaa et al) illustrates the fracture alignment to expect for a given well orientation with respect to the minimum & maximum horizontal stress. It predicts that a wellbore that is not properly aligned with the horizontal stresses will result in fractures that are misaligned with the wellbore. These misaligned fractures could result problems executing the fracture treatments and/or well performance issues.

Due to operational contraints coiled tubing (CT) enabled sand jet perforating was employed in the cased and perfed well. CT hydrajetting created three holes at 120 deg phased holes at each jetting location. In general, 03-04 jetting locations were used for each stage resulting in a total of 9 perforations. Based on the work done by Smith and Larry (see Figure 08), the breakdown pressure for a longitudinal fracture should be about ¾ of the breakdown pressure for a transverse fracture for the given stress state.

In order to verify the fracture orientation and length, microseismic monitoring was conducted for the first three stages in the MBH-H (longitudinal fractured well). Microseismic events indicate that the fracture aligned mainly along the wellbore with an average fracture tip-to-tip length of 170 m. The fracture height was about 95 m as shown in Figure 09. These fracture lengths were found to be about 20-25m longer than average fracture half lengths observed in case of transverse well microseismic results.

Figure 10 contains the surface injection pressures for two transversely fractured wells using different completion strategies (OH and Plug and Perf).

The relatively low surface pressure difference compared for the two completions of transverse fractures may be due to MBH-F being an OH ball drop completion, reducing the potential complexity/fracture tortuosity expected for a cased and cemented fracture completion. Figure 11 contains the surface pressure for the cased hole longitunal fractures. Note that these treatment pressures are further lower (300 to 400 psi) than the open hole tranverse fractures.

Figure 12(A) depicts the relative differences in cumulative production due to lower or higher Cfd for longitudinal and transverse fractures based on the work done by Soliman et. al. They also predicted that a longitudinal fracture should clean up easier than a transverse fracture given the difference between the nature of flow regimes in both fracture types. This paramerter was considered after low intial fracture fluid recovery was observed from transverse wells.

Fracture pressure history matching was conducted for each fracture stage. Figure 13 shows an example pressure match conducted on stage 04 with its matched fracture dimensions. Table 02 lists stagewise & average perf-to-perf half-lengths & fracture conductivities for these pressure history matches. The average fracture conductivity is 1500 md-ft. This results in an average Dimensionless Fracture Conductivity (Fcd) of 5.6 for a reservoir permeability of 1.5 md and the average adjacent stage perforation-to-perforation halflength of 67 m.

Figure 14 contains the measured PI’s for both the the longitudinal and tranversly fractured wells. Based on these actual measurements, the transverse well’s PI was about 1.43 times the PI of the longitudinal. This was about 15% lower than expected for the 07 producing fractures. This difference is likely due to the fact that the Soliman type curve assumes similar fracture conductivities for both the transverse and the longitudinal wells.

Two longitudinal horizontal wells and two transverse horizontal wells have been tested during appraisal phase of Mangala Barmer Hill. The bar plots in Figure 15(a) & (b) below represent cumulative production over time for each fractured well type in two different BH units ie. BH-10 & BH-06 respectively. The results indicate similar performance for both types of wells with the limited period production data as available during appraisal phase.

Transverse well fractures have a halflength of 164ft and 1000 mD-ft conductivity while longitudinal well fractures have a half-length of 250 ft and 1000 mD-ft conductivity. It is observed as per figure 16 that under depletion horizontal transverse well producer has marginally better production rates compared to horizontal longitudinal producer. At the end of 15 years of production forecast, transverse producer well recovers ~7% more oil compared to longitudinal producer well.

For any secondary recovery mechanisms e.g. water-flood, longitudinal wells may be preferred so as to get better flood-front movement and sweep (Pearson et.al.). With a transverse producer well, risk for early water breakthrough may be higher. A pilot test as per Figure 17 is presently being planned to evaluate incremental benefits of water-flooding in Mangala Barmer Hill with a combination of longitudinal and transverse producer well. These results will be analyzed to strategize the field development concept.
