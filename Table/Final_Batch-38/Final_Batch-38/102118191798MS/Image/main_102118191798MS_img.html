For example, Barhaug et al. (2014) and King and Wray (2014) published separate papers in which nonlinear regression models suggested an optimal stage length, treatment rate, and proppant mass per lateral foot for Williston Basin wells. Their study showed that stage length has a negative monotonic trend, meaning that more stages will increase the response variable (i.e., 180-day cumulative oil production). The authors went on to show that there is a plateau in the trend between 280 and 370 ft, and they explained that this plateau could be present because a predictor variable or combination of predictor variables need to be added to the analysis to smooth the data, or there may be very little or no benefit to decreasing the stage length from 370 to 280 ft (Fig. 1).

Mayerhofer et al. (2017) presented a hybrid physics-based MVA approach for Middle Bakken and Three Forks wells to try to overcome some of the drawbacks discussed above. They developed physics-based relationships between production outcome (i.e., 365-day cumulative oil per lateral length) and completion-driven parameters (i.e., proppant/fluid volumes pumped and fracture spacing) that can be used as "transformed" variables in the MLR. These relationships were obtained from history-matched fracture and reservoir models. The plot shows significant improvement from 150 to 800 lb/ft, but diminishing production improvements above 800 lb/ft. The curve shape and resulting proxy function are then derived from this plot. The function that approximates the results best is natural log-based, and it is shown in the plot. The natural log-based function provides the transformed relationship of proppant mass per foot of lateral in the subsequent MLR models. The authors also presented the results of reservoir-modeling sensitivities of 365-day cumulative oil production per foot of lateral vs. fracture spacing (Fig. 3).

Fig. 6 compares the predicted vs. observed Y values for the MLR and ACE models. The diagonal red reference lines indicate where the observed and predicted values would be equal. We compared how well both models perform on the data using the R2, training RMSE, and test RMSE values. The R2 statistic represents how much the model explains the variability of the response data around its mean, whereas the RMSE represents how far, on average, the residuals (observed minus predicted values) are from zero and is usually a better indicator of how well a model will predict (i.e., test RMSE).

As a whole, the Permian Basin remains the most active basin in the U.S. today. The total number of horizontal wells in the Midland Basin is currently over 6,000, of which at least 4,950 were completed in the Wolfcamp Formation. The Wolfcamp interval has stacked pay potential with different possible target zones in which to land horizontal wellbores. For this study, we focused on the Wolfcamp B interval. Fig. 7 (left) shows the Wolfcamp B section from a type log in Midland County, which is part of a larger commercial petrophysical and geomechanical dataset.

Fig. 8 shows the bivariate plots between the 365-day BO/ft vs. the predictor variables for the Midland Basin Wolfcamp B horizontal well dataset. The 365-day GOR was part of the predictor variables because it is likely tied to initial pore pressure and bubble point pressure. The stabilized 365-day water cut and 365-day GOR were used as proxies for geographical location and reservoir quality. In Fig. 8, the black dots represent the 1,825 wells and the red lines represent a linear regression of the response variable vs. each predictor variable. The linear regression of the 365-day BO/ft on the individual predictor variables gives a maximum R2 of 0.42 with TVD.

Table 8 shows the ACE regression results for this dataset. All predictor variables have p-values of less than 0.01, indicating that they are highly statistically significant. Fig. 9 presents the optimal transformations of the 365-day BO/ft and predictor variables obtained using the ACE model. This figure shows evidence of more complicated relationships between the 365-day BO/ft and the predictor variables as compared to the assumed linear relationships using the conventional MLR.

Fig. 10 illustrates the general workflow for combining the ACE model with the hybrid physics-based approach in this study. First, well completion and reservoir data were collected from various sources and then visualized using histograms and scatterplots. Next, the response and predictor variables were selected with a backward selection method. Simultaneously, fracture modeling to establish physical relationships between fracture geometry/conductivity and treatment size was performed.

The black dots represent the 1,825 Midland Basin horizontal wells in this study. The blue curve represents the predicted 365-day BO/ft from the hybrid physics-based MVA where all other predictor variables are fixed at their mean values.

As expected, regression of the transformed response on the transformed predictor variables for the ACE-augmented hybrid physics-based MVA model results in a lower R2 of 0.62 as compared to the full ACE model (0.64), but the R2 value is still higher than that obtained using the conventional MLR (0.54). Using the ACE-augmented hybrid physics-based model, the training and test RMSEs are 3.42 bbl/ft and 3.48 bbl/ft respectively in real space, which are lower than those obtained by the MLR.
