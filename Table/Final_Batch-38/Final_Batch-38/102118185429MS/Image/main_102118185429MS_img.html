The Barmer basin is a narrow NNW-SSE graben structure, considered to be a failed rift arm of Cambay basin. The northern part of the basin has thick high quality fluvial sandstone reservoirs in the Fatehgarh formation that overlies a pre-rift basement of Lower Cretaceous. The gross thickness of the Fatehgarh formation increases in the footwall portion towards north in a down-to-the-west fault system. A similar thickness trend is observed in the unconformably overlying Paleocene-Eocene lacustrine Barmer Hill formation as well as the younger shale rich Dharvi-Dungar Formation and the fluvial sediments of Thumbli-Akli Formation. The basin has gone through a differential uplift in the later stage of evolution resulting into shallower northern margins (Compton et. al., 2009, Dolson et. al., 2015) (Figure-1).

The Barmer Hill formation of Paleocene is present throughout the basin. The thickening of the Barmer Hill sediments towards the downthrown side of the faults strongly suggests a syn-rift depositional history (Dolson et. al., 2015). Unlike the underlying Fatehgarh formation where the sandstone reservoirs are deposited through fluvial sedimentation processes, Barmer Hill sediments are predominantly lacustrine deposits with subsidiary fluvial fans and fan deltas along western and eastern margins (Figure-2).

The formation has been divided into 12 units based on their petrophysical characteristics. The upper Barmer Hill is considered to be the gross reservoir, within which BH-12, BH-10, BH-8 and BH-6 are dominated by hydrocarbon saturated siliceous laminated porcellanites (Figure-3). The Barmer Hill porcellanite reservoir is separated from Fatehgarh formation by a thick argillaceous unit (BH5 to BH 1) part of which is identified as the source rock for the entire Barmer Basin (Naidu et al., 2012).

The laminations in the upper Barmer Hill consist of diagenetic porcellanites, silt-sized quartz grains with high porosity (25-35%) and low permeability (0.1-4mD), organic rich and minor alumino-silicate clay(Guha et.al., 2013). The laminations are very pronounced at every resolution formed as a result of hemipelagic sedimentation within the lacustrine environment (Figure-4).

The gradual change of silica phase occurs through series of dissolution and precipitation processes. In this process two major phenomena are observed; first the transformation of Opal-A to Opal-CT, followed by the transformation from Opal-CT to microcrystalline quartz (Behl et. al., 2007). The phase transformation is characterized by the change in the rock properties such as order of crystallinity, porosity, density etc. Each diagenetic phase has distinguishable physical properties (Table-1); they show characteristic patterns and textures in XRD and SEM respectively (Figure-5).

The silica diagenesis depends on several factors other than temperature and pressure. The detrital clay content plays a major role in the diagenetic processes. In the first phase of silica transformation volume of shale slows down the reaction where as it accelerates the reaction at the next phase transition boundary (Isaacs, 1981) (Figure-6& 7).

The organic origin of the siliceous rocks of Barmer Hill and presence of biogenic Opal-CT in the outcrop has been discussed by several researchers (Chowdhury et. al., 2011) The porcellanites at outcrop is exceptionally pure and comprises upto 100% Opal-CT having porosity of 50-56 p.u. with grain density of 2.1-2.22gm/cc (Figure-8).

Evidences of transition from Opal-A to Opal-CT has not been reported anywhere in the basin, but the transition zone of Opal-CT to microcrystalline quartz, is preserved in deeper reservoirs. In the subsurface, siliceous Barmer Hill rocks are mostly microcrystalline in nature (Figure-9).

The study summarizes understanding developed from the data of Bhagyam, DP, NL, Mangala and Aishwariya fields, where the Barmer Hill reservoirs are dominantly porcellanites (Figure-10). Data from more than 500 wells were taken into consideration to analyze the porosity and density trends and characterize the silica phases present in this area. The reservoir zones were delineated by applying a Vshale cut-off of 40%. The net reservoirs were further classified on the basis of finer V-shale bins, namely 0-10%, 10-20%, 20-30% and 30-40% to define lithological facies. Zone wise averages of porosity and density were derived from respective petrophysical logs for each litho-facies class observed in wells.

The cleanest porcellanites (within V-shale range of 0-10%) across all the fields show variable decreasing trends in porosity within the range of 45 p.u. to 5p.u. The porosity-depth data of each field were observed to be clustered at specific depth bands (Figure-11) but showed similar trends. Such clustering of petrophysical parameters of hemipelagic sediments can only be explained by impact of regional tectonics (basin uplift) and diagenetic alterations.

Once the uplift corrections are applied, all the field data get positioned to a notional maximum depth of burial, where the porosities appear to be merging into the same cluster, showing similar depth trends (Figure-12). The variations within the depth trends however are indicative of diagenetic alterations prior to the regional uplift.

Reduction of porosity in sedimentary rocks due to the overburden is best demonstrated in the shale units present within a formation. In Barmer Hill and Fatehgarh formations, a shale compaction trend of ~2 p.u./100m is observed. Along the entire depth range, the porosity-depth trend of the fluvial sands (> 90% silica) of underlying Fatehgarh formation is also comparable to the shale compaction trend (Figure-13).

On analyzing the data for all the lithofacies in Barmer Hill porcellanites, two distinct porosity trends were observed. At shallower depths, till 750m TVDss, a sharp decline in porosity is observed that measures upto 6 p.u./100m. This trend is more evident in cleaner porcellanites, i.e. lithofacies with low Vshale content. A step change in porosity is observed at 750m TVDss and 1400m TVDss (Figure-14).

The porosity data for all the lithofacies classes have been analyzed to establish the role of detrital matter in silica diagenesis. In Mangala Barmer Hill, the zonal average porosity varies from>30 p.u (in cleanest porcellanites, 0%-20% Vshale) to <20 p.u. (in poorest porcellanites, 30%-40% Vshale) (Figure-16).

The Mangala and Aishwariya field data (high and low Vshale) are schematically represented as green and red boxes respectively, in the porcellanite phase diagram of the Monterey formation (adopted from Behl,2007) (Figure-17).

The X-Ray Diffraction (XRD) results of samples from Mangala Barmer Hill indicate coexistence of silica in Opal-CT and microcrystalline quartz phases. The general pattern of these sample are to develop crystallinity with increasing depth and detrital contents. Thus, the shallowest and cleanest porcellanites generally display poorest crystallinity in XRD studies. The sample form 630 m TVDss shows a wider base at 20 quartz peak and a lower crystallinity index than euhedral quartz crystals (Figure - 18).

Under SEM, a cluster of platy quartz is observed at the shallowest (740m TVDss) sample. The intermediate (760m TVDss) sample comprises of subhedral and platy disc shaped crystals, and in the deeper sample (780m TVDss) the euhedral crystals appear most prominently (Figure-19).
