The name of ‘Ghail Formation' comes from the type locality ‘Wadi Ghail' where the formation is approximately 500 m thick (Maurer et al., 2008). The Ghail Formation belongs to the Permian-Triassic Ruus al Jibal Group (RAJ), which overlies the Hagil Formation and is overlain by the Milaha Formation (Figure 1).

F. Maurer, R. Rettori and R. Martini (2008) Triassic stratigraphy, facies and evolution of the Arabian shelf in the northern United Arab Emirates . International Journal of Earth Sciences, 97, 765– 784 .

Skeletal packstone contains bivalves, gastropods, echinoderms, foraminifera, algae, mollusks and some peloids, which are constituted of fine- to medium-crystalline dolomite. In Figure 2, picture (a) shows an abundancy of bioclasts, which could be gastropods, bivalves and echinoderms. In picture (b), the rounded tubular fragments could be echinoderms or algae. Some small bioclasts are unrecognizable.

Peloidal packstone has packstone texture, while peloids are the dominant grain types. The grain size is fine to medium. Typically, sorting varies from moderate to poor. Additional grain types include gastropods, bivalve fragments, small benthic foraminifers, echinoderms and small coated grains (Figure 3).

Packstone-wackestone texture is common in the "transition" zone between massive beds and laminated beds. This lithofacies has texture varying from mud-dominated peloidal packstone to wackstone (Figure 4). Both textures can be observed in different parts of the same thin section. The grain types include small benthic foraminifers, bioclasts including bivalves, gastropods, echinoderms, green algae, coated grains and peloids. In packstone-dominated thin sections, the main grains are peloids and coated grains.

This type of lithology can be observed in the transition zone from massive beds to laminated beds. In thin sections, large grains are observed, like mollusks, gastropods, and echinoderms fragments (Figure 5).

Wackestone is mainly formed in laminated beds of Ghail Formation (Figure 6). It is mainly mud supported. Few grains can be observed, including gastropods, bivalve fragments, echinoderms, small benthic foraminifers and pellets.

Fenestral fabrics are mostly developed in this type of texture and few bioclasts were observed (Figure 7). This kind of texture is mainly observed in the upper part of member B.

Mudstones are observed in the laminated beds of the Ghail Formation (Figure 8). Fenestral fabrics are very common within this lithology. All the pores are filled by dolomite cement.

This type of dolomite forms in mudstone or mud-wackestone (Figure 9). All the mud matrix was dolomitized. Very few allochems can be identified as ghost texture. Under SEM observation (Figure 10), the crystal shape can be recognized more clearly, containing subhedral to anhedral crystalline dolomite.

This type of texture is more common in member C of the Lofer cycle, where the original texture contains less mud. The average size of this type crystal is 20 um. The crystals are more euhedral, which means the crystals have enough space to grow (Figure 11).

This type of dolomite represents the replacement of matrix. The dolomite crystals are tightly packed, with some crystal-face junctions preserved (Figure 12). The dolomite has more cloudy-cores, which could be caused by non-mimic replacement of allochems, or the presence of inclusions. Most of the original textures are not preserved. Though, some ghosts of allochems can be identified in thin section. It is more common in the massive beds.

Medium crystalline dolomite was formed by replacement of grains (Figure 13). The matrix is represented by finer crystalline dolomite. The size of the dolomite crystals replacing the allochems is coarser compared to that in the matrix. Some of the original grains still can be identified, such as gastropods, shell fragments.

In thin section observations, this kind of pore-filling dolomite is very common (Figure 14). It is characterized by ‘drusy dolomite', where the crystal size increases from the rim toward the center of voids. Most dolomite crystals are subhedral to anhedral with many crystal-face junctions and straight, compromise boundaries. From the different size of the crystals, it indicates different generations of dolomite cement have occurred.

In the samples (Figure 15), the allochems are transformed into fine-grained calcite, and the original internal structures are lost, then calcite has been replaced by dolomite later. Many bioclasts and coated grains are completely micritized to form peloids.

In thin-sections, the dark micrite envelopes surround bioclasts and non-skeletal grains (Figure 16). When the grains are leached away, micrite envelope acts to preserve the shape of the grains during meteoric or early burial alteration. In the samples, all the bioclasts were dissolved to form moldic pores, then filled by dolomite cement later.

Dolomite cements are very common in the study samples from the Ghail Formation; dolomite cements can be found filling vugs (Figure 17). In vugs the crystal size increases from the rim toward the center of voids or interparticle pores in a typical drusy dolomite arrangement.

Stylolites and dissolution seams are resulted from chemical compaction, which were observed in thin sections (Figure 18). A stylolite is the most readily identifiable form of chemical dissolution. Stylolites are characterized by relatively high amplitude while solution seams show very low amplitude. The black material in stylolites and solution seams could be composed of varying combinations of iron oxides (Figure 19), bitumen, clay minerals and detrital organic particles.

Organic matter was observed along solution seam under fluorescence microscopy, which shows a bright green-fluorescent color (Figure 20). In many cases, the black material shows a finely laminated texture with dark translucent brown color in thin section, which suggests a major component of depositional clay. BSE/EDS study also indicate the presence of clay mineral along stylolites (Figure 21 and Figure 22). EDS analysis shows clay minerals rich in Al, Si, K, which could be illite.

In some samples, it can be observed some intercrystalline porosity formed due to dolomite dissolution (Figure 23).

In the samples, bioclasts and peloids have been fractured as a result of compaction (Figure 24). The micritized grains were crushed and then blocky dolomite cements filled the fracture (Figure 24 B).

In thin sections, two kinds of iron oxides can be observed, hematite and goethite. Under plane-polarized light, the iron oxides are opaque. By illuminating the surface of the sample with a tangential reflected light, red and yellow colored minerals can be observed (Figure 25). According to Tucker (2009), hematite would turn to red color in reflected light while goethite would be yellow to brown color.

Also, from the SEM/EDS results (Figure 26), the material could be goethite according to the shape and chemical component. Two different stages of iron oxides formation can be derived from petrographic and SEM observation (Figure 25). One type could be formed in an earlier stage, observed within the matrix. Another one is observed in the fracture, which is cutting across dolomite crystals. It could be formed at later stage. We cannot tell which happens first, hematite or goethite.

Calcite cement was observed along fractures, which was identified by Alizarin Red S staining. In the study samples (Figure 27 A), the grains were cut across by fractures. Both calcite cement and iron oxides were observed along fracture (Figure 28). The iron oxides were formed at a later stage.

Under BSE/EDS study, both calcite cement and organic matter were observed along the fractures (Figure 29). According to BSE results (Figure 29), the grey area should be dolomite, while the white part should be calcite. And the black material could be organic matter.

Stable isotope analyses were conducted on all rock samples collected from the outcrop, in which there were 46 samples performed on bulk rock and 9 samples performed on large dolomite cement. From the results, a cross plot has been generated relating the carbon versus oxygen isotopic signatures of the different types of dolomite (bulk-replacive vs. cements) of the Ghail Formation (Figure 30).

One representative sample was chosen to conduct the fluid inclusion analysis. All the fluid inclusions observed within the crystals are primary inclusions, in the size range from 1 to 20 μm. Two-phase (liquid and vapour) aqueous fluid inclusions are present in cloudy bands within dolomite crystals (Figure 31). Under UV light observation, the fluid inclusions in the sample are not filled by fluorenscent hydrocarbons (Figure 31 D).

Five fluid inclusions assemblages were chosen to measure the homogenization temperature (Th) and salinity (Tm). The range of Th is from 108.8 to 120.3 °C and the average Th is 114.3 °C (Figure 32). The range of salinity is from 14.9 to 18.1% NaCl eq. and the average salinity is 16.7% NaCl eq. (Figure 33).

Figure 35 shows data from dolomite samples of the Ghail Formation compared to Permo-Triassic marine carbonates. Note that dolomite cements are significantly lighter than the marine carbonates, indicating a high temperature origin. The bulk dolomites are heavier than the marine carbonates, indicating a low temperature origin.

The second approach is to compare dolomites of the Ghail Formation to other dolomites, whose origin has been established by independent parameters. According to Allan and Wiggins (1993), dolomite with δ18O values more negative than −6.5% PDB most probably had a high temperature origin and dolomite with δ18O values more positive than −2.5% PDB most probably had a low temperature origin (Figure 36). This could be considered as a useful guideline for interpreting dolomite origin.
