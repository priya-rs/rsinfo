The KSP of common minerals found in shale is summarized in Table 1. Basically, the higher a mineral’s KSP is, the more of it will dissolve in the water. Solubility is mainly affected by temperature and pressure. Most minerals observed dissolved more with increasing temperature. But carbonate minerals are more soluble in cold water. Larson and Buswall (1942) proved as temperature increases, KSP for CaCO3 decreases. Increasing pressure also leads to an increase in solubility of minerals. But for condensed phases, pressure effect on solubility is typically weaker than temperature.

The abundance of these elements in each sample is consistent with key inorganic geochemical characteristics of related chemofacies (Amin.et al., 2016). According to XRF results, rock samples from Lower Eagle Ford contain high concentration of Ca (16.0~25.1 wt. %) and low concentration of Mg (0.48-0.16wt. %) (Not shown). The great excess of Ca over Mg means calcite is the dominate mineral, which accounts for 40~62.5% of rocks.

Other elements do not show significant difference between chemofacies, although they are enriched in produced water. However, ANOVA cannot identify where the differences occurred between chemofacies. Tukey’s honest significance test (Tukey HSD) is another statistical test in conjunction with the ANOVA to find which chemofacies differ (Tukey, 1949).
