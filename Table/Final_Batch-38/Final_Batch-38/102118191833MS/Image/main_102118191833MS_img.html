The Lindero Atravesado block is located in the Neuquén basin in Argentina, 1,000 km from Buenos Aires city and 30 km from Neuquen city (Fig. 1). The area comprises 530 km2 (approximately 131,000 acres) with 246 wells drilled, of which 109 wells target the Grupo Cuyo tight gas formations: Lajas (main target) and Punta Rosada (secondary target). The rock charge occurred vertically and was generated by the same energy of the generation and expulsion of the hydrocarbon from the source rock, Los Molles, which was deposited in a marine platform environment and is in direct contact with the multiple tight stacked reservoirs of Lajas and Punta Rosada formations. The Lindero Atravesado block is located in the Neuquén basin in Argentina, 1,000 km from Buenos Aires city and 30 km from Neuquen city (Fig. 1). The area comprises 530 km2 (approximately 131,000 acres) with 246 wells drilled, of which 109 wells target the Grupo Cuyo tight gas formations: Lajas (main target) and Punta Rosada (secondary target). The rock charge occurred vertically and was generated by the same energy of the generation and expulsion of the hydrocarbon from the source rock, Los Molles, which was deposited in a marine platform environment and is in direct contact with the multiple tight stacked reservoirs of Lajas and Punta Rosada formations. A stratigraphic column of Lindero Atravesado block is illustrated in Fig. 2.

Grupo Cuyo is the first stage of a generalized flood of the basin and its fill, mainly clastic, is composed by Lajas Formation and Punta Rosada sandstones. These sandstones are the reservoir for the hydrocarbon generated in the Los Molles Formation (shale). This type of trapping allowed classifying the field as a Basin-centered gas accumulation (BCGA), where the source rock underlies the reservoirs and its charge is produced vertically due to the overpressure in the system. Migration process is favored in areas of the field where faulting is more predominant, and consequently shallower reservoirs like Punta Rosada are charged. This phenomenon is confirmed by logs and gas shows detected while drilling, and can be observed in the seismic cross sections presented in Fig. 3 (Ref. 1).

The Lajas Formation structural map shows two structural highs to the west and to the east of the Lindero Atravesado block. The structure goes upward to the NE, reaching the Río Neuquén neighbor block, also with a tight gas project from the same formations under development. The tight gas project in Lindero Atravesado is focused on the eastern and western areas of the block, where Grupo Cuyo depths are shallower. The eastern area was developed first. The western area will be developed after the eastern area. Due to the higher associated costs of drilling and completing wells in areas where the target is deeper than 4,600 meters, the central area is not currently commercially attractive for development. An isochronal structural map of the block and a West-East cross section across it can be observed in Fig. 4 (Ref. 1).

Wells were completed with a monobore 4 ½-in. casing, one vertical and the other deviated in an S-shape configuration. Target formations, Lajas and Punta Rosada, are tight sands with sand layers of 10 to 20 m thick, with 1 to 5 m shale barriers in between. Reservoir properties, including geomechanical and pretophysical, combined with completion techniques such as limited entry determined 11 stages to cover all layers, with 3 to 5 clusters per stage. Originally, a limited entry technique, 3 shots per foot (spf) was used (Fig 6).

Logs available were gamma ray, neutron and density porosity, resistivity, dipole sonic and others. Mechanical properties were determined using dipole sonic data and pore pressures correlated from neighboring wells. This area presents extremely high pore pressure gradients, in the order of 0.77 psi/ft, which impacts dramatically in fracture gradient. Very high fracture gradients, slightly greater than overburden were obtained, from 0.88 to 1.07 psi/ft, (Fig. 7).

Fracture designs were run using a conventional pseudo-3D simulator widely used in the industry. Original designs included x-linked gel, high pad percentage and 120 Tn average ceramic proppant (30/60 or 20/40) per stage, (Fig. 8).

Static bottomhole temperature is about 300°F to 320°F, which is over the limit for borate conventional fluids. To try to overcome this inconvenience, an extensive calculation of fracture fluid cooling during the job was made to analyze different stages of the fluid during the job supporting different maximum temperatures and residence time. After an exhaustive laboratory testing program, several satisfactory formulations were obtained, using refined guar base, borate x-linked and a very detailed engineering selection of breakers and breaker schedules. The first fluid stage of x-linked fluid had to maintain good viscosity at 300°F during 120 min. An encapsulated breaker for very high temperature at very low concentration was the best option. Results obtained can be seen in Fig. 9.

The selection of the breaker system for the first fluid stage required an intensive lab program and testing different formulation following BHT cooling effects. Several solid and liquid breakers were used and just one performed as required for these specific reservoir conditions and borate fluid system. In the first formulation, a high temperature liquid breaker was used and the results were not good enough, Fig. 10.

Before each fracture treatment, an injection test was performed to obtain an estimation of fluid leak-off (slickwater), and to identify any abnormal behavior (PDL, fracture height growth or height recession) and identify the ISIP value to help in geomechanics model calibration, Fig. 11 – Table 2.

The PVT fluid property data was obtained in lab tests of an exploration well in this field, the input data for calculations was reservoir fluid composition, and as a function of pressure, gas volume factor (Bg), gas viscosity and compressibility factor (Z). In this case, the lab result includes volume factor, gas viscosity and compressibility factor data. But in case those data weren't available is possible to calculate gas viscosity and compressibility factor with good accuracy as function of gas composition, Fig. 24.

Production logs were run on both wells. Logs in Well A were run after 83 days (Fig. 25) and in Well B were run 49 days after the start of production (Fig. 26). Production data includes fluid velocity, pressure and temperature.

Although similar fracture treatments have been performed along the productive layers, and those layers have similar petrophysics properties, both wells showed the same production behavior with most of the production flowing from the deepest cluster fractured, and less production from the upper clusters. Comparing the PLT data and the calculated permeability (k) times net pay height (H), two different production behavior responses were noted, with better production for the deepest clusters and poor production for the upper clusters with the same k.H contribution (Fig. 27). This particular behavior is still under study

Petrophysical analyses and core sample lab tests were performed on two samples of the upper formation (Punta Rosada Fm.) and two from the deepest formation (Lajas Fm.). This data shows significant differences in water relative permeability (Krw), gas relative permeability (Krg) and residual gas saturation (Sgmin) between both formations. Affecting the reservoir permeability with the relative permeability values, the effective permeability (Keff) times net height (Keff.H) and absolute permeability times net height (K.H) are quite different. Keff.H matches better the accumulated production data (Fig. 28).

Using both surface and bottom hole flowing pressure, the correlation of Cullender and Smith with surface pressure calculates a bottom hole flowing pressure with less than 4% offset of measured data in both wells. With those check points the correlated bottomhole flowing pressure was assumed as good, accurate data (Fig. 29).

After building the reservoir model, the next input was to simulate fracture dimensions after pressure and microseismic matching on the model to calculate production. First calculations showed a total production larger than the measured well production (Fig. 30), so a cut on production should be done to change fracture properties.

According to the production logging data, the production calculation was made assuming as active fracture treatments those with a contribution of more than 4% of total production, changing from eleven active fractures to seven on Well A, and from ten active fractures to six on well B, Fig. 31,Table 6.

This second approach on production calculations allows a closer matching but still resulted in higher than actual gas rates, which indicated that fracture dimensions had to be adjusted according to real production (Fig. 32).

Due the short production time, there were uncertainties to confirm with production matching the main cause of the lower production in the upper zone and adjusting the fracture length as function of gas inflow per fracture in a final matching. But the initial calculation showed a good initial approach with an effective fracture length of 70 % of the propped length (Fig. 33 – 34).
