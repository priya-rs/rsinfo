Production started in the field in 1991 and till date 13 oil wells have been completed in the reservoir and 27 wells have penetrated the structure. The oil-rim in the reservoir is overlain by some 70 ft of gas amounting to over 1 Tcf of gas cap gas. The goal of the next phase of the development is to blow down this gas cap and there is the need to determine the best time to commence the blowdown of the gas cap without joepardising the oil recovery. A properly history-matched model is required for this analysis. A map of the reservoir is shown in Fig.1 below.

A major problem has been getting a match of both the water production and the pressure depletion in the reservoir at the same time. A match of one results in a mismatch of the other. Figs. 2a – 2d show the quality of the reservoir level match that was obtained at the beginning of the current study using the previous match parameters. To further understand the reservoir, the adjoint technique was deployed in a model maturation mode to gain insight from adjoint recommended changes. The parameters that were adjusted in this mode were global permeability multipliers applied at the reservoir flow unit levels and fault seal capacities of the internal faults in the reservoir.

The MCMC technique was deployed using the proxies to get best case history matches and the posterior uncertainty space. Fig 4 shows the spread in the simulation result for cumulative oil using the prior” uncertainty band. The solid line in the plots indicate the measured data. Similar plots were generated for cumulative water and reservoir pressure. The plots envelope the measured data, indicating that there are combinations of parameters within the defined uncertainty band that will reproduce the measured data.

The “prior” uncertainty band is somewhat biased in this case as indications from the model maturation exercise are considered in building the band. The model maturation exercise which led to better understanding of the dynamic behavior in the reservoir helped in achieving this envelope of the measured data. Fig 5 show the the simulation results after the application of the MCMC technique. This represents results using the posterior uncertainty band i.e. the uncertainty band fully biased by considering the mismatch of the production data. The spread in the results is now closer to the reported data.

The posterior uncertainty band is then used again with ED workflow in the model prediction mode to generate representative P10, P50 and P90 models. Fig 6 shows the prediction of cumulative oil production from the selected models. There is a convergence of the cases due to the long period of historical data and the number of wells that have penetrated the structure and the reduction in the uncertainty by the history matching exercise.

Fig. 11 is a difference map showing the areas that the adjoint-based optimization technique recommended adjustments in the permeability during the fine-tuning exercise. The recommended changes are localized to a few places in the reservoir. This is an indication that based on the earlier model maturation and experimental design exercises, a reasonably good description of the subsurface has been achieved. If there had been radical changes recommended at this stage, this would have led to a recycle back to model maturation.

One further insight was gained from the recommended adjustments, is that it could lead to short term production improvement activities. To achieve a history match, the adjoint technique recommended permeability reductions greater than a factor of 2 in only two wells – Well 20 and Well 08 shown in Fig 12 below. The need to reduce permeability near a well could be due to low permeability areas not properly captured in the geological model or it could also be due to damage.

When the number of parameters are manageable, other techniques might compete favourably or even do better than the adjoint technique. Figs. 13 and 14 below show the performance of two non-gradient optimization techniques – a simulated annealing algorithm and a particle swarm optimization algorithm - in matching the reservoir level water cut data. The methods were tested in the model maturation mode where global parameters were adjusted to understand from the recommended adjustments the dynamics in the reservoir.

The adjoint technique should not regarded as a silver bullet that will force a match in every situation. Fig 15 below show the BSW for well 04 which could not be matched. Fig 16 shows the match of the oil rate for the well (the match was constrained on liquid rate). The match is reasonably good. Thus, from the point of view of the overall mismatch objective function, the impact of the BSW of this well is not significant and hence the adjoint gradients are not sensitive to it.

The effectiveness of the adjoint technique depends on the gradient-based optimization technique it is used with. Fig 17 below shows the performance of two gradient-based optimization techniques - LBFGSB and LSQR – in reducing the mismatch objective function. The gradients used by the algorithms were adjointbased. This match was carried out post the model maturation and application of the MCMC technique. The LBFGSB [9] is a memory-efficient quasi-newton method suitable for optimization problems with many variables.
