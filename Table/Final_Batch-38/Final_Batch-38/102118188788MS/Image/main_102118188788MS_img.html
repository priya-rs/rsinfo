The field is located in the North of Oman. It is a shallow 20Km long elongated carbonate monocline with an eroded fault escarpment and a hydrocarbon column of more than 400 m. This carbonate reservoir consists of seven sub-units of cretaceous age which are separated by extensive shale layers (see Fig.1), yet in dynamic interaction via faults and fractures.

Recognising the interplay of these factors, we are introducing the concept of shallow GOGD “floor wells” to optimise secondary oil rims development and accelerate recovery (see fig.2).

The field is a long monocline located in the tilted footwall of a partly-inverted NW-SE regional-scale normal fault. The general structure of the field has evolved during two major stages of deformation: Late Cretaceous (Alpine I) and Miocene (Alpine II). Structural features at all scales systematically belong to two main directional sets oriented respectively along WNW and NW strike directions. These two main sets of fracture orientation reflect the imprint at reservoir scale of the well-calibrated regional structural history2,3 (see fig.3).

This is a very shallow giant oil field. The hydrocarbons storage resides within the porous matrix (see fig.6) despite the overwhelming imprint of fractures on fluid transport and performance described in the previous chapter. Facies and reservoir quality are fairly variable throughout the stratigraphy; microporous bioturbated carbonate facies (high porosity and low permeability) are dominant, in particular in our units of interest.

Bioturbation textures: the dominant microporous wackstones/packestones facies are intensively bioturbated, with the exception of the presence of rudist rich leached facies at the top of the sequences; this result in “nodular textures” (see fig.7) which exacerbate the appearance of vertical heterogeneity of the unit based on small scale measurements (such as core plugs); overall, it is important to realise that the units are homogeneously bioturbated which has consequences for poor efficiency of water flood and relative permeability considerations previously reported by Abu-Shiekah5

The main control on porosity and permeability is now understood to be the water depth at the time of deposition influencing the initial fabrics and their diagenetic overprint: deposition at deeper water depth (at the bottom of the sequences) results on muddier facies that through dissolution transform into higher porosity despite lower permeability (see Fig. 8-right). The minor contrast but consistent stacking of rock quality has consequences for development, since we have observed1 a preferential de-saturation of marginally less porous intervals at the top of the sequences (apparently defying gravity but consistent with core data and log resistivity post injection).

The initial oil distribution in this field, before development started is characterised by the presence of tilted contacts as defined from early well data (see fig.9). We have interpreted1 such contact as a clear imprint on saturation of the most recent tectonic rotation of the structure (AlpineII) after the charge of the field with hydrocarbons. The workflow leading to the interpretation of the pre-production fluid fill cycle has been reported in a previous publication4.

There is evidence for a deep oil rim development in the main fracture network in this field which is produced through long horizontal high rate wells at the bottom of the structure. On the other hand, there is also log confirmation of pockets of gas in the matrix at different depths even within the same stratigraphy and within geographical proximity (see fig.11 where 100m delta gas contact in matrix was logged in the 90s). There is not a single GOC in the matrix for the field or even for a specific stratigraphic layer.

The interpretation of the gravity processes in our field is supported by dynamic data on performance of shallow wells, where individual well gas production is not related to the placement depth (see Fig. 12).

Analytical modeling has been backed up by additional dynamic simulation conducted using simple box models to address residual uncertainties associated with the floor well GOGD concept in a fractured reservoir (see Fig.13)

 

Pseudo-wells are used to mimic the continuous gas fluxes taking place across the reservoir that result in gas-filled fracture corridors. A pair of gas injector and oil producer wells is located updip and downdip fracture corridor respectively (see Fig.15). The injector is completed in the top layer (z=1), the producer is completed in the bottom layer (z=10).

Upon volumetric initialization of the box model, dummy wells (producers) in the fracture corridors are activated to reduce the average reservoir pressure to current operating pressure. Once the operating pressure is reached, the dummy producers in the fracture corridors are closed in, the development well in the matrix block is activated, and the injectors in the fracture corridors start injecting gas through a monitor to maintain an average constant pressure of 30 bar (see fig.16). The development well(s) produce against a gas constraint / GOR level that is considered to be the maximum allowable operating level. Based on simulation work a floor well can produce comfortably for about 10 years against this gas constraint (see fig. 17).

The reference model was used to assess the impact of subsurface parameters, as illustrated in Table 1 and Fig. 18. In addition, sensitivities to block size, well length and multiwall placement were also simulated with the reference subsurface parameters.

The key subsurface uncertainties influencing oil recovery in the simulation box model are the matrix permeability, the background fracture properties (permeability and spacing) and the gas oil capillary pressure (see Fig.18); the volumes associated to varying block size, depending on the fault density for different sectors of the field is important for the success of the individual wells (See Fig. 19).

When two floor development wells are active at the same time, the simulation results show indeed that theoretical incremental recovery from the matrix block with these two wells is minor when compared to a single well development (see Fig.20). In practical terms, the strategy to pair wells to ensure drainage of potential subseismic blocks and to shelter deeper wells from gas should be further investigated. It is recommended to place the GOGD floor development well as downdip as possible in the structure to benefit from the structure dip and maximize oil recovery from the matrix block. In pragmatic terms that might be difficult to achieve for the smaller grid blocks.
