The reservoir consists of three partially overlapping turbidite fans (as shown in Fig. 1). All of the turbidite fans consist of two geological facies: sand and shaly sand. The same set of facies are considered for providing saturation tables to the simulator. A major fault divides each of the fans into two halves, thus creating six equilibration regions (Fig. 1).

The three fans fall in a single pressure/volume/temperature (PVT) region with heavy oil+gas fluid characteristics. The reservoir is undersaturated in equilibrium conditions with initial reservoir pressure of 4,533 psia and saturation pressure of 4,500 psia. For the period of history, the Sam Hill oil field (the hypothetical oil field in consideration) consists of five producers and injectors with 2 years of history. The average water cut of the field is ~0% and average initial gas-oil ratio (GOR) of the field is ~230 v/v. The field is typically characterized by low water cut, low GOR, and high oil production rates, as shown in Fig. 2.

Fig. 3 shows the global history match. Overall, the simulated cumulative production for oil and water match that of cumulative history. However, stimulated gas production tends to be slightly higher at the end of year 2002.

After completing plans to address the HSE concerns and finalizing the reservoir management strategy, each of the prediction scenarios was simulated for next 20 years to classify the scenarios in terms of cumulative production, recovery factors, and economics. Field oil production rates for all the scenarios are shown in Fig. 16, and the oil recovery factor for all the scenarios are shown in Fig. 17. The combined analysis of both shows the mixed wells (horizontal and vertical) case with waterflood and the case with all vertical wells with waterflood as the two best scenarios.

The top three scenarios with the highest NPV values were selected for economic limit analysis. In this study, vertical wells with injectors, vertical and horizontal wells with injectors, and vertical wells with electric submersible pumps (ESP) and injectors have the highest NPV (Fig. 18).

Economic limit analysis is useful because it helps the reservoir engineer to plan for future improved recovery or an enhanced recovery scheme. A plot of cumulative before-tax cash flow (BTCF) was plotted against the forecast timeline. The point where the cumulative BTCF starts to decline is the point where the economic limit is reached, and further decision and analysis must be carried out to ensure maximum hydrocarbon recovery is achieved. Fig. 19 shows the general plot of a cumulative BTCF versus forecast timeline plot.

Once the development scenario has been decided, an economic sensitivity analysis on the after-tax cash flow was carried out. In this project, sensitivity variables included operational costs such as injection water, produced water treatment, and other operating expenditures that are subject to change of up to 50%. The same applies to production variables such as oil and gas price. The final decision will be based on NPV, recovery factor, economic limit, and after-tax cash-flow sensitivity. Fig. 20 shows one such sensitivity analysis of oil prices for different scenarios.

Different adjustments in the geological and fluid properties were required to match the observed response of every well. Well SH-1 had a small buildup, which required very small timesteps to accurately match the shut-in period (Fig. A-1). For well SH-2, the local grid must be refined to reduce numerical dispersion and match the gas movement more accurately. Porosity around this well seems to be overestimated by the geologist; hence, a pore volume multiplier of 0.7 was used to reduce the pore volume around the well.
