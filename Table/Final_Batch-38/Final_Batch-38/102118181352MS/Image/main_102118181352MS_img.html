The Johan Sverdrup field, discovered by Lundin Petroleum in 2010, is currently classified as the fifth largest oil discovery on the Norwegian continental shelf. The field, outlined in Figure 1, is located 140 km offshore on the western coast of Norway and covers an area of approximately 200 square kilometers. The reservoir section within the Intra-Draupne Formation Sandstone is relatively homogeneous with Darcy to multi-Darcysand, and the thickness is between 40-70 meters in the central parts of the field.

Figure 2 shows a general overview of the process. Previous studies showed that the kh was the main uncertainty parameter affecting the DST responses. Hence, the isochore thickness and reservoir permeability are included with uncertainty in order to obtain updated values. Other input parameters such as oil-watercontact, relative permeability, fault transmissibilities etc. are fixed throughout this study.

The residual field account for uncertainty in the seismic interpretation, it is modeled as a Gaussian random field with a spherical covariance function. The variogram range of the residual field varies according to a normal distribution, with a mean range of 2000 m, and a standard deviation of 1000 m, sampled values below 200 m is set to 200 m. Four random realizations of the residual surface are displayed in Figure 3.

As a mean to bypass this challenge, while still accounting for structural changes in the model during the data assimilation process, we pad the grid by adding 20 extra meters to h0, below the base of the Intra-Draupne Formation (20 additional layers). Then we activate/deactivate cells in this padded grid as the isochore thickness is changing (by setting the porosity to zero for cells in the padded grid) as illustrated in Figure 4. All four scenariosevaluated include the structural uncertainty.

Scenario 1: Constant horizontal permeability away from the wells, established using interpolation of permeability values obtained from the analytical PTA, shown in Figure 5.

We model the spatial variability similarly as the isochores (where the vertical variogram direction varies according to a normal distribution, with a mean range of 5 m, and a standard deviation of 3 m, where sampled values below 1 m are set to 1 m). Four realizations of the generated permeability fields are shown in Figure 6.

The standard deviation is 10 Darcy for both rock-types. Again, we use a spherical covariance function adding uncertainty in the range parameter as described in Scenario 2. Figure 7 displays two realizations showing the facies and permeability for one horizontal layer of the model.

Scenario 4: We apply the same approach as scenario 3, where we replace the mean of the Gaussian latent parameter with the background trend parameter shown in Figure 8. The trend parameter was manually constructed to improve the fit of the DST data. The probability of rock type 2 is increased close to 16/3-8 S and 16/3-5, while keeping a preference for rock-type 1 around 16/2-6 T2 and 16/3-4. Further, we model the permeability conditioned to the rock types as in scenario 3.

 

Figure 10 and Figure 11 display the simulated and observed pressure derivative response for the 200 models before and after conditioning to the observed data using an ensemble smoother. Please note that because of the high permeability and downhole shut-in, the early time unit slope during wellbore storage is finished within one second of the buildup and is therefore not visible in the observed pressure data with a sampling rate of one second. In the conditioning, we assume a log-linear increase in the tolerance from 0.01 log-cycles to 0.05 log-cycles for the duration of the buildup period.

Figure 12 illustrates the estimated median of the difference between the generated sand thickness, h, and the best guess surface, h0 for all members in the initial and updated ensemble consisting of 200 models each. As seen from this figure, the thickness in the initial ensemble of models is unbiased with respect to h, while for the updated ensemble, we observe clear spatial trends where both the thickness is increasing and decreasing.

Figure 13 further illustrates the effect of the DST data conditioning on the structural uncertainty. In particular, we notice that the P10 estimate for the thickness in the area between wells 16/3-8S and 16/3-5 approaches h0, implying that a certain thickness is required on the central part of the Avaldsnes High region to match the DST data in these two wells. Similarly, we need to reduce the potential upside (P90 estimate) for the thickness in the area northeast of well 16/3-4 to match the DST data.

Figure 14 and Figure 15 display the median and P10/P90 estimates for the permeability in one selected layer of the grid before and after conditioning to DST data. We notice the general trend of increased permeability in the area between wells 16/3-8S and 16/3-5, where the median permeability estimate increases from 50 Darcy to 60-80 Darcy and the P10 estimate is between 50 and 60 Darcy in large continuous areas.

Analyzing the bulk and in-place volumes before and after conditioning to the DST data (Figure 16) we see that the low case (P10) volumes are lifted 20 %, while the high case (P90) volumes are lifted 5 %. This effect is not surprising considering the effects seen in the estimated statistics for the thickness of Intra Draupne Sandstone shown in Figure 12 and Figure 13.
