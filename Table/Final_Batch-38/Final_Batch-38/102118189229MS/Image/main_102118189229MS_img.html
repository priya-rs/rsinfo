Kuwait Oil Company (KOC) has been producing oil from the Upper Burgan formation for over 60 years from the Raudhatain and Sabriyah oil fields of North Kuwait (Figure 1). Both fields are essentially very large dome shaped anticlines that have been highly faulted and are structurally complex. Raudhatain Upper Burgan is North Kuwait’s prolific producer under active water flood as well as with natural edge water drive. It’s a mature Reservoir with water cut all over the field but still with lot of production potential.

Raudhatain Upper Burgan (UB) is a multilayer clastic reservoir in North Kuwait under active waterflood since 2000. With four major flow units, poor sweep efficiency often results from spatial variation and/or heterogeneity in the permeability of the reservoir rock. These major and well-known different units, prevent the conventional analysis of Voidage Replacement Ratio (VRR) as other conventional reservoirs (Figure 2). The initial completion design did not address the injection profile control and the majority of water was going to the unit UB5.

The Upper Burgan Formation is the uppermost unit of the Burgan Formation. The Burgan Formation was deposited during the Albian period (Early Cretaceous) time and it is overlain by Mauddud Formation and underlain by Shuaiba Formation (Figure 3). The Upper Burgan reservoir is encountered at vertical depths of ~ 7600 feet at the crest of the structure in the Raudhatain field. The Upper Burgan reservoir in the Raudhatain field is approximately 160 feet thickness and the thickness varies in different parts of the field.

The Upper Burgan is represented by two end member depositional models in the Raudhatain field. These are a fluvial dominated, tidally influenced delta and a marine influenced shoreface model (Figure 4 & 5). In the Raudhatain Field four major stratigraphic surfaces have been identified and these are regarded as flooding surfaces or transgressive surfaces that can be correlated on a field wide basis. These stratigraphic surfaces separate the four clastic intervals that have been identified as the UB2, UB3, UB4, and UB5 layers. These clastic layers are the oil producing zones.

We can see from this typical Raudhatain well (Figure 6) that the UB5 and UB4 layers appear to be coarsening upwards sequences that represent the more marine shoreface deposits. The UB3 and UB2 layers however are thicker and often exhibit blocky or fining upwards sequences. These deposits are the fluvial dominated delta channel facies and are the highest reservoir quality rocks in the Upper Burgan Formation. The reservoir quality varies and is largely dependent on the rock type and depositional facies.

In order to manage the production, water flooding has been started in this reservoir since (2000) as a pilot, which was expanded in 2002 with seawater injection. Meanwhile, with the demand of pressure support and increasing number of producers, a new project in Upper Burgan for Effluent Water System has been started in 2014. Currently the injectors meet allowable that was specified based on simulation modeling target for Upper Burgan and it is approximately 200 MBWPD. The basic principle of WFR is from old Watermann patent form the 50’s. One piston is moveable and the other one is fixed as shown in Figure 16 with the size variable depending on the flow desired to be injected in that certain layer so the regulator is calibrated by predetermined flow rate.

Therfore, WFR would enable the operator to choke back injection rates in specific layers irrespective of the reservoir pressure, permeability, skin damage or any other factors that would normally affect flow. Each layer or zone is packed off to prevent any fluids within that zone of the wellbore invading another zone. An injection nozzle is located within each section. Several companies currently sell these valves based on the same principle. Figure 17 and 18 show some valves from one of these providers, normally the service include the valves and the tool for the instalation in the mandrel.

Step Rate Injectivity Test (SRIT) was done in two zones before installing the regulators. The aim of this test is to determine Formation Parting Pressure (FPP) so as to create fractures and channels prior to installing WFR valves. Figure 22 shows four plots, in the left side, the pressure and rate for the pump and in the right side the well-known SRIT plot for each layer.

The first implementation of WFR in Raudhatain Filed has proved the concept of improving vertical injection profile by enabling higher injection rates into layers that never received water before and regulating the distribution into all layers. As most of the water injected into the well (more than 70%) was traversing through one zone only before the job, the vertical distribution of injected water due to WFR approach resulted in choking the flow through this layer to about 60% and improve the flow through the remaining uncontacted layers like summirized in Table 1 and Figure 23.

In order to monitor the benefit, a survaillance plan was defined to track that increase of production and reserves by performing frequent PGOR tests and scheduling for PLT for the producers. Some of the preliminary well-testing showed increase of oil production after installing the WFR. In addition to that, monitoring the pump intake preussure as shown in Figure 24 was very important to see if reservoir pressure will increase in the offset producers.
