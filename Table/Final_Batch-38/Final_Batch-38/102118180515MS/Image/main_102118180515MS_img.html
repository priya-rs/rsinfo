Conventional PDC cutters drill by shearing the formation. Conical diamond elements (CDEs) drill formations by combining shearing and plowing rock-destruction mechanisms. The cutters’ unique conical geometry combined with ultrathick polycrystalline diamond (PCD) layer (Fig. 1) provide superior impact strength and wear resistance.

These conical elements are strategically placed across the bit face to contribute for a step change in drilling performance. These elements are placed from gauge to bit center (Figure 2) using a finite-element-analysis (FEA) -based modeling system. To ensure robustness of the CDE, significant research and development were invested to refine the design and manufacturing processes to optimize the element’s material properties. Although the CDEs are made of polycrystalline diamond similar to conventional PDC cutters, they are manufactured using proprietary equipment to achieve the necessary pressures to produce their significant impact strength and wear resistance. Compared with a conventional PDC cutter, a CDE is approximately twice as thick at the conical tip (Figure 3).

To quantitatively measure the CDE’s impact strength compared with a conventional PDC cutter, a series of laboratory tests were conducted using a single-cutter device. Both elements were dropped onto a hardened steel block with an impact force of 18,000 lbf [80,000 N]. The experiment would simulate transitional drilling of a PDC bit exiting soft shale then encountering hard limestone at an ROP of 60 ft/hr. The conventional PDC cutter suffered catastrophic failure on first impact which completely destroyed the cutter’s sharp edge (Figure 4).

Using data from both tests, hard-material scientists determined the CDE’s thick diamond table essentially doubles its impact strength and provides approximately 25–30% more wear resistance compared with a conventional premium PDC cutters (Figure 5). These properties suggest CDE bits have vast potential to improve run lengths and penetration rates in impact-prone applications including hard interbedded formations and conglomerates, in transitional drilling, and in formations that contain pyrite and chert.

After laboratory testing, engineers wanted to determine how the CDE would fail difficult rock formations. An FEA-based modeling system was used to avoid costly and time-consuming field trials. The virtual testing determined the CDE would produce concentrated point loading that can fail high-compressive strength formations more efficiently than conventional PDC cutters (Figure 6).

When developing the new CDE bits, designers surmised that the plowing action created by the conical diamond element would produce less torque than a conventional PDC cutter that aggressively shears formation. To test the hypothesis, extensive laboratory testing was performed that confirmed that conical elements drill with an average of 26% less torque than conventional PDC cutters (Figure 7).

Unwanted lateral and axial vibration that occurs during the wellbore construction process reduces drilling efficiency. The undesirable drillstring harmonics require more mechanical specific energy input to the system, which can lower ROP and reduce run lengths. Engineers used FEA modeling to compare a standard PDC cutter with the conical diamond element. Results showed that the CDE has a more balanced force profile when both are placed across the bit face (Figure 8).

In a bit design exercise, modeling confirmed the balanced profile equates to less lateral force per cutting element on CDE bits compared with conventional PDCs mitigating shock and vibration (Figure 9). The increased bit/BHA stability would translate into higher ROP and footage totals while prolonging the operating life of costly downhole measurement/steering tools. The reduction in vibration using CDE bits was then confirmed in a South Texas field test (Figure 10).

In field tests, CDE bits produced a significant amount of 2-mm cuttings by volume when compared with cuttings produced by conventional PDC bits in similar formations (Figure 11).

In Thailand, an operator wanted to exploit the Sin Phu Horm (SPH) gas field located in the province between city of Udon Thani and Khon Kaen of northeastern Thailand (Figure 12). SPH gas reservoirs are mostly the Permian Pha Nok Khao (PNK) carbonates. The reservoir formation was deposited in early to mid Permian as an inverted-horst platform shelf carbonate. To the east and west of the horst are deep grabens containing thick open marine siliciclastic sediments of the same age.

Achieving the drilling objectives to complete the well on time was a priority. Offset wells suffered from hole stability issues at shallow depths. Therefore, the best strategy for optimizing performance and minimizing drilling risk involves drilling the interval with minimum steering requirements and moving all directional requirements to a deeper interval. However the subject section comprised hard, dense, and abrasive sandstone, which posed steering difficulties, shock and vibration, and damage to the bit and BHA. With this strategy, the main challenge now is to achieve directional requirement (Figure 13), steering the well through hard, dense, and abrasive sandstones.

A rock-strength identification program was run on offset wells to determine the formations unconfined compressive strength (UCS) (Figure 14). This software package utilizes the input from from sonic, gamma ray, density, porosity, and mud logs to calculate the unconfined compressive strength. The study indicated that the formation drilled is mainly sandstone with UCS ranging from 18,000 to 21,000 psi with very high abrasion index and heavy impact indices.

The mineralogy chart (Figure 15) from the subject well indicated that illite and quartz content were higher throughout the interval. The presence of these minerals depicts how hard the formation was.

Roller cone TCI bits were the primary tool choice in the first two offset wells drilled in this field, which were vertical wells. The shorter interval drilled and reliability issues related to high total bit revelotuions, and torque symptoms caused numerous trips for tool changeout (Figure 16). However, in the next two offset wells, PDC bits were tried and resulted in multiple trips along with steering difficulties such as toolface fluctuations and high vibrations (Figure 17). The section TD was much shallower than the subject well.

Although it was evident the PDC’s shearing cutting action was the most effective rock failure mechanism and had the greatest potential to deliver higher ROP and extended run lengths compared with TCI, the durability of conventional fixed-cutter bits was not sufficient. The operator requested the provider implement a highly durable PDC bit (Figure 18) in the subject well as contingency option, the bit drilled only 407 ft [124 m] before being pulled out for poor ROP. On surface, the cutting structure showed initiation of ringout in the shoulder area.

To reduce the number of trips and improve the steerability, an innovative bit was designed with strategically positioned CDEs and conventional PDC cutters across the entire cutting structure from bit center to gauge (Figure 19). The bit’s cutting structure was modeled using proprietary simulation software, and analysis indicated that the new CDE bit type would improve performance and withstand the shock and vibration incurred while drilling the hard sandstone and limestone formation.

The 8½-in hybrid CDE bit was run on Well 4 following a TCI roller cone bit used to drill out the casing shoe (Figure 20). After completing the drillout run, the objective was to finish the remainder of the hole section using only PDC bits. Two CDE bits were run and demonstrated significant improvement in ROP, toolface control, and total meterage drilled. The CDEs worked as planned and protected the conventional PDC cutters from impact damage while drilling the hard sandstone intervals.
