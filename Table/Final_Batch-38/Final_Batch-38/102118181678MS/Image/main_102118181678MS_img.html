Geomechanical analyses were first conducted using using Advantek's log analysis software (@LogTM) applied to logs (Fig. 7) from Well #1. The goal in this case is to derive a set of mechanical properties along the injection well that allows us to determine when (or if) the formation will fracture; if a fracture is formed during injection, the analysis seeks to determine how the fracture itself would propagate (in particular, if the fracture would grow vertically through the containing layers).

The reservoir simulator showed that the reservoir can be divided into three regions during injection operations (Fig. 9). Inner region (displaced region) with brine composition equivalent to injected brine composition; middle region (transition region) where the injected and reservoir brines mixed together; outer region with composition of the reservoir brine.

The ratio of vertical to horizontal permeabilities (kz/kh) was assumed to be 0.1. The reservoir extended 10,000 feet in both x and y dimensions (Fig. 10) and constant pressure boundary conditions were used (with values set equal to the initial pore pressure). The injection wellbore was placed at the center of the aquifer and a neighboring offset well was noted to be approximately 0.25 miles from the point of injection.

Assuming the injected waste has a temperature of 60°F and using the geothermal gradient, the calculated formation temperature is 120°F. The injected waste will tend to cool down a cylindrical volume around the wellbore (Fig. 11). Although the formation temperature decreased during the injection operations, the simulator does not predict any thermal fractures to be formed during the injection operation.

Our results indicated that the permeability did not have any effect on the geochemical model – in particular, the concentrations of different ions and cations were identical for the two cases (Fig. 9). The higher permeability case showed a smaller radius of migration and a smaller transition region (Fig. 12). In both cases, migration extended further up-dip than down-dip. The only factor that the aquifer permeability affected is the formation permeability. Higher pressure values were noted at lower permeability case, which agrees with the results obtained in Fig. 5 (Figs. 13 & 14).

Two cases were run assuming different confining layers permeability (0.18 and 1.5 × 10−6 mD). The results showed that even after 50 years of waste injection at 10,000 bpd the concentration of the tracer in the upper and lower confining layer is zero. No vertical flow will take place and all the fluids will tend to flow in the aquifer sands with no leakage (Figs. 15 & 16).
