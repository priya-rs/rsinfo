A third history matching approach called ensemble based methods (Evensen, 2003) has gained relevance during recent years. In this algorithm, proxy model techniques can be combined with full field simulations. This creates a balance between having enough physics based full field simulations, with, extensive and rapid sampling of the uncertainty space by using proxy surfaces, thereby preserving accuracy in the results (Fig. 3).

The chance of certain events occurring, based on additional data such as field production history, are represented by the “likelihood”. The updates in our beliefs regarding the uncertainties, having incorporated the real data and its likelihood, are represented by the “posterior distribution”. In the MCMC method, posterior distributions obtained after assisted history matching are used for the prediction phase, thus, ensuring that a more robust view of the uncertainty ranges are used in generating the prediction and reserve forecasts (Fig. 4).

Parameter distri-butions were updated based on this analysis, where the model response indicated certain values to be more or less likely. Assisted History Matching was then performed by applying a Markov Chain Monte-Carlo and proxy based workflow. A large set of matched cases were rapidly generated from the use of proxy modelling and conditioned forecasts based on posterior distributions were obtained. The evolution of the history matches through this workflow is shown in Fig. 6. The final result was a stochastically generated P10-P50-P90 range of in-place and recoverable volumes.

In our experi-ence, it is better to purposely start with a wider range of static and dynamic uncertainties that could later be constrained based on insights gained from parameter correlation analysis in scoping cycles. Learnings from front-end manual history matching were also useful in framing uncertainties. Major reservoir uncertainties modelled were the field and channel facies extent, porosity and regional pore volume variations, water saturation, contacts, regional permeability variations, baffles, rock com-pressibility, residual gas saturation and connectivity & in-place volume of minor thin bed facies observed in some wells (Fig. 7).

In the current work, bottom-hole pressures (and where unavailable, tubing head pressures) for all the production wells were set as mismatch parameters for the calculation of the “Global” objective value. The “Standard Deviation” was set to 50. This value was back calculated by aiming to have the “Partial” objective value (“Base Value” in the blue box in Fig. 8) close to 1 for a very well matched case. This allowed the capability of inferring the quality of future history matches from the “Base Value” reported by the software. Correspondingly, at these settings, a reservoir model with near perfect matches for every well was expected to have a “Global” close to the number of mismatch parameters times number of wells active in the case (in this case, it equals to “Global” of 8).

A key parameter correlation analysis result is presented in Fig 9. It highlights the presence of a strong and meaningful V-shaped trend between the field in-place and “Global” objective value. Such a trend is not unexpected where the key volumetric uncertainties have been adequately scoped, and, where the field satisfies a key criterion that there are no majorly unconnected hydrocarbons. Physically, this trend is very meaningful and indicates that the connected gas in-place (primary pressure support) for this field must lie within a certain range to adequately represent the reservoir behaviour (represented by low “Global” values).

Another valuable application of such modern stochastic correlation analysis, (Fig. 10), is to get key insights in understanding parameter sensitivity in real world scenarios where conventional sensitivity analysis (such as Tornado plots) may not work. Typically, analyses such as Tornado plots are run to see the sensitivity of input uncertainties to model responses like in-place volume or reserves.

In each new cycle, key input parameter ranges were purposely varied to be on their conservative or optimistic end. The logic was to test if this volumetric convergence was an artifact of the way the uncertainties were framed or indeed a fundamental character of the reservoir itself. Each time, a similar converging trend and a narrow volumetric range was achieved, pointing to a consistent solution. Representative results from two cycles are shown in Fig 11a and 11b.

MCMC allows Bayesian optimization to be done in an unbiased manner but being a computationally expensive algorithm, a proxy modelling workflow was utilized in the software. The efficiency of the proxy workflow enables hundreds of proxy reservoir simulations to be run in a short time. The proxy model generation needs a training set of experiments for conditioning and robust predictivity and LHC scoping experiments were used for this. Out of 400 LHC scoping runs, top 150 cases with the lowest global value were selected for training a robust proxy (Fig. 14).

However, LHC typically has poor history matches since it is not designed for matching. On the other hand, MCMC is used as an effective assisted history matching technique but it has been observed in this study that matched cases tend to converge towards a high confidence and narrower volumetric range. Thus, the full set of cases (MCMC and LHC cycles) was pooled and an optimum filtering criterion applied to shortlist matches. This offered the best balance between covering a wide range of volumes and uncertainties and selecting higher quality matches. The combined 720 unfiltered cases show (Fig. 16) the familiar trend of volumetric convergence.

Filtered cases were used to stochastically define the P10 – P50 – P90 volumes and an acceptable cut-off was defined. A cut-off of “Global” < 50 was chosen, based on Fig. 17, to balance good matches with wider volume ranges. This was done as a higher “Global” cut-off of 100 did not add any more value in terms of widening the volumetric ranges but a tighter cut-off of 10 reduced the volumetric spread.

The filtered cases were considered representative of the dynamic behavior of the reservoir and utilized to estimate the stochastic P10-P50-P90 gas-in-place and recoverable volume as well as other parameter ranges. For purposes of completeness, the gas-in-place and recoverable volume histograms are depicted (Fig. 18a and 18b) and show a normal behavior. Fig. 19a and 19b demonstrate the final outcome of this study: a reduction in the uncertainty in this field’s recoverable volume and demonstrate the successful achievement of the overall objectives from this work.
