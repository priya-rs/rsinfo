The selected trial location was a single zone of a field on the Gulf of Mexico coastal plain containing a series of stacked Oligocene Frio sands. The chosen reservoir was 5 to 10 ft thick across the productive area and consisted of a comparatively clean sand, free from shale and interstitial cement. The interval was under active waterflood prior to the trial, with water cut measured at greater than 95%. A summary of the field attributes is shown in Table 2.

Water was injected immediately prior to polymer injection to stabilise the reservoir, and acquire a baseline to which polymer injectivity could be directly compared. Following this, polymer was injected at a constant concentration for a period long enough to fully sweep the trial area. Upon completion of the polymer injection stage, there was a further stage of water injection to investigate the dispersal of the polymer bank. At the end of the trial, the opportunity was taken to test operational scenarios related to deployment of reservoir-triggered polymers. Here, polymer was injected before the injector was shut-in to simulate an unplanned shut down and allow the polymer to trigger in the near-wellbore area. Following this, water injection was restarted to see if the triggered polymer could be moved away. The duration and injection volume of each stage is shown in Table 3.

The salinity began to rise again shortly after polymer injection started, increasing to 34,000 mg/l TDS, before gradually decreasing again to the end of the trial. This may have been due to changes in the injection rate during polymer start-up, short-term changes in production rate, or a coning-like effect at the producer where higher mobility and salinity fluids from areas to the east of the producer were drawn towards the producer. This is explored further through simulations. By the end of the field trial, near-complete tracer decay had been seen from all 3 tracers and quantitative analysis using the moments method (Shook, Pope and Asakawa 2009) was performed. Results from this analysis are shown in Table 4.

The rock properties were uniform in permeability, porosity, and net-to-gross. The field trial area was well swept by water so the models were initialised with no mobile oil phase. The thermal conductivity and capacity of the rock and fluids were, in the absence of field data, assumed uniform over the field. The downhole injection temperature was set to 55°C, based on continuous downhole temperature measurements, and the initial reservoir temperature was 76°C based on measurements taken post-drilling. This trial has been compared with other recently published polymer field trials. Details of the specific trials are shown in Table 7. Comparison is made on the basis of the mass of polymer injected per unit area of open formation, and the number of pore volumes injected through a cylinder of 10 m radius around the injector. A radius of 10 m was chosen for the comparison as, with fluid velocity being the inverse of the square of the radius, the radius of the high-shear region in which damage to the polymer chain usually occurs, will be less than this.
