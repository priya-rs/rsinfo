Figure 1 (a) shows the rheological profiles of untriggered and triggered polymer and demonstrates that, in its untriggered form, the inverted polymer in SSW has water-like viscosity. In comparison, the triggered polymer solution has a viscosity commensurate with an HPAM polymer of equivalent molecular weight and concentration. It can also be seen that the untriggered polymer acts a Newtonian fluid – that is, it is unaffected by shear. Once triggered, the polymer backbone is extended and indistinguishable from HPAM and therefore demonstrates the same non-Newtonian shear-thinning behaviour.

A map of the area of the field in which the trial was conducted is shown in Figure 2. The chosen area for the trial was near the crest of the field with few active wells in the vicinity. The nearest injector and producer were to the northwest of the field trial wells. There were no active wells to the south and the nearest active well to the east was more than 3,000 ft away. Production from the interval was steady over the period of the trial with minimal changes in injection volumes or average reservoir pressure.

Step-rate tests were run regularly during the trial to provide an estimate of the fracture pressure of the formation and to calibrate injectivity in models. The injection rate, brine density and temperature were recorded continuously, while production rates were measured by regular well tests. The rate of oil production was estimated by visual inspection of the twice-daily wellhead samples. A timeline of the key surveillance activities is shown in Figure 3.

The pressure history from the downhole gauge in the injection well is shown alongside the injection rate in Figure 4. Periods of water injection are shown in blue and polymer injection in orange. Water was first injected for 43 days, in order to create a cooled zone around the well, as would occur in real field applications, and to provide baseline performance. Polymer was then injected for 43 days and was followed by 24 days of chase water injection. The final stage of the test was to examine the impact of shutting in the well for a significant period during polymer injection. For this purpose, polymer injection was restored for 3 days, followed by a shut-in period of 3 days, and then chase water.

The bottom-hole pressure recorded during the PFO tests is shown in Figure 5. The increase of reservoir pressure around the well, from 1150 psi during waterflood to approximately 1800 psi by the end of polymer injection, is apparent.

Information on the rising reservoir pressure from the PFOs has been combined with the injection pressure data to produce the Hall plot of injectivity shown in Figure 6. The rapid drop in injection pressure described above is clearly seen by the change in gradient at the start of polymer injection.

Most of the injection, including all the polymer injection, is believed to have occurred below fracture pressure, as was desired, in order to avoid complicating the interpretation. A step-rate test during waterflood showed that fracture pressure was 3070 psi with initial reservoir pressure of 1150 psi, and it rose during polymer injection in response to the significant increase in reservoir pressure (Figure 7). A repeat test performed during the polymer flood showed a fracture pressure of 3170 psi.

Figure 8 shows the salinity and tracer concentration measurements of water samples collected from the production wellhead. Breakthrough time for the injected water was 4 days, and the rapid decrease in produced water salinity is a strong indication that a large proportion of the injected fluid was passing through a single layer in the reservoir. This is further supported by the quality of the match to salinity data from single-layer simulation models.

The concentration of polymer in the effluent is shown in Figure 9. Polymer was detected in surface samples at the producer 9 days after polymer injected started. In comparison, Polymer Tracer 1, injected on the first day of polymer injection, reached the producer in 3 days. Polymer concentration increased steadily post-breakthrough before decreasing when the production rate was increased to approximately 500 bbl/d. A return of the production rate to 150 bbl/d led to an increase in polymer concentration to the maximum 1000 mg/l level expected based on the injection concentration. This was achieved approximately 24 days after the start of polymer injection.

To derive the pre-exponential factor and activation energies we have used viscosity-trends with time and temperature from laboratory bottle-tests. The rate response appears to be different at different temperatures, which has required us to model the reaction with a temperature-dependent activation energy where each temperature range uses the Arrhenius dependence of the rate constant on temperature. The match to viscosity trends with time is shown in Figure 12 (a) and is acceptable in the range of temperatures present in the model. Note that at 50°C the kinetic model is slower than measured data, whilst at 70°C the viscosification is quicker. However, at the temperatures present in the model the match is acceptable.

Polymer retention has been represented in the model by a Langmuir-like irreversible adsorption isotherm. Whilst retention may be due to adsorption to solid surfaces or trapping within small pores, both mechanisms lead to a delay in propagation. The untriggered and triggered forms of the polymer are assigned two different adsorption levels, in recognition of the laboratory evidence suggesting the untriggered polymer has higher retention. The isotherms used are shown in Figure 13.

The basic rock properties and dispersivity in the model were tuned using the salinity of the effluent and concentrations of the waterflood tracer in wellhead samples during the waterflood phase of the trial. The match to the swept volume is accurate, and, as shown in Figure 14, the qualitative changes in salinity and tracer concentration in response to well control events are captured in the model. The skin of the injector and total compressibility were further guided by PTA. We found it was important to carefully tune the total compressibility in the model to provide an adequate match during periods when injection and/or production rates were changing. PFO2 at the end of the waterflood phase was also simulated using the reservoir model and a comparison with field data is shown in Figure 11.

The match to polymer concentrations is shown in Figure 15. The breakthrough time is captured in the model; however, there is a poor match in the period between 67 and 71 days. An investigation of the model at these times shows that the cause of the low concentration is due to a coning-like effect described earlier in this paper, resulting in mixing in the production well and dilution of the produced concentrations.

Figure 16 shows concentration maps of triggered polymers during polymer production. This effect may be seen on the concentration map at day 75, when the produced concentration is only 700 mg/l, however, the bank of polymer has gone past the production well. The concentration of produced polymer at this time in the model (and in the field) was controlled by the physics of ‘coning’ rather than polymer propagation characteristics. This, in part, is the source of the discrepancy between the model and the field data. The extent of ‘coning’ is governed by the production rate.

The predicted and measured injection pressures and well rates are shown in Figure 17. During the waterflood phase injection pressure is modelled to within 10% of measured rates. The 3% gas saturation modelled as a high rock compressibility was required to match the decline in injection pressure seen from day 5 to 25. The mechanical skin required in the model was +15. Whilst this is lower than the global skin of +25 estimated from PTA, the contribution to skin from thermal cooling is modelled separately in these simulations.

The trial described in this paper was the shortest in duration and injected the smallest mass of polymer but it compares favourably with other trials given the thickness of the reservoir interval. The quantity of polymer injected, in mass per unit area, was greater than, or of the same order as, other trials. The number of pore volumes injected through a 10-m radius around the injector was similar to the Matzen trial and significantly greater than others (see Figure 20). Injectivity reduction is a particular concern during polymer trials. In other trials, there was evidence of declining injectivity over time, attributed to the shear-thickening behaviour of HPAM polymers in the high-shear near-wellbore region (Lotfollahi, et al. 2016). Despite the comparable injection quantity, no injectivity decline was seen with this polymer; and the polymer backbones were protected when passing through the high-shear region.
