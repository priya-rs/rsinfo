The Jasmine Field is commonly used to represent the Jasmine and Ban Yen Field. These are located within Block B5/27 in the Gulf of Thailand, approximately 300 km southeast of Bangkok, on the northwestern flank of the Tertiary Pattani Basin in water depths of is 190 to 200ft (Figure 1). The Jasmine field was discovered in 1974 by AMOCO and Idemitsu, and is currently 100% owned, and operated, by Mubadala Petroleum (Thailand) Ltd. The Jasmine Field complex consists of a large number of extensional and strike-slip fault blocks containing multiple sandstone reservoirs, largely having individual oil and gas water contacts.

The five platforms of the Jasmine field have produced a cumulative 65 million barrels oil at the end of 2016. Oil production comes from over 160 pools across 30 fault blocks and 30 separate reservoirs as shown in Figure 2; hydrocarbon columns are relatively short. Historically, most wells were clustered near the crest of the structural highs, but more recently unswept oil has been developed by wells targeting further downdip areas. A detailed history of the field is discussed by Oglesby et al. (2014).

Above this reservoir the Jasmine reservoirs are typically 20-80 ft thick and comprise a series of medium- to coarse-grained sandstones interbedded with mudstones. The reservoirs are interpreted to be fluvial dominated sands, with possible subsidiary lacustrine and deltaic (mouthbar/shoreface) influence. Seismic images particularly from the upper portion of the reservoir succession, record meandering channel patterns. Net to gross is generally high at 30-40 percent, and reservoirs are mostly high porosity 22-28 percent, and high permeability. Figure. 3 shows a type log from the Jasmine field.

The BY31 horizontal well was proposed to develop the oil-bearing Y250 sandstone reservoir. The block has multiple oil-bearing reservoirs and was originally developed by only two deviated wells. Recent integrated evaluation suggested that at least two additional wells are required to further develop the oil reserves in the block. One of them is a horizontal well targeting the undeveloped oil in the Y250 reservoir as shown in Figure 4. Another proposed well is BY30, a deviated well targeting attic oil in the deeper Y245 reservoir that was not produced by the existing producers.

The BY31 schematic well plan prior to drilling the BY30 pilot/producer well is shown in Figure 5a. The horizontal well path aimed to follow the top of the Y250 reservoir, not a true horizontal path. Adjustment to the well plan was then made based on the BY30 well data. The landing point was 25 ft deeper than the initial plan prior to BY30 pilot/producer well. Figure 5b illustrates the revised well plan after drilling BY30 well.

The CE391 pilot well was proposed as an appraisal pilot to investigate the extent of an oil accumulation in the Y391 sandstone reservoir in this fault block. The Y391 oil accumulation was previously interpreted and has been produced only in the up dip part of the block. There is a strong stratigraphic control on the reservoir distribution as demonstrated by seismic attribute extraction and existing well data. Seismic mapping identified potential barriers between updip and the proposed downdip appraisal location as show in Figure 6.
