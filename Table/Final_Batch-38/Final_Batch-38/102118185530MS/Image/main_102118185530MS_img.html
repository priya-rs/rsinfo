The Vizcacheras field is located in the Cuyana Basin, in the Mendoza Province, Argentina (Fig.1).

Vizcacheras is a mature oil field producing from two fluvial sandstone formations, Barrancas and Papagayos (Fig.2a). The object of the study is the Papagayos formation that has been exploited since 1965. The field was initially developed with a 600 m well spacing. Subsequently central areas were infilled resulting in a 300 m spacing.

In the production history of Papagayos Fm., we see a rapid increment of water production in the early years (since 1968) due to aquifer inflow during the reservoir exploitation (Fig.3a). Currently, wells produce with 98% water cut. In addition, in the pressure history of Papagayos Fm.; we observe a gradual and slow decline in pressure over three decades due to the aquifer support (Fig.3b). The pressure reduced from 154 bar originally to the present pressure of 100 bar.

We used a Carter-Tracy analytical model to represent the aquifer. This model uses tables of dimensionless time versus a dimensionless pressure influence function. In this case, the table was for an infinite aquifer with constant terminal rate as given by Van Everdingen and Hurst. Table 1, present the final parameters used in the history match and we see in Fig. 4 the aquifer – reservoir cell connections.

Initially, we used the extensive history production data set and the static pressure measurements (RFTs) to constrain the history matching. The key matching parameters were the curvature of relative permeability curves, faults transmissibility and sands continuity in the northwest of the reservoir. We constrained simulation runs by liquid rate control and varied match parameters until global oil and water rates were simultaneously matched (Fig. 5). Once field rates were matched within an acceptable tolerance, we focused on the pressure match of individual wells with respect to RFT data.

The pressure gradient data showed little dispersion between wells throughout the reservoir for any given date, as we see in the example in Fig. 6. To replicate this, it was necessary to increase reservoir permeability significantly in comparison to the permeability needed in the first phase of history matching. We can see in Fig. 7a, the measured static pressure gradients corrected to 1000 mTVDSS datum.

Fig. 8, shows that in 2000 there was an increase in pressure that cannot be explained through the small recorded volume of injected water into the formation. At the same time as the pressure in Papagayos reservoir was seen to increase, large volumes of disposal water were started to be injected into the base of the Barrancas Fm., a high permeability, non-hydrocarbon bearing interval. This led to a hypothesis of pressure communication with the underlying reservoir.

Fig. 9, shows that we achieved a satisfactory pressure history match of individual wells.

As mentioned previously, we first matched RFT data. In the tracks of Fig. 10, we see simultaneously the simulated pressures and RFT data as yellow circles. It is observed that wells closer to the west, commonly known as Vizcacheras-West (Vizcacheras West, what is assumed to be an isolated accumulation), have a lower pressure. This is thought to be because the aquifer has less impact on this sector since it lies further away from the OWC and water needs to pass through a more tortuous pathway to reach this zone.

Additionally, the model was used to simulate changes in water saturation through time. Fig. 11 shows simulated water saturation in a dip section of the full field model for different dates. We see that due to the high vertical permeability in the reservoir, gravitational forces dominate over the viscous forces, thus resulting in water under ride. Therefore, the aquifer swept the lower sand, leaving oil at the top, the aforementioned attic oil. This supports the hypothesis that the remaining oil is trapped hydrodynamically.

Another example supporting the mobile attic oil hypothesis is observed in well logs. We have the case of well O and well O(I), the latter a replacement well drilled 29 years after the former in the same location. For each well, Fig. 12a shows in the first track the SP log, in the second the resistivity log and in the third the simulated water saturation. In the original well, the interval with the highest resistivity is comparable with the highest oil saturation. In the replacement well, that is 30 meters away, it is observed that the zone with oil saturation was reduced and is located in the upper part of the sand.

A second alternative considered was injecting in a line drive. To determinate the optimal location of the injectors we analysed the well production efficiency. The field was divided into six zones as a function of the current mobile oil saturation; these regions are defined in Table 3.Fig. 13, shows the percentage of oil production from wells belonging to a zone with regard to initial mobile oil in that same region.

The forecast simulator shows that the flank water injection scenario produces a 1 % incremental oil to the end of concession over the NFA case (Fig.14). It can be seen that although this is only a small percentage in comparison with the field historical production, it is still a substantial oil volume of what was once the most productive field in YPF’s portfolio.

We proposed a pilot to reduce the development risk. The greatest concern about implementing the pilot was whether it would be possible to see the effect of energy increase on oil production when using only two injectors. We assessed different locations for the Pilot and we finally selected the Southeast area of the field. This location has a lower density of producer wells, which should result in a greater sensitivity to injection response. Fig. 15 shows that well 1 and well 2 are the wells that should be monitored in the pilot.

In the experiment the flow cell was initially saturated with water and after that with oil to Swir. Subsequently we injected water at 23 cm3/h. Fig. 16, shows a sequence of the cell oil saturation at different times in the experiment. When reaching a 98% water cut (Similar to the current water cut on Vizcacheras), we see that oil still remains on the top of the cell.

To represent the augmenting of the aquifer that we are proposing, we doubled the injection rate to see if we can sweep the attic oil. Fig. 17, shows three consecutive pictures, two hours apart, of the oil saturation in the cell. Between figures 1 and 2, at a 98% water cut, the difference in the oil profile is almost imperceptible. In figure 3 there is a significant thinning of the attic oil and hence a change in the oil saturation as a result of increasing the water injection rate.
