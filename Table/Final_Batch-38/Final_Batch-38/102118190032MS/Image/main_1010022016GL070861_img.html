A 3D box of dimension 2 km × 2 km × 2.5 km in the x, y and z directions, respectively, is our computational domain as shown in Figure 3. A dome-shaped reservoir with four layers is located within the depth interval of 1.4 km to 1.6 km. The reservoir is confined between overburden and under burden and is intersected by a non-conductive fault dipping 80 degrees to west and striking north-south. We discretize the domain with approximately 60,800 hexahedral elements.

We assume that the model is saturated with water and use hydrostatics to initialize pressure. The topography of the reservoir in a vertical cross-section is shown in Figure 4. The wells are located in the hanging wall and footwall blocks of the fault. For the four cases studies considered here, the well in the hanging wall block is a producer with a constant flow rate of 3000 bbl/day. The well in the footwall block is different among the four cases as shown in Table 2. The reservoir permeability is kept constant at 100 mD in all the four cases.

First, we will look at the pressure changes on the two sides of the fault the hanging wall side and the footwall side (Figure 5, Middle and Right) to verify that the changes are consistent with the well operations. Next, we analyze the changes in fault tractions and fault slip along the fault on a line the location of which is shown in Figure 5, Left. We also analyze the evolution of stress path on two points the Top point and the Bottom point (Figure 5, Left) carefully selected along the fault line.

Figures 6 and 7 show the pressure changes on the hanging and footwall sides of the fault at the end of the simulation at 435 days. Positive pressure changes, i.e. overpressure, result from injection and negative pressure changes, i.e. pressure depletion, result from production. One important thing to note is that on the footwall side of ProdSimilar, we see larger drop in pressure although the footwall side well is producing at a rate lower than the hanging wall side. This is due to a smaller pore volume of the footwall block reservoir compared to the hanging wall block reservoir (see Figure 4).

Slip nucleates on the fault at the bottom of the top reservoir layer where the change in effective normal traction is tensile and the change in shear traction is downdip. A downdip change in shear traction is consistent with the production-induced contraction of the reservoir. The slip direction at the nucleation point is downdip. This induces a downdip pull on the fault nodes in the overburden layer immediately above the reservoir.

When we observe Figure 12, we notice that the shear traction change should be magnified over time because both sides of the reservoir contracts in Prod and ProdSimilar cases. In the Inj and InjSimilar cases, the footwall side contracts and the hanging wall side expands. However, we see that the shear changes are actually magnified in the producer-injector case instead of the producer-producer case as shown in Figure 8. This can be attributed to the sign convention used in the simulator. The simulator uses the fault normal vector to attribute signs for the shear traction.
