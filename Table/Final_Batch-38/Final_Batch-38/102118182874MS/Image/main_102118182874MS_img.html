Oil formation volume factor (Bo) uncertainty varies from 1.39 to 1.9. Due to the complexity of Field U fluid properties, there is no clear relationship between Bo and depth as can be seen from Figure 4. There is both a lateral and vertical variation of Bo across field U. As a result of this complex variation, it is sometimes observed that at similar depths, the fluid property such as Bo varies greatly. The uncertainty in terms of Bo variation is given in Figure 5 per well in Field U. The tornado plot in Figure 3 shows the impact of Bo, and that Bo has the second most significant effect on the STOIIP. This impact was quantified to range between -5% and 8% on the STOIIP.

Figure 6 shows the uncertainty variation per well. In quantifying this range both log and core used. Both types of data were used in order to quality check each form of data against one another. In cases where there are large differences between the OWC calculated from log and cores, the respective well data was excluded as outliners. The tornado plot in Figure 3 shows that OWC has third most significant impact on the STOIIP, with a minimum impact of -6% and a maximum of 7% on STOIIP.

The height above free water level (HAFWL) or saturation height function (SHF) uncertainty varies between +/- 15ft. This variation is a function of the reservoir properties, the less the reservoir quality in terms of porosity for example, the higher change in capillary pressure hence higher HAFWL/FWL. The HAFWL/SHF was defined based on two wells (Well 18 and Well 49) where, Special Core Analysis were conducted including mercury injection capillary pressure (MICP) measurements. Figure 7 shows the variation on HAFWL as defined from Well 18 and Well 49. The impact of HAFWL is shown in Figure 3 and as can be seen varies for the minimum of -4% to 2% change in STOIIP.

The porosity uncertainty shows the least impact on STOIIP as can be seen from Figure 3. i.e. -1% to 2%change in STOIIP. The reason for this parameter having the least impact is based on the fact that most of the wells where data was arcquired always collected porosity. As a result, the porosity distribution between the wells, is well understood and can be predicted within reason. The distribution between wells is shown in Figure 8. As can be seen porosity varies between 25%pu & 5%pu.

Reservoir rock types were quantified from the two wells, were SCAL was conducted i.e. Well 18 and Well 49. As seen from Figure 9 the SCAL MICP and pore throat size distribution results different number of RRT was quantified in both wells. As a result of the integration of SCAL from both wells, a total of 5 RRT were defined. Each RRT was assigned its respectively relative permeability and capillary pressure based on measured data in the laboratory. Sensitivity on RRT involves varying the RRT from the worst to best RRT, its respectively relative permeability and capillary pressure and measuring its effect on the recovery. As can be seen in Figure 10 the tornado plot shows that RRT has the largest effect on the recovery factor with a minimum of -4% and a maximum of 2%.

As gas cap exists in the reservoir the extent of communication between the gas cap and the reservoir was investgated as part of the sensitivity. In order to mimic the communication between gas cap and the oil zone, transimissiblity was varied and its effect on recovery quantified. The gas cap effect on the recovery is shown in Figure 10. As can be seen the effect varies from 0% to 4%. The gas cap and oil in the reservoir is shown in the ternary plot on Figure 11.

The dense between the reservoir subzones varies, in terms of its development. With some more developed than others. In the crest the dense is less developed however, the dense in the flank is more developed. As such sensitivity was conducted by varying the dense development in form of transmissibility. As can be seen from Figure 10, variation of the dense development between subzones has a minimum impact on recovery factor of 0% and a maximum of 4%. Figure 12 shows the dense within the subzones that were modified.

There are 81 faults distributed around the reservoir as shown in Figure 13. The communication between these faults is an uncertainty and as such had to be investigated. As such sensitivity was conducted by varying the communication between the faults. Faults transmissibility was varied between fully sealing, partial sealing and fully open. The effect of varying the fault transimissiblity on the recovery factor is shown in Figure 10, as can be seen the minimum effect is -2% and the maximum is 0%.

Using the base case model, dynamic parameters were combined in order to conduct the uncertainty analysis. A study was conducted in order to understand the combined effect of the uncertainties on the history match. In certain cases where the history match was lost from the combination, as shown in Figure 14 this case were exluded from the prediction.

Based on the cases where changes in the uncertainties parameter did not affect the history match significantly, a base case, pessimistic case and optimistic case were created and is shown in terms of recover factory in Figure 15. As can be seen in the figure the optimistic case, base case and pessimistic case have the recovery factor of 52%, 48% and 43% respectively. Figure 15 shows that the optimistic case, base case and pessimistic case can maintain plateau to 2045, 2043 and 2039 respectively.
